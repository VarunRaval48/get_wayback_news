Obama and Lawmakers Seek Accord on Health Care

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By ROBERT PEAR and SHERYL GAY STOLBERG
Published: January 13, 2010

WASHINGTON — With a growing sense of urgency, President Obama and top Congressional Democrats held a marathon negotiating session on Wednesday in an effort to thrash out agreements on sweeping health care legislation that could provide insurance coverage to more than 30 million Americans.

Senior House Democrats said the talks were making progress, but had not produced firm agreements.

The discussions, which ran from 10:30 a.m. to 6:40 p.m., were, in effect, a substitute for a Congressional conference committee, the customary means of resolving differences between the House and the Senate on major legislation.

Mr. Obama stepped out of the meeting to telephone foreign leaders, including the prime minister of Canada and the president of Mexico, about the devastating earthquake in Haiti. But in their talks, Congressional leaders said, Mr. Obama focused on the health bill, expressed his preferences on several issues and tried to guide them toward compromises.

The White House has been eager to show substantial progress on the legislation before Mr. Obama delivers his State of the Union address in late January or early February. And the apparent tightening of the Senate race in Massachusetts — a special election to replace the late Senator Edward M. Kennedy is Tuesday — has left some Democrats nervous about the risk of losing the crucial 60th vote they need to pass the bill in the Senate.

Those who attended included Speaker Nancy Pelosi of California and the Senate majority leader, Harry Reid, of Nevada, as well as committee chairmen who have worked on the legislation.

In a joint statement Wednesday night, Mr. Obama, Ms. Pelosi and Mr. Reid said they had made “significant progress in bridging the remaining gaps” between the House and Senate bills. Having worked through all aspects of the legislation, they said, “We’re encouraged and energized.”

Representative Robert E. Andrews, Democrat of New Jersey, a subcommittee chairman who was briefed on the negotiations, said: “President Obama has his sleeves rolled up and is in the middle of the discussions. He is choosing the right moment to be engaged. His effectiveness will be higher because he waited until the last moment.”

The long talks at the White House were unusual and suggested that Mr. Obama, Ms. Pelosi and Mr. Reid wanted to close a deal as soon as possible. Lawmakers often meet with the president, but usually for no more than an hour or two.

Among the issues discussed was how to pay for the legislation — specifically, whether to impose a new excise tax on high-cost employer-sponsored health plans; whether to increase the payroll tax used to finance Medicare; and whether to apply the tax to investment income, like interest, dividends and capital gains.

Lawmakers said they and Mr. Obama also tried to come up with a compromise on whether to establish a single national health insurance exchange, as proposed by the House, or dozens of state exchanges, as proposed by the Senate. Democrats see the exchange as a competitive marketplace where consumers could compare and buy insurance policies with federal subsidies.

Mr. Obama has endorsed the excise tax, included in the Senate bill, as a way to slow the growth of health spending. But labor union leaders and House Democrats said emphatically on Wednesday that they still opposed the tax because it would hit many middle-income workers and retirees who had sacrificed pay raises for health benefits.

“Health care reform should be financed by tax surcharges on the wealthy and not by taxes on health insurance plans offered to middle-class workers, older persons and union members,” said Representative Lynn Woolsey, Democrat of California and co-chairwoman of the Congressional Progressive Caucus.

The chairmen of Verizon and AT&T weighed in with their concerns. In a letter to Congress joined by two union presidents, the executives, Ivan G. Seidenberg of Verizon and Randall L. Stephenson of AT&T, said the excise tax would “impact health plans covering tens of millions of workers” in various industries.

To avoid the tax, they predicted, many employers would cut back on dental and vision coverage. Mr. Obama began the negotiations with a short meeting with Ms. Pelosi and Mr. Reid at the White House in the Oval Office. They moved to the Cabinet Room, where they were joined by other Democratic leaders and committee chairmen. Congressional aides sat around a long table in the Roosevelt Room and were called into the talks when their expertise was needed.

House Republicans, staunchly opposed to the health care bill, said the secrecy of the negotiations violated Mr. Obama’s frequent promises of greater openness and undermined the legitimacy of any agreements that might be reached.

“Where is the transparency?” asked Representative Phil Gingrey, Republican of Georgia. “The American people need to know. We need openness and sunshine. With health care, we are talking about one-sixth of the economy.”

Democrats cited several reasons for the urgency of the health care talks. More people, they said, are losing health insurance every day.

“If we do nothing, if, God forbid, this bill crashes and burns, we could have 60 or 80 or 100 million people without insurance,” said Representative Eliot L. Engel, Democrat of New York.

Some public opinion polls show sagging support for the legislation, and Democrats said that any further delay would give Republicans more time to mobilize opposition to the bill. Moreover, several Democrats expressed concern about the impact of an upset victory by the Republican candidate for Senate in the special election in Massachusetts next week. Democratic leaders are eager to finish the health bill so they can turn their attention to jobs and the economy.

Lawmakers apparently spent little time discussing abortion at the White House. The House bill would impose more stringent restrictions than the Senate on insurance coverage of abortion.

Congressional leaders said they had talked to White House officials about differences on the expansion of Medicaid and subsidies to help low-income people buy private insurance.

Representative Frank Pallone Jr., Democrat of New Jersey, said House leaders were insisting that Medicaid be made available to people with incomes less that 150 percent of the poverty level ($33,075 for family of four), rather than 133 percent as provided in the Senate bill.
