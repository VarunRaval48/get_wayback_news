Its Owner Gone, a Music Shop Preserves a Fading Sound

Richard Perry/The New York Times

Doo-wop records fill the shelves at Clifton Music, a store run for years by Ronnie I, a promoter who died in 2008.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By PETER APPLEBOME
Published: January 27, 2010

CLIFTON, N.J.

Skip to next paragraph

Related

Times Topics: Peter Applebome

In the front of Ronnie I’s Clifton Music is a 2,000-pound granite tombstone dedicated in 1988 to the pioneering rock ’n’ roll singer Frankie Lymon of the Teenagers — bought with $3,000 in donations and then never used when his widow decided she did not want it.

Attached to the tin ceiling are old LPs and 45s, hanging overhead like shiny planets from some forgotten galaxy.

In the bins of CDs, LPs, 45s, and 78s are thousands of reminders of the music invariably thought of as the soundtrack to the 1950s — the Five Satins, Ink Spots, Spaniels and Harptones, the Cardinals, Wrens, Orioles and Larks.

What is not there, not anymore, is Ronnie I — Ronnie Italiano, the indefatigable, happily obsessed fan, promoter and entrepreneur of early group harmony rock ’n’ roll who ran the store from 1972 until he died in 2008 at 67.

Rock ’n’ roll will never die, as Danny and the Juniors noted long before Neil Young did. But, alas, we will, and most of what we did often disappears with us. So who knows how long Ronnie I’s shop can outlive Ronnie I. But on Main Street in Clifton, a respectful hat tip to this time capsule to a time capsule and the fragile sliver of musical history it still represents.

Ronnie I, who grew up in East Rutherford, hated the term doo-wop, which he considered an inauthentic parody of what he preferred to call vocal group harmony. But for the music forever associated with its intricate harmonies and bops, pows, yips and other vocal filigrees — “Earth Angel,” “Why Do Fools Fall in Love,” “Sh-Boom” — it is the term people know, hence those Doo Wop University sweatshirts on sale at the shop.

If Ronnie hated the term, he loved the music with a manic passion. So when the local record store came up for sale, he ditched his job driving a soda delivery truck and jumped in. For a few years he maintained a general music business. Then he went all-in on the sound he loved — no Elvis, no Bill Haley and His Comets; all doo-wop, all the time.

He sold it at the store. He recorded it on his Clifton record label. He brought dozens of groups back together to perform and record. He did radio broadcasts, put on shows, wrote about it, dreamed about it. Most important, perhaps, he founded the United in Group Harmony Association, which at its peak had 2,000 members (Paul Simon among them) who turned out for the hundreds of shows he put on.

“He worked 24 hours a day, seven days a week,” said Bill Olb, a friend from the store’s beginnings. “He was in the recording studio, he was in the store, he did more than 500 concert shows, 100 record releases, more than 60 albums and CDs. There’s no replacement for Ronnie I. Everyone knows that.”

It was an uphill battle. Even on oldies radio, oldies long ago came to mean the Beatles and the Supremes, not the Impalas and the Hurricanes. And then came a bout with cancer, a final trip from the hospital to host the group harmony association meeting and show No. 354, and then he was gone, March 4, 2008.

The store is now run by his widow, Sandra, who grew up in the 1980s in love with doo-wop while her friends were listening to Duran Duran. She met Ronnie at one of his shows in 1994, when she was 23, and married him a year later.

Mrs. I would like to keep the store going as long as possible, increasingly depending on the store’s online sales, but nothing’s certain.

“I think about it all the time,” she said. “If the economy picks up and people start buying, I’ll be here for a while. If it doesn’t, I don’t know. I’d hate to close the door to the shop. It would be closing the door on history, closing the door on my husband’s dream.”

Most of the faithful who used to hang out on Saturdays have moved to Florida or died. The stores in town increasingly reflect the influx of immigrants: a Colombian bakery across the street, Bent al Sultan Fashions a few doors down. Over an hour or two one day this week, two people walked in, one looking confused, the other looking for someone to repair a fax machine.

There was always something innocent and haunting about the music Ronnie I loved and something ephemeral, too — Moonglows, Limelites, Solitaires — fleeting sounds, silhouettes on the shade, music soon gone, but like Ronnie I’s little oasis, somehow still alive.
