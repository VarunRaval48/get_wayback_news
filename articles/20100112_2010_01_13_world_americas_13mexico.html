Mexico Holds Drug Suspect Accused of Grisly Tactics

Alexandre Meneghini/Associated Press

Teodoro Garcia Simental, center, one of Mexico’s most wanted drug traffickers, was escorted by federal police after being presented to the press in Mexico City on Tuesday.

By MARC LACEY
Published: January 12, 2010

MEXICO CITY — When it comes to gore, Mexico’s drug traffickers seem to compete among themselves for the title of most depraved. One will chop off the heads of victims. Another will string dead rivals from bridges or burn their genitals. Recently, hit men removed the face from a dead man and sewed it onto a soccer ball.

On Tuesday, Mexican authorities announced the capture of one of those who they said had been active in this game of one upsmanship, Teodoro Eduardo Garcia Simental, described as a ruthless drug lord based just south of the American border in Tijuana. Mr. Garcas trademark, when not trafficking marijuana and methamphetamine to the United States, was boiling rivals in barrels of lye in what has become known as pozole, for the Mexican stew, the authorities said.

Ramn Eduardo Pequeo, a federal police commander, said the authorities detained Mr. Garca early Tuesday morning at a home in La Paz, on the southern end of Baja California, and later presented him in Mexico City, news that was welcomed on both sides of the border. “This is a big-time arrest,” said Rusty Payne, a spokesman for the Drug Enforcement Administration in Washington. “We’re seeing a pattern here, a lot of high-profile captures.”

Mr. Garca, who went by the name “El Teo,” did not necessarily look like someone accused of boiling his victims. . The mug shot distributed by law enforcement officials showed him in a coat and tie, with chubby cheeks and a wisp of a moustache.

But Mexican prosecutors last year named him one of the country’s most wanted drug lords and put a bounty of more than $2 million on his head. They said he was responsible for much of the violence that had made Tijuana one of Mexico’s bloodiest cities. When not ordering the elimination of rivals to his drug business he was lining his pockets with the profits of kidnapping and extortion rings, Mr. Pequeno said.The Drug Enforcement Administration was also after him, plastering his photograph on wanted posters and mentioning him in testimony before Congress as a havoc wreaker in Mexico.

“Teo’s people are known for their extreme violence, mutilation of bodies, Pozoles, which is putting people in acid, kidnapping, leaving narco-messages on victims,” said Mr. Payne, the drug agency's spokesman.

The arrest comes three years into President Felipe Calderón’s war on Mexico’s drug cartels and is the second high-profile blow in less than a month. On Dec. 16, navy special forces killed Arturo Beltrn Leyva, the leader of a powerful cartel active in Mexico’s central states. Mexicans have grown weary of the violence unleashed by Calderns campaign, and the back-to-back successes give his strategy a boost.

“Today another Mexican cartel leader was taken off the street and is no longer able to carry out his bloody turf war,” said Michele M. Leonhart, the acting administrator of the Drug Enforcement Administration.

Mr. Garcas arrest also gave hope to relatives of some of the hundreds of people who disappeared in Tijuana in recent years. “We hope he’ll be interrogated well and will tell us where are relatives are,” said Fernando Ocegueda, whose son was kidnapped from his home in 2007 and who runs Citizens United Against Impunity, an organization of those whose loved ones have disappeared.

Mr. Garca, who is from the Mexican state of Sinaloa, the cradle of drug trafficking, had been a lieutenant in the once powerful Arellano Flix cartel that has long controlled Tijuana’s lucrative smuggling route to the United States, the authorities said. His brother, Cristbal, allied with the same cartel, was arrested in 2005.

As law enforcement officials began arresting a number of top cartel leaders in Tijuana in recent years, Mr. Garca, a former bodyguard, sought to run the floundering cartel himself, the authorities said.

To add to his firepower, Mr. Garca, allied himself with Sinaloa-based traffickers, including Joaqun Guzmn Loera, who goes by the name Chapo and is one of the country’s most sought-after drug lords, law enforcement officials said. They said he also smuggled drugs for La Familia, a Michoacan-based group known for its ruthless ways.

Although Mr. Caldern heralded Mr. Garcas arrest on Tuesday, others pointed out that arresting drug bosses leads to the creation of other drug bosses, including violent ones.

“Until now the tendency has been that these blows are important, but that their impact is relatively fleeting,” said Patricia Escamilla Hamm, who follows border security issues at the Colegio de la Frontera Norte in Tijuana. “Another leader takes his place. El Teo himself is an example of the flexibility of these cartels, the facility with which one leader replaces another.”

Ms. Escamilla predicted that the fight to replace Mr. Garcia might unleash even more violence because the Sinaloa cartel would need to defend its turf in eastern Tijuana, which is the area he controlled. “As long as drugs continue to enter the United States, there will be fighting,” she said.

Mr. Garcia carried out his reign of terror under the noses of the army and with the cooperation of corrupt police officers who allegiances he bought with drug profits. Although frustrated for years that they could not manage to catch Mr. Garcia, Mexican law enforcement officials had been slowly rounding up his men.

Last January, the army grabbed Santiago Meza Lopez, who told authorities that he had been Mr. Garcia’s “Pozolero,” the one responsible for destroying hundreds of murder victims for Mr. Garcia in giant vats of caustic solution. In March, soldiers arrested Jacome Gamboa, who was considered one of Mr. Garcia’s top enforcers.

Then, on Tuesday, Mr. Garcia himself fell in an early morning raid at his vacation home that federal officials said came after five months of investigation.

Elisabeth Malkin contributed reporting.
