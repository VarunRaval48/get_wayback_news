Tight-Lipped Colts Contrast Sharply With Blustery Jets

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JUDY BATTISTA
Published: January 20, 2010

INDIANAPOLIS — Gary Brackett started out toeing the Indianapolis Colts’ company line. A month after they opened the playoff door to the Jets by pulling their starters with a 5-point lead in a Week 16 game, the Colts are confronted in the American Football Conference championship game with the monster they helped create. It is an irresistible story line to everyone but the Colts. Now they are channeling their inner New England Patriots, carefully crafting a compliment-a-thon for their opponent and tiptoeing around the obvious: the Jets probably would not have gotten close to playing this month if Curtis Painter had not entered the sport’s lexicon.

“I’d be confident, too, if I’d won two road games I probably shouldn’t have,” Brackett, a linebacker, said of the Jets’ brashness during a playoff run that has included victories against the Cincinnati Bengals and the San Diego Chargers.

Brackett was then asked about the recent contention by the Jets that they were playing the Colts on equal terms even before Peyton Manning and company headed to the sidelines in the third quarter of the Jets’ 29-15 victory. Brackett’s face tightened. He paused a moment.

“Sunday at 3 o’clock,” he replied. “We’ll see what happens.”

Still, that was about as far off message as the Colts strayed Wednesday as they prepared for the A.F.C. title game Sunday. Before the Colts’ divisional game last week against the Baltimore Ravens, Coach Jim Caldwell told the players to stop answering questions about whether resting starters might lead to rust.

This week, the Colts seem aware that there is almost nothing to be gained by speaking candidly about facing the Jets again under starkly different circumstances. So they have stopped saying pretty much anything, which is undoubtedly to the liking of the team president, Bill Polian, who is known for keeping a tight grip on his franchise and on loose lips. Manning, normally an affable interview subject who delves into X’s and O’s, started off nearly every answer in his news conference with a variation on “I really can’t answer that.” He said he paid no attention to the Jets’ bluster. When he was asked jokingly if he expected to play the full game this week and not give way to Painter, a little-used rookie backup, he responded, “Right, I do.” Trash talking? Advantage, Jets.

The Colts’ reticence is understandable. Like the Jets under the effusive Rex Ryan, the Colts are a reflection of their coach, the cerebral and soft-spoken Caldwell. He approached the question of why the Colts did not talk as much as the Jets by noting the need for “an immense amount of authenticity” in football and how the emotions of a game will “strip the veneer” off any phoniness. Read: it is not his style. The N.F.L. is so popular, he said, precisely because not everybody agrees with every decision.

But there is something else. Almost nothing was expected of the Jets this season, so they can afford to be as loose as Ryan on their joy ride. But for the Colts, this season — as with almost every season of the Manning era — began with Super Bowl aspirations. And as bad as the national controversy was over the decision by Caldwell to rest his starters with a perfect season within reach, the Colts now face a far worse potential fate: if they lose to the team they could have knocked out of the playoff race, their season is ruined.

Receiver Reggie Wayne acknowledged that, unlike last time, the Colts have much more at stake than the Jets. If the Jets lose, they still made an unexpected run that positions them nicely for the future. If the Colts lose, their season is a crashing failure and they will be second-guessed well into the future for resting their starters.

So the Colts took to praising the Jets’ running game — the Jets’ Shonn Greene cuts back more than Baltimore’s Ray Rice — and to admiring their defense. Wayne called Darrelle Revis one of the best cornerbacks in the game. They discussed how physical the play will be. Defensive end Dwight Freeney, not known as the shy type, said he understood Ryan’s claim that he would be shocked if the Jets lost to the Colts. And about the Jets’ claim that they had played the Colts even when the starters were in? “The score was pretty much even,” he said. “I guess that is somewhat accurate.”

Really?

“They understand, I would think, that the game would have been a little different — I’m not saying we would have won — but the game would have been different,” Freeney said. “Week 16 is Week 16. This is the A.F.C. championship game. If they want to use whatever happened in Week 16 to motivate them, so be it.”
