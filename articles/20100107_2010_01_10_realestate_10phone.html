Listings and More, Just a Touch Away

By ELIZABETH A. HARRIS
Published: January 7, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

THERE are over 100,000 iPhone apps available on iTunes. Some help you find a restaurant, others let you find Waldo. There is also a growing selection of real estate apps that will help you look for a home, but not all of them are designed for the New York City market.

Enlarge This Image
Tony Cenicola/The New York Times

MOBILE MARKET The Blue Atlas app, left, lists places for sale and rent. Warburg Realty’s “Around Me” app provides neighborhood information.

A search of iTunes — by no means comprehensive — turned up several new or updated apps that have something to offer New Yorkers.

The apps share many basic features, like using the phone’s GPS navigation to show listings nearby and narrowing the field by things like price, number of bedrooms and property type. These programs are also available for the iPod Touch.

These programs and the companies that power them all work a little differently. And all of these applications are available free — because the providers hope to make their money elsewhere. Beyond that, some differences start to emerge.

Trulia, a Web site that aggregates real estate listings, released an update to its year-and-a-half-old iPhone app last month.

One new feature, for example, is the price-reduction filter, which allows you to tap into a timely market trend by looking only at apartments that have had a price drop. There is also a “popular homes” search, which brings up the listings that have received the most Trulia hits. This application does not carry advertising.

For Trulia, and many other companies venturing into iPhone territory, the purpose of an app is to create buzz and awareness of its brand.

“There is no direct revenue associated with our mobile right now at all,” said Ken Shuman, a Trulia spokesman. “If you’re not in mobile now, you’ll be lost.”

The Corcoran Group, a real estate brokerage based in Manhattan, started its free iPhone app in December. Unlike some of its competitors, Corcoran hopes the app will help sell real estate.

“We’re simply giving people a new way to search,” said Christina Lowris-Panos, Corcoran’s executive vice president for marketing and advertising. “It’s definitely more of a search tool rather than a lark to generate advertising and buzz for the brand.”

The application, which lists homes for sale or for rent, is intended for three markets: New York City, Long Island and South Florida. It uses the phone’s GPS to orient you in each of the three areas, based on your location.

Like other applications, this program can plot your location and filter searches based on criteria like price, size or open houses. But Corcoran, which has a vested interest in the properties that consumers see, offers only its own listings rather than a larger aggregated pool.

The main feature that distinguishes this application from others is the “Get Out Nearby” feature, which suggests restaurants and shopping in the vicinity, powered by BlackBook guides.

Nytimes.com is also releasing a new, free iPhone app, which is expected to be available on iTunes next month. It will have standard search functions for rental and for sale listings and an option to search for listings on which prices have been reduced. It will also allow you to filter for apartments offered for sale by owner.

Once you select a listing, you will be able to create a file within the app to add your own pictures and notes. (If you went to an open house for an apartment billed as “large and full of light” and found that it was a room in a basement, you could write “saw it; hated it,” as a reminder.)

“That’s a pretty unique feature for the real estate space,” said Ira Silberstein, a vice president of product development for Nytimes.com. “But the primary capability of the app is that people will really be able to do a robust search on the application while they’re mobile.”

The app will be able to be synched automatically with your nytimes.com profile and will display real estate articles from The New York Times.

The recently updated Blue Atlas Broker application offers apartments for sale and for rent. But it has another category as well: foreclosures. This allows you to search for distressed homes just as you would for a co-op or condominium.

This app, which offers national and international listings pulled from Google’s Real Estate database, has the usual filters and map features, but you can also search open houses more narrowly — today, tomorrow or next week. (The open house search on most apps brings up listings without regard to when the open houses are scheduled, whether it’s tomorrow or next Sunday.)

Another thing that sets this application apart is its purpose: to make money.

Blue Atlas is a small technology company that started up in 2008 in Cape Cod, Mass. Its business is not real estate but iPhone apps. “The goal is to provide service to customers,” said Anthony Amundson, the founder of Blue Atlas Technology. “We hope they find it valuable and buy it.”
