U.S. Opts for Younger Players Over Veterans for Olympic Team

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JEFF Z. KLEIN
Published: January 1, 2010

BOSTON — Rangers forwards Chris Drury and Ryan Callahan were named to the United States Olympic team on Friday, in an announcement after the Winter Classic at Fenway Park.

Devils forwards Jamie Langenbrunner and Zach Parise were also named to the 23-man roster, as was the injured Devils defenseman Paul Martin, who is recovering from a broken arm. Brian Burke, Team USA’s general manager, said Martin would be replaced on the roster if he had not recovered by the start of the Olympic hockey tournament on Feb. 16.

The names were unveiled on a rink that stretched between first and third base at Fenway, with youth hockey players wearing the sweaters of the selected Olympic team players. The last player named was the 35-year-old Bruins goalie Tim Thomas, who skated out to the roar of the crowd after winning the overtime game against the Flyers just minutes before.

“I pretty much had given up on playing in the Olympics,” said Thomas, who several years ago was playing in Finland and Sweden and has played in the world championships six times, but thought he would never attract the attention of USA Hockey selectors.

“Being named to the U.S. Olympic team,” Thomas said, “I feel like I’ve been waiting my whole life for this.”

The American team averages 26 years of age, and represents a changed guard from previous Olympic teams led by such veteran players as Keith Tkachuk, Mike Modano, Bill Guerin, Scott Gomez, Brian Rolston and Doug Weight, none of whom were chosen this time. The new group is speedy but a decided underdog to the medal favorites Canada, Russia and Sweden.

However, the Americans are strong in goal with Thomas and the Buffalo Sabres’ Ryan Miller, whom Burke called “the best goalie in the National Hockey League right now.” Burke said that at the Olympics the Americans would probably go with one goalie rather than alternate.

Burke said he chose Drury, the 33-year-old Rangers captain whose role has been diminished this season under Coach John Tortorella, for his leadership.

“We chose Chris Drury because he is Chris Drury,” Burke said. “That’s all you need to know about him.”

Burke said that he and the rest of the American team selectors had been consulting with Drury “from the start.”

Tortorella will serve as an assistant at the Olympics to Coach Ron Wilson, who also coaches the Toronto Maple Leafs, whose general manager is Burke.

The other assistant at the Olympics will be Islanders Coach Scott Gordon. No Islanders were chosen for the American team.

Only three of those selected have Olympic experience: Drury, Langenbrunner and Detroit defenseman Brian Rafalski.

The team’s top gun is the Chicago Blackhawks’ Patrick Kane, who at 21 is also the Americans’ youngest player. He leads all Americans in N.H.L. scoring, with 45 points in 40 games.

The roster:

Goaltenders: Ryan Miller (Buffalo), Jonathan Quick (Los Angeles), Tim Thomas (Boston).

Defensemen: Erik Johnson (St. Louis), Jack Johnson (Los Angeles), Mike Komisarek (Toronto), Paul Martin (Devils), Brooks Orpik (Pittsburgh), Brian Rafalski (Detroit), Ryan Suter (Nashville).

Forwards: David Backes (St. Louis), Dustin Brown (Los Angeles), Ryan Callahan (Rangers), Chris Drury (Rangers), Patrick Kane (Chicago), Ryan Kesler (Vancouver), Phil Kessel (Toronto), Jamie Langenbrunner (Devils), Ryan Malone (Tampa Bay), Zach Parise (Devils), Joe Pavelski (San Jose), Bobby Ryan (Anaheim), Paul Stastny (Colorado).
