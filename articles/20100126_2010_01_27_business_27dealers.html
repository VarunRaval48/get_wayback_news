At Least 1,550 Seek to Keep Car Dealerships

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By NICK BUNKLEY
Published: January 26, 2010

DETROIT — More than half of the dealers terminated by General Motors and Chrysler last year have filed to have an arbitrator determine whether they should be reinstated.

The American Arbitration Association, which is overseeing the review process established by Congress, said it had received paperwork from 1,550 dealers as of Tuesday morning. The deadline to file was Monday, but the figure is expected to rise because some submissions were still being processed.

About 2,800 dealers were eligible to file for arbitration under a law passed last month. That figure includes 789 Chrysler dealers that were forced to shut down last June and 1,300 G.M. dealers that will lose their franchise by October. An additional 700 G.M. dealers are losing the ability to sell one or more brands but remain with the company.

G.M. and Chrysler made the cuts as part of their bankruptcy reorganization plans. The companies said the terminations were needed to eliminate poor-performing stores and help make those that remain more profitable.

But the dealers fighting to be brought back argue that their absence hurts sales and alienates previously loyal customers.

“Our world has been rocked,” said Catherine Zimmer of Zimmer Motors, a third-generation dealership in Florence, Ky., that lost its Chrysler and Jeep franchises. “We still believe, based on 80 years of selling and servicing Chrysler and Jeep products, that we’re a good fit for the company, and hopefully we’ll get to show them that.”

Ms. Zimmer said her store, which still sells used cars, was Chrysler’s only presence on a busy row of dealers near the Cincinnati-Northern Kentucky International Airport. Chrysler sent out letters to her dealership’s customers, telling them they could drive to a dealership in Indiana, half an hour away.

Chrysler executives have said reinstating dealers could be detrimental to the company’s recovery, while G.M. executives have said reversing some cuts would not be a major burden.

“I’m not sure it will weaken us,” G.M.’s chairman and chief executive, Edward E. Whitacre Jr., said Monday. “It’s not going to slow our progress.”

The arbitration association did not know yet how many of the filings were made against each of the two companies. On Monday, when about 1,200 cases had been filed, Mr. Whitacre said about 500 or 600 were by G.M. dealers. That would indicate that most of the rejected Chrysler dealers had filed for arbitration.

Chrysler expects to spend hundreds of millions of dollars defending itself in the process, a person with direct knowledge of the company’s planning said. The person was not authorized to speak publicly about the matter.

Chrysler believes its arbitration costs will greatly exceed G.M.’s because its decisions were based more on how dealers fit into its overall network rather than on each dealership‘s individual performance, but it now must defend each termination separately, this person said.

Each case will be handled individually, in the state where the dealership was located, meaning each company must send teams of representatives to various cities simultaneously. Hearings are expected to begin in March, and the process is scheduled to finish by mid-June.

Chrysler has been weighing whether to sue to stop the process but had not made a final decision as of Tuesday.

John McEleney, chairman of the National Automobile Dealers Association, said he did not see any reason that either G.M. or Chrysler would be significantly damaged by having to take back some dealers. He said some Chrysler dealers who have filed do not want to resume a relationship with the company but merely believe their franchises were wrongly taken.

Mr. McEleney said many dealers probably would give up their fight in exchange for some payment, though there is no provision for that in the law that set up the arbitration.

“Many of the Chrysler dealers would be more interested in compensation than reinstatement, but that’s not an option,” Mr. McEleney said. “I’m hopeful that there are some settlements outside arbitration. I don’t think it would take ungodly sums of money in a lot of these situations.”
