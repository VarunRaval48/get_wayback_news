Opera

★ ‘CARMEN’ (Wednesday) Though it is difficult to make an opera as familiar as “Carmen” seem startling and fresh, the British director Richard Eyre does it in his gripping new production. Other than updating the Seville setting from the 1830s to the 1930s, to evoke the repressive era of the Spanish Civil War, Mr. Eyre presents a fairly traditional take on Bizet’s masterpiece. But by teasing out details that show the characters bucking against civic and sexual constraints, he uncovers the rawness and daring at the opera’s core. The production could have a quite different impact, though, as new artists take over for the two lead singers and the conductor. The vocally smoldering Russian mezzo-soprano Olga Borodina sings Carmen, and the young, appealing American tenor Brandon Jovanovich sings Don José. Still in the cast are the robust baritone Mariusz Kwiecien, who utterly looks the part of the suave and cocky matador Escamillo, and the rich-voiced soprano Barbara Frittoli as Micaëla. In his Met debut, the French conductor Alain Altinoglu takes over for Yannick Nézet-Séguin. (Through May 1.) At 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; $65 to $375. (Anthony Tommasini)20100121

‘LA CENERENTOLA’ (Friday) The Capitol Heights Lyric Opera presents Rossini’s bubbly Cinderella story in a new production meant to emulate the work’s 1825 American premiere staging. Richard Cassell, a veteran baritone, is the stage director and portrays Don Magnifico; Frances Devine, a French mezzo-soprano, sings Angelina opposite the Don Ramiro of Gregory Zavracky; Jeffrey Bradbury conducts. (Also next Friday.) At 7:30 p.m., Leonard Nimoy Thalia, Symphony Space, 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street , (212) 864-5400, symphonyspace.org; $35; $20 for 65+ and students. (Steve Smith)20100121

‘HOTEL CASABLANCA’ (Friday through Sunday) The enterprising Dicapo Opera Theater offers the New York premiere of a 2007 comedy by Thomas Pasatieri, based on George Feydeau’s play “A Flea in Her Ear.” Francine Harmon directs the production; James Lowe conducts. Friday and Saturday at 8 p.m.; Sunday at 4 p.m.; Dicapo Opera Theater, St. Jean Baptiste Catholic Church, 184 East 76th Street, Manhattan , (212) 868-4444, smarttix.com; $40. (Smith)20100121

★ ‘IL MONDO DELLA LUNA’ (Monday through Thursday) Haydn at the Hayden was a terrific idea. The Gotham Chamber Opera, in association with the American Museum of Natural History, is presenting an inventively staged production of Haydn’s fanciful opera “Il Mondo della Luna” (“The World on the Moon”) in an ideal space: the Hayden Planetarium. The director Diane Paulus has encouraged the appealing cast to indulge in all manner of generic comic bits and lame overacting. Still, it was intriguing to see this tender opera — about a protective father who is duped by a sham astrologer into thinking he has traveled to the moon, along with his marriageable daughters — presented in a place where images of the cosmos, projected on the domed ceiling, are incorporated into the staging. Neal Goren conducts the chamber orchestra in a version of the score sensibly pared down to 100 minutes, without intermission. Marco Nisticò, as the befuddled father Buonafede, heads the cast. At 8 p.m., Hayden Planetarium, Rose Center for Earth and Space, American Museum of Natural History, Central Park West at 79th Street , (212) 279-4200, ticketcentral.com; $30 to $125. (Tommasini)20100121

★ ‘SIMON BOCCANEGRA’ (Friday and Monday) Just turned 69, the tenor Plácido Domingo is fulfilling an improbable goal by singing the title baritone role in Verdi’s “Simon Boccanegra” at the Met. Mr. Domingo began his professional career at 18 as a baritone. But he soon recognized that he was a tenor, with a tenor’s vocal colorings and ping. Though the range of Boccanegra suits his voice at this final stage of his career, he brings tenorial colorings to his performance, which has to be counted as an intriguing experiment. He sings with unflagging vigor and burnished power, and infuses his portrayal of Boccanegra, a flawed but well-intentioned doge in 14th-century Genoa, with stylistic insight and a lifetime of experience. The soprano Adrianne Pieczonka brings shimmering, strong, pitch-perfect sound and lovely phrasing to the role of Amelia, Boccanegra’s long-lost illegitimate daughter. Marcello Giordani and James Morris grace the cast. James Levine conducts an insightful, nuanced account of a profound Verdi score. (Through Feb. 6.) At 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 262-6000, metopera.org; $65 to $375. (Tommasini)20100121

★ ‘STIFFELIO’ (Saturday and Tuesday) In 1993 the Metropolitan Opera presented a company premiere production of Verdi’s little-known opera “Stiffelio” as a vehicle for Plácido Domingo, who compellingly sang the title role of a 19th-century Evangelical Protestant minister facing a shattering spiritual crisis over the infidelity of his wife. Mr. Domingo is back for the revival of “Stiffelio,” the first Met performance since 1998. But this time he is in the pit, conducting. The title role is sung by José Cura, who reins in his burnished, sometimes wild tenor voice to give an intelligent and effective performance. As Lina, Stiffelio’s guilt-ridden wife, the soprano Julianna Di Giacomo has taken over for Sondra Radvanovsky, who sang it with affecting expressivity on opening night (and returns for the final performance of the run on Jan. 30). Mr. Domingo conducts capably. The real star, though, is Verdi’s inexplicably neglected 1850 opera. At 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; $20 to $375. (Tommasini)20100121

‘TURANDOT’ (Saturday and Thursday) The tenor Salvatore Licitra has taken over the role of Calàf in the Met’s revival of Franco Zeffirelli’s opulent production of Puccini’s “Turandot.” When Mr. Licitra made his Met debut in 2002, as a last-minute substitute for Luciano Pavarotti in “Tosca,” he had an undisciplined but muscular and vibrant voice. In recent years his singing has been erratic. In this production he is singing with more control and evenness, but he still sounds constrained and leathery. He has his moments, though. The soprano Maria Guleghina, as Turandot, slices through the orchestra with her steely voice. The soprano Maija Kovalevska is a rich-voiced, deeply expressive Liù. The exciting work of the conductor Andris Nelsons continues to be the news of this revival. But the final performance this season on Thursday offers a new lineup of artists: Lise Lindstrom as Turandot, Grazia Doronzio as Liù, Frank Porretta as Calàf, and Julien Salemkour conducting. Saturday at 1 p.m.; Thursday at 8 p.m.; Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; Saturday, sold out; Thursday, $20 to $375. (Tommasini)20100121

Classical Music

BARGEMUSIC (Friday through Sunday) This intimate and atmospheric floating concert hall is an ideal space to hear chamber music. On Friday, as part of the Here and Now series, the Verdehr Trio play works by composers including Jennifer Higdon, Bright Sheng and Russell Platt. On Saturday and Sunday the Salomé Chamber Ensemble, a conductorless string orchestra founded last year by young musicians, plays music by Vivaldi, Mendelssohn, Haydn, Dvorak and Philip Glass. Friday and Saturday at 8 p.m.; Sunday at 3 p.m.; Fulton Ferry Landing next to the Brooklyn Bridge, Brooklyn , (718) 624-2083, bargemusic.org; Friday, $25; $20 for 65+; $10 for students. On Saturday and Sunday, $45; $40 for 65+; $20 for students. (Vivien Schweitzer)20100121

DA CAPO CHAMBER PLAYERS (Sunday and Tuesday) This new-music ensemble had a long, productive relationship with the composer George Perle, who died last January. Perle is the focus of a tribute by the group, which is performing his Sonata a Quattro and “Nightsong,” both composed for the ensemble, as well as his Tryptych and the Cadenza and Allegro from his Cello Sonata. The program also includes works by Henri Dutilleux, Leo Kraft and Paul Lansky. Sunday at 8 p.m., Taplin Auditorium, Princeton University, Princeton, N.J., (609) 258-6842, k-c-p.com/dacapo; free. Tuesday at 8 p.m., Merkin Concert Hall, 129 West 67th Street, Manhattan , (212) 501-3330, kaufman-center.org; $20; $10 for student and 65+. (Allan Kozinn)20100121

BERNARDA FINK (Sunday) As part of the Art of the Song recital series, this fine mezzo (accompanied by Anthony Spiri) will open her program with Schumann, including the Five Lieder on poems by Friedrich Rückert and Schumann’s seldom-performed Maria Stuart Lieder (Op. 135), the composer’s final songs for solo voice and piano. She then sings selections by Granados, Rodrigo and Dallapiccola. At 5 p.m., Alice Tully Hall, Lincoln Center , (212) 721-6500, lincolncenter.org; $50 to $70. (Schweitzer)20100121

★ FOCUS! (Friday, and Monday through Thursday) The Juilliard School’s annual Focus! Festival, directed by Joel Sachs, is devoted this year to the creation of an American repertory in the years between (roughly) 1945 and 1970. Called “Music at the Center: Composing an American Mainstream,” the festival opens on Friday with Jeffrey Milarsky conducting the Juilliard Orchestra in a hefty program that includes Copland’s Nonet, Bernstein’s “Halil,” Barber’s sultry “Knoxville: Summer of 1915” and Schuman’s Symphony No. 5. (Each orchestral and chamber program includes a work by Schuman, who, as president of the school, created the festival in 1945.) On Monday Joel Sachs leads the New Juilliard Ensemble in Diamond’s Concerto for Orchestra, Schuman’s “Young Dead Soldiers” and works by Overton and Cowell. The series also includes chamber concerts on Tuesday (with Barber’s delicious “Summer Music” for woodwind quintet and works by Talma, Mennin, Gould, Schuman and Jack Beeson); Wednesday (music by Persichetti, Kirchner, Still, Piston, Menotti and Schuman); and Thursday (works by Schuman, Thomson, Ruth Crawford Seeger, Samuel Adler and Ned Rorem). The festival ends on Jan. 30 with a staging of Aaron Copland’s second opera, “The Tender Land.” At 8 p.m., the Juilliard School , 155 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center, (212) 769-7406, juilliard.edu; free. (Kozinn)20100121

FOUR NATIONS ENSEMBLE (Monday) This fine period-instrument band, directed from the harpsichord by Andrew Appel, offers a program of works introduced in Paris at the end of the 18th and start of the 19th centuries, in the period just before and after the French Revolution. Included are works by Devienne, Méhul, Rode, Bréval, Kreutzer, Jadin and Reicha. At 8 p.m., New-York Historical Society, 170 Central Park West, at 77th Street , (212) 928-5708, fournations.org; $40; $25 for students and 65+. (Kozinn)20100121

MARILYN HORNE FOUNDATION (Friday) The grand finale of the Marilyn Horne Foundation’s annual vocal celebration, “The Song Continues,” is titled “Dames at ‘C’ “ for a good reason — the high-flying program features no fewer than five sopranos: Keri Alkema, Elaine Alvarez, Amanda Majeski, Jennifer Zetlan and Kathleen Kim. Let the fireworks begin. At 7:30 p.m., Zankel Hall, Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $37 and $45. (Smith)20100121

HOUSTON SYMPHONY (Thursday) Hans Graf leads the Houston Symphony and the Women of the Houston Symphony Chorus in a colorful program that includes Stravinsky’s early Rimsky-Korsakov-influenced Scherzo Fantastique (Op. 3); Henri Dutilleux’s “Timbres, Espace, Mouvement”; and Holst’s painterly, cosmic suite, “The Planets.” Lately, orchestras have been enhancing Holst’s sonic spectacular with visual elements: a few seasons ago the Brooklyn Philharmonic performed the work with NASA photographs of the planets and their moons. The Houston Symphony’s performance will be accompanied by a high-definition film by Duncan Copp, also using NASA photographs. At 8 p.m., Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $31 to $92. (Kozinn)20100121

MET ORCHESTRA (Sunday) James Levine leads his Met players in two of the repertory’s exalted war horses — Schubert’s “Unfinished” Symphony and the Beethoven Fifth Symphony — at either end of a program that also includes nine Strauss songs, with the superb soprano Diana Damrau as the soloist. At 3 p.m., Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $53 to $169. (Kozinn)20100121

★ LE POÈME HARMONIQUE (Saturday) Presented by the Miller Theater, Vincent Dumestre and his elegant French early-music ensemble perform a rich, original setting of the Lamentations of Jeremiah by the 16th-century Italian composer Emilio de’ Cavalieri. At 8 p.m., Church of St. Mary the Virgin, 145 West 46th Street, Manhattan , (212) 854-7799, millertheater.com; $35; $30 for 65+; $15 for students. (Smith)20100121

SHANGHAI QUARTET (Saturday) At the tail end of last year’s Mendelssohn bicentennial celebration, this estimable ensemble and the pianist Inon Barnatan present the world and American premieres of recently unveiled piano and chamber works. At 6 p.m. there will be a panel discussion about why so much of Mendelssohn’s catalog remains unpublished. At 7 p.m., Metropolitan Museum of Art , (212) 570-3949, metmuseum.org; $40. (Schweitzer)20100121

SIX SATURDAYS WITH VIERNE (Saturday) John Scott, the organist and director of music at St. Thomas Church Fifth Avenue, offers a rare chance to hear the complete organ symphonies of the French composer Louis Vierne. This concert, the third in the six-part series, features the Symphony No. 3. (Through Feb. 13.) At 4 p.m., St. Thomas Church Fifth Avenue, 1 West 53rd Street, (212) 664-9360, saintthomaschurch.org; free, but donations are suggested. (Schweitzer)20100121

TOKYO STRING QUARTET (Saturday) This venerable ensemble offers Beethoven’s String Quartet in E minor (Op. 59, No. 2), and the pianist Markus Groh performs Beethoven’s Sonata in F sharp (Op. 78) and the Sonata in D (Op. 28), “Pastoral.” At 8 p.m., 92nd Street Y, 1395 Lexington Avenue , (212) 415-5500, 92y.org; $38 and $48; $25 for ages 35 and younger. (Schweitzer)20100121

★ PIETER WISPELWEY (Sunday) This scintillating Dutch cellist and his recital partner, the pianist Paolo Giacometti, present an all-Brahms program, including the composer’s two cello sonatas and a transcription of his Violin Sonata No. 1. At 2 p.m., Starr Theater, Alice Tully Hall, Lincoln Center , (212) 721-6500, lincolncenter.org; $45 to $75. (Smith)20100121
