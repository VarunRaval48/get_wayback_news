Pro-Wall Street Democrat Takes On a House Veteran

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By DAVID M. HALBFINGER
Published: January 26, 2010

She’s young, she just moved into the district and she is taking on an established and popular House Democrat in a primary bid she concedes is a huge gamble. She’s also a hedge fund lawyer, and proud of it.

All that may not exactly excite liberal primary voters. But Reshma Saujani, 34, has won the backing of some prominent Democratic donors for her bid to topple Representative Carolyn B. Maloney, a challenge that is turning heads on the Upper East Side of Manhattan and antagonizing some of New York’s best-known feminists.

It is a campaign steeped in identity politics — Ms. Saujani says she is the first Indian-American woman to run for Congress, and she is counting on South Asians to support her in droves. But perhaps more intriguing is her goal of becoming an advocate for the financial industry in the Capitol.

Ms. Saujani, in an interview, said she was “running on my Wall Street record, not from it.” She called Ms. Maloney “asleep at the wheel” for saying the economy was improving, and accused her and the entire New York Congressional delegation of failing to do enough to aid business. She said her hedge fund work was great preparation for representing the district, home to many of the financial industry’s players, and she chided Ms. Maloney for bashing banks and calling for a 100 percent tax on some bonuses.

“Instead of browbeating Wall Street, I want to invite them to help create jobs,” Ms. Saujani said, talking up an idea to connect investment houses with technology start-ups.

“If you go to Texas, you’ll never hear a Congressional member speak poorly of the oil industry,” she added. “In Michigan, you’ll never hear a Congressional member speak poorly of the auto industry. This is our bread and butter.”

For voters who do not work on Wall Street, though, Ms. Saujani’s work history might remind them of the stories that have battered its reputation.

In 2005, she helped start an investment fund for a company partly owned by Hassan Nemazee, a Democratic fund-raiser who was charged with bank fraud last year. Ms. Saujani said she left before the alleged crimes. She went to another fund started by the Carlyle Group, before it was hammered by credit-default swaps. Then she shifted to Fortress Investment Partners, which cratered in the market meltdown.

Ms. Saujani also has a healthy sense of what a junior representative can accomplish. Asked what might have been different had she been in Congress before the financial crisis, she said, “I think we could’ve seen it coming and prepared for it.”

Told of Ms. Saujani’s remarks, a spokesman for Ms. Maloney, George Arzt, had few words. “What’s her record?” he said. “What’s her accomplishments? What’s her connection to the district? There is none.”

Ms. Saujani moved into the district last summer, when Ms. Maloney explored a run for the Senate, in hopes of leapfrogging past other elected officials who coveted the House seat. She insisted that “age has nothing to do with” her candidacy. But she added that a third of the district’s adults were too young to vote when Ms. Maloney, 60, was first elected in 1992, and she suggested that “there reaches a point where you have to pass the torch and give others the opportunity to lead.”

More than a youth-in-revolt situation, though, her campaign reflects the support of some influential East Siders who are disdainful of Ms. Maloney and say they want someone with greater intellectual heft. (One of them, Maureen White, a former national finance chief for the Democratic Party and the wife of the financier Steven Rattner, has told friends she briefly considered running against Ms. Maloney herself.)

Boykin Curry, another financier backing Ms. Saujani, said he wanted a “policy lion” in the mold of Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan.

“This is the most educated district in America and one of the top contributors to candidates outside the district,” he said. “And we’re sending someone like we’re a second-rate town, who checks the box on issues and runs excellent constituent services, but who I don’t think anybody would say is adding anything to the debate.”

Ms. Saujani speaks her donors’ language: her political experience is as a fund-raiser, and in 2004 she pulled together $1 million from South Asians for John Kerry. As of Dec. 31, she had raised $403,000 for a race that could cost upward of $2 million. Ms. Maloney raised less than $200,000 in the quarter but had $1.75 million on hand.

Some Democrats see the drive to oust Ms. Maloney as curiously timed. An institution on the East Side (whose district includes a large chunk of Queens, including Astoria) she received an outpouring of sympathy when her husband of 33 years, Clifton H. W. Maloney, died in September while climbing in the Himalayas.

Even Ms. Maloney’s rivals in the New York Congressional delegation call her a relentless, effective advocate. In the past year she has won $200 million for the Second Avenue subway, pressed the case for billions in new compensation for 9/11 victims and seen the overhaul of credit-card regulations that she had championed since 2007 signed into law.

For her part, Ms. Saujani offers a compelling immigrant’s tale. Her parents lost almost everything in the early 1970s when Idi Amin expelled Indians from Uganda. They hid what few jewels they had in toothpaste and settled in Chicago after throwing a dart at a map. She made it to Yale Law School and later, at the law firm Davis Polk & Wardwell, handled asylum cases pro bono.

But her campaign has rankled many feminists, whose loyalty Ms. Maloney won decades ago for her work on mammograms, equal pay, reproductive rights and violence against women.

Marilyn Fitterman, a former state president of the National Organization for Women, appealed to Ms. Saujani in an e-mail message not to challenge Ms. Maloney “if you care anything about women’s rights.”

Geraldine A. Ferraro, the former House member and vice-presidential candidate, also urged Ms. Saujani to wait. “She’s ending her political career before it starts,” Ms. Ferraro said. “That’s not a threat, it’s a statement of fact. You don’t, if you’re a Democrat, challenge an incumbent Democrat who has a position of power to get things done just because you feel like this is something you want to do.”

Ms. Saujani, undeterred, noted that Ms. Maloney saw primaries as healthy when she considered taking on Senator Kirsten E. Gillibrand. As for paying her dues and waiting, she said, “Last time I read the Constitution, getting in line wasn’t in it.”

Then again, last July, more than 60 New Yorkers sent Ms. Maloney a letter imploring her “not to pursue a divisive primary fight” against Ms. Gillibrand because it would force a choice “between two tremendously capable, hard-working women.” Among the signatures: Ms. Saujani’s.
