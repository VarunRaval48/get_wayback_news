Freestylers Are Uptight About Keeping Clothes Loose

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MATT HIGGINS
Published: January 27, 2010

ASPEN, Colo. — When peer pressure and mocking do not work, the fashion police monitoring ski cross and snowboard cross at the Winter X Games can resort to more hands-on measures: pinching the fabric on an athlete’s pants between boot and midthigh should produce at least eight centimeters of play — roughly the length of a credit card. If not, well, the rules are not fixed. But there is the possibility of not being invited back to ESPN’s action sports extravaganza, which begins here Thursday on Buttermilk Mountain.

In snowboard cross and ski cross — freestyle disciplines in which four or more racers careen simultaneously down a course while negotiating bumps, berms, jumps and rollers in a scene that often leads to multiple passes and to pileups — there is more than fashion at stake. The issue of tight clothing has pitted elements of old school versus new school; Alpine versus freestyle; and function versus fashion.

“The image is what’s at stake,” said Nate Holland, a five-time X Games gold medal winner in snowboard cross — called Snowboarder X here — who will compete for the United States at the Winter Olympics in Vancouver next month. “I like to wear stuff that consumers can buy at stores. And they’re not going to buy a speed suit at a store that I’m wearing during a race.”

For years, an honor system in the snowboard cross and ski cross fraternity kept racers from straying from the sports’ stylistic sensibilities in an attempt to gain an advantage. But with both disciplines now in the Olympics, there are medals and potentially lucrative endorsement deals on the line.

In a quest for faster speeds, some racers have begun to flirt with a more streamlined look at World Cup events, prompting competitors to call for new rules and stricter enforcement that would preserve fairness and the sports’ cherished aesthetic.

“I think there is a need for a rule” about what can be worn in a race, said Holland, 31, of Sandpoint, Idaho.

The International Ski Federation, known as FIS, has created rules regulating what can be worn in ski cross and snowboard cross competition at World Cup events and the Olympics. But the rules for snowboard cross are not well defined: suits must be two-piece and speed suits are not allowed, but, according to Kathrin Hostettler, an FIS spokeswoman, there is no rule for measuring how tight they are.

This has forced snowboarders to self-police.

When Holland sees a fellow competitor wearing snug clothing at the starting gate, he will usually offer a gentle reprimand. But others have begun to resort to harsh words.

“I’ve seen other riders get heated up on it,” Holland said. “There definitely is backlash if you show up with something tight.”

During the early 1990s, big-mountain free skiers organized ski cross. Snowboard cross followed, populated by former freestyle riders. As a group, they had a loose vibe and loose outerwear.

“They were very antiestablishment and they were very anti-race suit,” said Casey Puckett, who qualified to compete for the United States ski team when ski cross makes its Olympic debut next month, but who will miss the Winter X Games with a shoulder injury.

“It was extremely important to them that there were no race suits involved,” he said.

Puckett, 37, had no problem with race suits. He had worn them while competing in Alpine disciplines as a member of the United States ski team at four Olympics, most recently the 2002 Salt Lake Games. But once his Alpine career ended, he saw how attitudes had changed.

“When I grew up in the ’70s, ’80s, ’90s, it was cool to be an Alpine skier and race in a downhill suit,” Puckett said. “And when snowboarding came, and free riding, it became uncool to be in a speed suit.”

At the Winter X Games, athletes have a say in creating and applying the rules of competition. Anyone in violation will be singled out during riders’ meetings.

“If somebody shows up with a race suit, they’ll get laughed at and they won’t be allowed to compete,” said Daron Rahlves, who will race in ski cross at the X Games on Saturday and for the United States next month at the Olympics. (The most decorated World Cup downhill skier in United States history, Rahlves switched to ski cross after his retirement from Alpine racing in 2006 and now prefers to race in normal ski wear.)

And because the X Games are by invitation, a racer who resorts to tight clothes runs the risk of not being asked back.

“If someone is showing up to races in tight clothes, we wouldn’t recommend that person for a spot,” said Holland, who is on the selection committee for snowboard cross at the X Games.

For ski cross, FIS rules require that outerwear be two pieces — pants and top — and not be made of rubber or similar materials. Pants must permit an eight-centimeter gap when pinched from the midthigh to the top of the boot — the same as the X Games — and shirts must allow for six centimeters of fabric around the elbow and biceps.

Still, Rahlves said that last season members of the Austrian and French ski cross teams wore what amounted to two-piece speed suits at World Cup races. And members of Canada’s team wore tight pants with cuffs around the boots. He and other racers complained to FIS officials about lax enforcement.

This year, officials have cracked down. Puckett said the material on his suit was inspected at the finish of every World Cup race this season.

Like Rahlves and Puckett, most of the world’s elite ski cross racers come from an Alpine background, rather than a free-skiing tradition. They were never indoctrinated with freestyle values the way most snowboarders were.

“Skiers will never have the same attitude or gentleman’s agreement that snowboarding will,” Rahlves said. “With skiing, it’s just bred in the racers and the teams that you take every advantage possible to win.”

But Holland has seen that skiing mentality creeping into snowboard cross, under the guise of fashion trends.

“I think the problem we have now is the emo look,” he said, “and people trying to use that as an excuse for wearing tight clothing.”
