The Difference Between Glitter and Gold for Johnny Weir

Jonathan Ferrey/Getty Images

Many relish Johnny Weir’s ornate, unapologetic style, while others believe the glitz has overwhelmed the skating, making his chances for an Olympic medal as slippery as the ice itself.

By JER LONGMAN
Published: January 18, 2010

SPOKANE, Wash. — No American figure skater is more engaging than Johnny Weir or more acutely balanced on that thin edge between art and sport. He clearly wants to be a star. The question is, can he still be a champion?

“You have to be a champion to be a star,” said Brian Boitano, the 1988 Olympic champion.

Wearing a touch of fox on his shoulder, Weir made the Olympic team for next month’s Vancouver Games despite an oddly passive and stumbling long program, which brought third place Sunday at the United States Figure Skating Championships.

Yet simple math, points awarded for a spin or deducted for a triple axel, fails to convey the conflict inherent in Weir’s attempt to reconcile performance and competition.

Many relish his ornate, unapologetic style, while others believe the glitz has overwhelmed the skating, making his chances for an Olympic medal as slippery as the ice itself. Underlying every move of Weir, a three-time national champion, is this question: What are the obligations of a skater who must play to the crowd and the judges?

That tension is evident in the way Weir, 25, skates and talks. He is enamored of the classic Russian style, yet sometimes he can appear more burlesque than Bolshoi. He wants to be “an athlete my country can be proud of.” At the same time, he said: “You’re putting on a show; you’re putting on a performance. I feel like that every day when I walk out of my house.”

When he walks into the rink each day, Weir wants his coach, Galina Zmievskaya, to say, “Look at you.” She has taught him to walk “like you’re the king,” head up, shoulders back, not seeing anyone, only the objective ahead — “the end of the carpet that’s been rolled out for you.”

Weir said: “My obligation has always been to bring the artistic side of my sport out. Jumps are jumps, and everybody can do those jumps. But not everybody can show something wonderful and special and unique and different.”

He is not particularly interested in making skating more mainstream or masculine, Weir said. No need to grab the average N.F.L. fan. “I wear pink,” he said. “I have no problem where my sport is as far as our fan base.”

Fans flew here from Japan, Russia and South Korea to watch him perform, Weir noted. The appreciation of the crowd, the emotion of fans standing and cheering his footwork and spins and jumps — “it’s the most amazing sensation you can get as a sportsman,” he said.

To make figure skating more mainstream would pigeonhole it in a way that would be constrictive, not expansive, Weir suggested.

“Figure skating is theatrical, artistic; it’s elegant, it’s extremely athletic,” he said. “There’s a very specific audience for that. I can say I don’t watch football games, so I don’t understand why a football fan would come to watch figure skating.”

His interviews can be as over the top as his costumes. After Friday’s short program, Weir was asked a clever question and gave an equally clever reply, almost all of the words being titles of songs by Lady Gaga.

“I enjoy Johnny Weir,” said Frank Carroll, the coach of Evan Lysacek, the reigning world champion, who finished second here. “He dances to his own tune. He doesn’t care what anybody else thinks. It takes a certain kind of self-confidence or really knowing who you are.”

Yet Olympic-style skating is a competition, not merely a show.

Jef Billings, an Emmy-winning skating costume designer, said he appreciated Weir’s singular personality but worried that his exaggerated style might undercut the seriousness of his skating.

“I hope it doesn’t affect the way people view him,” Billings said.

In Friday’s short program, Weir seemed cautious during his jumps before relaxing with a head-bopping playfulness. His costume, with its pink shoulder tassel and Lycra corset, though, was more striking than the technique of his skating.

“Not long ago, there was a balance in the contrast between the compelling, understated elegance of Weir’s skating and the too-too costumes he prefers,” wrote Philip Hersh, a longtime Olympics writer for The Chicago Tribune.

He added, “The balance has tipped toward shtick.”

At the 2006 Turin Olympics, Weir took second in the short program before fading to fifth, complaining that he missed a bus and became frazzled before the long program. Then he finished fifth at the 2009 national championships. Many wondered if his showiness had devolved into self-absorption.

“What he might be suffering from is one of my favorite things about figure skating — shameless self-promotion,” said Scott Hamilton, the 1984 Olympic champion. “I did it for 30 years. It depends on how you do it. It can rub some people the wrong way.”

This season, Hamilton said, he has noticed a renewed dedication in Weir, an intensity not previously evident. And Weir has chosen a redemptive theme for his long program. He wore gossamer sleeves Sunday and red sequins meant to suggest the fallen-angel aspect of his career.

Yet Weir was curiously remote and lacking in energy and speed as he attempted to ascend again to championship form. He reduced a triple axel to a single and stepped out of a triple Lutz in a combination jump.

Perhaps he was saving a perfect performance for Vancouver, Weir said. He did find something to be happy about, though.

“My costume looked pretty,” he said.
