JULIAN CASABLANCAS (Thursday) The Strokes, once the heirs apparent to New York’s downtown guitar scene, have become more of a breeding ground for solo side projects than a rock band proper. The vocalist Julian Casablancas is the latest Stroke to go rogue: his solo debut, “Phrazes for the Young” (Cult Records/RCA), is a dark, dissonant and oddly hopeful record. At 9 p.m., Terminal 5, 610 West 56th Street, Clinton , (212) 582-6600, terminal5nyc.com; $31 in advance; $35 at the door. (Amanda Petrusich)20100107

CHICHA LIBRE (Sunday) Chicha Libre, from Brooklyn, is built on the notion of genre melding: The band takes its name from cumbias Amazonicas, a Peruvian musical trend that mixes various South American traditions (Colombian cumbias, Cuban guajiras) with American surf rock, and infuses it with its own unique flourishes. With Melinda Doolittle and Nikki Yanofsky. At 8 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; $12 in advance; $15 at the door. (Petrusich)20100107

★ DOVEMAN (Tuesday) Doveman is the alias of the pianist and composer Thomas Bartlett, who is most famous for his collaborations with better-known indie-rock bands (Grizzly Bear, Antony and the Johnsons, the National). His solo work is hushed, reverent and mesmerizing. With Samamidon and the Poison Tree. At 8 p.m., Mercury Lounge, 217 East Houston Street, at Ludlow Street, Lower East Side , (212) 260-4700, mercuryloungenyc.com; $10 in advance; $12 at the door. (Petrusich)20100107

★ THE DUTCHESS AND THE DUKE (Friday) As the Dutchess and the Duke, Kimberly Morrison and Jesse Lortz play spare folk songs that linger and last. Their lyrics can be dark and riddled with heartbreak (“I can’t win, I can’t lose, and everything I do is wrong/One hand clawing at the other hand, tryin’ to hold on,” they sing in “Hands”), but at best, they’re invigorating and stompy, as in the excellent, Dylan-evoking “Reservoir Park. “ With Medication. At 7:30 p.m., Mercury Lounge, 217 East Houston Street, at Ludlow Street, Lower East Side , (212) 260-4700, mercuryloungenyc.com; $10. (Petrusich)20100107

FABOLOUS (Friday) Stylistically, the rapper Fabolous merges Northern and Southern traditions, but lyrically he embraces a kind of unapologetic materialism — the single “Throw it in the Bag,” from his newest album, “Loso’s Way” (Def Jam), is a boastful homage to shopping. With Mario. At 8 p.m., Fillmore New York at Irving Plaza, 17 Irving Place, at 15th Street, Manhattan , (212) 777-6800, irvingplaza.com; $40. (Petrusich)20100107

MARIANNE FAITHFULL (Wednesday) Although her music is often eclipsed by her personal and artistic collaborations — in particular, her much-publicized relationship with Mick Jagger in the late 1960s — Marianne Faithfull is a significant singer and songwriter in her own right. Over the past decade she has partnered with an impressive cabal of younger musicians (P. J. Harvey, Nick Cave, Damon Albarn, Beck, Jarvis Cocker and others). Her newest album, “Easy Come Easy Go” (Decca), is an unpredictable and enjoyable collection of covers. At 8:30 p.m., Allen Room, Frederick P. Rose Hall, Jazz at Lincoln Center, 60th Street and Broadway , (212) 721-6500, lincolncenter.org; sold out. (Petrusich)20100107

GLOBALFEST (Sunday) Now in its seventh year, Globalfest — a co-production of the World Music Institute, Joe’s Pub and Acidophilus: Live and Active Cultures — is a stellar showcase for emerging and established world-music acts. The diversity of its roster always speaks well to the eclecticism of that genre; this year’s incarnation includes Louisiana fiddle music (Cedric Watson and Bijou Creole), French gypsy jazz (Caravan Palace), Argentine club (Federico Aubele), Senegalese roots-reggae (Meta and the Cornerstones), New York salsa dura (La Excelencia), traditional Irish folksong (Cara Dillon) and more, with many artists making their New York (or United States) debuts. For the full lineup and set times: globalfest-ny.org. At 7 p.m., Webster Hall, 125 East 11th Street, East Village , (212) 533-2111, globalfest-ny.org; $40. (Petrusich)20100107

★ GZA (Saturday) One of the Wu-Tang Clan’s most thoughtful alumni, GZA (a k a “the Genius”) is a careful and nuanced lyricist, and his songs are more narrative than declarative, filled with tiny jokes and witticisms — always well delivered in his dry, frank voice. At 9 p.m., Gramercy Theater, 127 East 23rd Street, Manhattan , (800) 745-3000, ticketmaster.com; $22.50 in advance; $25 at the door. (Petrusich)20100107

NICK JONAS AND THE ADMINISTRATION (Friday) The tween-rock sensation the Jonas Brothers was initially conceived as a mouthpiece for Nick Jonas, the youngest (some might say cutest) Jonas of the bunch, but instead the trio took off as a family band. Now Mr. Jonas is seeking to reclaim a bit of autonomy, and who can blame him? Nick Jonas and the Administration, Mr. Jonas’s current side project, plays smooth and earnest pop-rock that belies its frontman’s 17 years. With Diane Birch, whose songs are retro-leaning in a nonspecific way (Zooey Deschanel meets Joni Mitchell, with the tiniest dash of Norah Jones), and whose new album, “Bible Belt” (S-Curve), is a promising debut, full of honest proclamations and golden horns. “I gotta go out and buy myself one of those little black dresses, ’cause I’m so tired of this T-shirt,” she trills on “Nothing but a Miracle.” At 8 p.m., Beacon Theater, 2124 Broadway, at 74th Street , (800) 745-3000, ticketmaster.com; $39.50 and $49.50. (Petrusich)20100107

EDWIN MCCAIN (Saturday) Edwin McCain, from South Carolina, enjoyed a bit of chart success in 1998 with the schmaltzy ballad “I’ll Be” (“I’ll be better when I’m older, I’ll be the greatest fan of your life,” he howled), but his less commercial work is scruffier and more interesting. After all, Mr. McCain is a great American romantic, and even when his work veers toward sentimentality, it still feels genuine. With Carrie Rodriguez. At 8 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; $25 in advance; $29 at the door. (Petrusich)20100107

★ MONDO MUNDO FESTIVAL (Saturday) Purveyors of “world music from the fringe,” Mondo Mundo is again hosting its annual music festival, featuring an eclectic and compelling lineup: the West African electronic band Burkina Electric, the Lebanese-born singer and songwriter Abaji, the Brooklyn-based soul singer Toshi Reagon and the Latin funk band Ocote Soul Sounds. At 8 p.m., Hiro Ballroom, 371 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (866) 468-7619, highlineballroom.com; $15. (Petrusich)20100107

PASSION PIT (Friday through Sunday) Passion Pit, a five-piece from Cambridge, Mass., makes gleaming, ecstatic electronic music that would be easy to choreograph a group dance to. Michael Angelakos’s heady vocals manage to sound both earnest and stylized: “Have you seen me cry tears like diamonds?” he asks in “I’ve Got Your Number,” from the band’s 2008 EP, “Chunk of Change” (Frenchkiss). At 9 p.m., Terminal 5, 610 West 56th Street, Clinton , (212) 582-6600, terminal5nyc.com; sold out. (Petrusich)20100107

GRANT-LEE PHILLIPS (Saturday) Mr. Phillips harks back to the earnest ambitions and expansive melodies of Bob Dylan and John Lennon, singing with the conviction that rock can still be heroic. With the pianist Vienna Teng (with Alex Wong), the alt-rock collective W.P.A. and the actor Jeff Daniels, who is also a singer and songwriter. At 8 p.m., City Winery, 155 Varick Street, near Spring Street, South Village , (212) 608-0555, citywinery.com; $25. (Jon Pareles)20100107

THE RURAL ALBERTA ADVANTAGE (Friday and Saturday) A trio from Ontario, the Rural Alberta Advantage plays whimsical, keyboard-filled indie-folk: there are declarations of love, and twittering percussive bits, and just the right amount of earnestness. Friday (opening for Passion Pit) at 9 p.m., Terminal 5, 610 West 56th Street, Clinton, (212) 582-6600, terminal5nyc.com; sold out. Saturday at 8:30 and 11:30 p.m., Mercury Lounge, 217 East Houston Street, at Ludlow Street, Lower East Side , (212) 260-4700, mercuryloungenyc.com; sold out. (Petrusich)20100107

★ SURFER BLOOD (Wednesday) This Florida band plays sunny, fuzzed-out pop music that draws on the bliss of Brian Wilson’s California, but — in the keep-it-real spirit of the East Coast — tempers it with a few layers of noise. Surfer Blood is second in a lineup that includes the Depreciation Guild and the headliner the Drums. At 8:30 p.m., Bowery Ballroom, 6 Delancey Street, near the Bowery, Lower East Side , (212) 533-2111, boweryballroom.com; $12 in advance; $15 at the door. (Petrusich)20100107
