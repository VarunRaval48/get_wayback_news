Wife/Mother/Worker/Spy

In the Company of Misery

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MICHELLE SLATALLA
Published: January 5, 2010

THE doctor’s receptionist phoned the other day, looking for my husband.

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Hadley Hooper

“Please tell him we’ve got some flu vaccine, if he still wants it,” she said.

I said I’d pass along the message as soon as he returned from a run.

A few minutes later, I heard the door slam, followed by the telltale slap-slap of his gorilla-foot shoes as he entered the house and headed into the kitchen, where he saw the note (“flu shots finally avail.!”).

“Who needs vaccine? Save it for children and the infirm,” he said heroically, peeling off his gorilla feet and heartily throwing them into the corner for someone else to pick up.

I am not superstitious. But I wish he didn’t taunt germs so openly. It is common knowledge that he does not do well once he is sick. It’s enough of a trial when he gets, say, a winter head cold at home. And now, with us about to go on vacation with the neighbors, I needed him to be at his best. He was already cranky about the prospect of traveling after Christmas to New York, which he claims is “a great place to live, but I wouldn’t want to visit there.”

It must have been a coincidence that symptoms had set in by the time we landed at Kennedy Airport last week during a windy arctic freeze. He said, “Blngh, ngh, ngh, ngh.”

“What?” I asked.

“I feel like I have bants in my nose,” he said.

“Bants?” I repeated.

“Ants. Fire bants,” he clarified. Then he coughed.

And I knew we had hit Stage 2, the sniffles.

In the best of times, my husband is not what you would describe as a good patient. He tends to take any illness, especially his own, gravely. In the 1980s, our engagement was almost scrapped one Thanksgiving after I declined to join his mother, aunt and cousin as they stood over his bed, wringing their hands while he moaned in the delirious throes of a 99.3-degree “fever.” He could barely take a little soup.

Even now, decades later, I know to treat his symptoms with the gravity and concern that is their due. And I believe I could have handled it, too, had I been alone. But I was not. There arose a terrible and unforeseen complication on our trip: One of the neighbors we were traveling with also had a head cold.

“Bants,” Bruce said, pointing to his own nose.

One hothouse flower had turned into a bouquet.

My husband looked crestfallen. He hates to share a cold.

“Do we have any bissues?” my husband asked urgently at baggage claim.

But before I could pull out a fresh travel pack of tissues, Stephanie, Bruce’s wife, took her husband’s arm and said, “You do look a little glassy.”

Alarmed, my husband coughed. “I’m so cold,” he said.

With the analytic clarity of more than 20 years of marriage, I knew where this was headed — an antihistamine binge — and it was not a good place.

By the next day, Stephane was giving Bruce pills one at a time and at four-hour intervals. And my husband already appeared to be enjoying some sort of concierge status at a nearby drugstore, where he was greeting the cashiers by their first names.

“Charles says this stuff will open me up like a can of beans,” my husband said, flipping yet another box of 12-hour time-release capsules onto the night stand to join the Sudafed, NyQuil, Dristan and Actifed, as well as a variety of cough, throat and nose drops.

The problem is that some of those medications were labeled as non-drowsy, which describes a range of products that should never be ingested by my spouse. Especially not at 11 p.m., after he has had a double espresso to round off dinner.

“I’ll be along shortly,” he said as I turned off the lights, and I left him there, in a chair gazing out the window onto upper Broadway, looking unnaturally calm.

I found him still sitting like that, hours later, when I awoke for some reason.

“Tell me, Michelle, have the lambs stopped screaming?” he asked in a quiet voice.

“You’re creeping me out,” I said. I would have been more disturbed, though, if this weren’t also a part of the general pattern of the last two decades’ winter head colds.

The drugs were making him jittery, unable to sleep. And mean.

“Tell me something about yourself, Michelle, something that hurt you a lot in your childhood,” he continued in his Hannibal Lecter voice.

“Enough of those pills!” I said. “Get into a hot shower and relax. You need to sleep.”

The next morning, neither he nor Bruce wanted to get up for breakfast (“No appetite,” he rasped).

“But we’re going to Barney Greengrass,” I said.

That perked him up like a fistful of non-drowsy medication. He has always considered himself a loyal subject of the Sturgeon King.

Old friends of Bruce and Stephanie met us at the restaurant. As luck would have it, one of them was a doctor who specialized in ear, nose and throat problems.

Bruce and my husband elbowed each other, jockeying to sit near the doctor. Then my husband sulked through the first half of breakfast as Bruce rudely monopolized her with a discussion of his symptoms.

Finally, though, she turned to my husband and asked, “What are you taking?”

“Sudafed,” he said, flashing a triumphant look at Bruce. “The 12-hour stuff.”

She shook her head.

“What color is your mucus?” she asked.

“Clear,” he said, delicately, but other people at the table still paused in mid-sturgeon.

“The problem is that stuff dries you out,” she said. “You really need to be taking it with something like Mucinex, which will keep you draining.”

At that bit of advice, he and Bruce started to bolt their breakfasts; there was a drugstore across the street.

I caught up with my husband there. He had just paid for his purchase.

“Way to go,” I said.

“Thanks,” he said, turning magnanimously to Bruce to offer him a hit of Mucinex.

“I got my own,” Bruce said. “Besides, I have to come back. They’re filling my prescription.”

“You got a prescription?” my husband asked.

“Antibiotics,” Bruce said, grim and brave.

We said goodbye to him on the street, and as we walked south on Broadway, my husband asked, “Can you believe Bruce got antibiotics? What a baby.”
