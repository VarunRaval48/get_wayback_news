Helen Gahagan Douglas, Hollywood Liberal

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By THOMAS MALLON
Published: January 7, 2010

Sally Denton’s new biography of Helen Gahagan Douglas, the actress turned New Deal politician, starts on a lyrical note — “Her life would begin and end in sun-filled rooms” — and never really calms down. Despite its concision, a rare and usually admirable thing in biography, the book doesn’t achieve the tonal or interpretive modesty that might give Douglas her due without inflating claims for her significance and her victimization at the hands of Richard Nixon, who defeated her in their 1950 California Senate race. Douglas’s life was more richly and judiciously presented by Ingrid Winther Scobie less than 20 years ago, in a biography called “Center Stage.”

But the story’s early parts still have enough verve to make Denton’s quick run-through appealing. Born in 1900, Douglas grew up mostly in Brooklyn, the daughter of a prosperous Irish Protestant engineer who kept his musically gifted wife from pursuing a singing career. Stagestruck by the time she was 5, Helen did badly in school but managed two years at Barnard before heading to Broadway over her father’s predictable opposition. (Mr. Gahagan is otherwise hard to pin down in Denton’s rendition: on one page he’s “stern and pragmatic” and on the next “outsized and ­boisterous.”)

The young actress could have gotten by on her looks alone (Heywood Broun called her “10 of the 12 most beautiful women in the world”), but she was choosy about scripts and then became serious about singing — perhaps to avenge her mother as well as to fulfill her own real talents. In the late 1920s, she underwent rigorous voice-coaching before she toured Europe singing German and Italian opera. She returned to Broadway out of necessity, in 1930, after losing her money in the stock-market crash.

Melvyn Douglas, her co-star in David Belasco’s production of “Tonight or Never,” became her husband and led her to California, where his film career prospered and hers never really took off — partly because of her own lack of interest in the movies. The marriage produced two children, and after its first decade proceeded for another 40 years in a friendly way, without physical intimacy or sometimes even much proximity. Although her husband strayed with other women, he also, through his involvement with organizations like the Hollywood Anti-Nazi League, “introduced Helen to progressive politics.”

During the late 1930s, what Denton calls her “intellectual enlightenment” quickly gathered steam. Douglas became passionately supportive of the New Deal’s efforts at home and abroad. She and her husband spent the night at the Roosevelt White House in November 1939, and over the next 12 months Douglas accompanied the first lady on a visit to migrant workers in California; sang “The Star-Spangled Banner” at the Democratic National Convention; and gave more than 250 speeches for the ticket of Roosevelt and Henry Wallace.

By 1944 she was ready to run for Congress from a Los Angeles district with a large minority population. She had to struggle to strip her good intentions of their condescending veneer — “I just love the Negro people!” she declared to a black church congregation — but she won a close victory and arrived in Washington just months before Roosevelt’s death and the end of World War II. The press tried to create a rivalry between her and that other Broadway alumna, Representative Clare Boothe Luce, but neither took the bait. The young and canny Lyndon Johnson, introducing her to the ways of the House, made Douglas a special interest, leaving her “fascinated and repelled” by his manners and moods. Their affair, an “open scandal” to many, lasted well beyond her six years in Washington.

It was Douglas, not Luce, who succeeded in getting a seat on the House Foreign Affairs Committee, a displeasing development for Harry Truman, whose replacement of Wallace on the 1944 Democratic ticket she had greeted with anger and tears. Denton insists that Douglas’s anti-Communism was “ardent” and even “strident,” but there’s scant supporting evidence in this biography or in the historical record. The control of nuclear weaponry was Douglas’s main policy interest, and in 1945 she imagined that this could best be accomplished by sharing the secrets of the atom with the Soviet Union. Most crucially, she opposed Truman on military aid to Greece and Turkey, and said she voted for the Marshall Plan “because of its decency” and not “because of the cold war.” When it came to “decency,” anti-Communism didn’t ­really make the cut.

Debate over these matters was conducted, according to Denton, in a “maelstrom of fear and agitation, ignorance and xenophobia, fanaticism and opportunism.” This sort of overstatement persists — Denton writes that Douglas was “one of the few Democratic incumbents re-elected in 1946,” when nine out of the 16 running in California were returned to office — and it crescendoes in the author’s treatment of the Nixon-Douglas Senate race. Denton never really acknowledges that, along with a lot of bullying smears — the distribution of a pink-papered flyer comparing Douglas’s voting record to that of the far-left New York congressman Vito Marcantonio — Nixon also spread a number of salient facts.

Denton claims that the former ambassador Joseph P. Kennedy contributed a large sum of money to Nixon, but doesn’t mention that his son Jack publicly expressed his own pleasure in the victory of Tricky Dick. (The nickname is Douglas’s admittedly immortal contribution to political nomenclature.) Denton’s previous books, including “The Bluegrass Conspiracy” and “The Money and the Power” (written with Roger Morris), have sometimes concerned organized crime and cabals, and here she shapes an unconvincing theory that Douglas’s campaign may have been sabotaged when Mob associates “embedded” the lawyer Paul Ziffren in its finance operations. But, as Denton notes, it was Douglas’s primary opponent who had first called her the “Pink Lady,” and it was the defection of voters from her own party that defeated her in the general election. Truman quite reasonably refused to campaign for her. The essential reason for Douglas’s loss would be most clearly offered by the congresswoman herself, on plain white paper, in her memoirs. “There was the United States fighting Communism, and I was the person who said we should limit the power of the military and try to disarm the world and get along with Russia.”

It is mostly, of course, Nixon’s later spectacular rise and fall that has kept alive any political memory of Helen Gahagan Douglas. By the mid-1960s, her name was a nostalgic rhyme for “Ronald Reagan” in a Tom Lehrer song. After reaching the White House, Lyndon Johnson had her lead a delegation to Liberia but ignored a letter Douglas sent him about a visit she had made to the Soviet Union: “Dear Lyndon, dear, dear Lyndon, . . . Nikita Khrushchev is an old man and he wants to help the cause of peace in his final active years.” At the end of his presidency, during which Douglas traveled the country protesting the Vietnam War, Johnson refused to shake her hand at a White House reception. Helen Douglas deserved better than that, but she has never really been entitled to the long sentimental shower of laurels that continues even now with this new biography.

Thomas Mallons new book, Yours Ever: People and Their Letters, has just been published. He is now writing a novel about the Watergate scandal.
