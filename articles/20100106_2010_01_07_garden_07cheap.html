On the Cheap

The Rocky Road Home

Fred R. Conrad/The New York Times
By JULIE SCELFO
Published: January 6, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

FOR more than 25 years, Michael Logozzo, a native of Brooklyn, lived overseas, working various jobs — as a chef in Venice and in Rome, as a magazine editor in Paris and as a television host and fund-raiser in Warsaw. In 2003, he moved into a guarded compound in Kabul, Afghanistan, overseeing the acquisition of property for Roshan, a telecommunications company, and helping run a charitable program to feed Afghan children.

AN UNPLEASANT SURPRISE After two days of traveling, Michael Logozzo arrived home expecting to see a furnished apartment.

But as the battle against the Taliban intensified, and car bombings and kidnappings increased, members of Mr. Logozzo’s large extended family in New York City — he has 14 brothers and sisters — began lobbying him to come home.

“They thought it was insane that I would take a job there to begin with, and that it was more insane to stay longer than my original six-month commitment,” he said. “None of them have the travel bug I do, and they were scared for my safety.”

Last July, he agreed to move back after his contract ended in December, and he visited Manhattan to find an apartment. After a week of looking, he signed an $1,850-a-month lease for a 550-square-foot studio with a sleeping loft in Chelsea.

Back in Kabul the following week, Mr. Logozzo, 58, realized that after so many years of living overseas he owned almost no furniture, aside from an antique Biedermeier dining table and chairs and a braided wood armchair that had been in storage for years. Concerned about whether he would be able to furnish his apartment as nicely as he wanted to, he responded to a message on the New York Times Web site (the message is no longer online) offering to help people with “a furniture budget that feels too tight” by matching them with a professional designer.

In an e-mail message from Kabul, Mr. Logozzo wrote, “I’ll be starting from scratch and I have a budget with some definite design ideas but I could really use some help putting it all together.”

After living in a war zone, he added, he looked forward to having a comfortable, well-furnished place: “I just want to make it home.”

Although he had $10,000 to spend — considerably more than most people who responded to the notice — Mr. Logozzo also had an entire apartment to outfit: he needed a complete living room, including a sofa with a pull-out bed; lighting; a rug to cover his wood floors; window treatments; and plenty of places to display the objects and artwork he had collected during his travels. And he was skeptical that he could get it all for that amount.

Richard Mishaan, a Manhattan interior designer and the author of “Modern Luxury,” who is introducing a new line of lighting with the Urban Electric Company next month, agreed to take on the project free. In August, Mr. Logozzo returned to New York for another short visit and the two met at the nearly empty apartment.

During the jovial hourlong session, they swapped stories about their travels, looked at pictures in Mr. Mishaan’s book and discussed Mr. Logozzo’s design aspirations.

Among the many things Mr. Logozzo hoped to change, he told Mr. Mishaan, were: “the air-conditioner, which I absolutely hate because it sticks too far out into the room. I hate the radiator cover. I hate the staircase the way it is. I hate the sliding doors on the closet — they’re cheap and nasty.”

He also told Mr. Mishaan that he liked sisal, he entertains frequently and he did not want the space to look cluttered. “One thing you have to know, I’m completely a clean freak,” he said.

Mr. Logozzo pointed to one particular room in Mr. Mishaan’s book as an example of what he was looking for: a room the designer had done for a showhouse, with muted maize walls, brown leather and neutral accent pieces. “I’m coming back from a really very stressful time,” he said. “I want it to be quiet.”

And he said he shared a belief Mr. Mishaan expressed in the book: that first impressions are important.
