Secrets Kept Last Week May Lead to Victories This Week

Tony Romo, expecting more pressure from the Eagles in their rematch, said, “That's why I think we're going to see every blitz ever invented.”

By JUDY BATTISTA
Published: January 4, 2010

Ken Whisenhunt folded up his Arizona Cardinals play card and essentially tucked it in his pocket Sunday. Marvin Lewis rested the Bengals’ top running back and three defensive starters. The Philadelphia Eagles played a startlingly passive defensive style.

Were they rolling over in the season finale? Not exactly.

All three teams, blowout losers Sunday, had something to preserve: the element of surprise. In a scheduling quirk that will send coaches deeper into the recesses of their playbooks, three N.F.L. wild-card games will be rematches of games played Sunday, an oddity that has happened only nine previous times since 1990.

On those occasions, one team won both games four times. The teams split five times. One was the 2001 Jets, who beat the Oakland Raiders in the final regular-season game to clinch a wild card but lost the playoff game.

The Jets, who will play the Cincinnati Bengals on Saturday afternoon after trouncing them, 37-0, on Sunday night to clinch a playoff berth, played the rematch with the Raiders in Coach Herman Edwards’s first season. The 2001 Jets, much like the Jets on Sunday night, could not afford to hold back with a playoff berth on the line. When the Jets lost the second game, Edwards said the back-to-back cross-country flights contributed to how worn down the Jets were in the second half of the wild-card game. But he said Jets coaches gleaned insight from the first game, surely something coaches this week are searching for, too.

“You get a good feel for your opponent,” said Edwards, now an ESPN analyst. “You look at a matchup, you see how you played the first game. The second game, you say: ‘Let’s really go at this guy. We’re going to game plan around these three guys,’ where maybe in the first game, you don’t do that.”

Before Sunday night’s game, Edwards said the Bengals had used a vanilla offensive plan — read: not too many tricks — for much of the season, relying on the running game to keep games close. With Cedric Benson out, the Bengals ceded the running game against an inspired Jets team.

Lewis left quarterback Carson Palmer in the game into the third quarter, but he was clearly balancing health versus a desire to play well. And at the same time, he was not giving the Jets’ defensive coaches an up-close look at Benson.

Instead of firsthand experience, the Jets must rely on game film to see how other teams attacked him. Still, the Bengals’ awful performance cannot do much for their confidence entering the rematch, something which the Eagles, who have lost twice to the Dallas Cowboys this season, may wrestle with, too.

“It gives you a little bit of an edge when you win; you figure, ‘We beat these guys,’ ” Edwards said. “If you lose that late in the year, you question a little bit. If it was a play or two, that’s one thing. If you lose by two scores, uh-oh.”

That probably does not apply to the Cardinals, who entered the game determined to show the Green Bay Packers almost nothing in anticipation of the wild-card game. Whisenhunt said last week that he approached his game plan as he usually did but that he would adjust when it became clear that the Cardinals would face the Packers again. The Packers’ Mike McCarthy took a different tack. His team has been on a roll — it had won six of its previous seven games before Sunday — so he did not want to disturb the momentum it had developed. The result: the Packers played their regulars for much of the game and won, 33-7.

“If we do play them in two weeks, great, we’ll be ready,” he said before Sunday’s game. “If we don’t, we’re going to make sure we’re ready for them this week.

“That’s our approach. Momentum is a benefit. That’s obvious. We have a lot of momentum the second half of the season, so you want to keep that going.”

The Eagles’ plan is more curious. They were playing for the National Football Conference East title, but they made the decision not to aggressively rush quarterback Tony Romo, but rather to drop into coverage. They did not want to show too many blitzes? Perhaps. And the result was a 24-0 Cowboys victory that was more physically dominating than the final score showed. Romo and almost everybody else expect the Eagles to look far different Saturday night.

“We’re probably going to see a completely different team next week because I don’t think they liked what they saw today,” Romo said Sunday night. “That’s why I think we’re going to see every blitz ever invented.”

Edwards said: “If there is any wrinkle you might show in the playoff game, you might have a blitz where you say, ‘We’re not going to show it this week.’ You get through it and you don’t show anything.”

Eagles Coach Andy Reid is the only head coach in the wild-card round with experience playing an opponent back to back. So he may be wondering if he made the right decision Sunday. After all, the last time he did this, in 2001, the Eagles beat the Tampa Bay Buccaneers twice and made it to the N.F.C. championship game. Perhaps the Cowboys, of all teams, learned something from that.

Next Time With Meaning?

On Sunday, Commissioner Roger Goodell, in remarks made on CBS, suggested that the N.F.L. might come up with a plan to offer incentives — maybe in the form of extra draft picks — for teams to play their starters in meaningless late-season games. This became the fan cause du jour after Indianapolis pulled its starters against the Jets in Week 16, scuttling a shot at an undefeated season and opening the door for a Jets victory, which went a long way toward helping them into the playoffs. The idea is that fans do not pay top dollar to watch the equivalent of end-of-season exhibition games.

When the competition committee takes up the issue in the spring, opponents of a plan to encourage teams to play all-out should cue up just one video: the one of Wes Welker blowing out his knee Sunday without even being hit. The Patriots exercised their right to decide who plays — the substitution patterns were hard to figure, but that’s beside the point — and fans got their money’s worth by watching the starters.

But maybe the irate Colts fans should ask Patriots fans if they would have preferred that their players rested, so Welker would be healthy to start the playoffs. And maybe the N.F.L. also has to weigh whether it is good for the game to have a marquee player, and one of the most likable players in the league, out of action in the playoffs. Should teams announce on the Friday before games which starters they intend to rest? Fine. That gives fans the option of skipping the game and trying to sell their tickets if they do not want to see Curtis Painter under center.

Competitive fairness is a valid concern, and there is probably no perfect solution to the issue of a team, like the Jets, benefiting by playing games in which their opponents decide to rest their starters. But shouldn’t coaches be able to weigh what their team needs most — momentum versus health — without the implicit threat of a draft-day disadvantage. Draft picks are supposed to help the league’s weakest teams. Make it too low a pick, and no team would care enough to play starters for it. Make it too high a pick, and the N.F.L. would endanger one of the underpinnings of parity. Good luck, N.F.L., coming up with a viable fix for this.

A Hard Year Ahead

Brutal schedule watch, 2010: the Patriots. They will host the Colts in their annual throwdown, but they will also have Minnesota, Green Bay, Cincinnati and Baltimore at home, and San Diego and Pittsburgh on the road. And, of course, they will play two games against the Jets.
