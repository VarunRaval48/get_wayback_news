Special Report: Men's Wear

Knit Nifty

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By JESSICA MICHAULT
Published: January 18, 2010

MILAN — The men’s wear shows on Monday started with a laugh at Pringle of Scotland.

Skip to next paragraph
Chris Moore/Karl Prouse

Pringle of Scotland put the emphasis on knits in outerwear.

Before the clothing hit the catwalk, a three-minute tongue-in-cheek animated film by the Scottish artist David Shrigley explained how the brand had made its knitwear for the past 195 years. Once everyone was properly educated on the house’s history, it was time for the designer Clare Waight Keller to display the brand’s future. And it looks very bright.

The collection was all about knitwear as outerwear. So out came the nubby sweaters, sporty coats with sleeves crafted in densely woven yarn and blazers enveloped in curly goat-hair vests.

The classically tailored suits were topped with knife-pleated kilts (a trend this season) that looked smart and decidedly wearable. But it was the variations and innovations in knitwear construction that truly showed how committed the brand is to both fine craftsmanship and modernity.

This is not the Pringle your grandfather used to buy.

Poverty Chic

Expect the unexpected when you go to a Vivienne Westwood show. For the fall/winter 2010 season, that meant designer duds for derelicts.

On a catwalk covered in cardboard boxes, models walked, and sometimes staggered, along in a hodgepodge of outfits that looked, one hopes intentionally, like they had been dug out of a Dumpster.

The models, their faces and hair stiff as if with frostbite, were swathed in layers of fabric. Ms. Westwood packed on the bold-print chunky sweaters, boxy oversized jackets and bulky fake fur coats to give the collection a hefty feel.

She paired these with her famed drop- crotch pants, wide-waist trousers (suspenders are a must) and all-in-one jumpsuits for a result that was decidedly survivalist in style.

Layering It

John Varvatos was in a romantic mood, staging his winter 2010 show in a Renaissance-era church, its ceilings covered in colorful frescoes and the catwalk backlighted by dripping candles.

The collection was a study in light layering on a slim silhouette. Using charcoal gray, black, cream and just a touch of plum, the designer cleverly offset the layers with multiple fastening options, so a suit jacket could zip up taut or button close classically.

There was nothing to fault in the clothing, but the final result was a show that lacked forward momentum.

The Big Mix

Neil Barrett baptized his collection “iconic hybrid” and turned his attention to the idea of mixing sartorial staples to create textural tension. This is ground that has been trod before but, over all, Mr. Barrett pulled it off.

The concept worked best when it melded buttery black leather with classic wool suiting in subtle ways, less so when the idea became blocks of contrasting fabrics.

The wrinkled washed suits adorned with fabric-covered buttons had a refined rock ’n’ roll attitude, and the white cotton T-shirts bonded with a slab of pressed fabric should be well-priced winners.

Knit Nifty

The “Sport Couture” show by Dirk Bikkembergs kept the message simple. You want classic designer sportswear while you’re skiing the slopes, mountain climbing or riding you Yamaha motorcycle? I’m you man.

And a sports freak you will have to be to pull off the designer’s form-fitting sweaters, body-hugging pants, pullover knit vests and lace-up knee-high boots.

There was a feeling of pure unapologetic masculinity to this show, with its red, white and blue color palette for day and classic grays for athletic evening attire. And that vibe surely was helped by the smattering of Italian military cadets in dress uniform that filled in the ranks at the large show venue.

City Samurai

Building on its urban aesthetic of last season, the Giuliano Fujiwara show focused on tailoring with attitude. “It’s aggressive,” Masataka Matsumura, the brand’s creative director, said backstage before the show. “Right now only those who have a fighting spirit will survive.”

That translated into a strong collection of samurai sportswear for city streets. Long pleated skirts added fluidity to the bulk of black jackets worn over thick knits that curved up into lapels and drooped down the leg.

Checked shirts tied at the waist, while the bands of knitwear that encircled the torso flirted with the notion of modern- day obis. And count on the jewelry accessories — razor-sharp removable lapels and gold collars — from a new licensing agreement with Giovanni Bosso Atelier to get a lot of editorial attention.

As for the sporty ankle boots, they look to be a smart choice for future urban warriors.
