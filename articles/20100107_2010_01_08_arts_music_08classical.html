Opera

‘THE BARBER OF SEVILLE’ (Saturday and Sunday) The Bleecker Street Opera, a recent offshoot of the late, lamented Amato Opera, mounts its second offering: a new production of Rossini’s antic comedy, directed by Theresa K. Pond and conducted by David Rosenmeyer. (Through Jan. 17.) Saturday at 3 p.m.; Sunday at 7 p.m.; Theaters at 45 Bleecker Street, at Lafayette Street, East Village , (917) 617-1399, bleeckerstreetopera.org; $35 to $40. (Steve Smith)20100107

★ ‘CARMEN’ (Friday and Tuesday) Though it is difficult to make an opera as familiar as “Carmen” seem startling and fresh, the British director Richard Eyre does it in his gripping new production for the Metropolitan Opera. Other than updating the Seville setting from the 1830s to the 1930s, to evoke the repressive era of the Spanish Civil War, Mr. Eyre presents a fairly traditional take on Bizet’s masterpiece. But by teasing out details that show the characters bucking against civic and sexual constraints, he uncovers the rawness and daring at the opera’s core. And he draws involving and courageous performances from a willing cast, led by the Latvian mezzo-soprano Elina Garanca, who makes a sultry, sexy and impetuous Carmen. As Don José, the tenor Roberto Alagna blends rough-hewn ardor and French refinement. The robust baritone Mariusz Kwiecien utterly looks the part of the suave and cocky matador Escamillo, and the rich-voiced soprano Barbara Frittoli is a vulnerable Micaëla. In his Met debut, the young Canadian conductor Yannick Nézet-Séguin draws a compelling account of this ingenious score from the Met orchestra. (Through May 1.) At 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; sold out. (Anthony Tommasini)20100107

‘DIE DREI PINTOS’ (Saturday and Sunday) The Bronx Opera opens its season with what is billed as the first New York staged performances of Weber’s comic opera “Die Drei Pintos,” left incomplete at his death in 1826 and finished in 1888 by Mahler. The company presents the work (which Weber intended as a comic counterpart to his Faustian opera “Der Freischütz”) in a new English edition by Benjamin Spierman, who directs the production. Michael Spierman conducts. Saturday at 8 p.m.; Sunday at 2 p.m.; Lehman College, Lovinger Theater, Bedford Park Boulevard West, at Goulden Avenue, Bedford Park, the Bronx , (800) 838-3006, brownpapertickets.com; $15 to $30. (Vivien Schweitzer)20100107

★ ‘DER ROSENKAVALIER’ (Saturday) After nearly a decade away from the role, the soprano Renée Fleming is singing the Marschallin in the Met’s revival of Strauss’s “Rosenkavalier.” She is joined by her colleague from her last run in the opera, Susan Graham, singing Octavian, one of this essential mezzo-soprano’s signature achievements. Ms. Fleming may have lost some of the vocal sumptuousness she brought to the Marschallin before. But she sings the role splendidly. In ways that matter, she gives a richer and more nuanced and poignant performance as this middle-aged princess, who seeks delight and escape from the inexorable passage of time in an affair with her smitten 17-year-old cousin. Ms. Graham remains nearly ideal as the impetuous young male lover. The appealing soprano Christine Schäfer takes over the role of Sophie for the final four performances. The bass Kristinn Sigmundsson is a suitably boorish and vocally booming Baron Ochs. The veteran maestro Edo de Waart conducts a lithe account of Strauss’s most beloved opera. (Through next Friday.) At 1 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metmuseum.org; $150 to $375 tickets remaining. (Tommasini)20100107

‘STIFFELIO’ (Monday and Thursday) Verdi’s “Stiffelio” was a flop at its 1850 premiere in Trieste, its integrity compromised by the demands of censors. Seven years later Verdi presented an extensive revision of the work titled “Aroldo.” But in recent decades “Stiffelio” has come into its own and has had notable productions, including the Metropolitan Opera premiere in 1993, staged by Giancarlo del Monaco as a vehicle for Plácido Domingo in the challenging title role: an early-19th-century German evangelical minister who learns of the infidelity of his wife, Lina, and comes to forgive her (though, conveniently, after his avenging father-in-law has killed Lina’s lover). Last at the Met in 1998, “Stiffelio” returns on Monday in the original production, this time with the tenor José Cura as Stiffelio and the soprano Sondra Radvanovsky as Lina. For this revival, Mr. Domingo moves to the pit to conduct this uneven but rich Verdi score. (Through Jan. 30.) At 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; $20 to $375. (Tommasini)20100107

‘TURANDOT’ (Saturday and Wednesday) The tenor Salvatore Licitra has taken over the role of Calàf in the Met’s revival of Franco Zeffirelli’s opulent production of Puccini’s “Turandot.” When Mr. Licitra made his Met debut in 2002, a last-minute substitute for Luciano Pavarotti in “Tosca,” he had an undisciplined but muscular and vibrant voice. In recent years his singing has been erratic. In this production he is singing with more control and evenness, but he still sounds constrained and leathery. He had his moments, though. The soprano Maria Guleghina slices through the orchestra with her steely voice as Turandot. The soprano Maija Kovalevska is a rich-voiced, deeply expressive Liù. (The impressive Lise Lindstrom sings Saturday’s performance; Ms. Guleghina returns on Wednesday.) The exciting work of the conductor Andris Nelsons continues to be the news of this revival. (Through Jan. 28.) Saturday at 8:30 p.m.; Wednesday at 8 p.m.; Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; $20 to $375. (Tommasini)20100107

Classical Music

AMERICA-ISRAEL CULTURAL FOUNDATION GALA (Sunday) This foundation, which supports Israeli culture, celebrates its 70th anniversary with a gala concert featuring artists including the violinists Shlomo Mintz and Pinchas Zukerman, the pianists Orli Shaham and Yefim Bronfman and the cellist Mischa Maisky. The program includes works by Schumann, Schubert, Mendelssohn, Mozart, Dvorak and Avner Dorman. At 7 p.m., Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $40 to $125. (Schweitzer)20100107

BARGEMUSIC (Friday through Sunday) Elena Ulyanova offers a promisingly virtuosic piano recital on Friday in the intimate confines of the coffee barge turned concert hall on the Brooklyn side of the East River. Her program includes Chopin’s Andante Spianato et Grande Polonaise Brillante; Liszt’s Tarantella (from “Années de Pèlerinage, Second Year”); Rachmaninoff’s Sonata No. 2 and Prelude in G; Debussy’s “Brouillards” and “Ondine”; and a Soler Sonata. On Saturday and Sunday the violinist Mark Peskanov and the pianist Niklas Sivelov collaborate on a program of Bach and Handel violin sonatas. Friday and Saturday at 8 p.m.; Sunday at 3 p.m.; Bargemusic, Fulton Ferry Landing next to the Brooklyn Bridge, Brooklyn , (718) 624-2083, bargemusic.org; $35; $30 for 65+; $15 for students. (Allan Kozinn)20100107

CHAMBER MUSIC SOCIETY OF LINCOLN CENTER (Thursday) Seating only 100 listeners, the Rose Studio is an ideal space to enjoy chamber music. On Thursday musicians including the clarinetist David Shifrin and the pianist Alessio Bax perform Martinu’s Trio for Flute, Cello and Piano; Saint-Saëns’s Tarantelle in A minor for Flute, Clarinet, and Piano; Villa-Lobos’s Choros No. 2 for Flute and Clarinet; and Fauré’s Trio in D minor for Clarinet, Cello and Piano. At 6:30 p.m., Rose Studio, Rose Building, 165 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center , (212) 875-5788, chambermusicsociety.org; sold out. (Schweitzer)20100107

★ SASHA COOKE (Sunday) Watching this lavishly gifted young mezzo soprano come into her own on the recital platform and the operatic stage has been among the more consistently rewarding pleasures of the last few seasons. Here, accompanied by the skillful pianist Ken Noda, she presents an hourlong recital featuring Schumann’s “Liederkreis” and selections by Rossini, Mozart and Bernstein. At 11 a.m., Walter Reade Theater, 165 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center , (212) 875-5456, lincolncenter.org; $20. (Smith)20100107

★ PHILIPPE JAROUSSKY (Thursday) Although this exciting young French countertenor is most frequently encountered in Baroque repertory, last year he ventured into the more humid clime of chanson on a CD titled “Opium,” to memorable effect. Mr. Jaroussky and his accompanist, the pianist Jérôme Ducros, present selections from the album, including delectable treats by Fauré, Chausson, Massenet, Chaminade and Hahn. At 7:30 p.m., Weill Recital Hall, Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $40. (Smith)20100107

JUILLIARD CHAMBERFEST (Monday through Thursday) Juilliard’s annual chamber music extravaganza features students of all levels who have received intensive coaching throughout the winter. The festival kicks off on Monday with musicians from Juilliard’s new historical performance department and the Royal Academy of Music. Concerts later in the week feature a wide range of repertory. (Through Jan. 16.) Monday through Thursday at 8 p.m., Paul Hall, Juilliard School, 60 Lincoln Center Plaza; Wednesday at 1 p.m., Peter Jay Sharp Theater, 155 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center , (212) 769-7406, juilliard.edu; free. (Schweitzer)20100107

ANTHONY MCGILL (Saturday) The principal clarinetist in the Metropolitan Opera Orchestra, Anthony McGill here presides over a chamber program that includes Schubert’s “Shepherd on the Rock” and Schumann’s Three Fantasie Pieces, as well as the Schumann song cycle “Dichterliebe.” Mr. McGill’s colleagues are Monica Yunus, soprano; Terry Cook, bass-baritone and Caroline Stoessinger, pianist. At 7 p.m., Metropolitan Museum of Art , (212) 570-3949, metmuseum.org; $40. (Kozinn)20100107

★ MET CHAMBER ENSEMBLE (Sunday) Though you could wish that James Levine’s taste in contemporary music were a little broader, you have to admire his intense advocacy of formidable modernist composers like Elliott Carter and Milton Babbitt. He conducts the works of these giants with authority and palpable excitement. His program with the Met Chamber Ensemble on Sunday includes bracing and inventive works for voices and chamber ensemble by Mr. Babbitt (“The Head of the Bed”) and Mr. Carter (“Syringa”). Judith Bettina, Kristin Hoff and Evan Hughes are the vocal soloists. As a treat, the program concludes with a lighthearted Strauss rarity, “Der Bürger als Edelmann” Suite. At 5 p.m., Zankel Hall, Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $56 and $72. (Tommasini)20100107

NEW JERSEY SYMPHONY ORCHESTRA (Friday through Sunday) Thomas Wilkins conducts the New Jersey Symphony Orchestra in its Italian Festival this weekend, featuring the violinist Sarah Chang playing Vivaldi’s “Four Seasons.” The program also includes Lully’s ballet music, Respighi’s “Ancient Airs and Dances” Suite No. 3 and Stravinsky’s “Pulcinella Suite.” Friday at 8 p.m., New Jersey Performing Arts Center, 1 Center Street, Newark; Saturday at 8 p.m., State Theater, 15 Livingston Avenue, New Brunswick, N.J.; Sunday at 3 p.m., Community Theater, 100 South Street, Morristown, N.J. ; (800) 255-3476, njpac.org or njsymphony.org; $20 to $82. (Schweitzer)20100107

NEW YORK PHILHARMONIC (Friday, Saturday, Tuesday and Thursday) Alan Gilbert and the pianist Yefim Bronfman collaborate on the Prokofiev Second Piano Concerto as the first half of a program that also includes the Rachmaninoff Second Symphony, both in New York (on Friday and Tuesday) and at Long Island University (on Saturday). On Thursday the baritone Thomas Hampson joins Mr. Gilbert and the orchestra for “The Wound Dresser,” a Walt Whitman setting by John Adams. The program also includes Schubert’s “Unfinished” Symphony, Haydn’s Symphony No. 49 (“La Passione”) and Berg’s Three Orchestral Pieces. Friday at 8 p.m.; Tuesday and Thursday at 7:30 p.m.; Avery Fisher Hall, (212) 721-6500; nyphil.org; $31 to $119. Saturday at 8 p.m., Tilles Center for the Performing Arts, at the C. W. Post campus of Long Island University, Brookville, N.Y. , (516) 299-3100, tilllescenter.org; $50 to $110. (Kozinn)20100107

★ ORANGE MOUNTAIN MUSIC PRESENTS (Sunday) Most of the recordings issued by the Orange Mountain Music label have featured the music of its founder, Philip Glass, whose lovely “Songs and Poems for Solo Cello” will be played here by Wendy Sutter, the cellist for whom they were composed. But this showcase also includes music by other composers, including Trevor Gureckis and Mick Rossi, and a performance by the FoJoTo String Trio, a gathering of brilliant, protean artists: the kora player Foday Musa Suso, the banjoist Tony Trischka and the guitarist Joel Harrison. At 7 p.m., Le Poisson Rouge, 158 Bleecker Street, near Thompson Street, Greenwich Village , (212) 796-0741, lepoissonrouge.com; $15. (Smith)20100107

SIX SATURDAYS WITH VIERNE (Saturday) John Scott, the organist and director of music at St. Thomas Church Fifth Avenue, begins a six-week series devoted to the great organ symphonies of the French composer Louis Vierne with the Symphony No. 1 in D minor. (Through Feb. 13.) At 4 p.m., 1 West 53rd Street, Manhattan , (212) 664-9360, saintthomaschurch.org; free, but donations are suggested. (Smith)20100107

ANDREAS STAIER (Tuesday) This eloquent fortepianist offers an all-Haydn program, in commemoration (slightly belated) of the bicentenary of the composer’s death in 1809. Included on his program are the Sonata in C sharp minor (Hob. XVI: 36), C minor (Hob. XVI: 20) and two in E flat (Hob. XVI: 49 and 52), as well as the Variations in F minor (Hob. XVII: 6). At 7:30 p.m., Zankel Hall, Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $38 and $48. (Kozinn)20100107
