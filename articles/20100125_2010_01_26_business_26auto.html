To Attract Shoppers, G.M. to Pay Debt to U.S.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By BILL VLASIC
Published: January 25, 2010

DETROIT — Like Lee Iacocca at Chrysler, Edward E. Whitacre Jr. thinks the best way to lure consumers back to a bailed-out automaker is to pay back the loans from American taxpayers.

In his first comments as the permanent chief executive of General Motors — after deciding to drop his interim status — Mr. Whitacre said Monday that G.M. will retire its remaining $5.7 billion in debt to the federal government by June.

Then he wants G.M. quickly to arrange a public stock offering that would allow taxpayers to cash out their 60 percent ownership stake in the automaker.

Mr. Whitacre came out of retirement after a long career with AT&T to become chairman of G.M.’s board when it emerged from Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection last July.

He took over as chief executive on an interim basis when the board requested and received the resignation of Fritz Henderson in December. After two months on the job, Mr. Whitacre was asked last week by G.M. directors to finish what he started.

“I was both honored and pleased to accept this role,” Mr. Whitacre told reporters at a briefing at G.M. headquarters in Detroit. “So I’m going to do it for a while.”

Paying back the Treasury Department, as well as the Canadian government, will represent a “significant milestone” for G.M. in its comeback from near-collapse in 2009, he said.

The debt does not come due until 2015. But Mr. Whitacre said G.M. is moving toward becoming a profitable company faster than expected, and wants to clear up its obligation as soon as possible.

“We intend to make one payment in June, if not before,” he said.

He is taking a page from the playbook of Mr. Iacocca, who sought loan guarantees from the federal government in 1979 for Chrysler.

When Chrysler paid back $1.2 billion in loans ahead of schedule in 1983, its sales improved quickly.

Mr. Whitacre is hoping for the same reaction from consumers when G.M. repays its loans. Independent surveys show that some car buyers have avoided G.M. dealerships because of the bailout, and gravitated to products made by the Ford Motor Company, which survived without government aid.

G.M. has already paid back $1 billion of the $6.7 billion that the United States lent the company as part of its broader G.M. rescue effort that totaled about $50 billion.

The bulk of the funds were converted into preferred shares and common stock in G.M.

Mr. Whitacre said he had no timetable for a public stock offering, but emphasized the importance of an “exit strategy” for taxpayers from their ownership position.

The senior member of President Obama’s auto task force, Ron Bloom, said a quick return of the taxpayer funds was a “good idea,” and reflected the progress G.M. had made since it came out of bankruptcy.

“We do not feel that this puts G.M. in any way, shape or form in harm’s way,” Mr. Bloom said in a conference call with reporters.

He added that the government played no role in the G.M. board selecting Mr. Whitacre as the company’s permanent chief.

Mr. Whitacre, 68, has already put his stamp on G.M.’s management team by promoting several younger executives and establishing clear profit-and-loss responsibilities for its regional operations.

He said he did not envision taking the chief executive post when he agreed to lead G.M.’s board last summer.

“I had planned for it not to happen, but it happened anyway,” he said.

People close to the board’s decision said that while a search for a permanent chief executive was still in its early stages, directors concluded that no other candidates filled G.M.’s needs better than Mr. Whitacre.

“The place needs some stability,” Mr. Whitacre said. “And I guess that’s me.”

His outsider status has already allowed Mr. Whitacre to drastically streamline G.M.’s ponderous decision-making processes, said one industry analyst.

“As he reviews the business, he’s grilling people on why they do things that they do, the way they do, as only an outsider can,” said Jeremy P. Anwyl, chief executive of the auto research Web site Edmunds.com.
