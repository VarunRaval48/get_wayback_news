Run to Twilight Indoors at Millrose

Hiroko Masuike for The New York Times

The former Super Bowl players Willie Gault, left, and Tim Dwight will run in a 60-meter race at the Millrose Games Friday.

  • Sign In to E-Mail

By GEORGE VECSEY
Published: January 27, 2010

Willie Gault and Tim Dwight have known the thrill of running with the football in the Super Bowl. On Friday, they will recapture the art of bursting out of the blocks as sprinters in the Millrose Games.

With three other players from Super Bowl teams, Gault and Dwight will participate in the Super LX 60-meter sprint in the 103rd version of the Millrose Games, the grand old track and field meet at Madison Square Garden.

Before calling the race a gimmick, take a look at the narrow-waisted, slim-hipped Gault, 49 years old, still looking like the whippet who caught four passes for the Bears in Super Bowl XX, a lot of X’s ago. Two years ago, Gault set a record for his age group, running the 200 meters in 21.80 seconds — compared with the current world record, 19.19, by Usain Bolt.

Gault never stopped thinking of himself as a sprinter and hurdler when he was faking around Darrell Green of Washington for a 75-yard touchdown in the 1984 conference championship game. Gault then ran a diagonal route to the end zone out of his deep love for the fans in Robert F. Kennedy, wanting to give them a few more split seconds of observing a sprinter at work.

He gave up the hurdles two years ago when one of them reached up and took him down, like a tackler grabbing him by the ankles. Tore his rotator cuff, so now he sprints. At 49.

The four other runners all have N.F.L. credentials. Dwight, 34, was a receiver and return man for five teams and ran back a kickoff 94 yards for a touchdown for Atlanta in Super Bowl XXXIII.

Anthony Dorsett Jr., 36, started at free safety for Tennessee in Super Bowl XXXIV and also played for the Raiders in XXXVII. Phillip Buchanon, 29, played six games in 2002 for the Raiders, who then played in Super Bowl XXXVII. He was still playing for Detroit this season. And Ryan LaCasse, 26, played 12 games for the Indianapolis Colts in 2006, before their Super Bowl title. He weighed 257 pounds as a defensive end, but now weighs 225 as a strength coach at Syracuse.

Gault has never stopped being a track man, working out with a club in Los Angeles and traveling to masters meets. He won a hurdles race at the Millrose Games as an undergraduate at Tennessee. He qualified for the United States team that did not participate in the 1980 Moscow Olympics, but did win a gold medal as an American bobsledder in the 1988 Winter Games at Calgary, Alberta. He leaves no doubt that track is the sport of his heart.

“It’s not a team sport,” Gault said Wednesday. “It’s a pure sport. Mano a mano.” He said it was “unfortunate” that “corporate America” has put so much money into football, saying that “Americans love violent sports.”

He excelled at avoiding the worst of the violence. He played 11 years for the Bears and the Raiders and never missed a game, partly because of a Japanese acupuncturist, Hiroshi Shiriashi. The two are still friends, he said. In his only Super Bowl, with Shiriashi on duty, Gault caught four passes for 129 yards and returned four kicks for 49 yards against the Patriots. He was the ringleader of the Bears’ dance performance known as the Super Bowl Shuffle.

Dwight had to do a lot of work to get back in sprinting shape. He finished in the N.F.L. in 2007 and has kept his edge by participating in triathlons and in his first marathon. Once a Big Ten champion in the 100 meters for Iowa, he was all over the place two weeks ago, but thinks he may be getting in shape to race.

•

He trained last week by attending the conference championship game in New Orleans, won by his former San Diego teammate Drew Brees.

The impending Super Bowl revived memories of Dwight’s big moment on Jan. 31, 1999. Atlanta was trailing the Broncos, 31-6, when Dwight caught the kickoff on the 6-yard line. “We were using a wedge, which is outlawed now, too dangerous,” Dwight said. “I had a tight end, two defensive linemen and a defensive back in front of me. I had some meat. I stayed patient and then I popped out of the wedge.”

It was not yet time to unleash his inner sprinter. Dwight had been taught to run directly at the kicker, to slow him down and confuse him, before veering away. He did it to his buddy Jason Elam before turning on the burners. Atlanta still lost, 34-19, but he had a Super Bowl touchdown.

As they prepare for their sprint Friday night, Dwight is picking New Orleans in the Super Bowl because he thinks Brees is due for a good game. Gault is picking the Colts because of Peyton Manning at quarterback and because “the Indianapolis defense is better than the New Orleans defense.”

There will be no defense in the sprint Friday night — just five men who played for Super Bowl teams, now running straight ahead. You tell them it’s a gimmick.
