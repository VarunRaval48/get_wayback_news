Special Report: Menswear

YSL and a Naked Screen

By SUZY MENKES
Published: January 22, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

PARIS — With social networking the subject of the moment, Yves Saint Laurent made a bold decision to link its men’s wear to a new generation. The house let the photographer Bruce Weber produce a film that was funny, sexy and striking enough to go viral around the Net.

Rick Owens focused on a sleek, streamlined cut.

But viewers won’t be getting even a glimpse of the perfectly cut coats, the jackets with a tendency to be rounded up at the base of the spine or even the drop-crotch pants that are a favorite of the designer, Stefano Pilati.

Instead, it was all bared bodies of young men larking about in the open air — inter-cut with a period movie about a female photographer persuading women in the early 1960s that nudity is natural and even, as Mr. Weber’s commentary put it, “funny.”

Mr. Pilati has used artists to create an on-screen mood board for his collections. It all seems a complex way to promote the YSL image — just as the clothes in the collection sometimes seem complicated to a fault. But there was much to like, especially the jersey wrap coats, with fringed scarf belts and the arrow-straight overcoats falling vertically down the body. The pleated tunic tops peeping from suit jackets, the cover-all jumpsuits and the complex pants with one leg in a skirt are part of the designer’s soft-and-hard vision for the Saint Laurent man. But it is not the cover-up clothes but the Weber movie that got the twitters going.

Rick Owenshas achieved something exceptional: retaining the spirit of the fashion underground while making his clothes ever more sleek and luxurious. And he has almost abandoned weird ideas — unless you count the rather beautiful leather gauntlets with fur at the fingertips.

Instead, everything seemed streamlined in cut, from the wide-shoulder jackets at the show’s start to the corduroy coats, often with a tunic like a draped scarf underneath. The wild side came in the animal magic: reptilian boots, an alligator jacket and implants of fur. Ever since Mr. Owens worked with Revillon, he has understood fur’s elegance. The show, with gleaming finishes and gilded patterns on skins, had an otherworldly feel that still was grounded in reality.

There was no nonsense from Louis Vuitton because Yves Carcelle, the company’s chief executive, had come up with a mantra: “We must celebrate travel.’‘

So the show featured a deft mix of traditional tailored tweeds and techno puffa jackets, or the trad/mod mix with a techno parka over a double-breasted cardigan. Significantly, it also had a wide range of the famous LV bags on the runway: in tan leather to contrast with the predominantly gray clothing palette; classy backpacks and duffels, mixing fabrics like nylon and leather. Shoes also were strong, especially clog army boots and ones with zippers at the ankle.

In a welter of security checks and scares, it is hard to see romance in today’s travel, but the LV designer Paul Helbers used a Weiner Werkstätte theme to give depth to the show and added a modern touch when the German artist Christian Schoeler painted three bags as a tribute to art students and their customized bags.

Dries Van Noten built into his show its only staging trick: the models carried their own music down the runway, the soundtrack rising and fading as they walked. It was smart and modern — like the collection, which the designer described as both “playful” and a riff on fabric heritage.

Working with English mills, Mr. Van Noten implanted Harris tweed, symbolic of the old-style English gentleman, into sleeves and mixed traditional fabrics like club stripes and Prince of Wales checks throughout collection. Although the concept of contrast sleeves — often in stripes — became laborious, the show had many other streamlined pieces and over the last few years Mr. Van Noten has moved from the ethnic to modern classic with skill and style.
