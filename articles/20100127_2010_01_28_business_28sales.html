Carmakers Pounce on Toyota Owners

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By NICK BUNKLEY
Published: January 27, 2010

DETROIT — For years, General Motors has been on the defensive, steadily losing sales to Toyota. Now it finally sees a chance to turn the tables. On Wednesday, G.M. began offering discounts and no-interest financing specifically to Toyota owners.

“We’re responding to the concerns of many Toyota owners who have asked our dealers for help,” said a G.M. spokesman, Tom Henderson. “We’re reacting by offering a solution.”

Toyota was racing to find its own solution on Wednesday, as it tried to resolve its accelerator pedal problem and minimize the damage to its reputation for safe vehicles. The pedal flaw has created an opening for competing car companies like G.M.

Analysts expected Honda, Hyundai and Ford to benefit most. Edmunds.com posted a photo of a Honda dealership near Dallas that programmed its digital sign by the street to read, “Our gas pedals don’t stick!”

“I think there’s got to be instant worry for all Toyota owners,” David Thomas, senior editor of Cars.com, said. “It’s that mental image of a runaway car. There can’t be a worse association for any automaker to have.”

The car-rental company Avis, which also operates Budget, said it was removing 20,000 Toyotas from its fleet. Hertz, Enterprise, National and Alamo also said they would temporarily stop renting Toyotas.

The Indiana-based supplier that made the questionable pedals at a factory in Canada said it had started shipping replacements to Toyota plants. But it was unclear how quickly the parts would be ready for installation on the assembly lines and at dealerships for owners of millions of affected vehicles.

Federal regulators said Wednesday that Toyota was legally required to stop selling the eight models covered by last week’s recall. But the company took five days to announce the stoppages.

Such a drastic step raised concerns among many owners of those vehicles, even though Toyota insisted they were safe and that the problem was rare.

Late Wednesday, Toyota added 1.1 million more vehicles to its November recall, which already was the largest in its history. That recall, related to the vehicles’ floor mats and the design of the floor and pedal, now also covers the 2008 to 2010 Highlander and the 2009 to 2010 Corolla, Venza and Matrix. It also includes the 2009 and 2010 Pontiac Vibe, a mechanical twin of the Matrix.

The Corolla, Matrix, Vibe and 2010 Highlander already were included in last week’s recall and the sales suspension.

“It raises a lot of red flags,” said Regina Weiss of Brooklyn, who owns a 2010 Toyota Prius hybrid. That car is not being recalled, though earlier models were part of a November recall.

“I want to know that it’s safe,” she added. “It may be safe, but I’m not getting the sense that they know what’s wrong or really understand the problem.”

In a filing with the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, Toyota said the latest problem was in the pedal assembly of affected vehicles. When the heater is turned on in a car that is cold, internal water condensation on the cold parts can cause a sliding component inside the pedal assembly to stick instead of moving smoothly.

Toyota dealers were bracing for a hit to their sales, on top of a miserable sales year in 2009.

The models that Toyota has temporarily stopped selling accounted for 57 percent of its sales last year — the Camry, Corolla and Avalon sedans, Matrix wagon, RAV4 crossover, Tundra pickup and Sequoia and Highlander sport utility vehicles.

“The way times are now, a week lost of business is going to affect us, no doubt,” said John Tyburski, the general manager at Germain Toyota in Sarasota, Fla.

Though this is generally a slow month for dealers, Mr. Tyburski said his business would probably miss out on the sales spike it usually gets at the end of the month. The dealership would typically sell 50 vehicles this weekend. The number is expected to be much lower, Mr. Tyburski said. He is weighing how best to compensate his staff because many depend on commissions.

On Wednesday, some of the employees wandered through the lot placing red-and-white “Not for Sale” stickers on the recalled models.

Jake Fisher, a senior engineer with Consumer Reports, said drivers of the recalled models were very unlikely to experience a stuck pedal. But the ramifications of the problem are so serious, and the recall so large, that many consumers could have serious doubts about Toyota, he said.

“It took decades for Toyota to build up this reputation for quality and reliability, and this news is going to erode a lot of that reputation,” Mr. Fisher said. “If you’ve been thinking about a Camry, you might get a Honda Accord right now. If you’re looking at a Corolla, you might try a Ford Focus or a Hyundai Elantra.”

G.M., with its new incentives, clearly hopes to get into the mix. Through this week, G.M. is offering up to $1,000 toward paying off a Toyota lease, zero percent loans for 60 months or a $1,000 discount.

CTS, the supplier that Toyota cited as the source of accelerator pedals in the recalled vehicles, issued a statement Wednesday saying it had been working with Toyota “for a while to develop a new pedal to meet tougher specifications.”

It said testing had finished and the parts were beginning to ship to the affected plants, which Toyota plans to shut down next week.

CTS said it knew of, based on information from Toyota, “fewer than a dozen instances” involving a sluggish pedal. None involved a pedal that became stuck in a partly depressed position. It also blamed Toyota for the problem.

“The products we supply to Toyota, including the pedals covered by the recent recall, have been manufactured to Toyota’s design specifications,” the statement said.

Javier Hernandez and Stuart F. Brown contributed reporting.
