Square Feet | The 30-Minute Interview

Robert M. White Jr.

By VIVIAN MARINO
Published: January 7, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

Mr. White, 45, is the founder and president of Real Capital Analytics, a Manhattan-based market research company that tracks commercial real estate sales and trends globally.

Andrea Mohin/The New York Times

Multimedia
In order to view this feature, you must download the latest version of flash player here.

Before starting R.C.A., in 2001, Mr. White spent 14 years working in real estate investment banking and as a broker, at Granite Partners, now called Savills, and at Eastdil Realty.

Q. What prompted you to start the company?

A. My friends on the Street —whether they are in M & A or municipal bonds — could always say, “In my industry the volume last week was X-hundred billion.” Down to the penny. Nobody knew how many office buildings were traded in any given year or how big the volume was in this marketplace.

I started collecting information and publishing quarterly reports for clients, and as we traveled around pitching business, I could see their eyes light up when they saw this marketplace quantified. A lot of the standard metrics that Wall Street uses to analyze financial markets were applicable, and I thought, hmm ... there’s something there.

Q. What is R.C.A.’s main focus?

A. We focus exclusively on the capital side — the sales. We have 108 countries where we’re tracking deals right now. We have data partnerships and researchers, and throughout the staff we have 17, 18 language capabilities.

Q. Let’s get right to the numbers for the New York market.

A. In 2009, the investment market came to a near-standstill in Manhattan. For all property types, 2009 will be below $7 billion in terms of sales in the New York City metropolitan area.

That compares with $77 billion in 2007, so we’re at 10 percent of volume from two years ago. It’s also a big drop-off from 2008, which wasn’t a good year, either; in 2008, we had $26 billion in sales, which still earned us the No. 2 spot globally, behind Tokyo. When you think of it, $7 billion in deals is nothing — it’s not much more than the price for Stuyvesant Town.

Office sales are about $2.7 billion. In 2008, it was $15 billion, and at the peak, in 2007, it was $50 billion.

Q. How many transactions?

A. For office properties $5 million or greater in Manhattan, there were 23. In 2007, there were 188; and in 2008, 84.

Q. And for all property types?

A. It’s 86 transactions. In 2008, 250 transactions; and then the peak in 2007, it was 488 transactions, including some buildings that were traded twice.

Q. What was the biggest deal?

A. The biggest, and probably the most symbolic deal, was Worldwide Plaza. It was one of the last of the famous Macklowe Properties assets.

Q. Where does New York rank globally right now?

A. The bad news is Manhattan has been knocked off the perennial perch as the No. 1 market. It’s not even in the top 10 of all property types — it’s down to No. 13. In the office sector, New York fell to No. 8.

Q. Which cities precede it?

A. Let’s just talk office: there’s London, Tokyo, Paris, Seoul, Shanghai, Hong Kong and Moscow. Even Moscow had more office sales than New York!

Q. Do you find any of those markets of particular interest?

A. I’m watching very closely the pricing of office buildings in London, because that city is a very good template for where Manhattan is headed next. Manhattan and London have the world’s largest financial, economic and cultural centers, so capital from all over the globe wants to be invested in real estate in usually one of those two markets. London started its downward descent maybe a year before New York, but in the past six months, and especially in the past 90 days, activity has exploded.

Q. Is there a similar rebound on the horizon in New York?

A. A key factor in the turnaround in Europe has been foreign investors. They are responsible for about 80 percent of the deals in London over the past six months. And much of that money would like to be in New York. As cap rates fall in London and prices rise again, New York starts looking more attractive on a relative pricing basis.

Q. Do you own real estate?

A. I own a brownstone on the Upper West Side. I bought it 15 years ago for $475,000, and used a second mortgage on it to start the company. Now I’m trying to decide: Should I renovate this place to a single-family, which could get the big bucks, or maybe hedge the bet and do a duplex?

A version of this interview appeared in print on January 10, 2010, on page RE10 of the New York edition.
