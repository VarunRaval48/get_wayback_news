The Week Ahead: Jan. 17-23

Two experienced choreographers are looking backward and forward in the coming weeks. One of them, Pascal Rioult, begins the first of two programs by his company, RIOULT, with his very first dance, “Harvest,” a 1992 work that is an evocation of peasant life. Also on that program are his whirls to Ravel’s “Bolero” (2002) and premieres to Bach’s “Well-Tempered Clavier” and “Sonata for Violin and Piano No. 6,” both collaborations with the video artist Brian Beasley, who has created realms in which real and virtual dancers interact. The second program is a majestic production from last year, “The Great Mass” (Mozart). First program: Tuesday and Jan. 24 at 7:30 p.m., Thursday and Saturday at 8 p.m. Second program: Wednesday at 7 p.m. Friday at 8 p.m., Saturday and Jan. 24 at 2 p.m., Joyce Theater, 175 Eighth Avenue at 19th Street, Chelsea, (212) 242-0800, joyce.org; $10 to $49.

Jawole Willa Jo Zollar’s URBAN BUSH WOMEN, 25 years old this year, will revisit its past with a series of programs. In the first of them, “Zollar: Uncensored,” Ms. Zollar returns to the studies of female sexuality she created in the 1980s but dropped from her repertory when told by well-meaning advisers that they were too controversial for touring. Now she’s combined some of this older choreography with fresh material to make an essentially new work. Who knows if it will also be controversial? Wednesday through Friday at 7:30 p.m., Saturday at 7:30 and 10 p.m., Dance Theater Workshop, 219 West 19th Street, Chelsea, (212) 924-0077 or dancetheaterworkshop.org; $25.

Theater

Ben Brantley

Of all the soigné, quip-dispensing and unquenchably self-dramatizing characters created by Noël Coward, few came closer to self-portraiture than Garry Essendine, the beleaguered actor-protagonist in “PRESENT LAUGHTER,” the 1939 comedy that opens in revival this Thursday at the American Airlines Theater. “Garry Essendine is me,” Coward said, atypically disregarding grammar for the sake of emphasis. As Coward’s biographer Philip Hoare pointed out, it wasn’t so much Essendine’s surface sophistication or narcissism as his appetite for control amid chaos that made him so revealingly like his creator. And actors throughout the years have had a high old time portraying Essendine’s exquisite agony as his carefully ordered world threatens to crash down upon him: Coward himself, of course, but also Clifton Webb (in the play’s belated Broadway debut in 1946), George C. Scott (1982) and Frank Langella (in 1996). Now Victor Garber takes up Essendine’s cigarette holder and silk dressing gown in a Roundabout Theater Company production directed by Nicholas Martin. Though an assured Broadway veteran, Mr. Garber is probably best known as the not- quite-trustworthy authority figure from the television spy drama “Alias.” Expect him and Mr. Martin, who delivered a bracingly unconventional “Hedda Gabler” (starring Kate Burton) in 2001, to find some ambiguous shadows within Essendine’s urbanity. 227 West 42nd Street, Manhattan, (212) 719-1300, roundaboutheatre.org; $66.50 to $116.50.

Film

Stephen Holden

Two of the stronger films in the 19th New York Jewish Film Festival depart from the beaten path of historical documentaries and feature films, many of which focus on the Holocaust.

Haim Tabakman’s fateful drama “EYES WIDE OPEN,” set in contemporary Jerusalem, observes the unforgiving response within an ultra-Orthodox enclave to a gay relationship involving one of its own. When Aaron (Zohar Strauss), a deeply religious married father of two who runs a kosher butcher shop, begins a doomed affair with his assistant Ezri (Ran Danker), a homeless younger outsider whom he allows to live in his store, the consequences are swift and devastating. The movie makes you feel the intense social pressures within a tightly knit religious community.

Adam Elliot’s claymation feature, “MARY AND MAX,” follows the pen-pal relationship of Mary Dinkle (the voice of Toni Collette) a lonely 8-year-old girl living outside Melbourne, Australia, and Max Horovitz (Philip Seymour Hoffman), an obese 44-year-old Jewish New Yorker with Asperger’s syndrome. Their relationship spans 20 years. Altogether the festival, which runs from Jan. 13 to Jan 28, is showing 32 features and shorts from 13 countries at the Walter Reade Theater, the Jewish Museum and the Jewish Community Center in Manhattan.

“Eyes Wide Open” plays Saturday and Jan. 24 at the Walter Reade Theater; “Mary and Max” plays on the same days at the Walter Reade Theater. For complete festival information, visit FilmLinc.com.

Television

While the news on Broadway this season has been the dominance of screen actors, the New York stage has done what it can to redress the balance: Matthew Morrison (“South Pacific”) and Lea Michele (“Spring Awakening”) star in “Glee,” Jesse Tyler Ferguson (“The 25th Annual Putnam County Spelling Bee”) stars in “Modern Family.” This week the balance tips a little further with the premiere of “THE DEEP END” (ABC, 8 p.m. Thursday), a new lawyers-in-lust drama whose cast includes the Tony Award winner (for “Dirty Rotten Scoundrels”) Norbert Leo Butz. His character, a Southern-fried member of a high-powered Los Angeles firm, is named Rowdy; his job is to recruit and teach the young, attractive lawyers around whom the story lines revolve. Billy Zane stars as a dictatorial partner; Matt Long plays an idealistic first-year associate, an older version of the character he played five years ago in “Jack and Bobby.”

It’s a big week for new series: in addition to “The Deep End” they include the dramas “Human Target” (Fox, 8 p.m. Sunday), “Life Unexpected” (CW, 9 p.m. Monday) and “Spartacus: Blood and Sand” (Starz, 10 p.m. Friday) and the reality shows “The Buried Life” (MTV, 10 p.m. Monday), “Be Good Johnny Weir” (Sundance, 10:30 p.m. Monday) and “Paranormal Cops” (A&E, 10:30 p.m. Tuesday). The most hotly anticipated of them all, though, is probably the “Battlestar Galactica” spinoff “CAPRICA” (Syfy, 9 p.m. Friday) — at least among those who haven’t already watched the pilot on DVD or online, where it was released last April.

Classical

At a point in his career by which many opera singers have settled into respectable retirement, the celebrated Spanish tenor Plácido Domingo continues to seek new challenges. Mr. Domingo first performed in Verdi’s “Simon Boccanegra” at the Metropolitan Opera in 1995, playing Gabriele Adorno, a tenor role. But in 2007 Mr. Domingo announced that he intended to take on the title part, one of Verdi’s greatest baritone roles. On Monday, three days before he turns 69, Mr. Domingo will fulfill that ambition during the Met’s latest revival of Giancarlo del Monaco’s 1995 production.

Mr. Domingo, who is currently conducting an earlier Verdi opera, “Stiffelio,” at the Met, does not come to his latest challenge cold. He presented a scene from “Simon Boccanegra” during the Met’s 125th-anniversary gala last March and played the role complete in October at the Staatsoper Unter den Linden in Berlin. Reviewing a performance during that run for The International Herald Tribune, George Loomis wrote, “Mr. Domingo’s noble performance establishes Boccanegra as one of those rare roles where stature transcends voice type.”

Marcello Giordani, a tenor well versed in mustering thrills, plays the part of Gabriele Adorno here, with the admirable soprano Adrianne Pieczonka as Amelia Grimaldi and the seasoned bass-baritone James Morris as Jacopo Fiesco; James Levine will conduct. Through Feb. 6; Monday and Friday at 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center, (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; $20 to $375.

Pop

On Wednesday one of the most popular musical acts in New York, whose music dominates radio playlists and sells by the million, opens a four-night run in one of the city’s most famous concert halls.

Yes, that’s all certainly true for LADY GAGA, who is playing at Radio City Music Hall. But it also applies, on a scale that might surprise many pop fans, to AVENTURA, a leading Latin group from the Bronx that will be playing four nights at Madison Square Garden, which can hold about three times as many fans as Radio City.

Aventura is the biggest name in bachata, a sweet and heartsick ballad style with humble, rural origins in the Dominican Republic. In the 1990s it was modernized by Juan Luis Guerra, the sophisticated, jazz-trained king of Dominican music, and in the hands of Aventura, which released its first album in 1999, bachata has become a slick, syrupy sound custom made for radio and for arenas full of young women who chant along to every aching word.

The group’s latest album, “The Last” (Premium Latin), released last year, features guest appearances by Wyclef Jean, Ludacris and Akon, and it reached No. 5 on the Billboard album chart, an achievement usually reserved for so-called crossover acts that make it big through compromise and audience pandering. But Aventura has reached the top while staying true to its roots. Of course it helps when your roots are silky melodies and sweetly crooned celebrations of “amor, amor, amor.”

Wednesday, Thursday, Feb. 1 and Feb. 2, at 8 p.m., (212) 465-6741, thegarden.com; $64 to $129.50. All shows sold out except Feb. 2.

Art

Some 75 exhibitors will gather for the 56th WINTER ANTIQUES SHOW, starting Friday at the Park Avenue Armory. The show includes antiquities through the 1960s, ranging from ancient Roman glass to midcentury Modern. One third of the exhibitors specialize in Americana; the others in English, European and Asian fine and decorative arts. A committee of 160 experts from the United States and Europe vets every object for authenticity, date and condition. Proceeds benefit the East Side House Settlement, a community resource center in the South Bronx that focuses on education and technology. Through Jan. 31, at 67th Street and Park Avenue, (718) 292-7392.

The Connecticut painter PETER WAITE is known for rendering both the grand (large-scale cultural buildings) and the mundane (boardrooms, prison cells, subway entrances). Mr. Waite works in highly detailed color, often on aluminum panels. The work was informed by his father, a historian of Nazism, who took his son around Europe to view monuments to power. Starting Friday, Mr. Waite’s paintings will be on view at the Winston Wächter Gallery of New York and Seattle. Through Feb. 27, 530 West 25th Street, Manhattan; (212) 255-2718, WinstonWachter.com.
