Morgan Stanleys Quarter Is Weak, Unlike Its Pay Pool

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By GRAHAM BOWLEY
Published: January 20, 2010

By almost any measure, 2009 was a lean year for Morgan Stanley — except when it came to pay.

Despite the first annual loss in its 74-year history, Morgan Stanley earmarked 62 cents of every dollar of revenue for compensation, an astonishing figure, even by the gilded standards of Wall Street. In all, the bank set aside $14.4 billion for salaries and bonuses.

Those numbers, disclosed on Wednesday, along with news that the bank eked out a modest profit in the final three months of 2009, drew predictable fire from Washington policy makers and outside pay experts. With Morgan Stanley’s perennial rival, Goldman Sachs, expected to report blowout profits and pay figures on Thursday, the stage is set for yet another round of bonus complaints.

Still, in a gesture to the public mood, Morgan Stanley’s new chief executive, James P. Gorman, said Wednesday that his entire bonus would be paid in the form of deferred stock, with no cash at all. The outgoing chief, John J. Mack, had already announced he would take no bonus for 2009, after presiding over an era when he escalated risk-taking and the firm nearly went out of business.

Morgan Stanley justified its startling compensation ratio by saying its revenue had been artificially depressed by one-off accounting charges.

Those changes were linked to the appreciation of the bank’s debt, a sign that investors believed the bank was recovering from the financial crisis.

Adjusting for that effect, its compensation levels would have been around 50 percent of its revenue, closer to the industry average, it said.

But critics said those justifications ignored the fact that Wall Street’s resurgent revenue and bonuses were possible only after billions of taxpayer dollars had been pumped into Morgan Stanley and other banks, directly through the federal bank bailout program, as well as indirectly through a panoply of other generous government programs.

“They are churning out these compensation packages but never deal with the underlying issue of all the governmental programs,” said Jonathan Koppell, a professor of politics and management at Yale School of Management. “Where is the column adjusting their performance for that, too?”

Morgan Stanley’s pay figures drew heat in Washington. Representative Peter Welch, Democrat of Vermont, who this month proposed a 50 percent tax on all bonuses above $50,000, said in an interview: “This is totally self-serving. These are people who have the keys to the vault and are putting the money in their own pockets rather than lending to small businesses and building the economy.”

The last two years have been wrenching for Morgan Stanley. It nearly went out of business after what amounted to a run on the bank in 2008, and was saved by government money and cash from Japanese and Chinese investors who took stakes in the firm.

It was faulted again this year when it cautiously refused to take on risks in its sales and trading operations. While more aggressive rivals like Goldman Sachs bounced back quickly to heady profits, Morgan Stanley only swung back in the third quarter.

On Wednesday, Morgan announced its timid return to profitability continued in the fourth quarter — it earned net income from continuing operations of $413 million. For all of 2009, on a diluted basis, which includes new shares that have been sold, the bank lost 93 cents a share.

Making his debut in the conference call with analysts and the media to announce the results, Mr. Gorman said 2009 had been “a year of transition” but 2010 would be “a year of execution” as the firm enjoyed the results of changes to its businesses. These include a big expansion into the less volatile retail brokerage business of selling stocks and bonds to ordinary investors.

The biggest disappointment in the latest quarter came at the firm’s sales and trading unit, which is at the core of its business but which did not meet analysts’ expectations. But the firm said it had increased risk-taking in its trading business during the quarter, and Colm Kelleher, chief financial officer, said a program to hire up to 400 traders globally was on track and would add firepower to its operations. They would be “up to speed” in the first quarter of 2010, he said.

In an interview, Mr. Kelleher said the new traders were being hired from across Wall Street, including from firms like Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan, but he said they were not being paid excessively to join Morgan Stanley. He defended the firm’s compensation levels by saying Morgan had to compete for talent: “Do I think the industry is overpaid? Yes. Can we jeopardize the franchise, being St. Sebastian and getting pierced by arrows? No. There is no point in being martyrs here.”

Morgan Stanley has shaken up its senior management. In addition to the ascension of Mr. Gorman, and the move by Mr. Mack to the chairman’s role, Mr. Kelleher is becoming co-head of its institutional securities business, in part to fix the trading operations. He will be replaced by Ruth Porat, from the investment banking side of the firm.

Peter Nerby, an analyst at Moody’s, said Morgan Stanley was well positioned compared with rivals such as Citigroup and Bank of America, whose consumer banking businesses are suffering from the weak economy.

“This is not gangbuster profitability,” he said. “But one of the advantages Morgan Stanley has is it is not burdened by a big consumer book.”

Morgan will probably reveal the size of Mr. Gorman’s pay package this week. Three-quarters of other senior executives’ compensation is being paid in deferred cash and stock. But high as Morgan’s bonus pool is, it will almost certainly be eclipsed in absolute dollar terms by Goldman, which set aside $16 billion for compensation in the first nine months of 2009 alone.
