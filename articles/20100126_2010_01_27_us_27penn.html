Specter Feels Squeeze From New Friends and Old

Jessica Kourkounis for The New York Times

Senator Arlen Specter has expressed enormous confidence in his ability to survive. “I am not discouraged,” he has declared.

By KATHARINE Q. SEELYE
Published: January 26, 2010

DOYLESTOWN, Pa. — It seems either a cruel or fitting twist. Senator Arlen Specter left the Republican Party last year and became a Democrat to save his political career. Now, at 79, he suddenly finds that the party he switched into may not provide a safe haven.

The stunning upset by a Republican Senate candidate last week in Massachusetts has filled Democrats across the country with dread, forcing some into retirement and prompting more Republicans to enter races they might have sat out. And it has invigorated Mr. Specter’s rivals on the left and right, both of whom are trying to capitalize on his having switched parties.

Representative Joe Sestak, a second-term Democrat who is backed by progressives and who is challenging Mr. Specter in the May primary, has driven Mr. Specter to the left.

If Mr. Specter wins the primary, he will face former Representative Patrick J. Toomey, the Republican candidate for the Senate who six years ago narrowly lost the Republican nomination to Mr. Specter.

It was Mr. Toomey’s strength in the polls last year that prompted Mr. Specter to switch parties in the first place. As a former president of the Club for Growth, a conservative advocacy group, Mr. Toomey said he expected to rally enthusiasts from the Tea Party movement, which bolstered Scott Brown, the Republican who won in Massachusetts.

“And Pennsylvania is a lot more conservative than Massachusetts,” Mr. Toomey said.

Mr. Specter acknowledged in an interview even before the Massachusetts election that the national mood could work against him. But he also expressed enormous confidence in his ability to survive. Other Democrats who are retiring, he said, are doing so out of fear of losing — they had not been beaten but were merely discouraged.

“I am not discouraged,” he declared.

Polls show Mr. Toomey either in a dead heat with Mr. Specter or somewhat ahead.

Mr. Sestak, a retired Navy admiral, clearly faces an uphill fight against Mr. Specter, a fixture in state politics for four decades. Mr. Sestak has lagged behind him in the polls and raised only half the money the senator has.

In addition, Mr. Specter is backed by the Democratic establishment, including President Obama and the state party apparatus. That may not be a big advantage with voters this year, given the signs of anti-incumbent fervor, but it could help Mr. Specter in his first intramural test, which comes Feb. 6, when the state Democratic committee is to make its endorsement.

T. J. Rooney, the state Democratic chairman, said in an interview that he expected Mr. Specter to win the endorsement. He said Mr. Specter would have a better chance than Mr. Sestak of holding the seat in November because he had a stronger campaign infrastructure and would be “more true to our base.”

Mr. Rooney also said that despite Mr. Specter’s 30 years in the Senate, “he has a better ability to run as an anti-incumbent” because he had voted against his own party, whichever one, many times. Mr. Sestak almost welcomed Mr. Rooney’s comments, noting that they underscored the themes that he hammers on the campaign trail: that Mr. Specter is a creature of the establishment and a political opportunist with no core principles.

Mr. Obama, Mr. Sestak told voters here, “needs allies who aren’t going to be a flight risk after this primary.”

As a Democrat, Mr. Specter has been maneuvering to the left.

When he was a Republican, he had an “A” rating from the National Rifle Association, but as a Democrat he voted last year against that powerful gun lobby on a measure it backed to allow concealed weapons to be carried across state lines.

Last year, Mr. Specter opposed the so-called card-check measure that would make it easier for labor unions to organize; but since then he has been working to find a compromise, which labor has so far supported.

Perhaps most prominently, he has staked out the liberal, antiwar position on Afghanistan, opposing Mr. Obama’s troop surge.

Mr. Specter visited the war-torn region over the holidays. He said his antiwar position was “not part of a political matrix,” though analysts see it as a calculated move to try to fire up the party’s liberal base.

Despite being to the left of Mr. Specter on many issues, Mr. Sestak supports the Afghanistan troop surge. How much this matters to voters remains to be seen. At a public forum here, Randy Sklar, 57, a retired mortgage broker, asked Mr. Sestak if the surge was worth the cost.

Mr. Sestak gave a rambling response, which Mr. Sklar described after the meeting as “incoherent.” Nonetheless, he is supporting Mr. Sestak even though Mr. Specter better reflects his view on the war.

Why? “Because Arlen Specter is a hypocrite,” Mr. Sklar said, reflecting a sense that in this race, Mr. Specter himself is the issue.

Even as Mr. Specter tries to appeal to the left, he is also trying to win over conservative Democrats, especially the state’s powerful A.F.L.-C.I.O.

“This thing is not locked up,” said Bill George, the union’s longtime leader.

Mr. Specter also has to be careful not to alienate Democratic women, who support him for backing abortion rights despite what they view as his demeaning treatment of Anita F. Hill two decades ago in Clarence Thomas’s Supreme Court confirmation hearings. The matter arose last week when Mr. Specter told Representative Michele Bachmann, a Minnesota conservative, in a radio interview to “act like a lady.”

Ms. Bachmann had interrupted him several times, and he had lost his patience. He later called her to apologize.

Mr. Specter’s campaign is based on his experience, constituent service and ability to bring home federal money. But mainly he casts himself as an independent who acts according to his conscience, even if he does not make everyone happy.

“People have picked on me for a long time, from the single-bullet theory to when I was bald,” he said.

The first reference was to his work on the Warren Commission, which concluded in 1964 that Lee Harvey Oswald had acted alone in assassinating President John F. Kennedy; the second was to his loss of hair after chemotherapy in two bouts of Hodgkin’s disease (he also has had surgery to remove a brain tumor and has had heart bypass surgery).

“I don’t pay attention to them,” Mr. Specter said of his critics. “I just go about my merry way, and so far, I’ve always managed to get 51 percent.”
