Kids Today: Tethered to Electronic Devices

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
Published: January 28, 2010

To the Editor:

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Lars Leetaru

“If Your Children Are Awake, Then They’re Probably Online” (front page, Jan. 20) confirmed my observations as a high school English teacher of the increased use of electronic devices by the young.

Over the recent December break, I challenged my students to enjoy the richness of life free of entertainment technology for three days, and then to write about their experiences.

Some were able to get through the 72 hours only by unplugging their computers and televisions and turning their iPods and iPhones over to helpful and enabling parents. Others succumbed to the siren call of technology before the sun had set on the first day.

Although many characterized their media-deprived time as “torture,” “boring,” “the worst experience of my life,” others discovered the pleasures of baking (and burning) cookies, playing board games, drawing, conversing with family members and having snowball fights with cousins.

I would encourage other teachers to try this exercise with their students. Perhaps educators across the country could organize a national day of liberation from electronic devices when young people would reflect on a life unplugged.

Rebecca Smith
Bayside, Queens, Jan. 20, 2010

•

To the Editor:

Once upon a time, before video games, televisions, iPods and cellphones, we had to entertain ourselves. And most of us did so by playing outside. Youth spend 50 percent less time engaged in outdoor activities than kids did 20 years ago, and now we learn in “If Your Children Are Awake, Then They’re Probably Online” that they are “plugged in” for more than 50 hours each week.

Studies indicate that children who spend time in nature are more likely to develop positive attitudes toward the environment in adulthood. Today’s kids will have to deal with tomorrow’s environmental challenges. While climate change jeopardizes our very existence, inactivity threatens the health and well-being of our children; about one-third of American kids are overweight.

It is time to make a concerted effort to pull the plug and reconnect our kids with the wonderful and truly wireless world outside.

Jacqueline Ostfeld
Washington, Jan. 21, 2010

The writer is national youth representative for the Building Bridges to the Outdoors project of the Sierra Club.

•

To the Editor:

If more schools and families would shift toward creative media opportunities for kids, then academics, parents and educators might stop pulling their hair out about the out-of-control use of electronic devices documented by the Kaiser Family Foundation.

There needs to be a tidal shift in outlook by adults. The first stage of recovery is admission and acknowledgment. Now, the real work can begin. Engage, create, innovate and challenge kids with media. They can and will rise to heightened expectations.

But if we continue to bemoan their media consumption without an appropriate antidote, then no progress will be made any time soon.

Matt Levinson
Hillsborough, Calif., Jan. 20, 2010

The writer is assistant head of school at the Nueva School.

When do these kids do their homework?

Tim Burke
Middletown, N.J., Jan. 20, 2010

The writer is a college English teacher.
