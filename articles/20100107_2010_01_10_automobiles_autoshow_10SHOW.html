Detroit Auto Show

Time for a Repair Job and a Reality Check

Paul Sancya/Associated Press

Workers preparing the Volkswagen display at the North American International Auto Show in Detroit.

  • Sign In to E-Mail

  • Reprints
By JERRY GARRETT
Published: January 7, 2010

“DETROIT” is shorthand for the American automobile industry in the way that Hollywood is a catch-all term for movies and television. Just as the studios don’t make many films in Hollywood anymore, automakers don’t build many cars in Detroit. Yet like its Tinseltown counterpart, the Motor City remains a beacon, even if the glow has dimmed.

COMING OUT The next-generation Audi A8 will make its auto show debut in Detroit.

Comparisons to Detroit’s past glories are inevitable as the industry gathers downtown for the 2010 North American International Auto Show. Two days of press previews start Monday — the show is open to the public Jan. 16-24 — and organizers promise at least 40 new models. (Caveat: at auto shows, the word “new” is liberally applied.)

This year, beneath the manufactured hoopla and forced enthusiasm, the mood could be almost post-apocalyptic: Pontiac and Saturn have vanished. Saab’s death seems imminent. The Chinese are buying Hummer. General Motors and Chrysler, each trying to rise after being laid low by bankruptcy, will come to the party without top hat or tails; Chrysler will not formally introduce any production-ready cars; G.M. will have only a couple.

Chrysler used to be the king of car show choreography, staging elaborate model introductions — minivans playing leapfrog, Jeeps crashing through convention-hall windows, cattle drives through the streets. This year, the company has not scheduled even a single news conference. Instead, its Chrysler, Dodge and Jeep vehicles will share a display area with its new overseer, Fiat.

This show may lack some of the usual air of anticipation, not to mention smoke machines, pulsing music and glamorous models pulling sheets off pie-in-the-sky concept cars.

Other international auto shows have been taking their lumps as well. The 2009 British Motor Show was canceled, Frankfurt was downsized and non-Japanese automakers pulled out of Tokyo. But the reigning shows may not be losing relevance so much as they are being stretched to the breaking point by their proliferation.

Not long ago there was a core of blockbuster events: Detroit, Geneva, Frankfurt, Tokyo. These have been joined by shows in emerging markets that aspire to prominence — in Australia, China, India and the Middle East.

Manufacturers are struggling to come up with enough new models and concept cars — crowd-pleasing design studies that often provide hints of future vehicles — to promote at so many shows. So they are increasingly trying to engage the public with alternatives like simulators, hands-on exhibits and interactive displays.

“You will notice that Ford will have not one concept vehicle on the floor this year — not one,” said Marisa Bradley, Ford’s manager for the Detroit show. “Instead we are focusing on real vehicles, in real time, that are the real deal.”

Sam Locricchio, a spokesman for the Detroit show’s organizers, said the event’s darkest hour probably came last year when the economy seemed to be on the precipice and auto sales were plunging. Things are looking up, he said, for 2010.

“The world didn’t think we could pull off the show last year, but it not only survived but surprised,” Mr. Locricchio said in an e-mail message. “This year, I don’t think we would ever say, ‘Yup, hard times are done!’ But, there is a certain spirit of, if not all-out optimism, certainly direction.”

Ford, which withstood Detroit’s cataclysm and didn’t take a government bailout, brought out 14 new or revised models last year. But did that spurt leave the model pipeline nearly empty?

“No, not true at all,” Ms. Bradley said. She pointed to the redesigned Focus compact that will be revealed at Detroit, as well as a freshened Lincoln MKX crossover. “We will have no shortage of new vehicles and new technologies.”

In an example of the trend toward direct public engagement, G.M. will have a driving route around Cobo Center, where the show takes place, so consumers can try its cars. (Alas, the Chevrolet Volt plug-in hybrid will not be offered.)

Inside Cobo, G.M. has tried to revamp its 85,000-square-foot exhibit so that visitors won’t see the ghosts of Hummer, Pontiac, Saturn and Saab. Filling those holes will be interactive displays and exhibits of new technology like direct-injection engines and the Volt’s electric propulsion system. In contrast to Ford, G.M. plans to roll out concept cars from each of its four remaining brands (Buick, Cadillac, Chevy and GMC).

Cobo has itself changed since last year’s touch-and-go show, which several manufacturers abandoned at the last minute. German automakers threatened a mass exodus over the center’s faulty wiring, malfunctioning heating and air-conditioning, leaking roof, clogged toilets and inoperative security and fire-safety systems. Organizers have overseen some $3 million in repairs and other improvements to Cobo in recent weeks; millions of additional dollars in upgrades are promised.

Although Porsche still refuses to show vehicles here — the company says it doesn’t sell enough cars to Detroiters — other German brands will be represented. BMW is showing a new hybrid, the Concept ActiveE, Mercedes-Benz will formally introduce the E-Class Cabriolet and Audi will display its redesigned A8 flagship sedan.

Most automakers, it seems, are just happy to be back — to have survived. But they have fewer new models: many were canceled or put on hold for financial reasons. And the industry is trying to make a transition to greener cars.

That transition will be prominent. The basement, usually packed with start-ups, customizers, accessory companies and small foreign automakers, will instead feature a short EcoXperience driving route for green vehicles. On the main floor, an eclectic mix of electric vehicles will populate a new Electric Avenue display.

These E.V.’s, along with hybrids and other alternative vehicles, are likely to be stars at the show: Audi, BMW, Hyundai, Tesla and Toyota will be among those offering glimpses of high-mileage cars of the not-so-distant future.

Kurt Antonius, a spokesman for American Honda, which will unveil its CR-Z hybrid sports coupe here, said: “This is really two auto shows. One is for the public; some huge percentage of auto show attendees are within six months of actually buying a car. That is an absolutely prime demographic for us — one we can’t find anywhere else.

“The other show is for the industry. As an industry gathering, Detroit has no equal. It is the place to see and be seen. In that regard, it really is like a premiere in Hollywood.”
