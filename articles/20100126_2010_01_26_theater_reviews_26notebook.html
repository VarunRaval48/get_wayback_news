Opposites in Style, Equals in Stature

Richard Termine for The New York Times

Elaine Stritch singing Stephen Sondheim at the Café Carlyle, where she is performing his work through next week.

By CHARLES ISHERWOOD
Published: January 26, 2010

Great musical theater does not always take place in the precincts around Times Square, as I was forcefully reminded last week. In a span of three days I caught two of the most memorable musical theater performances I’ve seen so far this season. Neither was part of a Broadway show.

Plácido Domingo, center, as the title character in the Met's “Simon Boccanegra,” with Adrianne Pieczonka and Marcello Giordani.

Returning to the baritone repertory after many decades as a tenor, Plácido Domingo was mighty and majestic in the title role of the Metropolitan Opera’s “Simon Boccanegra.” And at the Café Carlyle, Elaine Stritch elucidated the chilly verities in the work of Stephen Sondheim with piercing precision.

Although they work in different genres and have vastly different vocal styles — not to mention resources — both Mr. Domingo and Ms. Stritch continue to impress and inspire with their commitment to performing, their dedication to stretching their talents in new directions.

And both continue to work like Trojans despite having reached an age at which many of their peers have moved into retirement. Mr. Domingo celebrated his 69th birthday last week, a few days after the “Boccanegra” opening; Ms. Stritch will turn 85 next week, celebrating onstage on the last night of her monthlong run at the Carlyle.

Their reasons for soldiering on are not hard to fathom. As these two marvelous performances made clear, Mr. Domingo and Ms. Stritch inhabit the music they interpret with a similar ferocity. Mr. Domingo wears many hats in the operatic world, and Ms. Stritch’s guest appearances on the comedy “30 Rock” are a consistent delight, but these two artists fulfill their gifts most powerfully when they are onstage before a live audience.

Mr. Domingo runs both the Los Angeles and Washington opera companies, and conducts regularly too; he was in the pit for “Stiffelio” the night after his opening in “Simon Boccanegra.” This immersion in practically all facets of opera has caused some to suggest that he’s stretched himself too thin. But his thrilling turn as a trouble-plagued doge in 14th-century Genoa in Verdi’s opera ranks as one of the most resonant and affecting performances I’ve seen him give. Mr. Domingo may enjoy being a one-man opera industry, but his contribution to the art form will ultimately rest on what happened — what continues to happen — in performance.

What happens in “Simon Boccanegra” is particularly remarkable. Italian opera is both mocked and perhaps a little beloved for librettos stuffed with lunatic coincidence and convoluted plots. They are presented not just with a straight face, but also with a full-throttled emotion that we are expected to respond to. It’s not always easy to suspend all that disbelief. “Simon Boccanegra” rivals even Verdi’s “Trovatore” in its pileup of absurdities. (It also contains the most delicious supertitle translation I’ve seen: “Die you evil pirate!”) Mr. Domingo achieves the implausible feat of making the narrative inanities utterly immaterial.

Opera regularly traffics in primal and heightened emotional states — grief, despair, rage, ecstasy — that would challenge the greatest stage actors to bring to life even in artfully wrought drama. Opera singers must strike a particularly tricky balance: the performance must be big, to match the size of the music and the scope of the feeling, but size must not swamp truth.

Mr. Domingo gets the balance just right. He has the ability to excavate the real emotion in even the most contrived circumstances. In “Boccanegra” it is more or less impossible to avoid the occasional hoary gesture. The prologue ends with Mr. Domingo atop a pedestal, grabbing his head in woe. But his portrait of a father whose joy at the discovery of his long-lost daughter is poisoned by the knowledge that she has fallen in love with his dire enemy is rendered with a delicacy and conviction that make this impossible story improbably moving. His attenuated death scene in the last act is marked by a dignity and pathos — and a rigorous honesty — that allow you to forget the follies of the plot entirely.

It is as impossible to imagine Mr. Domingo singing Sondheim as it is to fathom Ms. Stritch singing opera. The vocal acting required for grand opera is of a far different order from the kind that suits the songbook of Mr. Sondheim. The quiet desolations and ambiguities of human relationships are the matters of most concern to this chronicler of the intricate disillusions of experience. Most of his musicals are free of throbbing, sobbing love stories. (Even the love triangle in “Passion” is depicted in a cool, ruminative mode.)
