Music Review | 'Ruddigore'

Gilbert, Sullivan and Some Not-So-Helpful Ghosts

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By STEVE SMITH
Published: January 18, 2010

Just why “Ruddigore,” the 10th of the 14 operettas created by the peerless team of Gilbert and Sullivan, has failed to catch on over the years is hard to figure out. Introduced in 1887 in the wake of “The Mikado,” a runaway hit, “Ruddigore” was initially deemed a lesser work. Though its fortunes changed after a series of revisions, it is rarely encountered today.

The problem, if “Ruddigore” has one, might be the target of its satire: a strain of 19th-century melodrama long faded from view. But in a new production that concluded the latest season of the New York Gilbert & Sullivan Players at City Center on Sunday afternoon, the director and conductor Albert Bergeret and his lively company showed that “Ruddigore” has enough charm and drollery to enchant a modern audience.

The action centers on two would-be lovers in a seaside village equipped with a full-time cadre of professional bridesmaids: Rose Maybud (Sarah Caldwell Smith), a maiden of unimpeachable virtue, and Robin Oakapple (David Macaluso), a worthy but shy farmer. Just as circumstances unite them, Richard Dauntless (Dan Greenwood), Robin’s seafaring foster brother, reveals that Robin is actually Sir Ruthven Murgatroyd, descended from a long line of baronets cursed to commit at least one crime daily.

Tormented by ghostly ancestors who step out of their portraits in a gallery to tally his crimes, Robin finds a loophole to break the curse. In the end, naturally, all is bliss.

Among a uniformly excellent cast, Mr. Greenwood stood out for his robust voice and agile dancing. Richard Alan Holmes was appropriately campy as Sir Despard Murgatroyd, the younger brother on whom the curse settled in Robin’s absence. Caitlin Burke deployed twitches and blurts with a virtuoso flair as Mad Margaret, and David Wannen was a dark, dashing phantom Sir Roderic Murgatroyd.

The orchestra played with style and gusto. Simple but imaginative sets by Albere, including a portrait gallery inspired by Edward Gorey’s illustrations, and David Auxier’s spirited choreography added to the production’s appeal. An inserted reference to President George W. Bush earned laughs, but the funniest moment in “Ruddigore” — a bit of gently lacerating self-parody in the scampering patter trio “My Eyes Are Fully Open” — belonged to its creators.
