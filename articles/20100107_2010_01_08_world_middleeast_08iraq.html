Move Made to Bar Iraqi From Ballot

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By NADA BAKRI and ANTHONY SHADID
Published: January 7, 2010

BAGHDAD — An Iraqi parliamentary committee moved Thursday to bar a Sunni Muslim lawmaker from national elections in March, outraging his supporters and threatening to worsen sectarian tension here.

The lawmaker, Saleh al-Mutlaq, a prominent Sunni politician, and his group, the National Dialogue Front, were among those disqualified on the grounds of promoting the banned Baath Party of former President Saddam Hussein.

While the decision is not final — Iraq’s election commission must ratify it — Mr. Mutlaq also suggested that he had no real recourse and warned of the implications.

“They fear my popularity,” he said in an interview. “They think that banning me from the elections will isolate the Sunnis, stop them from taking part and marginalize them.”

The decision, by the Accountability and Justice Committee, was perhaps the most significant development since Parliament ratified legislation last month to organize the March 7 vote. The ramifications could be vast.

Mr. Mutlaq and his group had emerged as a leading force in predominantly Sunni provinces, which were underrepresented in the last parliamentary vote, in 2005. His absence could foster a situation that American officials here have long feared: another election reinforcing Sunni disenfranchisement as the military begins its withdrawal of tens of thousands of combat troops.

Mr. Mutlaq, 61, left the Baath Party in 1977 but has openly courted its still-substantial support among Sunnis. On Thursday, he accused the Shiite government of neighboring Iran of pushing the decision to bar him from the election and limit Sunni representation. He said the move followed the arrest of hundreds of his supporters in the past two months. But he appealed for calm and said he would seek to overturn the committee’s decision, whatever his chances.

“Banning us lacks credibility, is illegal and is wrong,” Mr. Mutlaq said. “Iraqis will not accept this. We will not give up, and we will appeal.”

His supporters were similarly grim, worrying of a repeat of the 2005 vote, which many blame for deepening tensions that led to horrific sectarian strife in 2006 and 2007.

“The Iraqi people are going to pay the price,” said Rasim Mahmood al-Ubaidi, 36, who lives in Baquba, northeast of Baghdad. “Mutlaq’s alliance is a peaceful political movement, and its disqualification might lead it to carry weapons against the state.”

Falah Shanshal, a member of the parliamentary committee, said the decision was based on proof that Mr. Mutlaq was promoting the Baath Party of Mr. Hussein, which is specifically banned under the Iraqi Constitution. He said that the decision now rested with the Independent High Electoral Commission and that he expected it to be ratified.

“We have to apply the law, and they are promoting the Baath Party,” he said.

Musaab al-Aanie, a spokesman for the electoral commission, said that the group had not yet received the decision but was prepared to hear any appeal.

The decision comes amid an unsettled time in some Sunni regions, particularly Anbar Province, a vast desert province in western Iraq.

Mr. Mutlaq and his ally, former Prime Minister Ayad Allawi, competed with other groups to court the province’s main currents: Baathist, tribal and religious. The police have blamed political infighting for some of the violence in Anbar, especially in Falluja. Other high-profile attacks, they say, appear to be the work of insurgents connected with Al Qaeda in Mesopotamia, the homegrown terrorist group.

In a well-coordinated attack there Thursday, four explosions in quick succession tore through a remote town before dawn, killing and wounding counterterrorism officials, police officers and a lawyer. At least seven people were killed, most of them relatives of Lt. Col. Walid Slaiman, the head of the counterterrorism unit in the town. The explosions — in Hit, 85 miles west of Baghdad — also wounded seven people, the police said.

The officers killed and wounded were investigating insurgent attacks in Anbar, where the American military plans to withdraw more than half of its 7,500 troops by month’s end.

“There are terrorist cells there, and they are benefiting from any security breach to carry out more attacks,” said Sheik Muhammad Abu Wissam, a tribal leader.

Last week, two suicide bomb attacks in Ramadi killed 24 people and severely wounded the governor, Qasim Abed al-Fahadawi. The Islamic State of Iraq, a group linked to Al Qaeda in Mesopotamia, took responsibility for that attack.

Mr. Mutlaq, candid and disheveled, has been prominent in Iraqi politics since soon after the American-led invasion in March 2003. With a doctorate in agronomy, he prospered in agriculture under Mr. Hussein’s government. After the dictator’s fall, Mr. Mutlaq took part in a committee to help draft a constitution for Iraq. (He opposed the version that was adopted.) Unlike many Sunnis, he participated in the 2005 election; his supporters won 11 seats.

With the waning of support for the venerable Iraqi Islamic Party, once the country’s largest Sunni party, Mr. Mutlaq’s alliance with Mr. Allawi and Vice President Tariq al-Hashemi seemed poised to perform well in crucial Sunni regions.

Mr. Mutlaq has long urged reconciliation with former Baathists and denounced what he views as undue Iranian influence over Iraq’s government.

Omar al-Jawoshy contributed reporting from Baghdad, and an Iraqi employee of The New York Times from Anbar Province.
