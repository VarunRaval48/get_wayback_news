FOR CHILDREN

‘ADVENTURES WITH DORA AND DIEGO’ (Friday through Thursday) The young Latino stars of the Nickelodeon animated television series “Dora the Explorer” and “Go, Diego, Go!” have taken their expeditions into new territory at the Children’s Museum of Manhattan, whose exhibition has separate Dora and Diego environments, including Diego’s Animal Rescue Center and Dora’s Fiesta. Intended for ages 2 to 6, the interactive, bilingual displays are designed to teach language, science and music skills, as well as facts about Latin America. From 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Tisch Building, 212 West 83rd Street , (212) 721-1223, cmom.org; free with museum admission: $10; $7 for 65+; free for under 1 and members.20100114

‘ARCHAEOLOGY ZONE: DISCOVERING TREASURES FROM PLAYGROUNDS TO PALACES’ (Friday, Sunday, Tuesday and Thursday) Children will step into the shoes of an explorer like Indiana Jones in this permanent exhibition at the Jewish Museum, but the adventures will be purely scholarly. Still, there is plenty of excitement in analyzing artifacts like a jar handle, a clay jug and a bangle and figuring out the purpose behind ancient pieces like a Greek helmet and a bull-shaped vessel. This interactive show also includes a recreated room from the Ottoman period (about 1900), where young archaeologists can dress in costume. Hours: 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m., except Fridays, open until 4 p.m., and Thursdays, open until 8 p.m.; 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92nd Street. , (212) 423-3200, thejewishmuseum.org; free with admission: $12; $10 for 65+; $7.50 for students; free for under 12 and members.20100114

‘B.O.T.C.H.’ (Friday through Monday) If you’ve always wondered why New York City has so many water-main breaks, broken parking meters and bus stops that feel like sets for “Waiting for Godot,” Jon Agee and Daniel Feigelson have thought of a plausible reason: B.O.T.C.H. That stands for the Bureau of Turmoil, Chaos and Headaches. It is also the title of a very clever jazz musical for which Mr. Agee wrote the book and lyrics and Mr. Feigelson composed the music. Tada! Youth Theater first performed the show in the early 1990s and is now reviving it; somehow it still seems timely. (Through Feb. 15.) Friday at 7 p.m.; Saturday and Sunday at 2 and 4 p.m.; Monday at noon and 2 p.m.; Tada! Theater, 15 West 28th Street, Manhattan , (212) 252-1619, Ext. 128, tadatheater.com; $20; $8 for 16 and under; $25 and $15 for premium seating.20100114

JOHN CARLIN & THE KIDS MUSIC UNDERGROUND (Sunday) Mr. Carlin has the right kind of experience to impress both children and rock-savvy parents. He spent almost a decade as an early-childhood music educator, and his old band toured with Radiohead and P J Harvey. He and his group are also billed as “the Sly and the Family Stone of kids’ music.” At noon, Joe’s Pub, at the Public Theater, 425 Lafayette Street, at Astor Place, East Village , (212) 967-7555, joespub.com; $15; $12 minimum at tables. (Table reservations: 212-539-8778.)20100114

‘CELEBRATE THE CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT’ (Sunday) In his “I Have a Dream” speech, the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. spoke of children of different races joining hands as sisters and brothers. In this program for ages 5 and older at the Children’s Galleries for Jewish Culture, young visitors will honor Dr. King’s birthday by making hand-shaped cushions. They can also create other crafts commemorating the civil rights struggle — pins and friendship bracelets — and read and illustrate the story of Rosa Parks. From 2 to 5 p.m., 515 West 20th Street, No. 4E, Chelsea, (212) 924-4500, Ext. 1, cgjc.org; $8; free for members.20100114

‘DEAR EDWINA’ (Friday through Monday) What if Ann Landers had been a child — and she could sing? Then she might have been Edwina Spoonapple, the young heroine of this charming musical by Zina Goldrich and Marcy Heisler (“Junie B. Jones”). Edwina is an advice columnist who delivers her counsel tunefully, and this show, being revived after a run that ended in April, details her quest to be in a festival. (Through Feb. 15.) On Fridays this month, the show’s authors will also present “Marcy & Zina Encores,” a free musical concert after the evening performance. Friday and Saturday at 7 p.m.; Saturday through Monday at 11 a.m. and 1 and 4 p.m.; DR2 Theater, 103 East 15th Street, Manhattan , (212) 239-6200, dearedwina.com; $39.20100114

‘THE DEVIL YOU KNOW’ (Friday through Sunday, and Wednesday and Thursday) Children may not know this particular devil, but their parents might: he’s based on the evil tempter in the Stephen Vincent Benét classic Faustian short story, “The Devil and Daniel Webster.” It’s been adapted countless times, and on this occasion the playwright and director Ping Chong has turned it into a puppet production by collaborating with the company Phantom Limb, known for its innovative marionette works. Incorporating a contemporary spin, the show is recommended for ages 10 and older. (Through Jan. 24.) At 7:30 p.m., with additional 2:30 p.m. shows on Saturday and Sunday; La MaMa Etc., 74A East Fourth Street, East Village , (212) 475-7710, lamama.org; $30.20100114

‘FOCUS’ (Friday, and Tuesday through Thursday) See the world of New York through a young artist’s lens. This exhibition, organized by Stephanie Pereira, features almost 40 photographs, as well as documentary videos and animations, by students from elementary through high school. Working with the nonprofit Magic Box Productions, which brings arts programs to schools, the young creators have dealt with both portraiture and the urban landscape. (Through mid-Feb. 2010.) From 8:30 a.m. to 5 p.m.; group tours available; the Gallery at 180 Maiden Lane, between Front and South Streets, Lower Manhattan , (914) 630-0256, magicboxproductions.org; free.20100114

‘4 WINGS, 2 LEGS, UKULELES AND A TALE’ (Saturday and Sunday) What child could resist a program with a name like that? This showcase of rock, dance and yes, ukuleles, is the season’s opening event in the Family Matters Series, a group of performances for young audiences at Dance Theater Workshop. Insects and tropical creatures are among the themes, and at least one of them will be real: a monkey that’s the pet of the artist who calls herself YaliniDream. At 2 p.m., Dance Theater Workshop, 219 West 19th Street, Chelsea , (212) 924-0077, dancetheaterworkshop.org; $15; free for children.20100114

‘THE FROG PRINCE’ (Saturday and Sunday) This isn’t the guy in the Disney film, but an onstage sufferer from Galli’s Fairytale Theater who would also like to give up the amphibian life for his true calling as royalty. Written and directed by Johannes Galli, the company founder, the show, for ages 3 and older, enlists the audience in helping the frog to break the spell. At 3 p.m., 38 West 38th Street, third floor, Manhattan , (212) 810-6485, gallitheaterny.com; $20; $15 for children.20100114

‘HOUSE ABOUT IT!’ (Friday through Thursday) This exhibition gives new meaning to playing house. Created by the Staten Island Children’s Museum, the show, whose centerpiece is a child-size house under construction, introduces young visitors to all aspects of the building trades. Activity stations include an architect’s office, a space to examine foundation materials and a workbench to practice sawing and drilling. The house offers opportunities for tiling and shingling, and a dollhouse lets young decorators ponder fabrics and furnishings. Hours: Friday, and Tuesday through Thursday, noon to 5 p.m.; Saturday through Monday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; 1000 Richmond Terrace, Livingston , (718) 273-2060, statenislandkids.org; free with admission: $6; free for members and children under 1; free for grandparents on Wednesdays. This Monday admission is free for everyone.20100114

THE JIMMIES (Saturday) Many children have probably heard Ashley Albert: she does voice-overs for Nickelodeon, cereal commercials and MTV, among other jobs. But what she really loves to do with her voice is sing, and in this concert she’ll be leading her group, the Jimmies, a power-pop band with high-jumping, rock-star antics and lyrics that are supposed to be clever enough to tickle adults. At 11 a.m., Leonard Nimoy Thalia, Symphony Space, 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street , (212) 864-5400, symphonyspace.org; tickets in advance: $18; $12 for children. Day of show: $19 and $13. For members: $12 and $10.20100114

MARTIN LUTHER KING’S BIRTHDAY EVENTS IN CENTRAL PARK (Saturday and Monday) Children can celebrate the diversity of New York City in two activities from the Central Park Conservancy, one that looks to the future and the other to the past. From noon to 3 p.m. on Saturday, young visitors can participate in a community art project that honors the city’s many cultures at Belvedere Castle, south of 81st Street. Then on Monday at 2 p.m., families can take a historical journey through a walking tour devoted to Seneca Village, the first known community of African-American property owners, established on land that is now within the park. Meeting at the southeast corner of 85th Street and Central Park West, (212) 772-0210, centralparknyc.org; free.20100114

‘LITTLE RED RIDING HOOD’/‘LA CAPERUCITA ROJA’ (Saturday and Sunday) The wolf suffers an identity crisis in this puppet musical version by Manuel Antonio Morán, presented in an easy-to- understand mix of Spanish and English by Teatro SEA, which specializes in Latino theater for children. It seems that Little Red can’t be persuaded that the wolf is really a wolf, and soon he begins to doubt himself too (with good reason: what wolf talks and sings?). (Through Jan. 23.) At 3 p.m., Los Kabayitos Puppet Theater, Clemente Soto Vélez Cultural Center, 107 Suffolk Street, near Rivington Street, Lower East Side , (212) 529-1545, sea-online.info; $15; $12.50 for 2 to 12; free for under 2.20100114

‘THE LITTLE MERMAID’ (Saturday and Sunday) Disney brought this Hans Christian Andersen tale to life with a large cast, but Manhattan Children’s Theater’s new production does it with only two actors. Here called Flotsam and Jetsam, they portray all the characters in the story of a mermaid who longs to join her human love. Adapted by Mike Kenny, the show unfolds against a set of underwater ruins. (Through Feb. 21.) At noon and 2 p.m., 52 White Street, near Church Street, TriBeCa , (212) 352-3101, mctny.org; theatermania.com; $20; $50 for priority tickets, which include front row seats, a signed program and a preshow cast meeting (must be bought by the preceding Wednesday).20100114

‘MAGIC AND ZONE: THE COMEDY MAGIC OF RICH MAROTTA AND TWILA ZONE’ (Wednesday) Some magic shows have all the deadly seriousness of Harry Potter navigating a really bad day at Hogwarts. But apparently not this one. Rich Marotta, a stand-up comedian, and his wife, Tamara Marotta, a k a the pink-haired and often bumbling Twila Zone, profess to combine laughter with illusions, creating a performance for ages 8 and older that offers magic and sends it up at the same time. At 8 p.m., Bleecker Street Theater, 45 Bleecker Street, at Lafayette Street, East Village , (212) 239-6200, magicandzone.com; $45; single tickets at $31.50 each, or family tickets at a rate of $25 each, or $100 for up to four tickets are available at the box office only.20100114

‘MAKE A DIFFERENCE’ (Saturday through Monday) Even America’s youngest citizens can help improve the world, and this weekend of activities at the Children’s Museum of Manhattan will honor six teenagers who already have, as well as a leader who gave his life in that cause: the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. The events on all three days include workshops for ages 5 and older to begin making a huge mosaic celebrating Dr. King’s legacy and, for preschoolers, a “Many Colors” collage activity. (The full schedule is at cmom.org.) On Monday only there will be performances by the Harlem Gospel Choir at 2 and 3 p.m. and an awards ceremony for the teenagers at 3 p.m. Tisch Building, 212 West 83rd Street , (212) 721-1223; free with admission: $10; $7 for 65+; free for under 1 and members.20100114

‘MUSICAL TALES AND ADVENTURES: PETER AND THE WOLF & THE VOYAGE IN THE NEW WORLD’ (Saturday) The Knickerbocker Chamber Orchestra is inviting young listeners on some intriguing journeys in this hourlong concert. And one of those trips will feature a famous guide: Neil Gaiman, best-selling author of children’s novels like “Coraline” and “The Graveyard Book,” will be the narrator of Prokofiev’s “Peter and the Wolf.” The program will also include pieces by Ives and Ellington; the tenor Jason Danieley singing in the world premiere of “... And Bold to Fall Withal (The Voyage in the New World),” a work about Henry Hudson’s travels by the orchestra’s founder and conductor, Gary S. Fagin; and a concluding family ballroom dance to Tchaikovsky. At 7 p.m., World Financial Center Winter Garden, West Street, south of Vesey Street, Lower Manhattan , (212) 945-0505, artsworldfinancialcenter.com; free.20100114

‘ONCE AND FOR ALL WE’RE GONNA TELL YOU WHO WE ARE SO SHUT UP AND LISTEN’ (Friday through Sunday) If you go to the Duke on 42nd Street and shut up and listen, you’ll find out just what this is: a play performed — and partly written by — a group of teenagers. Receiving its New York debut through the New Victory Theater as part of the Under the Radar Festival, this Belgian-British production for adolescent audiences may not be polite, but it is prizewinning: it earned three awards at the 2008 Edinburgh Festival Fringe. Friday at 7:30 p.m.; Saturday at 2 and 7:30 p.m.; Sunday at 3 p.m.; Duke on 42nd Street Theater, 229 West 42nd Street, Manhattan , (646) 223-3010, newvictory.org; sold out.20100114

‘PINKALICIOUS, THE MUSICAL’ (Saturday and Sunday) It’s time to think pink again. This show for ages 4 to 12, from Vital Children’s Theater, is in yet another revival, so if you missed it earlier you can catch it now at the Bleecker Street Theater. Elizabeth and Victoria Kann have adapted their children’s book, in which the pink-obsessed title character finds out that sometimes being in the pink can be too much of a good thing. (John Gregor wrote the score and some of the lyrics.) (Through February.) At 1 p.m., 45 Bleecker Street, at Lafayette Street, East Village , (212) 579-0528, pinkaliciousthemusical.com; $29.50; $25 for groups of 10 or more.20100114

PRESERVATION DETECTIVES’ FAMILY PROGRAM (Sunday) The Eldridge Street Synagogue, the landmark 1887 building where many Jewish immigrants worshiped, offers its “Preservation Detectives” tours every Sunday, when children 5 to 10 can view artifacts, investigate neighborhood lore and play roles of the past. This month the theme is “Treasure Trove,” and young participants will learn what Sabbath customs and recipes Eastern European immigrants brought with them, prepare braided challah and make their own mezuzot (decorative cases for prayers, which are affixed to doorposts). At 1 p.m., 12 Eldridge Street, between Canal and Division Streets, Lower East Side , (212) 219-0302, eldridgestreet.org; $15 a family.20100114

RYAN SANANGELO (Sunday) What problems can you solve with spaghetti? Hunger, of course, but Ryan SanAngelo comes up with some other intriguing ones in his picture book, “Spaghetti Eddie.” At this show, part of the Bowery Kids series, Mr. SanAngelo will read his book aloud (it’s for ages 4 to 8) and perform rock and rap numbers from his two CDs, “Play All Day” and “Awesome, Dude!!!” Doors open at 11:30 a.m.; show at noon; Bowery Poetry Club, 308 Bowery, near Bleecker Street, East Village, , (212) 614-0505, bowerypoetry.com; $10.20100114

‘SESAME STREET: A CELEBRATION OF 40 YEARS OF LIFE ON THE STREET’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) Sesame Street may not have an official location, but a good chunk of it is in Brooklyn for the next couple of months. This exhibition at the Brooklyn Public Library, commemorating the television show’s 40th anniversary, includes objects like animation cels, scripts, sheet music, props and even a few Muppets. (Through Feb. 21.) Hours: Friday, 9 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Saturday, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Sunday, 1 to 5 p.m.; Tuesday through Thursday, 9 a.m. to 9 p.m.; Central Library, Grand Army Plaza, at Flatbush Avenue and Eastern Parkway , (718) 230-2100, brooklynpubliclibrary.org; free.20100114

‘SNOOPY AS THE WORLD WAR I FLYING ACE’ (Friday through Thursday) Most of the military life commemorated at the Intrepid Sea, Air & Space Museum is real, but for the next few months the institution will honor some imaginary battles: those waged by the “Peanuts” character Snoopy as a flying ace inside his Sopwith Camel (a k a his doghouse). Organized by the Charles M. Schulz Museum and Research Center, this exhibition features digital prints of Schulz’s original drawings. The museum will also display “Peanuts”-inspired artwork created by sailors aboard the U.S.S. Intrepid (the aircraft carrier that is now the museum’s home), who used the images to personalize their work and living spaces. (Through April 30.) From 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., Pier 86, 46th Street and 12th Avenue, Clinton , (212) 245-0072, intrepidmuseum.org; free with admission: $22; $18 for 65+ and college students; $17 for ages 3 through 17 and veterans; free for active military, retired military and under 3.20100114

‘SNOW WHITE’ (Saturday and Sunday) How can a theatrical approach be both resolutely traditional and irreverently avant-garde? The border between those extremes is exactly where Company XIV dances — sometimes on point, sometimes with folk-infused boot stomps — in its fascinating new “Snow White.” As young purists might demand, this dance-theater adaptation includes a delicate heroine, a handsome prince and an evil queen. But as no one might expect, the staging also offers shadow puppetry, opera singers performing classical arias, and a taped score that ranges from Tchaikovsky to klezmer to Louis Armstrong. That it all works so well is to the credit of Austin McCormick, who founded the company and conceived, choreographed and directed the hourlong show, which is best for those over 5. At 3 p.m., 303 Bond Street, near Union Street, Carroll Gardens, Brooklyn , (212) 868-4444, companyxiv.com; sold out.20100114

TAP DANCE OPEN HOUSE (Saturday) You don’t need tap shoes or tap experience, just a willingness to try a brief class at this event from the American Tap Dance Foundation. Young hoofers from preschoolers through teenagers are invited to kick up their heels and sample the fun. From noon to 1 p.m., American Tap Dance Center, 154 Christopher Street, near Greenwich Street, No. 2B, West Village , (646) 230-9564, atdf.org; free.20100114

‘UNCLE PIRATE’LAUREL GRAEBER 57372 ‘UNCLE PIRATE’ (Saturday through Monday) Most fourth graders aren’t very interested in their adult relatives’ jobs. But how many could honestly say that they have an uncle who’s a pirate? That’s the situation in which young Wilson, the hero of this new musical by Ben H. Winters and Drew Fornarola, finds himself. (And yes, the guy is an old-fashioned, Jack Sparrow-style pirate.) Based on the book by Douglas Rees, the show is the latest children’s offering from Vital Theater Company. At 11 a.m. and 1 p.m., McGinn/Cazale Theater, 2162 Broadway, at 76th Street, fourth floor , (212) 579-0528, vitaltheatre.org; $25. LAUREL GRAEBER 20100114
