Citing Tough Race, Dodd Steps Aside

Brendan Hoffman for The New York Times

Senator Christopher J. Dodd in his office on Capitol Hill in September 2008.

By DAVID M. HALBFINGER
Published: January 6, 2010

EAST HADDAM, Conn. — Christopher J. Dodd has been in politics 36 years, including an ill-starred presidential run in 2008 and a record three decades as a United States senator from Connecticut. He can, then, count votes with anyone in Congress. But with his popularity at a nadir, he was not sure he could count enough votes in his home state to assure his election to a sixth term.

Mr. Dodd is also 65, and in the past year he has battled prostate cancer, helped bury an older sister (his closest sibling) and Edward M. Kennedy ( his closest friend in the Senate), and seen another former Senate colleague, Jon S. Corzine, go down to defeat after a nasty re-election fight for governor in New Jersey.

A fight just as tough would have been the only way for Mr. Dodd to survive a Republican challenge in November, his advisers say. And even if he could imagine scraping his way to victory, the senator told the advisers, he did not have the stomach for the fight.

To hear Mr. Dodd tell it on Wednesday, not one but all of those things together — a lifetime of accomplishment, a bruising year, a forbidding way forward — equipped him to make up his mind and bow out of any re-election effort. He did so, he said, early on a cold Christmas Eve, an hour after voting to overhaul the health care system, when he visited Senator Kennedy’s grave at Arlington National Cemetery.

“In the long sweep of American history, there are moments for each elected public servant to step aside and let someone else step up,” he said, standing outside his flag- and wreath-bedecked clapboard home, a 19th-century schoolhouse on Main Street in East Haddam, with his 8-year-old daughter, Grace, resting her chin on his elbow, and Christina, 4, in her mother’s arms. “This is my moment to step aside.”

Mr. Dodd’s aides and White House officials all strove to say that he was not pushed to this decision. They said the president, vice president and leading Democrats like Senator Robert Menendez of New Jersey, chairman of the party’s senatorial campaign committee, had done everything Mr. Dodd had asked of them; President Obama and Vice President Joseph R. Biden Jr. had even appeared at fund-raisers in Connecticut in recent months.

But they were all also mindful that Richard Blumenthal, the popular Connecticut attorney general who has eyed the Senate for years, might have a better shot of winning in November.

“None of us are irreplaceable,” Mr. Dodd said. “None of us are indispensable. Those who think otherwise are dangerous.”

His announcement, which, he noted, was made on the Christian Feast of the Epiphany, dominated conversations in Connecticut. Supporters expressed dismay at the loss of Mr. Dodd’s seniority, and said that his image problems might have been worse in his bachelor days and that he could have pulled out a victory if the economy recovered.

But at the New Colony diner in Bridgeport, Charles Cunningham, 70, an independent voter, greeted Mr. Dodd’s announcement with a word: “Good.”

He said he believed Mr. Dodd should have faced far more serious treatment for one of the issues that had dogged him for more than a year: the fact that he had received favorable personal loans from one of the giant mortgage firms implicated in the nation’s financial crisis. He said the Senate ethics committee’s decision not to seriously criticize or sanction Mr. Dodd was a mistake.

“He got a special deal; he’s a senator,” Mr. Cunningham said. ‘’He should be held to a higher standard than the ordinary person, but they held him to a lower standard.”

Mr. Dodd had all but owned Connecticut politics for much of his adult life, and he was reared to it. His father was Thomas J. Dodd, a three-term senator whose career ended ignominiously: After he was censured for converting campaign funds for his personal use, the Democratic Party dumped him in 1970, and he ran as an independent, clearing the way for Lowell Weicker, a Republican, to capture the seat.

The younger Dodd, who idolized his father, served in the Peace Corps in the Dominican Republic before law school and won election to Congress in the post-Watergate Democratic avalanche of 1974. Six years later, pursuing what many saw as an effort to redeem the family name, he campaigned for Senate, biking across western Connecticut and pressing the flesh in union halls and shopping centers, handily defeating James Buckley in the race to succeed the retiring Senator Abraham A. Ribicoff.

The gregarious and shrewd Mr. Dodd was in many ways a natural in the Senate, aides and colleagues said, whether dismantling a nominee before the Foreign Relations Committee in his first few months on the job, championing early childhood education, or pressing legislation on voting rights, health care or the environment.

He stayed at it long enough to accumulate a hefty list of accomplishments, including playing a central role in the federal rescue of the financial system in 2008 and the economic stimulus package early last year. And he stepped in as acting chairman of the Senate health committee when Mr. Kennedy battled brain cancer.

Roy Occhiogrosso, a political consultant who worked on Mr. Dodd’s 2004 campaign, said his signature may turn out to be writing the Family and Medical Leave Act. “If the measure of a politician is for how long their impact will be felt, arguably, in Dodd’s case, it’ll be forever,” he said.
