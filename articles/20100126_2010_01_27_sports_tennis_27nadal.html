A Cascade of Injuries Tests Nadals Strength

Joe Castro/European Pressphoto Agency

Rafael Nadal injured his right knee during his quarterfinal match against Andy Murray on Tuesday, and retired in the third set.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

By JOE DRAPE
Published: January 26, 2010

MELBOURNE, Australia — Last year on the same court, Rafael Nadal was on top of the tennis world, seemingly invincible. He was ranked No. 1 and had won his first Australian Open in a dazzling five-set, four-hour victory over Roger Federer, arguably the greatest player in the world. The match was so wrenching that the usually unflappable Federer broke down in tears. It was Nadal who graciously offered a consoling hug.

Late Tuesday at Rod Laver Arena, Nadal looked exceedingly human when he walked to the net and offered a hand to Andy Murray. He was retiring from the match. Nadal was down two sets, and was trailing by 3-0 in the third and being run ragged by the scrambling Scotsman. He had felt something go amiss in his right knee in the previous set.

Murray was stunned. He was in control of the match, but Nadal had played superb points, and Nadal was not playing as if he was hurt. The first hint that something was wrong came in the previous game when Nadal asked for a medical timeout and a trainer appeared to work on Nadal’s knee.

“It just all happened so sudden,” Murray said. “I was very surprised. I’ve seen Rafa play matches where he’s obviously been in a lot of pain and discomfort. Here last year, he won back-to-back five-set matches in the semis and finals.”

The end of the match may have come abruptly, but Nadal’s health and play have been in decline since last February when he injured the same knee in a three-set loss to Murray in Rotterdam, the Netherlands. He has been interrogated daily here about the condition of his knee, and his body language as well as the numbers show that Nadal knows that he is not the same player who has been ferocity-in-motion the last five years.

He said it out loud after retiring from his match with Murray. He was frustrated, and perhaps worried that the tendinitis in knee will never get better.

“If you see my matches 2005 or 2004, 2006, I run a lot compared to now, no?” he said. “So today I played against one of the best players of the world, and I think I wasn’t run more than him. So I played aggressive. I played having the control of the point, a lot of the time with the forehand and the backhand, too, returning aggressive. If you see my matches three years ago, that’s didn’t happen, no? Remember, we are playing on hard courts, and probably in this surface I have to play my best tennis.”

Nadal perhaps knew he was going the wrong way in May when he lost to Federer on clay, his favorite surface, in the Madrid Open final. It was just his second loss to Federer on clay and first on any surface since 2007. Later that month, he was bounced from the French Open, where he had been champion for the four previous years, by Robin Soderling in the fourth round. It ended Nadal’s record 31-match winning streak at Roland Garros.

In June, he announced he was skipping Wimbledon because he hurt.

“I played underneath myself for the last few months,” he said at a news conference at the All-England Club.

By July, he lost the No. 1 ranking to Federer, who won Wimbledon for his record 15th Grand Slam title. Soon after returning to competition in August, Nadal strained his abdomen, which bothered him at the United States Open. He needed an injury timeout during a third-round victory over Nicolas Almagro, and voiced his frustration after the match.

“I am a little bit tired to talk about injuries,” he said.

Still he made it to the semifinals before losing in straight sets to the eventual champion Juan Martín del Potro. Nadal’s slide has been noticed by other players as well. Entering the Australian Open, he had failed to beat a top 10 player in his last 11 matches.

“He hasn’t won a tournament for a long time,” Federer said. “Still, he’s not the type of guy that anyone in the top 10 wants to play against. He still has and will always have the mental strength and toughness and that physical game that he has, you know.”

But Federer suggested that Nadal’s roll might be coming to an end.

“The end of the season, it’s always harder for him to win tournaments because the surface speed gets faster and faster as the season goes on,” Federer said. “There’s a lot of competition at the moment.”

And what does Nadal think? Despite having to retire with a lame knee, he is encouraged and trying to remain upbeat about his prospects. He played well here in spots, and believes there is much to build on.

“I think my level was very high,” he said. “I had big chances to win both sets. And I think against probably the player who is playing better right now, I was very close. So I go out from this tournament very happy about my level because the perspective is much better right now than two or three months ago.”
