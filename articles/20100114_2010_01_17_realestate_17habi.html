Totally in His Element

Ozier Muhammad/The New York Times

AFICIONADO Donald Albrecht, a curator of architecture and design, in his prewar apartment. More Photos >

By CONSTANCE ROSENBLUM
Published: January 14, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

SOME New Yorkers long to live in spanking new apartment buildings outfitted with sleekly designed lobbies and basement health clubs. To many other New Yorkers, nothing is as desirable as a prewar apartment, with its artfully arranged rooms and walls so thick that some latter-day residents have trouble getting wi-fi service.

And it would be hard to find a more passionate admirer of this type of housing than Donald Albrecht, 58, the curator of architecture and design at the Museum of the City of New York, who also works as an independent curator.

“Prewar apartments had well-defined spaces,” he pointed out, “along with interstitial spaces off the main spaces, such as foyers and hallways, that gave the illusion of even more space. And when it came to one-bedroom apartments, there seemed to be the feeling that they had to have lots of amenities, like closets, fireplaces and large kitchens.”

Given his affection for such structures, Mr. Albrecht considers himself supremely blessed to live in Southgate, a set of five Art Deco buildings on East 52nd and 51st Streets designed by Emery Roth that is considered one of the city’s signature prewar apartment complexes.

Shortly after Southgate opened in 1930, the architect Philip Johnson and Alfred Barr Jr., the first director of the Museum of Modern Art, lived in 424 East 52nd Street, which is Mr. Albrecht’s building. The book “New York 1930” includes a photograph of Johnson’s apartment, which was designed, natch, by Mies van der Rohe.

Mr. Albrecht was so taken with this image that the other day, in a conversation about Southgate’s lustrous pedigree, he pulled out the doorstop-sized volume, opened it to the page with the photograph and accompanying floor plan, and shifted it this way and that to point out the resemblance between Johnson’s space and his own.

Mr. Albrecht, who bought this one-bedroom apartment five years ago for about $500,000 and spent nearly $50,000 in renovations, seems almost regretful that he wasn’t around during those years. Because of his deep affection for the period, he has filled the space with furnishings and decorative accents that vividly conjure the golden age of the Manhattan apartment house.

His aesthetic is evident the moment you walk through the front door. In the small foyer, you are greeted by what looks like trompe l’oeil, a closet that has been transformed into a Lilliputian library by means of a glass door. “It’s the oldest trick in the book,” Mr. Albrecht explained. “Taking a little bit of space and framing the opening to make the space seem bigger.”

Mounted on the wall to your left are a pair of red and gold modern Chinese doors, possibly from a cabinet, which were bought in Lambertville, N.J., at the antiques market where Mr. Albrecht found many of the pieces that give class and pizazz to his apartment. Off to the right, a galley kitchen leads into what was originally a dining nook.

Upon seeing this space, Louis Poupon, an architect friend who helped Mr. Albrecht redesign parts of the apartment, immediately announced, “This is your office.” And now it is. The dark wood of the built-in desk, topped with a slab of hunter green linoleum, matches the trim of the cabinet just to the left. The graceful arch that leads from the foyer to the living room is repeated by an archway here.

When you enter the living room, with its beamed ceiling, the first thing that catches your eye is a Venetian-style mirror, a silvery concoction that Mr. Albrecht describes as “one big Astaire-Rogers moment.”

“Neo-Baroque mirrors like this one — oversized, elegant mirrors with a humongous frame — used to be very popular,” he said. “Very Dorothy Draper,” he added, alluding to the high priestess of 20th-century interior design whose influence is conspicuous in these rooms and who was the subject of a show that Mr. Albrecht curated at the museum.

Nor is this by any means the only mirror here. Mr. Albrecht’s quarters are rich in reflecting surfaces, among them a round gray aluminum coffee table, that bring brightness to the apartment. Although the windows face south onto the Southgate courtyard, there’s not much sunlight. And sadly, at least in Mr. Albrecht’s opinion, the original casement windows were replaced by more environmentally friendly models.

“The casement windows weren’t energy efficient,” he said. “But they were very beautiful.”

Classics of modern design lurk everywhere. A Noguchi white paper lamp sits on a bed of silvery glass pebbles; another nestles in a corner. Harry Bertoia’s famous wire-mesh diamond chairs surround a simple metal dining room table by Muji. An off-white sofa from Las Venus, an East Village mecca for midcentury modern furniture, is covered with black and white fabric (Alexander Girard) whose colors are echoed by a zebra-print rug of the sort popular in the 1930s. Bright accents are provided by a trio of reddish-orange postwar chairs, pricked with the metal brads then favored by upholsterers.

The architectural element that resonates most deeply for Mr. Albrecht is the working fireplace, edged by red brick within a frame of white molding and topped by a print of a Roy Lichtenstein silkscreen of a pointing finger.

“The white molding and the red brick have a colonial-revival feeling that suggests the traditional hearth,” Mr. Albrecht said. “They give the room a great sense of warmth.”

But it’s back to midcentury modern in the bedroom, where a partial list of furnishings includes:

•

Steuben crystal rope-twist candlesticks, a modern-day version of a ’30s design. (Mr. Albrecht also curated a Steuben show for the museum.)

•

A wooden Scandinavian desk from the ’50s, courtesy of eBay.

•

Discarded chairs found on the street outside the American Museum of the Moving Image, where Mr. Albrecht used to work.

•

An Eames leg splint, a peculiar-looking plywood device created to help World War II soldiers that is a work of art in its own right.

Although the apartment’s décor feels relatively spare, Mr. Albrecht realizes that one detail could push it over the edge.

“You know, with that fireplace, you’re in danger of turning your apartment into a Norma Shearer movie set,” a friend once commented, alluding to the ’30s apartments that were often the setting for MGM pictures starring actresses like Shearer and Joan Crawford.

To which Mr. Albrecht replied, “And what’s wrong with that?”
