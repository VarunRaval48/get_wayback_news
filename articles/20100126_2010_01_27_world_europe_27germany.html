Germany to Add Troops to Afghan War

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By NICHOLAS KULISH
Published: January 26, 2010

BERLIN — Chancellor Angela Merkel of Germany promised Tuesday to send up to 850 more soldiers to Afghanistan, a compromise between the pleas of the United States and other allies for a larger commitment of forces and the political realities of a deeply unpopular war at home.

Mrs. Merkel announced at a news conference here on Tuesday that an additional 500 soldiers would be sent to Afghanistan, while 350 would be available for missions there as what she called a “flexible reserve force,” which could be assigned for events like elections.

According to the chancellor, German soldiers in Afghanistan will focus increasingly on training Afghan security forces, with the number of German trainers increasing to 1,400 from just 280. A rapid increase in the number of Afghans prepared to fight is a pillar of the counterinsurgency plan put forward by Gen. Stanley A. McChrystal, the top American and allied commander in Afghanistan.

Mrs. Merkel said the government would also raise the number of German police officers and nearly double the amount of development aid to Afghanistan, all toward the goal of supporting the Afghan government and security forces in preparation for an eventual withdrawal of foreign troops. Foreign Minister Guido Westerwelle said Tuesday that the government wanted to begin withdrawing its soldiers in 2011, but he stopped short of naming any firm deadlines.

Germany has about 4,300 soldiers in Afghanistan, mostly in the northern part of the country, the third largest contingent in the American-led NATO mission. The number of German military personnel in Afghanistan is strictly limited by parliamentary mandate and presently capped at 4,500. Analysts expect the proposed increase in troop strength, a middle-ground solution, to pass without trouble.

But the decision came after extensive and often acrimonious discussions between political parties and government ministries here. American officials had asked the German government for roughly 2,000 more troops to help fight the insurgency in Afghanistan, and hoped for at least 1,000, given the political constraints they knew the government was under.

“Merkel’s not one who would love to do more in Afghanistan,” said Henning Riecke, an expert on German security policy at the German Council on Foreign Relations. “She wants to be an ally, she doesn’t want to lose face, but she’s not the one pressing for more troops and more engagement.”

Allies fighting in the most violent provinces in the southern part of the country have criticized the German government for restricting all but a few of its soldiers to the safer northern part of the country and for keeping too many of its soldiers inside bases or in heavily protected convoys of armored vehicles, rather than mixing with the population.

Strains between the allies reached their peak in September after a German-ordered airstrike on two hijacked fuel tankers near Kunduz, Afghanistan, that killed at least 72 civilians, according to a United Nations report. The airstrike, and the political scandal that followed, led to a shake-up in the Defense Ministry and the dismissal of the army’s top officer.

Mrs. Merkel called the new plan outlined Tuesday a “defensive approach.” German aid for reconstruction and development, providing Afghans with new roads and clean drinking water, would nearly double in 2010 to $609 million from $311 million last year. Development aid is far more popular with the German public than military deployments, which opinion surveys here show are opposed by two-thirds of the population.

President Hamid Karzai of Afghanistan was scheduled to arrive in Berlin for talks with Mrs. Merkel on Tuesday night, before a conference in London this week on future strategy for Afghanistan.

The German government will also provide $71 million over five years to an international fund intended to encourage insurgents to stop fighting and reintegrate into Afghan society, a program supported by Mr. Karzai.
