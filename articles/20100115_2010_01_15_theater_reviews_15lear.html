THEATER REVIEW | 'LEAR'

MORE ON 'LEAR'

Blow, Winds! Deconstruct Thy Text!

Sara Krulwich/The New York Times

Okwui Okpokwasili as Goneril, April Matthis as Regan, and Amelia Workman as Cordelia, in an irreverent work inspired by “King Lear,” at Soho Rep.

By CHARLES ISHERWOOD
Published: January 15, 2010

Certainly the playwright Young Jean Lee does not lack for chutzpah. Last season this talented writer made a heady splash on the downtown scene with “The Shipment,” a play about black identity politics that was audacious — coming as it did from a Korean-American — but also smart and entertaining. Now Ms. Lee has written and directed a cheeky, freaky modernist gloss on “King Lear,” which many consider to be Shakespeare’s greatest play, if not the greatest play written in the English language, if not the greatest play bar none.

Pete Simpson, center, as Edmund in “Lear,” which focuses on the younger generation represented by Shakespeare.

“Nothing will come of nothing,” Shakespeare’s Lear darkly warns Cordelia in the opening scene. Ms. Lee alludes to Shakespeare’s frequent invocation of that negative word in her own play, titled “Lear,” which had its premiere Thursday night at Soho Rep. But it rang in my ears for other reasons as the curtain fell on this intermittently funny but mostly flailing attempt to excavate new meanings from the consideration of a celebrated text. From perhaps the most imposing something in the theatrical canon, Ms. Lee has constructed a big, fat nothing.

Can this have been quasi-intentional, a concerted effort to obliterate meaning by scrawling graffiti on one of the theater’s most profound texts? With this idiosyncratic playwright it’s hard to say. Certainly the nonsensical monologues and rambling, desultory dialogue that make up much of the play suggest that Ms. Lee intends to stage an assault on the conventional seductions of theater that Shakespeare has come to exemplify: gripping narrative, characters of psychological depth and complexity, language rich in lyric beauty expressing discernible truths.

Consider, if you will, this speech from Cordelia, which is fairly typical: “I am Cordelia and I am good and there are fine candy-spun things sweetening my dreams. I will cry into your ear and give you something to be sorry about. I will show how many pastries it takes to suffocate a baby calf. And your diaphragm will be safe from me, I will not remove it in soft handfuls, I will not extract your tongue with my teeth. Soon you will understand the sweetness of crying, of ears and sorriness and pastries and calves and diaphragms and tongues, each one as soothing as the last.”

As the Elizabethans would say, huh? And no, it makes no more sense in context.

Ms. Lee’s play is peopled only by the younger generation represented in Shakespeare, the king’s three daughters and the legitimate and illegitimate sons of his devoted friend Gloucester. It takes place just after the gruesomely violent scene in which Gloucester’s eyes have been put out and he has been thrown out to brave the elements blind. Lear too is at the mercy of the storm.

In the comfort of a glittery room in the palace (the terrific set, by David Evans Morris, is a gaudy bauble of pop baroque design), Goneril and Regan sit looking slouchy and bored. Edgar slumps on some stairs. The silence is finally broken by the spasms of guilt wracking Edmund (Pete Simpson). “I’m a bad person!” he spits out, an exclamation that starts things off amusingly, since few would argue that the conniving Edmund of the original was a moral paragon.

Nobody seems too engaged by this topic, so the conversation wanders in various directions. Edgar (the appealingly goofy Paul Lazar) suggests that Buddhism might be the answer. Edmund’s random confession that he thinks everybody looks fat gets an affirmative from Regan, played with peevish humor by April Matthis. “People’s bodies are just tragic,” she says languidly.

At times these confessedly selfish and self-involved folks seem to be working up material for a self-help book called “He’s Just Not That Into Thee.” Edgar, who scarcely resembles the passionately loyal son of the original, tells the women, “Men act devoted because they know you expect it, when actually they don’t think you’re that hot and we all get obsessed with other women all the time.”

The absurdity of this inane chitchat taking place as some of the most wrenching moments in theatrical history are occurring outside has a certain mordant humor. And the odd non sequiturs and quirky exchanges are often funny in themselves.

“I’m a lavish tipper,” says Goneril, played with imperious glamour by Okwui Okpokwasili, in a monologue in which she tries to explain her character and justify her bad behavior. When Cordelia (a poised, commanding Amelia Workman) wanders in, she exchanges friendly greetings with her sisters. “How was France?” Goneril asks. “Lovely, thank you,” Cordelia says. “And how is France your husband?” adds Regan.
