Go to Awards Season

CHARLOTTE FOREVER (Tuesday) This series of eight films starring Charlotte Gainsbourg, the gifted French actress who recently starred in Lars von Trier’s hot-button “Antichrist,” begins with Michel Gondry’s 2006 movie, “The Science of Sleep.” Ms. Gainsbourg is a young artist in Paris who is drawn into the dreams of a neighbor (Gael García Bernal), freshly arrived from Mexico. French Institute Alliance Française, Gould Hall, 55 East 59th Street, Manhattan , (212) 355-6100, fiaf.org; $10. (Dave Kehr)20100107

AN EVENING WITH JEFF BRIDGES (Saturday) This personable actor, long a critics’ favorite and this year a contender for an Oscar nomination (for his performance as a country singer in “Crazy Heart”), will appear for a conversation at 6:30 p.m., followed by a screening of his 1971 film “The Last Picture Show.” Walter Reade Theater, 165 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center , (212) 875-5600, filmlinc.org; $35. (Kehr)20100107

FRYDERYK CHOPIN AND THE CINEMA (Friday, Sunday and Tuesday) In observation of this composer’s bicentenary, the Film Society of Lincoln Center offers a selection of Chopin-related movies in monthly batches that continue through May. The January selection includes Aleksander Ford’s 1952 Polish feature, “The Youth of Chopin”; an assortment of performances recorded during the annual International Chopin Piano Competition; and James Lapine’s 1991 feature, “Impromptu,” in which Chopin (Hugh Grant) is pursued by a pesky young woman who writes under the name George Sand (Judy Davis). Walter Reade Theater, 165 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center , (212) 875-5600, filmlinc.org; $11; $27 for all three films. (Kehr)20100107

‘MISSION TO MOSCOW’ (Tuesday) During World War II several propaganda films were made in support of America’s wartime alliance with the Soviet Union, but few went as far as this Warner Brothers feature from 1943, directed by Michael Curtiz (who made at least one propaganda film for the Hungarian Communist Party back in the old country) and starring Walter Huston as Joseph E. Davies, the United States ambassador to Russia from 1936 to 1938. Based on Davies’s best-selling book, the film goes to astounding lengths to justify Stalin’s party purges and show trials. The Soviet prime minister Vyacheslav Molotov, for example, is played by the grandfatherly character actor Gene Lockhart. During the House Un-American Activities Committee hearings after the war, the film became a hot potato for all concerned, though only the screenwriter, Howard Koch, was actually blacklisted. The 7 p.m. screening will be followed by a discussion with the historian Ed Huls and the film critics Lou Lumenick, Farran Smith Nehme and Glenn Kenny. BAM Rose Cinemas, 30 Lafayette Avenue, at Ashland Place, Fort Greene, Brooklyn , (718) 636-4100, bam.org; $12. (Kehr)20100107

MOVIES

Ratings and running times are in parentheses; foreign films have English subtitles. Full reviews of all current releases, movie trailers, showtimes and tickets: nytimes.com/movies.

‘ALVIN AND THE CHIPMUNKS: THE SQUEAKQUEL’ (PG, 1:28) The singing male chipmunks return, and this time three singing female chipmunks are on hand as well. Chipmunk love is in the air as the two trios compete in a rock contest to try to win money to keep a school music program alive. The kiddies may love it — they certainly turned the 2007 original into box office gold — but grown-ups won’t find their childhood memories here; mostly they’ll be annoyed. (Neil Genzlinger)20100107

‘ANTICHRIST’ (No rating, 1:49) Women: evil or just misunderstood? Leave it to Lars von Trier to ask the tough question, and to provide answers that are both incoherent and appalling. (A. O. Scott)20100107

‘ARMORED’ (PG-13, 1:28) A blunt little B movie about a heist gone wrong. Not brilliant, but not bad either. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘AVATAR’ (PG-13, 2:46) With his latest, James Cameron has turned one man’s dream of the movies into a trippy joy ride about the end of life — our moviegoing life included — as we know it. A song to the natural world that was largely produced with software, an Emersonian exploration of the invisible world of the spirit filled with Cameronian rock ’em, sock ’em, the movie is glorious and goofy and blissfully deranged. (IMAX is optional, but try to see it in 3-D.) (Manohla Dargis)20100107

‘THE BLIND SIDE’ (PG-13, 2:00) Sandra Bullock does good, and then does good some more in this true story of a rich white Memphis family that adopts a homeless young African-American football prodigy. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘BROKEN EMBRACES’ (R, 2:08, in Spanish) Exuberant melancholy from Pedro Almodóvar. A blind writer (Lluís Homar) is pulled back into his past, when he was a filmmaker in an ill-fated love affair with an actress played by Penélope Cruz. (Scott)20100107

‘BROTHERS’ (R, 1:50) In Jim Sheridan’s remake of Susanne Bier’s 2004 film, Tommy (Jake Gyllenhaal) is back from prison just in time to see his brother, Sam (Tobey Maguire), go off to war, leaving behind a wife (Natalie Portman) and two young daughters. An earnest melodrama ensues, at times affecting but never entirely convincing. (Scott)20100107

‘THE CHASER’ (No rating, 2:04, in Korean) In Na Hong-jin’s relatively straightforward (by Korean standards) thriller, a former cop turned pimp notices that prostitutes are going missing — which costs him money — and sets out to find the cause. Tense, violent and, like most serious Korean movies, an indictment of institutional incompetence and violence, “The Chaser” is an exciting ride that lasts about 20 minutes and several plot twists too long. (Mike Hale)20100107

‘COCO BEFORE CHANEL’ (PG-13, 1:10, in French) Less a biopic than a study in the manners and morals of a bygone era, Anne Fontaine’s film follows the complicated love life and budding ambition of Gabrielle Chanel (Audrey Tautou) on her path from early poverty and heartbreak through independence toward eventual fame. Ms. Tautou, wary and astringent, is excellent, as is Benoît Poelvoorde, who plays Coco’s aristocratic lover, Étienne Balsan. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘CRAZY HEART’ (R, 1:51) An old country song — whiskey, women, second chances — about an old country singer, played with sly, exquisite craft by Jeff Bridges. (Scott)20100107

’DID YOU HEAR ABOUT THE MORGANS?’ There are barely enough titter-worthy one-liners in Marc Lawrence’s good-natured romantic comedy about high-achieving New Yorkers transplanted to Wyoming to prevent it from sinking under the weight of its clichés. (Stephen Holden)20100107

‘AN EDUCATION’ (PG-13, 1:40) Jenny, a London schoolgirl in the early 1960s — played with verve and brass by Carey Mulligan — learns the ways of the world at the hands of an older gentleman (Peter Sarsgaard), who turns out to be sleazier and less worldly than he seems at first. Adapted by Nick Hornby from a memoir by Lynn Bradley and directed by Lone Scherfig, this film is a bit too charmed by its own period atmosphere to take account of the uglier implications of the story it tells. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘FANTASTIC MR. FOX’ (PG, 1:28) A children’s adventure story or a Wes Anderson movie. Both, of course, and Mr. Anderson, using a painstakingly old-fashioned stop-motion animation technique, turns Roald Dahl’s slender fable into his most satisfying film since “Rushmore.” (Scott)20100107

★ ‘A FILM WITH ME IN IT’ (No rating, 1:29) The Irish narrative tradition of the bleakly hilarious is honored with gusto in this slender, supple comedy laced with agreeable poison. A struggling Dublin actor (Mark Doherty) and his buddy (Dylan Moran), an aspiring director, tweak a script when not attending to the corpses piling around them from a nasty succession of fatal accidents. The grim wit blends nicely with the offhand disdain for show-biz pretensions. (Andy Webster)20100107

★ ‘THE HURT LOCKER’ (R, 2:10) In this tense, insightful film about a United States Army bomb-disposal squad in Iraq, Kathryn Bigelow has made a crackerjack action picture that is also a drama of character, psychology and ideas. Jeremy Renner, as a soldier who approaches danger with the conviction of an artist and the compulsiveness of an addict, is simply amazing, and the rest of the cast, notably Brian Geraghty and Anthony Mackie, is nearly as strong. (Scott)20100107

‘THE IMAGINARIUM OF DOCTOR PARNASSUS’ (PG-13, 2:02) The most recent circus to pop out of Terry Gilliam’s head is a full three-ring affair, complete with puffs of smoke, glitter and grunge, some hocus-pocus, mumbo jumbo and Heath Ledger’s final screen performance. (Dargis)20100107

★ ’INVICTUS’ (PG-13, 2:14) Clint Eastwood’s film, with Morgan Freeman as the newly elected South African president Nelson Mandela and Matt Damon as Francois Pienaar, the captain of the country’s rugby team, is a stirring story of athletic triumph and racial reconciliation. It is also a subtle and intricate study in political leadership. (Scott)20100107

‘IT’S COMPLICATED’ (R, 1:59) Meryl Streep stars in a pleasurable, daffy if at times daft September-September romance from Nancy Meyers about a successful restaurateur who starts an adulterous affair with her ex-husband (Alec Baldwin). (Dargis)20100107

‘LOSS OF A TEARDROP DIAMOND’ (PG-13, 1:42) With strained, quasi-poetic language that fitfully tries to soar, this Southern period piece, which exhumes an obscure Tennessee Williams screenplay written in 1957, is a significant, though less than monumental, feat of reclamation. (Holden)20100107

‘THE LOVELY BONES’ (PG-13, 2:19) Adapting Alice Sebold’s best-selling sentimental Gothic about the afterlife of a murdered girl, Peter Jackson and his collaborators have created a brightly colored fantasia that is visually arresting but emotionally incoherent. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘ME AND ORSON WELLES’ (PG-13, 1:53) A charming and modest backstage tale set in 1937, when Orson Welles (Christian MacKay) was preparing a revolutionary modern-dress production of “Julius Caesar” at the Mercury Theater on Broadway. Zac Efron is the teenager who falls in with Welles’s company, and in love with Sonja (Claire Danes), the backstage manager. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘THE MESSENGER’ (R, 1:45) Ben Foster and Woody Harrelson are Army officers with the grim task of informing the families of soldiers that their loved ones have died overseas. Samantha Morton plays a widow who takes the news with calm sorrow. The terrific performances complement Oren Moverman’s sensitive, understated direction and the rich, observant script he wrote with Alessandro Camon. (Scott)20100107

‘MY SON, MY SON WHAT HAVE YE DONE’ (R, 1:31) Werner Herzog directs Michael Shannon in a true-to-life, if truer-to-Herzog, story about a Southern California man who one morning did in his mother. Chloë Sevigny, Willem Dafoe and Grace Zabriskie add some extra tang. (Dargis)20100107

‘NINE’ (PG-13, 1:52) Nein! (Scott)20100107

‘NINJA ASSASSIN’ (R, 1:36) World governments are hiring ninjas to do their dirtiest deeds, and only a rogue ninja (played by Rain, the South Korean former boy-band member) and an Interpol-style cop (Naomie Harris) can expose them. Perhaps in homage to his rippling star, the director, James McTeigue, drenches the film in water; but not even some lovely flashbacks — first love, first wounds, first downpour — can redeem a movie more committed to bloodletting than to any of its characters. (Jeannette Catsoulis)20100107

★ ‘POLICE, ADJECTIVE’ (No rating, 1:53, in Romanian) The second feature by the Romanian director Corneliu Porumboiu (“12:08 East of Bucharest”) is, at first glance, a modest, dourly funny procedural about the ethical predicament of a young detective. But its linear structure and uninflected style reveal a rich character study, full of insight into the workings of a country still shaking off the ingrained habits of a rigid and bureaucratic dictatorship. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘PRECIOUS: BASED ON THE NOVEL “PUSH” BY SAPPHIRE’ (R, 1:49) This potent blend of naturalism and melodrama, directed by Lee Daniels, unites a cast that includes the pop stars Mariah Carey and Lenny Kravitz, the comedian Mo’Nique and a fearless newcomer named Gabourey Sidibe in the story of an abused Harlem teenager struggling for dignity and survival. Ms. Sidibe, in the title role, is the key to the film, and her steady, moving performance is a revelation. (Scott)20100107

‘THE PRINCESS AND THE FROG’ (G, 1:35) It’s not easy being green, as the heroine of this polished animated fairy tale — the first from Disney with a black heroine — discovers on kissing a frog. But to judge from how this period movie deals with race, it is a lot more difficult to be black. (Dargis)20100107

‘THE ROAD’ (R, 1:59) How does the world end? Not with a bang or a whimper, but with polite applause for this noble, earnest adaptation of Cormac McCarthy’s novel, and something stronger for Viggo Mortensen’s performance as a father trying to help his young son survive a postapocalyptic nightmare. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘A SERIOUS MAN’ (R, 1:45) Old Testament mishigas from Joel and Ethan Coen, who retell the Book of Job as a suburban shaggy-dog story about a harmless physics professor (Michael Stuhlbarg) whose life comes unraveled. The usual Coen brothers’ whimsy, with its edge of nihilism, takes on a surprising metaphysical resonance. You don’t have to be Jewish to like this movie, but it will hurt more if you are. (Scott)20100107

‘SHERLOCK HOLMES’ (PG-13, 2:05) Reimagining the great Victorian detective as a swaggering action hero is not a crime, and Robert Downey Jr. (with some help from Jude Law as his sidekick, Watson) makes it fun for a while. But a dull story and Guy Ritchie’s preening, hyper-active, half-wit sense of style kill the fun long before the crimes are solved. (Scott)20100107

‘A SINGLE MAN’ (No rating, 1:39) Colin Firth, with a magnificent depth of feeling, plays the grieving, closeted gay professor of the film’s title in Tom Ford’s ambitious and touching adaptation of the 1964 landmark novel by Christopher Isherwood. (Dargis)20100107

★ ‘SITA SINGS THE BLUES’ (No rating, 1:22) This one-woman animation by Nina Paley is part Sanskrit epic (the Ramayana, in a cheeky modern retelling) and part break-up memoir. It’s all pretty wonderful, thanks for Ms. Paley’s visual resourcefulness and the vintage jazz recordings of Annette Harshaw. (Scott)20100107

★ ‘A TOWN CALLED PANIC’ The Belgian animators Stéphane Aubier and Vincent Patar, expanding their cult-favorite television show into a feature film, have produced an epic yet cozy adventure set in an idealized European countryside and an alternate underwater universe. Their cast of plastic toy figurines — led by the bickering housemates House, Cowboy and Indian — have more personality than most flesh-and-blood actors. (Hale)20100107

‘2012’ (PG-13, 2:38) Roland Emmerich serves up Old Testament-style destruction with a smile. (Dargis)20100107

‘THE TWILIGHT SAGA: NEW MOON’ (PG-13, 2:10) Ready, set, squeal! Chris Weitz directs, Kristen Stewart mopes, Robert Pattison sparkles and Taylor Lautner growls. (Dargis)20100107

★ ‘UP IN THE AIR’ (R, 1:47) A laugh-infused stealth tragedy from Jason Reitman about a corporate assassin (George Clooney) whose life is upended both by a no-nonsense colleague (Anna Kendrick) and a seductively high-flying female executive (Vera Farmiga). (Dargis)20100107

★ ‘WHERE THE WILD THINGS ARE’ (PG, 1:40) Spike Jonze directs this alternately perfect and imperfect if always beautiful live-action adaptation of the Maurice Sendak children’s book about a solitary boy, Max (Max Records), liberated by his imagination. The film has been pitched to a distinctly older audience than the picture book’s original readers, and some younger children might be scared by the snuffling, growling wild things (voiced by James Gandolfini, among others). (Dargis)20100107

‘THE WHITE RIBBON’ (R, 2:25, in German) Germany before World War I: child abuse, class resentment and other seeds from which Nazism will sprout, presented in gorgeous black and white and with dour self-importance by Michael Haneke, the most be-laureled scold in modern European cinema. (Scott)20100107

‘THE YOUNG VICTORIA’ (PG, 1:44) As the young Queen Victoria, Emily Blunt flounces, frowns and finally swoons for Rupert Friend’s Prince Albert in a frivolously entertaining portrait directed by Jean-Marc Vallée and written by Julian Fellowes. (Dargis)20100107
