Talk to The Times: Micheline Maynard, Aviation Correspondent

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
Published: January 11, 2010

Micheline Maynard, the senior business correspondent for The New York Times who covers the airline industry and aviation, is answering questions from readers Jan. 11-15, 2010. Questions may be e-mailed to askthetimes@nytimes.com.

Skip to next paragraph

For the latest questions and answers, click here.

Ms. Maynard, who is based in Ann Arbor, Mich., joined The Times staff in 2004 as a business reporter covering airlines, and she returned to the aviation beat as senior correspondent in 2008 after three years as chief of the paper's Detroit bureau. She also contributes regularly to the Culture and Dining sections of the paper, and is the author of The Prius Diary series on The Times's environmental business blog, Green, Inc., relating her own conversion from an S.U.V. to a hybrid-electric car. Her work has appeared in The Times since 2000.

Ms. Maynard also has written for Fortune magazine, and she has been a staff writer or bureau chief at a number of news organizations including USA Today, Newsday, U.S. News & World Report, and the Reuters News Service. She began her career as a legislative correspondent for United Press International in Lansing, Mich., and served as an intern in the White House Press Office.

She was named a media fellow by the Japan Society of New York in 2002, and also was a Knight-Wallace Fellow at the University of Michigan in 1999-2000. And in 1989-1990, she was chosen as a Knight-Bagehot Fellow in Business and Economics Journalism at Columbia University.

She is the author of four books, including "The End of Detroit: How the Big Three Lost Their Grip on the American Car Market," published in 2003, and most recently "The Selling of the American Economy: How Foreign Companies Are Remaking the American Dream," which was published in October.

Can You Fly?

Q. To report on aviation, it would seem useful to be a pilot. Are you a pilot?

If not, how do you sort out what makes sense in early reports of an aviation accident?

— Dave Montgomery

A. I am not a pilot, but I come from an aviation family. My dad flew with the 57th Bomb Wing during World War II and spent 25 years with American Airlines. I still have some memories of what it was like to fly before airline deregulation in 1978.

Along with a lifelong interest in flying, I've taken the class offered by the Boeing Company called Airplanes 101 that teaches industry people and journalists the fundamentals of flight. I also know many people who have pilot's licenses and who are more than willing to share their expertise.

People in the industry have learned that when an airplane accident happens, the first reports can often be wrong. There's a balance between reporting based on eyewitness accounts and waiting for the experts to come to conclusions. In those early hours, we try to make sure we don't jump to conclusions, even though it is tempting to speculate.

Frequent Flier Programs

Q. The big question after the film "Up in the Air" is this: What kind of frequent flier status do you have? What are the best mileage programs?

A. I had gold status on Northwest Airlines before it merged with Delta Air Lines, and I'm a basic frequent-flier member on other airlines. I'd defer to my colleague Michelle Higgins, who writes the Practical Traveler column, for information on frequent flier programs.

When the Dreamliner Arrives

Q. How will the aviation industry change as the Boeing 787 Dreamliner enters service? What will the effect be on Airbus?

— Sal DiGiovanni, Charlotte, N.C.

A. The Boeing 787 could be a game changer in one respect: efficiency. It is designed to be more fuel efficient, quieter and more comfortable for passengers than current jets. One airline chief executive told me it is the airplane equivalent of the Toyota Prius.

As with the Prius, it will take a while for the Dreamliner's impact to be felt, and of course, Boeing has to prove it can build them in a steady fashion. But in 10 years, its effect should be apparent.

Airbus got an unexpected break from the many delays in the 787 program. But 787 customers by and large are sticking with the plane rather than cancel orders or switch to Airbus.

No Regrets

Q. Seems to me that every time the topic of decreased airline travel comes up in The Times, the points made are that business travel is off because of the recession, while discretionary travel is off because people don't like the security lines and hassles, and don't like having to pay extra for a blanket, checked bag, and so on.

But my main reason for never wanting to fly again is the frequent delays and the lack of apology for them. It seems that the airlines are so demoralized by the general situation that they've forgotten how to say "We're sorry."

Is this, in your opinion, the result of cluelessness, or is there a liability issue here?

— Patricia R. Sweeney, Boise, Idaho

A. If airlines apologized for every delay, they'd be apologizing all the time, especially at the airports on the East Coast. They're not clueless, nor is it a liability issue, but there are many things that cause airport delays that are beyond an airline's control. Bad weather and air traffic congestion are the most frequent causes.

There are things you can do as a passenger, however, to assure yourself a smoother trip. Flying early in the day, before the skies become congested, seems to help. It's also possible on many airline Web sites to check the on-time records of individual flights, which allows you to choose flights that tend to perform better.

And airlines do apologize, as JetBlue did numerous times when its planes were stuck in ice over Valentine's Day weekend in 2007.

How to Complain

Q. It has become all too common to read about the negative ordeals that passengers experience while on airplanes. With this in mind, what are your thoughts about airline passengers being proactive? I know that the Department of Transportation — through its Aviation Consumer Protection Division — accepts consumer complaints in regard to airline service, but how effective is this service, and do you recommend it? How else can an airline customer lodge a complaint and have his or her issue successfully resolved?

—

David Tulanian, Los Angeles

A. I hear constantly from airline passengers who are upset with their travel experiences. (For the record, The Times cannot intervene on your behalf, but I do pass along any complaints that I receive.)

If you feel you have a legitimate complaint, I'd recommend pursuing every avenue, and I'd start first with the airline. Every carrier has a process for dealing with complaints, and the details can be found on their Web sites.

The Transportation Department's Aviation Consumer Protection Division is another place to turn. The department publishes a monthly report on complaints in six areas — flight delays, mishandled bags, passengers who are bumped from flights, other consumer complaints, reports to the Transportation Security Administration, and airline reports on death, loss or injury to animals.

You can find a lot of advice on the Web about complaining to airlines. Airsafe.com provides some tips on what to include in your complaint, whether you deliver it by e-mail, postal mail or a telephone call to a hotline.

Before you complain, though, it is important to know which kinds of things you can successfully complain about and which will be a waste of time. The old joke about the weather is unfortunately true: Everybody complains about it, but the airlines don't have to do anything for you if you are delayed because of weather problems. Thousands of people found that out during the recent winter storms.

Safety as a Selling Point

Q. I lost a graduate-school classmate in the crash near Buffalo, N.Y., several months back in 2009. The terrible death suffered by Alison des Forges turned my research attention to the subject of airplane and airline safety.

I want to know how to make safety the major selling point for ticket buyers, as it became for purchasers of automobiles several decades back. Given the Obama administration's embrace of corporate welfare thus far, I cannot imagine the federal government's regulatory apparatus doing anything whatsoever to promote safety for passengers, pilots, and flight crew in the foreseeable future. Consumer pressure, therefore, seems the only option. I welcome any analysis and insight you will offer.

— Geraldine McTigue

A. I'm so sorry for the loss of your classmate. But I am not sure airlines can be pressed to make safety a selling point, nor do they need to. Despite tragic accidents like the one in Buffalo, airplanes are an extraordinarily safe mode of transportation, at least as measured in fatalities.

No passengers died aboard scheduled commercial airlines in the United States in 2007 or 2008, a period in which the airlines carried 1.8 billion people. Sadly, the crash in Buffalo broke that string.

Here is another way to think of it: Every day, there are more than 25,000 airline flights in the United States. Virtually all of them will arrive at their destinations without any incident at all, let alone a crash.

By contrast, on Monday night, I drove home in a snowstorm from the North American International Auto Show. I watched three cars spin out on the highway in front of me, and my local all-news radio station said there were accidents on every major highway in the area.

Airline safety is still regulated by the government, and commercial airlines are all held to the same safety standards. It's one of the expectations that passengers can have in the United States.

Does It Have to Be an Ordeal?

Q. Do you get any sense that the airlines take seriously how unpleasant it is to fly these days? I have switched travel plans in some instances to driving rather than put up with the abuse I get when flying coach. It's the only business I know of where you can spend several hundred dollars and be treated like a nuisance. Is anyone at the legacy airlines aware of this, or would they rather just see coach passengers stay home?

— Name Witheld

Q. Will flying ever be enjoyable again?

— Mark Kachel

A. It depends on your definition of "enjoyable." We all have a vision of days not long ago when we could dash to the airport with minutes to spare, jump on a plane, have a friendly flight attendant immediately materialize to take our wraps and bags and provide drinks, and settle into a comfortable seat with plenty of legroom. (I'm leaving out airline food.)

At least part of that vision is no longer possible, because of airport security concerns. These days, instead of cutting it close, it's better to set aside as much time as you can spare, because you won't know what to expect when you get to the airport.

It's certainly possible to obtain the rest of the comforts on the list, but they generally come at a price — either the cost of a business or first-class ticket, an upgrade, or plenty of frequent-flier miles. And let's remember that back before deregulation, only about 5 percent of the American public flew regularly, because plane tickets were so expensive. For many families, retirees and people on a budget, "enjoyable" means a price they can afford, and the creature comforts matter less to them than simply being able to travel.

However, you can travel at a reasonable price and get an endurable, if not enjoyable, experience. Fans of JetBlue and Southwest would certainly speak up for their airlines. Especially early in the day, I've been on planes that were uncrowded, and where the travel process went as smoothly as ever. It can be done.

Are the Airlines Like the Automakers?

Q. Can you talk a bit about the similarities/differences between the automotive and aviation industry, particularly in light of Alan Mulallys success at Ford after coming from Boeing?

— Virginia Hudson

A. Great question. You might be interested in this video interview I did with Mr. Mulally a few years back.

Autos and airlines are both mature industries whose success depends on customer satisfaction. In the past, they depended heavily on hourly labor, only to see jobs depleted by manufacturing technology. They are both industries with long development lead times, and they are both regulated by the government. The auto and airline industries have each seen the impact of new entrants, the auto companies over the past 50 years and the airlines since deregulation in 1978.

They are industries that captured the imagination of generations of Americans. They tend to attract true believers, who could not imagine themselves working at anything else. And they are each a part of American lore, dating back roughly to the same point in history (Henry Ford founded the Ford Motor Company in 1903, the year of the Wright Brothers' first flight).

Mr. Mulally brought Ford a fresh outlook, much as he took a fresh look at airplanes while at the Boeing company. As an engineer, he is able to speak to Ford's product development teams in a way that few chief executives there have been able to do. He was already familiar with the Toyota Production System from his years at Boeing, which gave him a good grounding in automotive production, even though it is done on a much broader scale than airplane manufacturing. The car business is so complex that it is difficult for outsiders to understand it, but Mr. Mulally's technical expertise has stood him in good stead.

Checking Fees and Lost Luggage

Q. Will airlines that charge for checked bags issue a refund for bags that are lost or seriously delayed?

— Christopher Kittle

A. Passengers have specific rights in the case of lost luggage, which go well beyond getting a refund on a bag fee. Bags are expected to reach passengers within 24 hours of their arriving flight, and are considered "lost" when they have been missing for 21 days or more. Between 24 hours and 21 days, passengers can seek compensation, and the Transportation Department has told airlines they must be prepared to compensate each traveler for up to $3,300 in expenses. More information on baggage can be found here.

There is some useful information here on the paperwork and other steps to take if your bags are lost or delayed.

My advice to travelers has always been to keep a list of at least the major items in your suitcase, a trick I learned from airline employees — and if there are specific brand names (like St. John, Calvin Klein, Brioni), write those down too. Some people snap a photo of the contents of their suitcase, and that can't hurt — although in the rush to pack, I'm not sure most folks would have that much time. Note the brand of your suitcase, too, because if it can't be found, you're going to want to claim for the case as well as the contents.

Hub and Spokes vs. Point-to-Point

Q. Could you comment on how you think airlines will be structured in 10 or 15 years? Most of the big airlines seem to use the hub-and-spoke system, but the wonderful regional jets seem like they might increase the number of direct flights between cities.

I am also curious about how the huge Airbus A-380 might change the industry.

Finally, what causes the major airlines to have such serious financial problems? Is it new entrants skimming passengers off the more profitable routes or legacy costs or having t service so many destinations or something else?

— John Byrd, Durango, Colo.

A. I don't think there's any question that passengers prefer the point-to-point system. I'm one of them. I still marvel at the idea of getting on a plane in Detroit, sitting for 13.5 hours, and getting off in Tokyo, considering that it took my maternal grandparents three weeks to sail from Northern Europe to Montreal. I actually think we will have more point-to-point flying in our future, for two reasons. One is the growth among carriers like Southwest, JetBlue and AirTran, which emphasize point-to-point flying over the hub-and-spoke system. Another, as you mentioned, is the use of modern regional jets, which make point-to-point flying possible for network carriers, too.

However, and it's a big however, the network system will not go away any time soon. There is too much time, money and organization invested in the hub-and-spoke infrastructure. The big airlines simply require their networks in order to funnel passengers on to major cross-country and international flights, and the economics of flying to small cities simply do not allow as much point-to-point flying as passengers would prefer.

Industry executives often voice frustration at the state of the airlines right now. They have just spent a decade cutting jobs, trimming costs, retiring airplanes and automating as much of the travel process as they can, and are still losing money. The Web is partly to blame: Travel sites allow passengers to keep a close eye on fares and have only heightened their price awareness. Airlines probably also spent far too much of the last decade engaged in fare wares, only to find themselves unable to charge fares that cover what it really costs to carry passengers.

As I know from covering the auto industry, eventually this all comes to a head. Though it's hard to kill an airline, anyone who worked for TWA, Eastern or Pan Am can tell you that carriers do disappear.

Why Not Abolish Carry-Ons?

Q. I was wonder what your thoughts might be on the notion of dropping all carry-on luggage except for purses as a means of speeding up T.S.A. screening. I realize it's a major change, but wouldn't the trade-off in speed and efficiency of security screening be worth it?

— Bob Beat

A. Over the Christmas holidays, Air Canada put severe restrictions on what passengers could bring on board, limiting them to a small purse, which they defined as something that would hold a wallet and a cell phone and not much else. (My friend Rachel Sklar of Mediaite.com wondered, "Have they ever met a woman?") And after a plot was discovered to blow up planes over the Atlantic, British Airways briefly banned all carry-on baggage.

I don't think a complete ban on carry-on bags would work for most travelers, and airlines certainly don't want to see regulations that might deter business travelers, many of whom go nowhere without their laptops. But as fliers know, travelers routinely stretch the definition of "carry-on." Some of the suitcases I've seen hoisted into overhead compartments would carry me through two weeks in Europe. I think it's reasonable for officials to enforce guidelines on the size or weight of carry-on bags, and it certainly would speed things up at security.

Nonrefundable Fares

Q. I recently cancelled a $109 nonrefundable flight on Delta. Since the change fee is higher than that, that money is basically donated to Delta. Do any alrlines at least allow you carry a credit from nonrefundable cancelled flights to be used instead for baggage fees or seat upgrades?

— Tom Mutchler

A. This is why it's important to read the fine print. When an airline says a flight is non-refundable, it's non-refundable. Delta states its policy clearly on its Web site.

Essentially, you cannot apply what you have paid for an airfare to anything but another airfare. Baggage fees and seat upgrades are in addition to the base airfare. Frustrating, I know, but that's why non-refundable tickets are usually the cheapest ones that an airline offers.
