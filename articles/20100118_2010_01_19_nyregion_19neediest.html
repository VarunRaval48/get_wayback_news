Call From Haiti Heartens Struggling Father

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JENNIFER MASCIA
Published: January 18, 2010

It was just before 3 p.m. on Saturday when the call came from his daughter, telling them she was alive.

“I was at church, but my wife was here. She picked up the phone,” said Jean Lafleur, 53, who immigrated to Brooklyn from Port-au-Prince, Haiti, in 2004.

His older daughter, Johanna, 27, an accountant in Pétionville, a middle-class suburb of Port-au-Prince, had not been heard from since the earthquake on Jan. 12.

“My wife was very happy,” Mr. Lafleur said, his eyes shining. “The kids were very happy, too. They kept asking, ‘Where’s Johanna?’ ”

“The kids” are Johanna Lafleur’s five younger siblings, who live with their parents in Canarsie, Brooklyn: Jean Marc, 24, Frantz, 21, Pierre, 20, Marie, 14, and Jean Jr., 8.

Mr. Lafleur said he wished he could see Johanna. “I’ve been waiting for so long,” he said. “It’s been six years.”

In Haiti, Mr. Lafleur was a radio technician and a reporter at Radio National Haiti, which until February 2004 was controlled by Jean-Bertrand Aristide, the country’s president. After a violent rebellion forced Mr. Aristide from power, Mr. Lafleur said he felt intimidated by forces aligned with the new president.

“They see you as a person who could not even walk in the street because they don’t like who was working with Aristide,” he said. During a 2004 visit to New York, he petitioned the United States for refugee status. His request was granted, and his wife, Virginia, and his five younger children were able to join him here in 2007. Johanna was one year past the age limit to be considered under her parents’ status.

In New York, Mr. Lafleur supported his family by working in restaurants and handing out fliers in the street, and in 2008 he got a job at Kennedy International Airport cleaning planes.

A year ago, he fell on ice near the Delta Terminal, injuring his right knee and two disks in his back. He could not move his neck for a month, and he hobbled around on crutches after a stay in Jamaica Hospital Medical Center.

He attends physical therapy twice a week and is not yet able to work, nor has he been able to complete the year he has left at the Globe Institute of Technology, where he was studying health care management.

“I’m still not 100 percent of myself,” he said.

Until two months ago, Mr. Lafleur received $200 every two weeks in worker’s compensation. His only income right now is the $697 a month he receives in Social Security disability payments for Jean Marc, who is severely mentally retarded and autistic, and $800 a month in food stamps. The family of seven somehow fits into two bedrooms, and until recently, Jean Marc was sleeping on a mattress on the floor in his parents’ bedroom.

Mr. Lafleur opened up about his financial hardships to a priest at his church, who referred him to Francine Melamed, the director of home and community-based services at Catholic Charities Brooklyn and Queens, one of the seven beneficiary agencies of The New York Times Neediest Cases Fund. After a home visit in November, she was able to get a bed for Jean Marc with $475 from the fund. She is also looking to enroll him in a day program that will teach him social skills and provide vocational training.

Ms. Melamed also plans to request Neediest Cases money for January’s rent, which is $1,400, and $400 in rental arrears from December. Mr. Lafleur also owes the Globe Institute $4,000 in tuition, and he still wants to complete his degree.

On Saturday, however, financial hardship took a back seat to the joy he felt upon learning his daughter was alive. But the news wasn’t all good: Virginia Lafleur lost a cousin in the earthquake, and Johanna Lafleur’s house crumbled to the ground. She is staying with a cousin, who is also hosting 10 other people.

“Now it’s very important for me to get a job,” he added. “She lost everything.”
