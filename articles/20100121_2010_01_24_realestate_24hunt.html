The Hunt

Attracted to Walkable New York

Hiroko Masuike for The New York Times

John Southern and Polly Crongeyer love New York and its walkability.

By JOYCE COHEN
Published: January 21, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

IT was Manhattan’s street life that appealed most to John Southern and Polly Crongeyer, residents of Pensacola, Fla. Last year the couple, who are both retired, “were thinking about redesigning our lifestyle a little bit,” Mr. Southern said.

For them, that meant buying a pied-à-terre in Manhattan, where “you can’t walk a single block without seeing something that is different,” Mr. Southern said. “If you are in Pensacola or any other typical city in the U.S., you are in a car.”

Mr. Southern, originally from College Station, Tex., is a polymer engineer and a former research and development director at Monsanto. Ms. Crongeyer, from Naugatuck, Conn., and a former stockbroker, is now a photographer. (Her Web site is pollycrongeyer.com.) He was widowed and she was divorced. The two, whose sons were playmates decades ago, married in 1996.

Their house was destroyed when Hurricane Ivan tore through the Florida panhandle in 2004. During the rebuilding, the couple, now both in their early 60s, occasionally escaped to a friend’s place on the Upper East Side. “So we got to know New York in a way that was not just ‘come in and go out over the weekend,’ and we really fell in love with the intellectual aspects” of the city, Mr. Southern said.

Last summer, after a referral through friends, Mr. Southern contacted Hy and Myrna Rosen, a husband-wife team of agents at Bellmarc Realty. With their guidance, he scoured hundreds of listings at Streeteasy.com, looking for a one-bedroom co-op for $400,000 to $600,000.

“John is a wonderful researcher, whether he is researching polymers or real estate,” Ms. Crongeyer said. “He is very data driven. He was good at creating a grid of what we wanted.”

One week last summer, Ms. Crongeyer took an intensive photography workshop downtown, while Mr. Southern went on the apartment hunt with the Rosens.

The learning curve was steep. He was surprised to find so many places had a view only of an adjacent building. Mr. Southern estimated that 25 to 30 percent of the listings had only an en-suite bathroom — one entered via the bedroom and therefore unsuitable for guests.

He was also fascinated to find three kinds of air-conditioning: window, through-the-wall and central. He was familiar with only the third, which in New York seemed to exist only in new buildings. His objection to window air-conditioners concerned noise and aesthetics.

Downtown seemed too youth-oriented, so they concentrated on the Upper East and West Sides. Typically, the Rosens would map an itinerary and drive Mr. Southern around, with one waiting in the car while the other accompanied him upstairs. The group winnowed their choices to about a dozen. At the end of her photography course, Ms. Crongeyer joined in.

At the red-brick Toulaine, on West 67th Street, the apartment they liked, newly renovated and flooded with light, was a pricey $595,000, with a monthly maintenance of almost $1,350. They thought they could get better value on the East Side. (That one later sold for $575,000.)

So they settled on a high-floor apartment at Connaught Tower on East 54th Street, listed at $465,000. Maintenance was $932. The building even had a rooftop pool. The apartment was “a perfectly acceptable place from the point of view of meeting the criteria we set up,” Mr. Southern said. Their offer of $442,500 was accepted.

Mrs. Rosen then sensed something: “Polly isn’t happy,” she said.

“There was no excitement,” Mrs. Rosen added. “This is a very important thing, buying an apartment, and there was no spark.” Mr. Southern, being an analytical and quantitative sort, didn’t mind a cookie-cutter space. But his wife realized she did. “The apartment had an impersonal feel to it,” Ms. Crongeyer said.

The Rosens suggested that, before committing, they see a handful of places that had just fallen into their price range.

One was at the Cherokee on far East 77th Street. The complex of four buildings, declared a landmark, was originally for tuberculosis patients and has outdoor balconied staircases of cast iron. The renovated apartment, for $499,000 with a maintenance fee of $1,150, was on the ground floor, but there was a fence as well as a moat, so passers-by couldn’t get too close.

“That was an appealing and intriguing apartment,” Ms. Crongeyer said, “but it was much farther east.” They realized how important walkability was to them.

It wasn’t hard to decide on a corner apartment at 1065 Park Avenue near 87th Street. The place was “filled with light, even on a gray afternoon,” Ms. Crongeyer said. The asking price was $499,000, down from $699,000. They were O.K. with the through-the-wall air conditioners.

And its location was prime: near Central Park, Museum Mile and the 92nd Street Y. “We have never seen anything like that place in terms of what it has to offer to the geriatric set to get in shape,” Mr. Southern said.

The couple offered $479,000 but settled at the asking price of $499,000. Maintenance was just over $1,200. The board interview was scheduled for a September day when the couple were vacationing in Paris, a city they like for being nearly as walkable as New York. Instead of postponing the interview or flying back, they wondered whether they could interview via Skype, which they often use with friends and family.

“We should be using 21st-century technology if we can,” Ms. Crongeyer said.

The board agreed. Mr. Rosen arranged for a dry run to make sure everything worked.

It did, and the interview went without a hitch. “We were pleased that the board was open to that possibility,” Ms. Crongeyer said. “Their comment was they were rather excited about trying to do it this way.”

The apartment is still largely unfurnished. The kitchen is to be renovated, with the help of Mr. Southern’s son, John Southern III, a Los Angeles architect.

The couple eventually plan to spend half their time in New York. Their busy schedule already includes lectures, cooking lessons, nights at the opera, meals with friends, and walking wherever they can.

“Basically,” Mr. Southern said, “you can walk the downtown part of most American towns, the interesting part, in an hour or two. But in New York you can just walk and walk and walk. You are part of the ambience instead of isolated inside an automobile.”
