Mina Bern, Versatile Yiddish Actress, Dies at 98

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By JOSEPH BERGER
Published: January 12, 2010

Mina Bern, a plucky and versatile actress and singer who was one of the last links to the scrappy world of Yiddish theater in New York, died Sunday in Manhattan. She was 98 and lived on the Lower East Side.

She died of heart failure, said her friend Eleanor Reissa, a Yiddish entertainer who directed Ms. Bern in four shows.

The fabled world of the Yiddish theater on lower Second Avenue, where stars like Jacob Adler, Paul Muni and Molly Picon offered laughter and melodrama to factory workers and tenement dwellers in at least 14 theaters, was in steep decline when the Polish-born Ms. Bern arrived in New York in 1949. She and Ben Bonus, who was to be her second husband, helped keep it breathing a good while longer.

“Yiddish stopped being the lingua franca of the immigrant community, so the Yiddish theater held on with just a few people, and she and her husband were among those people in the 1950s, 1960s and 1970s,” said Zalmen Mlotek, artistic director of the National Yiddish Theater — Folksbiene, which, at 95, says it is the world’s oldest continuously producing Yiddish company. Others who pitched in were Menashe Skolnick, Joseph Buloff, Seymour Rexite, Miriam Kressyn, Zypora Spaisman, Fyvush Finkel and Shifra Lerer, the last two still alive.

Ms. Bern was known not as much for her dramatic powers as for her cabaret singing and comedic flair. She would play a coquette in one sketch, a small-minded busybody in another and, in a third, an old mother whose children will let her live with them for only a short time. Nahma Sandrow, author of “Vagabond Stars: A World History of Yiddish Theater” (Harper & Row, 1977), said Ms. Bern “was adorable in sketches.”

“She had these lively blue eyes and used to act very innocent, even when she was playing someone who was irritating,” Ms. Sandrow said.

Ms. Bern and Mr. Bonus, entrepreneurs as well as performers, toured the United States, Canada and Latin America with an assortment of Yiddish revues. They sometimes designed the scenery, sewed the costumes and sold the tickets themselves. Audiences who saw them remember duets that have entered the Yiddish repertory, like the tart lament “Vos Dergeystu Mir Di Yorn?” (literally “Why Do You Ruin My Years?”).

They brought shows like “Let’s Sing Yiddish” (1966) and “Light, Lively and Yiddish” (1970) to Broadway, losing tens of thousands of dollars they didn’t have as assimilation, education and worldly success gnawed away at their hoped-for audience. For at least two seasons in the 1960s, they even operated a theater on Second Avenue.

In an interview with The New York Times after Mr. Bonus’s death in 1984, Ms. Bern said they went to places “where people didn’t hear Yiddish so much” so that the culture would not be forgotten. “He had a sort of mission inside him,” she said of her husband.

But friends said that that was true of her as well, for she entertained well into her 90s. In 2002 she played the woman who tends the mikvah, the ritual bath, in a production of “Yentl,” the play based on Isaac Bashevis Singer’s tale. In 2005 she performed a one-woman show about her life. She also took roles in Hollywood movies calling for older immigrant women, among them “Crossing Delancey” (1988), “Avalon” (1990) and “I’m Not Rappaport” (1996).

Ms. Bern was born Mina Bernholtz on May 5, 1911, into a Yiddish-speaking home in Bielsk Podlaski, in what is now Poland. (Today’s Yiddish actors are as likely to have learned their Yiddish in college classes as at home.) A distant relative was a Yiddish theatrical figure, and as a young woman she joined Ararat, a popular producer of kleynkunst (“little art”), the sophisticated variety shows that included satirical sketches and monologues.

When the Nazis attacked Poland in September 1939, she and her daughter fled east, and she entertained audiences in the Soviet Union. At some point — there are conflicting accounts — she spent time entertaining Polish refugees in Uganda and then ended up in British-mandate Palestine, performing Yiddish revues in a land where people were emphasizing Hebrew as the new language of an emboldened Jewish people. In 1949, she came to New York with the revue “Shalom, Tel Aviv.”

She is survived by her daughter, Renya Pearlman of Israel, from her first marriage. She also leaves two granddaughters and four great-grandchildren.

In 1999 Ms. Bern was awarded an Obie for “sustained excellence.” She had recently opened in Ms. Reissa’s Yiddish musical “Sweet Dreams,” produced by the Folksbiene.

“She could be feeling terrible, but the minute she hit the stage she was superlatively alive,” Ms. Reissa said. “She always made sure she had a good part and always tried to improve her lines, and it was all for the best.”
