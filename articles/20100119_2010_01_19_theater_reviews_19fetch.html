THEATER REVIEW | 'FETCH CLAY, MAKE MAN'

The Slugger and the Punching Bag

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By CHARLES ISHERWOOD
Published: January 19, 2010

PRINCETON, N.J. — The playwright Will Power places an unlikely pair of contenders in the theatrical ring in the new play “Fetch Clay, Make Man,” which is having its premiere at the McCarter Theater Center here. In one corner we find Muhammad Ali, the world-renowned prize fighter and semiprofessional poet born Cassius Clay. Opposite him is Stepin Fetchit, the actor who made it big in Hollywood by performing a cruel comic stereotype of servility and laziness.

Although the play opens with Ali promising to pummel his opponent for colluding in the public degradation of his race, the threat is soon revealed to be a joke. These seemingly mismatched symbols of black pride and black shame are not adversaries but allies, and Mr. Power’s play was inspired not by fancy but by fact. Ali and Stepin Fetchit were known to be friends during the 1960s, when Ali was entering his prime, and Fetchit’s movie career was all but over. A photograph of the two at a news conference, with a caption describing Fetchit as Ali’s “secret strategist,” threw Mr. Power for a loop and set his imagination simmering.

The idea of discovering the sympathetic connection between men whose images have become fodder for such diverse cultural commentary provides the play with a vital spark of interest. And Mr. Power, the talented playwright and performer whose musical restyling of a Greek tragedy, “The Seven,” was acclaimed Off Broadway several seasons back, creates vivid dramatic facsimiles of these public figures. It’s great to see Ben Vereen, well cast as Fetchit, and Evan Parke, equally terrific as Ali, bring the two men to life with matching authority.

But the play, which has been sleekly directed by the Broadway veteran Des McAnuff, never finds a compelling dramatic arc. It bobs and weaves, moving between themes and plot points without digging deeply into any of them, or into the central relationship between the men. Mr. Power seems hesitant to make the leap of fully imagining the complicated psyches behind the public images, so their friendship ultimately seems as mysterious at the play’s conclusion as it does at the beginning.

“Fetch Clay” mostly unfolds in 1965, as Ali is preparing for a much-publicized rematch with Sonny Liston in Lewiston, Me. Seeking new sources of inspiration, he sends for a bewildered Fetchit and implores him to reveal the secrets of the great black boxing champion Jack Johnson, Fetchit’s friend many years before.

Although Fetchit professes ignorance of the mysterious “anchor punch” that Ali believes was the secret to Johnson’s success, Ali allows this down-at-the-heels actor to settle in with his entourage. This includes his right-hand man Brother Rashid (the strong John Earl Jelks), a militant member of the Nation of Islam, of which Ali had become a controversial public symbol, and his wife, Sonji (a compelling Sonequa Martin), who met Ali before his conversion to Islam and is beginning to bridle at the constrictions of the life.

Flashbacks to scenes from Fetchit’s early days in Hollywood reveal the gaudy perquisites of his movie success — he was the first black actor to become a millionaire — and the private (and of course public) humiliations that were the price he paid. In 1929 Fetchit is being wooed by William Fox (Richard Masur), the head of Fox Studios, who lavishly praises his talent but also casually refers to him as “son.”

Later Fox rebuffs Fetchit’s request to play more complex roles, and goes even further by suggesting that he subsume his private self in his film persona. Pointing with dismay to an article in The New York Times in which Fetchit speaks eloquently about his love for classical music, he suggests this might be bad for business. Fetchit reluctantly agrees, but insists on a hefty salary hike, a hired car and various other symbols of his importance to the studio; fully aware of the ignoble nature of the roles he’s forced to play, he can only assert his authority — and assuage his wounded pride — by insisting on ostentatious displays of his commercial success.

Mr. Vereen gets the chance to illustrate the stark divide between the savvy, dignified private Fetchit, whose original name was the imposing Lincoln Theodore Monroe Andrew Perry, and the racist caricature he portrayed on film. (He died in 1985.) A seething resentment can be sensed underneath the wary cool with which Fetchit negotiates with Fox. In brief snippets from the routines that made Fetchit famous (and infamous), Mr. Vereen also reveals the glowing pride of an entertainer whose gifts sometimes managed to transcend the offensive uses to which they were put.

Mr. Parke’s Ali has his own image problems. He’s keenly aware that his public dedication to Islam has made him a controversial figure, and a defeat at the hands of Liston could throw a wrench in the myth-making machinery he has been carefully stoking. Mr. Power supplies Mr. Parke with some terrific bits of rhymed braggadocio. But the man behind the bluster remains a little blurry and insubstantial, despite Mr. Parke’s bright charisma.

The various subplots Mr. Power weaves together — Ali’s discovery of his wife’s checkered past, the rumors that Ali will be the target of violence in the wake of the mystery surrounding Malcolm X’s murder — add surface conflict to the story without supplying more layers to the character.

The back-and-forth between Ali and Fetchit occasionally makes for lively humor, particularly when Ali briefly contemplates making a movie of his life, at the urging of Fetchit, who is angling for his own Hollywood comeback. Ali is not enthusiastic about the idea of Fetchit’s friend John Ford taking the helm.

“What would my fans say if they go to the movies and see me, the greatest of all times, gettin’ beat up by some cowboy,” he grouses. “I mean I wouldn’t mind if I lost to the Mummy or something like that ... If I gotta get knocked out, I’d rather lose to a supernatural brother rather than a raggedy old cowboy.”

But the rapport between them never takes on any strong emotional coloring. The set, a stylized representation of a boxing ring designed by Riccardo Hernandez, is a blazing white that suggests the dominant culture that each had to spar with every day. And yet only briefly, late in the play, does Fetchit make the point that his hard-won and much-derided status as a national figure may have helped pave the way for the entry of more progressive images of black Americans in the cultural atmosphere.

“I snuck in the back door, so you could walk in the front,” he says bitterly.

It’s a potent idea that Ali sweeps aside with a typical burst of self-aggrandizing blather. A great fighter needs to be an expert at dodging blows, of course, but for “Fetch Clay” to be a truly compelling exploration of its intriguing subject, many more dramatic punches need to land.

FETCH CLAY, MAKE MAN

By Will Power; directed by Des McAnuff; sets by Riccardo Hernandez; costumes by Paul Tazewell; lighting by Howell Binkley; projection design by Peter Nigrini; sound by Darron L West; music by Justin Ellington; movement consultant, Lisa Shriver; production stage manager, Alison Cote; producing director, Mara Isaacs; director of production, David York. Presented by the McCarter Theater Center, Emily Mann, artistic director; Timothy J. Shields, managing director. At the Berlind Theater at the center, 91 University Place, Princeton, N.J.; (609) 258-2787). Through Feb. 14. Running time: 2 hours 30 minutes.

WITH: John Earl Jelks (Brother Rashid), Sonequa Martin (Sonji Clay), Richard Masur (William Fox), Evan Parke (Muhammad Ali) and Ben Vereen (Stepin Fetchit).
