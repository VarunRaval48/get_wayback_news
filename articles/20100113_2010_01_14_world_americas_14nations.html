U.N. Workers Struggle as Co-Workers Are Unaccounted For

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By NEIL MacFARQUHAR
Published: January 13, 2010

UNITED NATIONS — Throughout the day, both at the United Nations headquarters and in Haiti itself, staff members struggled on Wednesday to concentrate on organizing a immense relief effort and not to focus on their friends and colleagues still buried under the rubble.

The collapse of the organization’s headquarters in Haiti during the earthquake on Tuesday appears to have produced a death toll larger than any previously endured by United Nations employees in a single day.

“It is a very difficult moment for all of us,” said Susana Malcorra, the under secretary general who organizes logistics for peacekeeping forces around the world. She said it was particularly tense “for people on the ground who are in shock, and who have their colleagues buried there and feel themselves that they have not done enough to take them out.”

Working around the clock did nothing to diminish those feelings, she added.

In front of the Secretariat, the blue United Nations flag with its white map of the world was lowered to half-staff. Throughout the day, the death toll mounted with terrible slowness, emphasizing that most of the at least 140 missing employees were believed to be still buried.

“It is clearly one of the most tragic days for United Nations peacekeeping,” said Alain Le Roy, the under secretary general of peacekeeping operations.

By the end of the day, the United Nations confirmed that 16 of the 9,000 peacekeeping troops and police officers in the country had died in the earthquake: 11 Brazilians, 3 Jordanians, 1 Argentine and 1 Chadian. Seven of the Brazilians died when a building collapsed on top of their checkpoint, Mr. Le Roy said, but otherwise the details of what happened to the organization’s employees remained sketchy. Seven severely injured people had been airlifted out, with 56 wounded counted so far.

The bulk of the missing, as many as 100 people, were thought to be stuck in the collapsed wreckage of the Christopher Hotel, the United Nations headquarters in what had been a calm enclave in the hills far above the streets of Port-au-Prince, the capital. There were no sensors in Haiti to detect movement or heat in the mounds of broken masonry. Earth-moving equipment was also in short supply, and teams were moving with trepidation lest they do more harm than good, Ms. Malcorra said.

Another 40 or so United Nations employees were missing at a sprawling compound that housed the alphabet soup of United Nations agencies, including its development program, its AIDS organization and its organization for women. Ten more employees had been in a villa nearby.

As the earthquake struck, Mr. Hedi Annabi, the Tunisian chief of the mission, and Luiz Carlos da Costa, his deputy, were holding a meeting on one of the top floors of the five-story Christopher Hotel with eight members of a police delegation from China. The building, erected in the 1960s, had been reinforced, Ms. Malcorra said. But 10 people who were pulled alive from the rubble of the building had all been on the lower floors, Mr. Le Roy said.

There were reports throughout the day that Mr. Annabi had died, but United Nations officials said they were unable to confirm it, that there was still a chance that people would be pulled alive from the wreckage once rescue teams had landed.

“It is clear that they might be dead,” Mr. LeRoy said. The personnel records are all in the collapsed building, making accountability even harder.

Mr. Annabi and Mr. da Costa were among the earliest men ever to join peacekeeping when operations first started in the 1990s, and they mentored scores of younger staff members around the world. We are all incredibly shaken and broken up, but what are we going to do, stop working? said one staff member, who did not want to be quoted by name.

Many people fought for posts in Haiti. It was an easy, direct hop from La Guardia Airport in New York, and life was not so bad in the Caribbean. But few had their families with them, so people from headquarters were calling the loved ones of those who were unaccounted for on Wednesday to break the news. It is not an easy conversation, said Ms. Malcorra.

The United Nations lobby holds a memorial to people killed in the line of duty. The biggest ones recently were from terrorist bombings — 22 dead including the leader of mission in Baghdad in 2003, 17 United Nations staff members among more than 40 victims in a bombing of two buildings in Algiers in 2007. Everyone expects a higher toll in Haiti.

Perhaps the hardest part, Ms. Malcorra said, was trying to tamp down the desire to concentrate all possible resources simply on rescuing United Nations personnel. We also need to balance the priorities, she said, to balance between the desire to just rush to the aid of the United Nations staffers while so many Haitians also need help.
