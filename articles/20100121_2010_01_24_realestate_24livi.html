A Static View in a Changing Neighborhood

Tina Fineberg for The New York Times

SEEING GREEN The six piers just below the Brooklyn Heights Promenade are headed for a new life as Brooklyn Bridge Park. In fact, the opening of the newly natural Pier 1, off to the right, is imminent. More Photos >

By JAKE MOONEY
Published: January 21, 2010

THE view from the Brooklyn Heights Promenade, a five-block walkway that has overlooked New York Harbor from its perch at neighborhood’s edge since 1950, is almost the same as it was 60 years ago. The Lower Manhattan skyline rises up, seeming close enough to touch, against a backdrop of clouds; tugboats push barges to and fro; cars and trucks race underneath on the cantilevered Brooklyn-Queens Expressway.

In the opposite direction, the vista includes the back gardens and balconies of the lucky few whose homes let them look out at the scene full time.

But for these residents, and for visitors, one aspect of the view from the promenade is very different: the six adjacent piers along the Brooklyn waterfront, cleared of warehouses, are gradually being converted into Brooklyn Bridge Park.

In place of forklifts and pallets are piles of paving stones and rows of trees waiting to be planted. The first section of the park, on Pier 1 next to Fulton Ferry Landing, is scheduled to open soon, with another, on Pier 6 at Atlantic Avenue, to follow in the spring.

Progress on the $350 million park has been halting, and controversial among some neighbors. Still, it is a vast and dramatic new amenity for Brooklyn Heights, where change usually comes slowly and residents value the quiet elegance of the area’s historic blocks.

“You come across the Brooklyn Bridge and suddenly to your right there’s this green lawn,” said Lisa Detwiler, a senior associate at the Corcoran Group and a longtime resident. “For all of us who live in Brooklyn Heights, it’s really exciting because we’ve all been hearing about it for the last 10 years.”

Before moving to a prewar co-op building at the corner of Hicks and Montague Streets in August, Ian Primrose and his partner, David Ly, lived on Bleecker Street in Greenwich Village, where brunch options were close but sleep could be difficult.

“We got our fair share of late-night marital breakups and people going out to parties,” Mr. Primrose said of the ambience outside their window. In the Heights, though they are on a low floor, there are no such disturbances. He said the final sale price for the two-bedroom unit was $755,000, which means that even with maintenance fees, they are spending less per month than they were on their one-bedroom rental in the Village, which cost $3,550.

There are trade-offs: the unit has no outdoor space, as the couple had hoped for, though the promenade is a short walk away. They left behind favorite restaurants like the Spotted Pig and Mary’s Fish Camp, though Brooklyn Heights has some pleasant alternatives, and a drink is a few dollars cheaper. Also, the commute to their jobs in Midtown is longer, if only by about 10 minutes.

They are getting to know neighbors, many of whom moved to the area 30 or 40 years ago, drawn by many of the same things that attract residents now.

“We feel like we know a lot more people in the neighborhood in six months than in the old place in three and a half years,” Mr. Primrose said.

WHAT YOU’LL FIND

This grand-piano-shaped neighborhood is roughly 12 blocks deep, and about five blocks wide at its widest point. It is protected by the Brooklyn Heights Historic District, which was created in 1965 — the first in the city. There are also 50-foot height caps limiting development. Unlike predominantly brownstone neighborhoods like Park Slope and Fort Greene, though, Brooklyn Heights has a mixed housing stock, with smaller wood-framed dwellings and former carriage houses dotting its brownstone blocks.

“The housing quality is beautiful,” said Judy Stanton, the executive director of the Brooklyn Heights Association, which is celebrating its 100th anniversary this year. “Every 19th-century architectural style is represented here.”

“It’s very close-knit,” she added. “It feels sort of like it’s a neighborhood that’s looked after.”

The Heights includes a few smaller sections. A corner at the southern end, by one underpass on Joralemon Street that will lead to Brooklyn Bridge Park, is called Willowtown and has its own neighborhood association. At the northern end, the “fruit streets” — Orange, Pineapple and Cranberry — form another enclave, with a small restaurant row where they cross Henry Street.

The main commercial district is on Montague Street, which stretches from the courthouses of Downtown Brooklyn westward to the promenade. Heights residents share custody of the strip with downtown office workers, who flock to its bank branches, chain stores and low-priced restaurants. There is also a supermarket, and the duo of Starbucks and Connecticut Muffin, which form a hub of the social scene.

Still, Robert Perris, the district manager of Community Board 2, which represents this area of about 20,000 people, says that because of high rents, many storefronts have gone to chain retailers that serve office workers, rather than to neighborhood stores.

A major presence in the area is the Watchtower Bible and Tract Society, also known as the Jehovah’s Witnesses, which has had its world headquarters in Brooklyn Heights for a century. The society’s main offices are at the neighborhood’s north end, but it owns residences large and small, including the Hotel Bossert on Montague.
