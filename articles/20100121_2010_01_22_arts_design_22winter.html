Art Review | Winter Antiques Show

Just the Place to Find an Egyptian Sarcophagus

David Goldman for The New York Times

The Winter Antiques Show at Park Avenue Armory includes a six-foot urn by Paul Manship. More Photos >

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By KEN JOHNSON
Published: January 21, 2010

If you are in the market for a 14,000-pound, six-foot-tall urn carved from a block of pink Tennessee marble and encircled by a neo-Classical frieze of Indians hunting buffalo and engaging in intertribal warfare, hurry over to the 56th annual Winter Antiques Show before someone else snaps it up. Designed by the sculptor Paul Manship in 1914, this stunning object is on view at Gerald Peters, one of 75 art and antiques dealers gathered to dazzling effect under the capacious roof of the drill hall at the Park Avenue Armory.

Maybe you prefer something you can take home without the aid of heavy machinery, like the beautiful, extraordinarily delicate glass vase created by the British designer Christopher Dresser about 1895. (It is at Associated Artists.) Made of pale green glass imbued with flecks of gold, it has a bulbous, dimpled lower half, like an overripe grapefruit. Wrapped by a fine, spiraling line of glass, its slender upper half culminates in a broad, flat lip. If the maverick ceramicist George Ohr had been a glassmaker, he might have produced something like this.

Among the best of high-end antiques extravaganzas, the Winter Antiques Show, which begins on Friday and runs through Jan. 31, presents a practically encyclopedic range of art, decorative objects, furniture, tribal arts and crafts, and much more. There are dealers in ancient art, medieval suits of armor, Art Nouveau, American folk art and Modernist design.

The cutoff for the newest material is 1969. The oldest objects date back thousands of years. A complete Egyptian sarcophagus at the Safani Gallery is from about 1000 B.C. With the proud face of a woman carved in high relief on the cover and the rest adorned by finely painted hieroglyphics representing texts from the Book of the Dead, it is one of the stars of the show.

Most of the booths are built to resemble luxurious antiques shops, while some are like small museum galleries, and others seek to create more theatrical effects. Hans P. Kraus Jr., a dealer in 19th-century photography, has constructed his to evoke a room in Lacock Abbey, the 13th-century home of William Henry Fox Talbot, the photography pioneer. It has a false bay window with a misty view of a gnarly old tree outdoors copied from a photograph that Fox Talbot shot from one of his windows.

A glass-top case contains memorabilia borrowed from Lacock Abbey, now a museum dedicated to Fox Talbot’s life and work. It displays the print from which the tree in the window was copied; a surprisingly modern-looking color chart made by Fox Talbot; and a book of pressed botanical specimens that his mother collected, identified and dated.

Among Fox Talbot’s photographs of trees, buildings, sculptures and other subjects on the walls is a sweet 1840s portrait of one of his daughters, about 10. Unlike most portraiture from photography’s early years, in which scowling sitters seem stiff and angry because of the long exposure times, she appears unusually alive as she looks off to her right, as if momentarily distracted by some fleeting event.

Another historic site has inspired the booth of Elle Shushan, who specializes in portrait miniatures, which were typically made with watercolor on ivory. The walls are covered by fabric loosely painted to resemble a room in the home of a wealthy 18th-century Bostonian named Harrison Gray Otis. On an actual shelf is a full-figure portrait of a woman painted in exhaustive detail by William Charles Ross. At 10 ½ by 15 ½ inches, it is thought to be one of the three or four largest ivory miniatures known to exist.

Another item of considerable rarity is a Tiffany lamp from a series called “Flowering Lotus” at the Macklowe Gallery. It has a dome of mostly green and yellow stained glass and a bronze base of finely formed lotus leaves. It is one of only about eight from the series that are known to exist.

Even less common is a 17th-century red lacquer, multitiered Chinese food box at the booth of Nicholas Grindley, who says he knows of only one other like it.

Often you come upon unexpected but illuminating juxtapositions of objects. On a pedestal at the entrance to Rupert Wace Ancient Art is a Near Eastern sculpture of a duck carved from basalt and dating from the first half of the first millennium B.C. Its smoothly simplified shape calls to mind the semi-abstract sculptures of fish and birds by Constantin Brancusi.

At the far end of the booth stands a gorgeous marble Aphrodite dating from the first or second century A.D., whose supple headless and armless body is framed by billowing drapery. The Roman sculpture and the duck conjure a fascinating triangulation of the ancient, the classical and the modern.

At Peter Petrou there is a strikingly lively, polychromed wood sculpture of a standing, naked baby Jesus made in Spain about 1650. Its anatomical subtlety contrasts with another polychromed wood sculpture of a female figure nearby. With her egg-shaped head, cylindrical arms and conical gown, she is like a big marionette; in contrast, the baby Jesus seems magically alive.

At Bernard Goldberg Fine Arts a funny, half-life-size Cubist Spanish dancer made in 1914 by Jacques Lipchitz is framed by three grand leaded-glass windows by George W. Maher (1905-6) in which the sinuous forms of flowers are rendered in clear glass. Languid elegance meets energetic punch. Also at this booth, don’t miss a small, knockout painting of white flowers against a red background by Marsden Hartley.

Though outnumbered by utilitarian and decorative objects, there are many other remarkable works of fine art. Daniel Katz has a plaster bust of Antoine Watteau by Jean-Baptiste Carpeaux from 1883. Made with expressionistic élan, it is a study for a bigger-than-life commemorative statue of the painter for Valenciennes, France, the hometown of both artists.

Adelson Galleries has paintings and drawings by the American Impressionist Mary Cassatt, including a breathtakingly lovely portrait of a pensive girl in a big red hat, painted with infectious gestural freedom.

A decidedly odder painting is at the Tillou booth. Made in 1845 by the American artist John Vanderlyn for a Hudson River steamer called the Santa Claus, it represents Saint Nick climbing into a chimney. Far from Vanderlyn’s familiar neo-Classical style, it has a comical, cartoonish look, as if it had been made by a skillful folk artist. Note that this Santa wears a gray cloak, carries toys and trinkets in woven baskets and is not fat, though he does have a beard and a jolly mien.

The Winter Antiques Show continues through Jan. 31 at the Park Avenue Armory, 643 Park Avenue, at 67th Street; (718) 292-7392, winterantiquesshow.com
