Molina Rebuffs Mets to Re-Sign With Giants

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By DAVID WALDSTEIN
Published: January 19, 2010

The Mets spent much of the off-season trying to persuade Bengie Molina to become their starting catcher, and they were relatively confident they could entice him to accept a one-year deal with a vesting option for a second season.

In the end, that confidence got them nowhere. On Tuesday, Molina spurned the Mets’ final offer of $5 million for 2010, and the option, and instead accepted less money ($4.5 million) to remain with San Francisco for one more season. The news was a blow to the Mets, causing an afternoon of consternation at Citi Field.

“It’s my understanding that he liked their deal better and felt more comfortable there,” Mets General Manager Omar Minaya said. “We made what we felt was a good offer, the right offer, and he decided to choose something else.”

Minaya said the Mets might consider making an offer to the free-agent catcher Yorvit Torrealba, with whom they have been in contact. Turning to him would be intriguing, and perhaps costly, because Torrealba filed a continuing grievance against the Mets after they backed out of a three-year deal with him two years ago.

The Mets and Torrealba agreed on a three-year, $14.4 million contract before the 2008 season. But the Mets changed their minds, citing residual damage that Torrealba had sustained from a rotator-cuff tear, damage the Mets said they discovered during a physical examination.

The Mets’ decision hurt Torrealba financially when he tried to strike a deal with another team. In the end, he re-signed with Colorado, for two years and $6.75 million.

Last month, the Mets and Torrealba attended a hearing on the grievance, conducted by the baseball arbitrator Shyam Das. A ruling is not expected for several months. If the Mets want to settle the grievance and pick up a catcher all in one move, signing the 31-year-old Torrealba would certainly do it. But Torrealba’s agent, Melvin Roman, would undoubtedly want any new deal to include extra money to make up for what they feel they lost out on two years ago.

That might be daunting. The Mets, with their many medical mysteries, would also have to explain why they would now want a player they previously rejected for health reasons.

Torrealba, who started 125 games the last two seasons as part of a Rockies platoon, hit .291 in 2009 in 213 at-bats and was a key player for Colorado last September in the team’s successful bid for the wild-card spot. Torrealba is also talking to Texas, Seattle and San Diego..

If the Mets decide to stay within their organization, they will likely rely on last year’s starter, Omir Santos, and an off-season pickup, Henry Blanco.

Meanwhile, Minaya continues to look for pitching. The Mets attended Ben Sheets’s workout in Louisiana on Tuesday, although his injury history is an issue. They are also in talks with Joel Piniero and have spoken to the agents for the Japanese left-hander Hisanori Takahashi.

The Mets also avoided arbitration with outfielder Jeff Francoeur, who agreed to a one-year, $5 million contract; and relievers Pedro Feliciano, who agreed to a one-year, $2.9 million contract; and Sean Green, who accepted a deal for one year at $975,000.

INSIDE PITCH

Darryl Strawberry and Dwight Gooden were formally welcomed back into the Mets fold Tuesday when they, with Davey Johnson and Frank Cashen, were selected for induction into the Mets Hall of Fame on Aug. 1. All four were members of the 1986 World Series championship team, and they expressed delight at the honor, especially Strawberry and Gooden. Both players said they considered themselves Mets at heart, and Strawberry said the biggest mistake he ever made was leaving the team as a free agent.
