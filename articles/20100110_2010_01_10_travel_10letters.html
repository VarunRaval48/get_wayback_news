Letter: Stendhal and Parma

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
Published: January 10, 2010

To the Editor: I enjoyed Adam Begley’s “Stendhal in Parma” (Dec. 27) because it’s nice to read an article about a lesser-known city that has “a square of rare beauty and antiquity, and a corresponding absence of commercial ventures associated with tourism” (after all, we certainly read enough about Rome, Venice, Milan and so on). However, I couldn’t believe Mr. Begley never mentioned a book that helped to put Parma on the map for a lot of us — John Grisham’s “Playing for Pizza” (2007). It is about a former N.F.L. player who agrees to play “football americano” in Parma.

The book is a riot to read, and one is transported to the palazzi, duomi and teatri of Parma. You can practically feel yourself racing around the streets of the city in an old Fiat. Reading “Playing for Pizza” is better than any guidebook.

Steven Speiser
Orlando, Fla.

To the Editor: The article on Stendhal and Parma delights and offers many accurate insights. For further reading, I suggest Tobias Jones’s “Dark Heart of Italy” and my book, “Mother Tongue: An American Life in Italy,” both about the city. I discuss Stendhal and Parma’s psychology in a chapter called “Portrait in a Convex Mirror,” a reference to Parmigianino, who painted there. In “The Charterhouse of Parma,” when Fabrizio thinks of starting a new life in America, the duchess tells him, “What a mistake you are making. You won’t have war, and you’ll fall back into a cafe life, only without the smartness, without music, without love affairs.”

Wallis Wilde-Menozzi
Parma, Italy
