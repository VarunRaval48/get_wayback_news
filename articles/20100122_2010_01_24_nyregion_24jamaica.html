Jamaica High May Close, but Memories Will Live On

Julie Glassberg for The New York Times

Jamaica High School

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By SHARON OTTERMAN
Published: January 22, 2010

INSIDE the high-ceilinged vestibule of the Georgian Revival showplace that is Jamaica High School, a red sign reads, “Eighty percent of success is just showing up.” Alas, the school’s average attendance rate this year is 79 percent.

They Passed Through Jamaica High

JAMAICA NOTABLES Clockwise from top left: Bob Beamon, ’66, Olympic champion; Michael Weiner, ’58, now known as Michael Savage, conservative author and talk show host; Letty Cottin Pogrebin ’55, Co-founder of Ms. Magazine; Judith Shapiro, ’59, President, Barnard University.

Jamaica, which spent most of a century as a jewel in the city’s public school system, is now slated to be closed for poor performance. At a recent public hearing, hundreds of parents, teachers and alumni defended its record, and angrily asked why the city did not do more as the school’s graduation rate slipped below 50 percent starting in 2002.

The original Jamaica High opened in 1896 with a few hundred students in what was then a rural enclave of central Queens. The current building opened in 1927 and, in its heyday, had an enrollment topping 5,000. From the letter sweaters and pleated skirts of the late 1950s to the secretary of education’s naming it one of the nation’s outstanding high schools in 1985, Jamaica maintained a strong reputation. Among the graduates found in the pages of its yearbook, Folio, are Walter O’Malley, longtime owner of the Brooklyn (and, later, Los Angeles) Dodgers (class of 1922); the author and composer Paul Bowles (1928); John N. Mitchell, President Richard M. Nixon’s attorney general (1931); Stephen Jay Gould (1958); and the men who became the Cleftones (1955).

But through the 1990s, many of the area’s top students chose other schools, and a spike in violence in 2007 led to more transfers. The violence has dipped and graduation rates have climbed slightly since then, but enrollment remains around 1,500. With a vote scheduled for Tuesday on the closing of Jamaica and 19 other schools, six of its most successful former students recalled their days in the school on the hill.

Bob Beamon 66
ACTIVITIES Basketball, track
BECAME Olympic gold medalist (1968) in long jump I was a special-needs kid, and I got my big break at Jamaica High. I was out of control, in middle school, and the court sent me to the alternative school, or I would have been incarcerated until I was 21. So the first person Ive got to say thanks to was the dean, Larry Ellis, and he was the track coach. He was a gentleman who understood second chances for kids. I had just won the Junior Olympics and I had a big spread in The New York Mirror. I took it to him and I said, Can I transfer here? so I could pursue graduating as well as pursue sports. The dean immediately took me to the principals office, and said, Let us take a chance.

Ato Boldon (attended 1989-90)
ACTIVITIES Soccer, track
BECAME Olympic medalist sprinter (1996, 2000) and sports broadcast analyst Me getting discovered in terms of track and field only happened because the track coach, Joe Trupiano, saw me playing soccer and he thought I would be good. I was a junior, and my memory is of learning how to start. Blue gloves on the ice and snow on the weekend, with Joe Trupiano. I went on to be one of the best starters ever, and you can link it right back to that pavement. Our workouts were literally down the hall, down the steps, down the other hall, and then back up. I think it might have given me shin splints, but Jamaica High School also made me tough. After that, in my whole career, I only missed one race due to injury.

Judith Shapiro 59
ACTIVITIES Drama Club, newspaper
BECAME President of Barnard College I was active in theater. That was a way to make up for the fact if you werent social, in the way girls sometimes traditionally are in high school; it was a way of getting attention and succeeding. I remember the director of the theater program had been Helen Hayess understudy on Broadway. Many of our teachers were first-generation born in the United States. Mrs. Guren and Mrs. Newman, these were the French teachers, and they were superb. Now kids will come out of high school to Barnard after taking high school French and be in an introductory course. When I got to Brandeis, my first course in French was a very advanced course on the novel. We were reading Sartre in high school.

Letty Cottin Pogrebin 55
ACTIVITIES Hilltopper newspaper, dance
BECAME Co-founder of Ms. magazine and the National Womens Political Caucus We had about 5,000 students, and yet I never felt overwhelmed. I always felt there were little interest groups you could feel individualized by, like little islands in this great sea. I was in the modern dance corps, and I remember feeling like I was in a little bohemian part of the school. Then I dated the captain of the basketball team, so I was part of that as well. That was about as good as a girl could get in those days.

Michael Weiner 58
ACTIVITIES Rifle squad, chemistry
BECAME Conservative author and talk-radio host, known as Michael Savage Nobody took the gun and shot anybody with it, no one went berzerk. The rifle teachers were men who fought in Korea, and I remember we shot Mossberg .22 target rifles, in between other classes. My memory is of the smell of gunpowder, the smell of the sulfur and how it would clear the brain. The majority of kids were children of immigrants driven like crazy to succeed by the parents, so it was super competitive, the Harvard of high schools. If you didnt go to college, you might as well have had leprosy. Our geometry teacher was Irish. He was missing a finger, and everyone said he had been in the I.R.A. He would read your grades aloud on every test, and you had to come to the blackboard. It was like Rita, you did very, very well, you got a 98, but God forbid, if you got under 80, you might as well aim to be a sanitation worker. It wasnt tough love at that point, it was straight out ridicule and shame.

Fran Capo, 77
ACTIVITIES Track, fencing, debate, photo club, film club
BECAMEComedian, worlds fastest-talking woman (according to Guinness) I remember three teachers specifically. My art teacher, Mr. Koomerian, who always told us to look at anything with a creative eye; my accounting teacher, who said, Always keep your records straight and you cant get in trouble; and my typing teacher, who said, Learn how to type; someday you will need it.
