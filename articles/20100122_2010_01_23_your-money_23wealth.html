At Bonus Time, Less Appetite for Toys

Marilynn K. Yee/The New York Times

Joseph W. Spada, managing director at Summit Financial Resources, said clients are trying to safeguard their assets.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By PAUL SULLIVAN
Published: January 22, 2010

WITH all eyes on banks and their bonus pools, there is the question of what happens to the bonus money the bankers receive this year. Will they return to flashy spending or invest it more conservatively?

Skip to next paragraph

Paul Sullivan writes about strategies that the wealthy use to manage their money and their overall well-being.

Paul Sullivans Columns

After all, bonus pools are climbing again. Goldman Sachs, the benchmark for bonuses and the target of public ire, disclosed Thursday that its average employee would receive nearly $500,000 in bonus and compensation, up from the $340,600 an employee it paid last year. This figure is fairly misleading, because bonuses are not doled out evenly. Executive assistants will get far less than the average, while star traders will be paid much more.

In past years, the cliché image of bonus season was of a flush banker walking into a Maserati dealership and driving out in his new toy. That type of spending will be more the exception than the rule this year, advisers said, for two reasons — neither of which has to do with public perception. The first is the need to have cash on hand to cover basic expenses, a lesson learned the hard way in the depths of the financial crisis last year.

The second reason is the uncertainty of bankers over how much of their bonuses they will actually get, and when they will get it. Because most banks have tried to head off criticism of the size of their compensation packages, they have chosen this year to split the bonuses between cash and restricted stock. In some cases, that stock can be forfeited if the executive or his division performs poorly in the future. These so-called “clawback provisions” are making their recipients more conservative — and the financial planning more difficult.

Shawn A. Rubin, managing director for the wealth management division at Morgan Stanley Smith Barney, said this uncertainty had his clients thinking long and hard about what to do with their bonuses. “There are even more questions when something is clawback-able, as it were,” he said. “How do you plan for something you might not get?”

This question is at the center of the changing conversation advisers are having with their clients. They say their clients are focused on three areas: current needs, safe investments and managing for uncertainty. While the numbers at stake are bigger than the average investor has to worry about, the lessons to be learned can be applied at both ends of the scale.

NEEDS If there was one thing last year’s economy taught seemingly savvy financiers, it was the importance of having money on hand to pay the bills. The memory of selling securities to cover expenses — mortgages, school fees, general daily life costs — is fresh in many bankers’ minds and has influenced how much money they are allocating to low-yielding but safe investments.

“Clients are going to be a little more cautious with overspending,” said Christopher Zander, a partner at Evercore Wealth Management. “With the cash portion of their bonus, they need to make sure they’re allocating a portion to household expenses.”

This may seem obvious, but in the frothy years of the boom, many bankers chose to be fully invested and then borrow against those investments to pay current expenses. That way, the thinking went, their investments would continue to grow at a higher rate than the interest on the loans they took out. But then Lehman Brothers collapsed , and it seemed that others might follow. The margin calls on those loans were sudden and unwelcome.

“The feeling now is, ‘I’m more concerned about the return of my money than the return on my money,’ ” said Joseph W. Spada, managing director at Summit Financial Resources in Parsippany, N.J.

So what bankers are doing is putting more of their money in low-yielding money market accounts and less in private equity and hedge funds that have provisions restricting the withdrawal of money. The portion of bank bonuses being paid in restricted stock has the same effect as locking up money in a hedge fund: it is money the bankers may have at some point, but it is not something they can use now. That also means borrowing against it to finance their standard of living is not an option.

SAFETY So what investments are bankers making? For the former masters of the universe, some of the options are decidedly average.

Mr. Spada said he had several clients pay down the principal on their mortgages. In doing so, they guaranteed themselves a return better than most bond portfolios are producing. He cited one financial executive who used his bonus to pay down the $2 million interest-only mortgage on his home. That had the virtue of lowering his monthly payment and bringing him closer to the $1 million threshold to deduct mortgage interest on his taxes. More significant, however, is that by paying down his mortgage, he got a tax-free return equivalent to the 5.5 percent interest rate on his mortgage. To get that return on a taxable bond investment, the client would have to earn 8.46 percent.

Another version of this real estate play is upgrading to a nicer home. “When the money hits their bank and they see it on their screen, you’ll see a very short-lived bump in real estate,” Mr. Rubin said. “A lot of these guys say having hard assets is better. Having a mortgage against a house is not a bad way for them to be positioned.”

Those with a little more risk appetite have been turning to hedge funds that are structured like mutual funds or exchange-traded funds, Mr. Zander said. Those hedge funds allow investors to withdraw their money when they want.

The desire for this more liquid structure is a direct byproduct of the credit squeeze. When hedge funds invoked clauses in their investment agreements that kept investors from taking their money out — so-called gating provisions — investors scrambled to raise money from other assets. “After going through this in 2008, individuals are more mindful of liquidity,” Mr. Zander said.

UNCERTAINTY Overshadowing all these decisions is the fear of inflation. However much policy makers say inflation will not be a risk for some time, people in financial services are already worrying about it.

William Tedford, executive vice president of Stephens Inc., an investment bank in Little Rock, Ark., predicts that housing prices could hit bottom in the first half of the year and loan demand could pick up. He said he feared that the Federal Reserve would lag behind in increasing interest rates quickly enough to stave off inflation.

To hedge against this, Mr. Tedford is encouraging clients to put 10 to 15 percent of their assets in commodities. “I may be years ahead of worrying about this thing, but the risk is great enough,” he said. “I’m beginning to lose a little sleep over it.”

Inflation is something bankers are already factoring into how they are investing their bonuses. “They’re nervous about so much money in circulation,” Mr. Spada said. The antidote is buying gold and commodities through exchange-traded funds.

The paradox here is rich. At the nadir of the financial crisis, gold was the safe haven for people worried the world was ending. Now it could become the asset for those concerned that the government money spent to save that world will end up hurting it down the road.
