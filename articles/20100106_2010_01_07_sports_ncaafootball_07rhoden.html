Saban Is Seeking His Statue of Liberation

By WILLIAM C. RHODEN
Published: January 6, 2010

NEWPORT BEACH, Calif.

At every Alabama home game, Nick Saban is reminded of the mountain he has not climbed as the Crimson Tide’s football coach.

Each time he leads his team down the walk of champions into Bryant-Denny Stadium, Saban can look to his right and see four bronze statues, one for each Alabama coach who has led the Tide to a national championship:

Each statue has a wall behind it with the coach’s name and the championship year: Wallace Wade, 1925, 1926, 1930; Frank Thomas, 1934, 1941; Bear Bryant, 1961, 1964, 1965, 1973, 1978, 1979; Gene Stallings, 1992.

There is an empty space with a blank wall next to the four statues, reserved for the next coach to lead Alabama to a national title. Crimson Tide fans are hoping against hope that Saban will complete an undefeated season against Texas on Thursday night and that his larger-than-life image will fill that void.

Saban, however, said that posterity was not a priority on his agenda.

“I really have never thought about that, honestly, and very few people have brought it up,” he said Wednesday.

“This game is not about me, it’s about the players,” he said. “What’s going to make me happiest, if we have success in this game, is that the players get the opportunity to experience that and accomplish that because it’s something that they’ll probably remember for the rest of their life.”

Saban is clearly not into statues and monuments, but every generation needs its own mythology, legends who define a particular time and moment. Bryant defined Alabama football, framed a football culture and represented a volatile moment in Alabama’s racial evolution.

A new generation needs a new Bear, and this one has been a long time coming.

•

Alabama won its last national championship in 1992; it began a freefall immediately after that didn’t stop until Saban arrived in 2007. In January 1993, the talented defensive back Antonio Langham signed with an agent, causing Alabama to be put on probation in 1995. The Tide was hit with scholarship limitations and a one-year postseason ban.

Stallings was fired after the 1996 season, and the next 11 years were defined by peaks and valleys, including a five-year probation from 2002 to 2006 and Mike Price’s hiring and firing before coaching a game after off-field indiscretions were uncovered

Saban, meanwhile, was experiencing his own peaks and valleys. He led Louisiana State to a B.C.S. title in the 2003 season, then left for the N.F.L. and the Miami Dolphins. After two years, Saban left the Dolphins for Alabama and became one of the nation’s highest-paid collegiate coaches.

Paul Finebaum, who covered Alabama since 1980 and is a radio host for a popular Birmingham radio station, said Saban was seen as a savior for whom no price was too high.

“The fans were so desperate and so hungry that when they hired Saban, it was the first time the school looked like it was serious,” he said.

Finebaum remembers when Saban arrived for his interview. “Fans were at the airport like Elvis was returning from the war,” he recalled. “I’m not trying to do a biblical comparison, but Alabama fans were out in the desert for a long time.”

Karen Steckol, the president of the faculty senate for the last two years, said that the faculty community was open to the Saban hiring, $4 million yearly salary and all.

“When he was hired, there were comments that were made that the salary was an awful lot of money,” Steckol said. “But after that first initial jolt that people had, everyone has felt very positive about the fact that he is on our faculty as a football coach.”

Steckol added: “We’re one of the few universities in the country that’s hiring people and adding new faculty when everybody else is cutting back. A lot of that has to do with the success of the football team. We are so happy to be where we are at the moment in football, in academics, in the quality of the student that we’re attracting, in the number of the students that we’re attracting. The salaries have been increased because of the number of students. The university is on a real high right now.”

Saban went 7-6 in 2007, then went undefeated during the regular season in 2008 before losing to Florida in the Southeastern Conference championship game. Alabama is undefeated again.

There will never be another Bryant, but Saban has already accomplished something no other Alabama coach has — he produced Alabama’s first Heisman Trophy winner in Mark Ingram last month.

Saban may not be interested in posterity. But a generation of young Alabama fans, weary perhaps of hearing their parents and grandparents talk about the great Paul Bryant, wants its own Bear.

With a victory over Texas on Thursday, they may finally have one, and that empty space along the walk of champions will finally be filled.
