Hole Opened, and Greene Hit His Stride

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By DAVE CALDWELL
Published: January 13, 2010

FLORHAM PARK, N.J. — Even now, two very good years later, Shonn Greene remembers what it was like to work in the warehouse at a furniture store nearly 1,000 miles from home for $8 an hour. It was not exactly hard work, but it was not a lot of fun, either.

Greene, a rookie running back for the Jets, had let his grades slip, and he was academically ineligible for the 2007 season at the University of Iowa. As he attended classes at nearby Kirkwood Community College, he needed money, so he picked up a job at McGregors Furniture in Coralville, Iowa.

“It was a very humbling experience,” Greene said this week.

Greene felt as if he had been stripped of his career. He had been a star at Winslow Township High School in Atco, N.J., before spending a year at Milford Academy in Connecticut to prepare for college.

Greene rejoined the Hawkeyes in 2008 and had a superb junior season, rushing for 1,850 yards and 20 touchdowns. He entered the N.F.L. draft a year early, and the Jets, who thought Greene could have been a first-round draft choice, traded up 11 spots to grab him after he slipped to the third round.

“I was going in as a rookie, so you’ve got to be humble and wait your turn,” Greene said.

But here he is, playing for an N.F.L. team in his home state, not much more than 100 miles from Sicklerville, N.J., his hometown. Even after injuring his ankle in training camp, Greene has rebounded to replace the injured Leon Washington as a threat.

Greene rushed for 135 yards — 39 of them on a game-tying touchdown run in the second quarter — in Saturday’s 24-14 wild-card playoff victory against the Cincinnati Bengals. Greene’s teammates hope he can help them thwart the San Diego Chargers in a playoff game Sunday.

“He brings a different tempo,” fullback Tony Richardson said. “He’s a different back.”

Greene, 24, is built like the sleeper sofas he used to haul at McGregors. At 5 feet 11 inches and 226 pounds, Greene bowls people over as he darts through holes, unlike the smaller and shiftier Washington.

“You don’t put a square peg in a round hole,” Richardson said, comparing Greene with Washington. “Shonn is not trying to run away from anybody.”

Greene rushed for 144 yards in the game in which Washington broke his right leg — a 38-0 victory Oct. 25 over the Oakland Raiders. He finished the regular season with 540 yards rushing, which included 95 in the Jets’ surprising 29-15 victory Dec. 27 against the Indianapolis Colts.

He did not play in the Jets’ first two games and did not carry the ball until the fourth game. But he finished the season with 108 carries and averaged 5 yards a carry. He makes mistakes: he lost a fumble in the first quarter of the 37-0 victory Jan. 3 against Cincinnati, but the Jets kept the ball because the Bengals were offside.

Rex Ryan, the Jets’ first-year coach, said last week that he stopped Greene on the sideline after the fumble and said: “Let’s go, kid, pound it. Ground and pound, let’s go, baby.”

Greene did not fumble in 21 carries in the victory Saturday against the Bengals. Thomas Jones appeared to be slowed by a knee injury, gaining 34 yards on 15 carries, and Cincinnati had a better idea of how to contain Brad Smith when the Jets lined up in the Wildcat formation.

But the Jets still had Greene. They had the ball six minutes longer than the Bengals. If they can reprise the ground-and-pound philosophy Sunday, they might be able to keep the ball from the Chargers (13-3), who have scored at least 20 points in all their games.

“It’s nice having a solid 1-2 punch,” center Nick Mangold said, referring to Jones and Greene.

Quarterback Mark Sanchez also values the Jets’ depth. “To have a running game like we do takes so much pressure off the quarterback, especially a rookie quarterback,” he said.

Although Washington still uses crutches, he said this week that he expected to be fully recovered by the start of next season. He said he was enjoying watching the Jets win without him. He also said Greene does not look much like a rookie anymore.

“He’s a rookie just like Mark Sanchez,” Washington said. “They’re deep in the playoffs now. I’m impressed with the way he’s carrying himself.”

Greene said he wanted to do whatever he could to “keep this train rolling.” His memory, he said, is short, but he does not want to forget what it felt like to have football taken away from him because of something he failed to do.

“I kind of take that like a learning tool,” he said of his job at the furniture warehouse.

Greene was surrounded by reporters and cameras at his locker at the Jets’ training facility, a man in the news. He said he felt as if he had come a long way in two years. He smiled when he said, “Back then, I never thought all of this would come out of it.”
