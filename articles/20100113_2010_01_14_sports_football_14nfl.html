Crennel Latest Ex-Patriot To Join the Chiefs’ Staff

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By THE ASSOCIATED PRESS
Published: January 13, 2010

Romeo Crennel, the defensive coordinator on the New England Patriots’ Super Bowl teams, agreed Wednesday to join the Kansas City Chiefs in the same capacity, his agent said. Crennel, who was out of football this past season after hip surgery, will be reunited with Charlie Weis, who was New England’s offensive coordinator and agreed last week to take that job with the Chiefs.

Crennel’s agent, Joe Linta, said Crennel would begin working for the Chiefs in a “couple of weeks.” The Chiefs declined to comment.

Crennel and Weis will be joining a franchise that has won only 10 games in three years and went 4-12 in 2009 in Todd Haley’s rocky rookie season as a head coach. As defensive coordinator, Crennel will replace Clancy Pendergast, whose status on the staff has not been redefined.

JOHNSON TOP OFFENSIVE PLAYER Tennessee running back Chris Johnson, who set a league record for yards from scrimmage (2,509) and was the sixth player with a 2,000-yard rushing season, was voted the Associated Press N.F.L. offensive player of the year.

Johnson earned 38 ½ votes from a panel of 50 sportswriters and broadcasters who cover the league. Johnson easily beat New Orleans quarterback Drew Brees, who received nine votes.

“I kind of realize what I did, and I feel like I had a dream season,” said Johnson, who scored 16 touchdowns (14 rushing), second to Minnesota’s Adrian Peterson. He also tied an N.F.L. record by rushing for at least 125 yards in six consecutive games.

Colts quarterback Peyton Manning, the league’s most valuable player, received one and a half votes, and Chargers quarterback Philip Rivers got one.

Johnson is the first Titan to win the honor, but the third player in franchise history. Quarterback Warren Moon won in 1990 as a Houston Oiler, and running back Earl Campbell took the award from 1978-80 with the Oilers.

MINNESOTA DREAMING Cowboys Coach Wade Phillips has a good reason to believe running back Marion Barber will do everything possible to overcome a knee injury in time to play a second-round playoff game in Minnesota.

“He’s been mad at me the last two years because I didn’t let him play in the preseason game there,” Phillips said, chuckling. “So I know he wants to play there just from that.”

Barber has never played in his home state as a professional. But he is dealing with a sore left knee that limited him to three carries last Saturday and forced him to miss practice Wednesday.

ANOTHER GOODBYE FOR SEAU Junior Seau, a 12-time Pro Bowl linebacker who returned to the New England Patriots this season, said in remarks broadcast on Showtime on Wednesday night that he had played his last game.

Seau first retired in August 2006 after 13 seasons with San Diego and three with Miami. But he signed with the Patriots four days later and played 27 games for them over two seasons. He retired again after the 2007 season, then came out of retirement for the final four games in 2008 before retiring after that season.

Seau played seven regular-season games this season, all as a backup, and made 15 tackles.

“That’s going to be my last game,” he said on Showtime’s “Inside the NFL.”

LEADER OF THE HOGS RETIRES Joe Bugel, the Washington Redskins’ offensive line coach, stepped down after more than three decades in the N.F.L.

Bugel, who turns 70 in March, coached the dominant Redskins offensive line that became known as the Hogs in the 1980s, during Joe Gibbs’s first stint as head coach. It was the first offensive line to receive such national prominence, and Washington won two Super Bowls while Bugel was with the team.

Bugel later became a head coach with the Cardinals and the Raiders. He returned to Washington in 2004 when Gibbs came out of retirement.

IMPLOSION SET Dallas Cowboys haters can circle April 11 on their calendars. That’s when Texas Stadium, their former home, will be imploded.

Plans are under way to create spectator viewing areas, and cameras will be installed to track the planning process and the implosion itself. Maura Gast, the executive director of the Irving Convention and Visitors Bureau, said there is “great interest” among fans and tourists.

Texas Stadium was the Cowboys’ home for 37 years before the team left for the $1.2 billion Cowboys Stadium in Arlington this season.
