Are Bargain Hunters Replacing the Bears at Scottsdale?

Wheels: Storm Hits Russo & Steele Collector Car Auction

In January, when the triple-digit heat of summer is on vacation, central Arizona is left with surprisingly decent professional hockey, the winter lettuce harvest and bearable daytime high temperatures. But for those who are bigger fans of classic cars than of hockey or salad bars, it means only one thing — the feeding frenzy caused by a week of five major collector car auctions.

By now, even the most bearish and pessimistic pundits in the collector car world have stopped predicting a free fall in values, seemingly replaced by those speculating on when the market will return to its high of late 2007. The general consensus is that if that happens, it won’t be until the economy is once again as fit as Lance Armstrong. No matter, buyers have been out in force at the auctions, which began last Tuesday, despite high winds and torrential rains that are highly unusual here.

By the time the last gavel comes down on Monday evening, well over $100 million in collector cars will probably have changed hands at five very different sales.

The oldest of the auctions here, Barrett-Jackson, started the sellathon last week in its usual overwhelming style — its sales tent is seemingly larger than several Sam’s Clubs and a Costco put together, with 1,200 cars offered and 39 hours of coverage on Speed TV. The cars on the block this year are less ethereal and less expensive than in recent years, but certainly more in keeping with today’s economic reality.

Two high-end sales will undoubtedly vie for the highest average sale prices of the weekend: RM Auctions, which has the fanciest setting by far, at the historic Arizona Biltmore, and Gooding & Company, which camps out at an upscale shopping mall not far away. As their auctions last August in Pebble Beach, Calif., illustrated, the very top of the collector car market seems the least affected by the sluggish economy.

This year, RM is holding a special Thursday night sale of nothing but British cars.

Russo & Steele’s Scottsdale sale occupies the middle ground of the collector car market — roughly $100,000 to $500,000 — offering mainly American muscle cars and European sports cars. That part of the market seemed to be under the most stress last year. “For those looking to buy genuine collectible cars, as opposed to novelties, the current market offers some real opportunity” said Drew Alcazar, the founder and owner of Russo & Steele.

But the Russo & Steele auction, which began on Thursday, was interrupted that night by one of the most severe storms the area has experienced in years. Winds tore at two of the tents the company was using to house cars going to auction, collapsing one of them and sending parts of the other blowing onto the nearby 101 Freeway.

Several hundred vintage cars suffered at least minor damage, and no sales were made on Friday as fire officials kept the area cordoned off, saying it remained dangerous. Mr. Alcazar said Friday afternoon that he hoped to gain access on Saturday and to resume the company’s auction on Sunday.

Offering perhaps the most diverse and affordable group of cars is Silver Auctions, whose four-day sale takes place at a casino in Fort McDowell, Ariz., some 40 minutes away. Silver’s sale is really the only place where a collector on a budget can hope to drive home in something interesting for under $10,000.
