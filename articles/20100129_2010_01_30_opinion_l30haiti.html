Heart-Rending Stories: The Children of Haiti

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
Published: January 29, 2010

To the Editor:

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Karen Barbour

Re “Haiti’s Quake Set Children Adrift in World of Unfathomable Chaos” (front page, Jan. 27):

Daphne Joseph’s story about losing her mother and mistreatment by her aunt after the earthquake is absolutely heart-wrenching. Yet Daphne, in her misery, was able to express compassion and sorrow for children who had lost both parents in the catastrophe.

As the child of a single mother, she said, “Before the earthquake, I only had but Mama.” How can someone react to this comment?

This child, who had watched her mother’s body dumped into a wheelbarrow, was able to reach outside of herself to others. If there is any way of getting the message out there to Daphne, please let her know that people all over the world now know about her and that our thoughts and love are with her and all the other children who are in her situation.

Andrea Shankman
Bronx, Jan. 27, 2010

•

To the Editor:

Re “Girls’ Rescue From Haiti Expands Family by Two” (news article, Jan. 26):

As the director of a nonprofit organization working in Zimbabwe, I am deeply troubled by what reporting on the earthquake in Haiti has brought to light regarding the adoptions of Haitian children whose parents are still living.

Recently, The New York Times reported on the widespread consensus that assistance to orphaned and vulnerable children should focus on enabling relatives to continue caring for them whenever possible (“As African Orphanages Spread, Advocates Propose a Better Way,” front page, Dec. 6, 2009).

But press coverage of recent events in Haiti indicates that destitute parents in Haiti regularly relinquish their children to orphanages, for lack of external assistance.

It is time to open a critical inquiry into the nature of a system that prioritizes adoptions of non-orphaned children over providing effective aid to children living in vulnerable families.

Jennifer Kyker
Eugene, Ore., Jan. 26, 2010

The writer is executive director of Tariro: Hope and Health for Zimbabwe’s Orphans.
