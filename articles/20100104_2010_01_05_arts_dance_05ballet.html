A City Ballet Stars Reinventions

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By GIA KOURLAS
Published: January 4, 2010

When Peter Boal retired from New York City Ballet in 2005, he left a gap at the dance barre. As it happens, there just aren’t enough well-proportioned, innately elegant men with scrupulous line and demeanor to go around.

This week Mr. Boal, 44, returns to New York, not as a dancer but as the artistic director of Pacific Northwest Ballet in Seattle, a position he has held since he left City Ballet. (Last summer he signed on for another five years.) For its engagement at the Joyce Theater, running Tuesday through Sunday, Pacific Northwest will present a program of repertory works, including two created for it: Twyla Tharp’s “Opus 111” and Benjamin Millepied’s “3 Movements.” Also included are two smaller pieces, Marco Goecke’s solo “Mopey” and Edwaard Liang’s duet “Für Alina.”

Mr. Boal has brought 21 dancers from the company of 48 and selected repertory that he said he hoped won’t appear cramped.

“I’ve got to tell you, Ben’s piece almost fits on the stage at the Joyce,” Mr. Boal said in a telephone interview. “Twyla’s is going to be tight. Luckily, she saves the entire cast of 12 until the final minute of the ballet. But I thought, if we hold our breath and wait for a 2,000-seat theater with an orchestra pit and the funding to go with it, this might never happen. And a generation of dancers won’t be seen in New York.”

Mr. Boal, who speaks softly and with a hint of dryness, lives in Seattle with his wife, Kelly Cass, a former City Ballet dancer, and their three children. In less than five years at Pacific Northwest, directed earlier by Francia Russell and Kent Stowell, he has added a remarkable 57 ballets to the repertory.

On his arrival Mr. Boal made it a priority to acquire three of Ms. Tharp’s dances and commission two others, which were created during her eight-week residency with the company in 2008: “Opus 111,” set to a Brahms string quintet, and “Afternoon Ball,” a companion piece that will not come to the Joyce.

“There’s a freshness to ‘Opus 111,’ ” Mr. Boal said. “The movement is light, and it has a whimsical quality to it, yet the Brahms really gives it a gravity that Twyla plays off of. It really felt like things were born from the choreographer and dancers being in a studio together.”

Ariana Lallone, who was cast in both of Ms. Tharp’s new ballets, described the ordeal of working with her: “She pushes and pushes and pushes. She would catch you off guard and ask what you were thinking about. It wasn’t just about the choreography. She would say, ‘O.K., who are you today?,’ and you’d have to come up with a character. Nothing was literal in the dancing, but it was just so that you weren’t in the studio without thought, energy and purpose. Sometimes it was exhausting, but it got you to another level of dance.”

Mr. Boal likes to broaden the focus from individual pieces to a choreographer’s body of work, as he has also done with ballets by Jerome Robbins and Christopher Wheeldon, for audiences as well as dancers. “The idea is so that they can really discuss, as with Robbins, how ‘In the Night’ differs from ‘Fancy Free’ and what are their similarities?” he said. “Once the choreographers become the box office draw, the abilities that you have for programming are endless.”

He does something else unusual in terms of audience development. While many artistic directors remain in the wings, except for the occasional speech at a gala event, Mr. Boal follows almost every performance with a discussion. Audience members offer opinions about repertory, favorable and unfavorable, and Seattle audiences are not shy.

“I think it’s vital,” Mr. Boal said. “It’s a good signal to an audience that I am looking forward to hearing what they’re thinking, willing to discuss it, willing to be disagreed with and certainly more than willing to explain why I’ve made choices.”

The Joyce program will feature the return of Carla Körbes, a former New York City Ballet soloist who met Mr. Boal when she was a teenager in Brazil. He encouraged her to move to New York and train at the City Ballet-affiliated School of American Ballet. Now a principal with Pacific Northwest Ballet, she will appear in all the works except “Mopey.”

“What has been different for me is that I can go into his office and say, ‘I’m working too much,’ or ‘I’m working too little,’ or ‘I’m not getting this from my daily life,’ ” Ms. Körbes said. “I feel like Peter is trying extra hard to be a company with, as he calls it, an open-door policy. I feel like more of a grown-up. The ballet world, you get in as a kid, and that relationship can be maintained through your years, even though you are becoming a mature adult. And I like how here I can voice what I have in mind.”

In June Mr. Boal will add yet another new work to the repertory: Balanchine’s “Coppélia,” with sets and costumes by the Italian artist Roberta Guidi di Bagno. (Ms. Körbes noted with excitement that she was learning the part of Swanilda.) This may be Mr. Boal’s most personal addition to the company’s repertory; when he was 9, during a performance of “Coppélia” at City Ballet, he turned to his mother and told her he wanted to dance.

“It was the reason I got into this whole thing,” he said. “We’re not done talking about the brilliance of Balanchine, and I just felt like it fit perfectly into the balance of the repertory. Something has to balance William Forsythe’s ‘One Flat Thing, reproduced.’ ” In that machinelike work, 14 dancers weave their way around 20 metal tables.

“I also wanted to reassure the public that I have a place in my heart for a traditional full-length ballet,” Mr. Boal added. “As much as I love the innovative and the progressive, it’s wonderful to go back to what you fell in love with at 9.”
