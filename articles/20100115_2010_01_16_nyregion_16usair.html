Reuniting on the Hudson, but Smiling This Time

Richard Perry/The New York Times

Capt. Chesley B. Sullenberger III, left, the pilot who landed the plane in the river, celebrates on a ferry.

By JAMES BARRON
Published: January 15, 2010

Forty degrees 46 minutes 10.19 seconds north latitude, 74 degrees 16.69 seconds south latitude: Michael Leonard had been there before.

There was the weather: warmer than the other time, but still wintry. There was the sky: the bluish-pink of late afternoon, just like before.

This time was “awesome,” Mr. Leonard said. This time was “amazing.” And, yes, he said, “strange.”

A year ago Friday, he went there by airplane. The place was the Hudson River west of Manhattan and east of Weehawken, N.J., where US Airways Flight 1549 stopped after it splashed down. On Friday, he rode one of the ferries that had plucked the passengers from the water a year ago, and at 3:31 p.m. — the exact time of the emergency landing a year ago — he was standing on a deck, not on a wing, looking at the same muddy stretch of the river.

It took longer to get there this time. Flight 1549 was in the air for only about 90 seconds before the plane’s two engines swallowed some birds — Canada geese, according to the National Transportation Safety Board. So of course passengers toasted one another with Grey Goose vodka, a touch with a significance that took a while to register with some people on the boat.

Before the ferry pulled out, the passengers lined up for a group photograph, like people at a college reunion, only with countless television cameras zooming in. But this group did not say “cheese” or “applesauce,” they said “Sully,” the nickname for the pilot, Chesley B. Sullenberger III. And “Skiles,” for the first officer, Jeffrey B. Skiles.

The photograph, and the ferry ride, came on a day of reunions and reminiscences about what was supposed to be a 2-hour, 13-minute flight to Charlotte, N.C., that turned into a national news event. The 90 passengers and crew members attending (of 155 who were on board that day) began the day with a breakfast in a room facing the wrong way — the East River, not the Hudson — where they thanked the Red Cross for its relief efforts after the emergency landing.

They remembered climbing over seats and scrambling out of the airplane, lining up on the wings, tumbling into inflatable rafts and climbing onto ferryboats that came to the rescue.

They talked about meeting their fellow passengers — and some said they had more than kept in touch. Laura Zych and Ben Bostic, who were passengers on Flight 1549, began dating after a passenger reunion last summer.

Mr. Bostic said he remembered noticing Ms. Zych as they boarded the plane. “She was tall and glamorous,” he said. “Not only did we get a second chance at life, but a chance to have a life together.” He said he would ask Captain Sullenberger to be his best man, if the relationship should get that far.

Some at the breakfast referred to what happened on that chilly afternoon a year ago with a certain understatement. Captain Sullenberger, who brought the plane down safely after telling air traffic controllers, “We’re going to be in the Hudson,” called last Jan. 15 “a remarkably good day.”

Like so many others in the year since the emergency landing, Mayor Michael R. Bloomberg used the phrase “miracle on the Hudson.” But he added: “I’m not sure if it was a miracle. Chalk it up to a skilled set of people who trained for the worst. They show that training really makes a difference.”

Gov. David A. Paterson — perhaps hinting at his headaches in Albany, and with voters — called last Jan. 15 “the happiest day I have spent or ever will spend as governor.” He said that Flight 1549 was “a moment of hope for New York.”

But he also joked that he was looking forward, not back, and that he was an optimist. “Maybe there will be a miracle even greater than the miracle on the Hudson,” he said. “Maybe the Jets will beat San Diego.”

The ferry ride to the place where the plane hit the water began in a familiar place: the New York Waterway terminal, where West 39th Street meets the Hudson River.

“Being right here is strange,” said Charles Spiggle, an investment banker from Charlotte. “This is where they brought us and kept us corralled just like this with the press just like this. And they kept counting us and counting us, and then we figured out everybody must have been off the plane, which was a surprise, because after we first hit the water, I could see the back of the plane.” He said he called his wife, Kelly, and said, “I’m fine, but I don’t think everyone’s going to make it.”

But everyone did. And Elizabeth McHugh, who was in seat 20C, recognized the faces of rescuers — especially that of Ellen Blake, the accounts payable manager of New York Waterway. “One of the people I’d have thanked, if I’d known what her name was,” Ms. McHugh said.

Coming off the ferry that carried her to the New York Waterway terminal a year ago, “I was so frozen I couldn’t feel my feet,” Ms. McHugh said. “I had on this shirt. I had on these pants. I even have on the same underwear. But she was saying, ‘You have to get these clothes off.’ ”

Nate Schweber contributed reporting.
