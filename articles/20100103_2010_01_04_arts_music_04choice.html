FINDLAY BROWN
“Love Will Find You”

Brian Hineline/Retna

The singer Phil Vassar onstage in June at the Susquehanna Bank Center in Camden, N.J.

What year is this? For the 30-year-old English songwriter Findlay Brown, it’s somewhere around 1964-66, when Phil Spector’s Wall of Sound was the foundation for the Righteous Brothers, Roy Orbison was making rockabilly operatic and, in Britain, John Barry was scoring James Bond films and the Walker Brothers were singing booming, despairing pop.

Without condescension or camp Mr. Brown has teleported back to that era for his second album, “Love Will Find You.” The period-piece productions are by Bernard Butler, the former guitarist for Suede who replicated old soul for Duffy’s 2008 album “Rockferry.” For most of the album the drumbeats are stolid, string sections play swelling chords and upward swoops, and guitar lines float in movie-theme reverb. Mr. Findlay’s voice — not a Righteous baritone, but a tenor croon that he sometimes doubletracks in close Everly Brothers harmonies — appeals for love or proffers perseverance and devotion.

It’s an unexpected follow-up to Mr. Brown’s 2007 debut album, “Separated by the Sea,” which was also a throwback. It positioned him on the pop end of freak-folk, picking an acoustic guitar and singing about loneliness, with flickering electronics in the background. It came across as personal and, for secretly sensitive hipsters, au courant.

This album, by contrast, flaunts the 1960s pop that garage-rock and later punk-rock would treat as unhip: orchestral studio efforts rather than organic band collaborations. Even Mr. Brown’s voice is barely recognizable between albums. Except for the twangy, handclapping “That’s Right,” the chosen style is pointedly not the British Invasion of the mid-1960s. Mr. Brown’s new songs pay homage elsewhere: to Mr. Spector with the “Be My Baby” drumbeat of “Love Will Find You,” to Mr. Barry with the “Midnight Cowboy” introduction of “Teardrops Lost in the Rain.”

The sound is impersonal but also comforting in its scale, as stately and imperturbable as an ocean liner. Bruce Springsteen embraced a similar approach, strings and all, with “Girls in Their Summer Clothes” from “Magic” and throughout his 2009 album “Working on a Dream.” But he brought a bittersweet nostalgia to the music and thoughts of mortality to the songs.

Mr. Brown doesn’t push that far. He does, at times, slip some ambiguity into his pop edifices. “I Still Want You” might be about God rather than an ex-lover; it also has a processional beat and buzzy instrumental incursions that hark back to the experimental English pop producer Joe Meek. “Everybody Needs Love,” behind its Orbison-like melody, is the story of a woman who “sold her soul.” And the grand crescendo of “I Had a Dream” carries an odd, possibly messianic fantasy.

Mostly, however, “Love Will Find You” is Mr. Brown’s announcement that he’s a craftsman and a chameleon, not tethered to any style; it’s skillful but detached. (In retrospect it also makes “Separated by the Sea” sound like a genre exercise.) What next? Psychedelia? Doo-wop? Funk? No doubt Mr. Brown will have studied well.

JON PARELES

PHIL VASSAR
“Traveling Circus”
(Universal South)

Over and over again on “Traveling Circus” Phil Vassar gets right to the point. A country singer by trade and an affable, comforting conversation partner by disposition, he has opening lines that set the mood, and the mood is often cloudy or outright grim.

“Looks like you’ve had one of those God-awful days,” he sings on “Lemonade.” “John Wayne” begins with a depiction of atomized average-Joeness: “Just another four wheels on the two-lane off to work/Just another lunchbox, punch the clock, with my name across my shirt.” Even “Tequila Town,” which flirts with Jimmy Buffett blitheness, is rooted in hurt: “Hey buddy, what’s your deal?/It’s been a long week, yeah/I know how you feel.”

Mr. Vassar, empathetic and tender, is among the best soft-rock singer-songwriters working. That doesn’t count for much in Nashville, though, where his male peers like to flaunt virility, rural values or an outlaw streak. (All of the above, preferably.)

Mr. Vassar has played those games, but after a decade, he has learned his strengths. “Traveling Circus” is his fifth studio album, the least mindful of the competition and one of his best: it’s enthusiastically unfashionable. He has long preferred piano to guitar, and his songs largely eschew country signifiers in favor of emotional specificity and directness. (How committed to soft rock is Mr. Vassar? See “Where Have All the Pianos Gone,” which sings the praises of Elton John and Billy Joel: “I’ve played ‘Rocket Man’ and ‘Piano Man’ ’til 3 a.m./Now I’m singing my own songs/This is who I am.”)

In spite of Mr. Vassar’s modest voice this album’s great moments are uncommonly poignant, especially on a pair of songs about the ghosts of relationships past. “Everywhere I Go,” written by Mr. Vassar with the great Jeffrey Steele, is affecting, and “A Year From Now,” the album’s highlight, is dignified and beautiful, like a vintage Richard Marx ballad with Southern flourishes:

I like to think that I’m all right

That I’m not empty every night

Without your touch

To fill me up

And I don’t miss your face

Beside me on that pillowcase

But I still do

But I won’t reach for you

A year from now

This is Mr. Vassar at his best, which makes “Bobbi With an I,” a rambunctious, sloppy number about a male friend who cross-dresses on weekends, even dimmer, with Mr. Vassar a blowzy frat boy winking through stereotypes and double entendres. Bobbi is “a quarterback sacker” and, if you’re not careful, a possible contagion too: “You better watch how much you drink/He might look better than you think.” The worst song here, and the most anomalous, it’s one of the few places where Mr. Vassar appears to want to fit in. But he doesn’t, and he should be relieved about it. JON CARAMANICA

THE INBETWEENS
“Quantum Cowboy”
(Layered)

You might hear any number of possible precedents in the music of the Inbetweens, a young but serious guitar-bass-drums trio. “Quantum Cowboy,” the band’s first album to be released on an actual label — Layered Music, out of Madison, Wis. — offhandedly evokes space rock, surf rock, Ethiopian jazz, Ennio Morricone and Tortoise. (There’s some overlap there.) What it might suggest most is an update of the trio led in the early 1990s by the guitarist Bill Frisell, with Kermit Driscoll on bass and Joey Baron on drums.

That’s not a bad thing, given that Mr. Frisell’s trio was one of the great unclassifiable groups of its time, an improvising unit that worked as lovingly with recursive structures and twangy atmospherics as it did with all manner of song form. The Inbetweens are just as open and evasive, but maybe more pointed and in a bit more of a hurry. They sound distinctly of their moment and entirely sure-footed in their identity as a band.

The group’s three members — Mike Gamble on guitar, Noah Jarrett on bass (and occasional synthesizer) and Conor Elmes on drums — first crossed paths as students at the New England Conservatory of Music and then moved together to Brooklyn from Boston in 2002. Their rapport is strong and seemingly unconscious, one reason that “Maia,” the most yearningly melodic of five original themes on the new album, accumulates gravity as well as detail, finishing so much deeper than it began. Even a twitchy track like “Slap-Happiness” ends up sounding like a complete statement, with a sense of purpose.

Mr. Gamble is the animating force here: it’s his wide-awake, pop-literate style that snaps the Inbetweens into place. (His deft deployment of electronic loops, so often a distraction in settings like these, manages to serve the music too.) But Mr. Elmes is just as vital, with his forward-tumbling momentum, and Mr. Jarrett works squarely at the crossroads of melody, harmony and rhythm. The sturdiness of their collective sound is one bit of good news. Another is that they don’t seem to have exhausted their options.

The Inbetweens will perform in Baltimore on Jan. 20, in Philadelphia on Jan. 21 and at Nublu in the East Village on Jan. 22; myspace.com/theinbetweensmusic.

NATE CHINEN
