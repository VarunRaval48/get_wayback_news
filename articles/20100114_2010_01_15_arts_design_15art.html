Museums

AMERICAN FOLK ART MUSEUM: ‘THOMAS CHAMBERS (1808-1869): AMERICAN MARINE AND LANDSCAPE PAINTER,’ through March 7. With this extraordinary show, a star is born, or rediscovered, or at least given a big museum survey for the first time since 1942. Chambers painted typical 19th-century fare — Hudson River landscapes and marine views of ships, harbors and naval battles — in an entrancing, slightly demonic style that was neither strictly realist nor naïve. His paintings are like chorus lines singing and dancing their hearts out, ever so slightly off key and out of step. They amplify the understanding of the visual appetite by satisfying it so thoroughly. Chambers may have been the first American artist to have grasped — as Cézanne and Seurat would — the implicitly geometric nature of reality. It makes sense that even in 1942 he was called “America’s first modern.” 45 West 53rd Street, Manhattan , (212) 265-1040, folkartmuseum.org. (Roberta Smith)20100114

AMERICAN FOLK ART MUSEUM: ‘APPROACHING ABSTRACTION,’ through Sept. 5. Self-taught artists are noted for producing weird, wacky and otherwise eccentric objects. This exhibition of about 60 works from the museum’s permanent collection proposes that the best of them are also adept abstractionists. Few works are nonpresentational, and most have a vibe of strangeness, but almost every piece is as compelling for its form and technique as for its idiosyncratic imagery. 45 West 53rd Street, Manhattan , (212) 265-1040, folkartmuseum.org. (Ken Johnson)20100114

BROOKLYN MUSEUM: PATRICIA CRONIN: ‘HARRIET HOSMER, LOST AND FOUND,’ through Jan. 24. The contemporary New York artist Patricia Cronin channels the life and art of the 19th-century American sculptor Harriet Hosmer (1830-1908) in a show of watercolor depictions of Hosmer’s works, both those that survived and those no longer traceable. Ms. Cronin has also published the watercolors — including her ghost images of missing works — in a scholarly book that stands as a Hosmer catalogue raisonné. The result is a complex package: a total work of art that is a historical document of the careers of two artists and a homage by one woman to another. 200 Eastern Parkway, at Prospect Park , (718) 638-5000, brooklynmuseum.org. (Holland Cotter)20100114

BROOKLYN MUSEUM: ‘WHO SHOT ROCK & ROLL: A PHOTOGRAPHIC HISTORY, 1955 TO THE PRESENT,’ through Jan. 31. This entertaining exhibition presents more than 175 images of rock stars by 105 photographers. It includes album cover art, candid snapshots, publicity portraits and pictures of live performances. The downside is that only a few photographs rise to the level of fine art, and the general focus is on only the most famous and familiar musicians, from Elvis Presley to Kurt Cobain. There are some nice surprises, though, like a small Polaroid of Courtney Love on which she wrote a rude salutation. 200 Eastern Parkway, at Prospect Park , (718) 638-5000, brooklynmuseum.org. (Johnson)20100114

GUGGENHEIM MUSEUM: ‘ANISH KAPOOR,’ through March 28. This exhibition by the celebrated British sculptor consists of just one work, but it’s a doozy. Viewable only from three partial perspectives, “Memory” is an enormous, egg-shaped volume of Cor-Ten steel, wedged into a boxy side gallery like a dirigible that drifted off course and got stuck. One approach offers only a square window allowing you to behold the impenetrable, seemingly infinite darkness inside. Call it a womb with a view. 1071 Fifth Avenue, at 89th Street , (212) 423-3500, guggenheim.org. (Johnson)20100114

JEWISH MUSEUM: ‘ALIAS MAN RAY: THE ART OF REINVENTION,’ through March 14. This show, a traditional retrospective with an irresistible biographical hook, gives roots to an artist who preferred to be seen as rootless. Born Emmanuel Radnitzky to Jewish émigrés from Russia, Man Ray reinvented himself as one of the luminaries of the interwar avant-garde. Yet his garment-industry background had a material hold on him, visible in references to mannequins, coat hangers and patterns. More than 200 works, including paintings, photographs, films, assemblages and his signature rayographs, link Man Ray’s frequent shifts between mediums to a more profound condition of restlessness. 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92nd Street , (212) 423-3200, thejewishmuseum.org. (Karen Rosenberg)20100114

★ METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART: ‘AMERICAN STORIES: PAINTINGS OF EVERYDAY LIFE, 1765-1915,’ through Jan. 24. This exhibition of 103 figurative paintings and portraits that the museum justifiably calls “iconic” spans the Colonial period to World War I, touching on issues like the settling of the West and the Civil War and its legacy. Many of the works are masterpieces by artists like John Singleton Copley, George Caleb Bingham, William Sidney Mount, Winslow Homer, Thomas Eakins, Mary Cassatt and George Bellows that will in all likelihood not be gathered together anytime soon. Others are little-known gems of genre painting; many more are less gemlike genre works full of plots, props and stilted emotions. The result is refreshingly evenhanded. Art historical viewpoints previously too often considered mutually exclusive — pro-narrative versus pro-form — are synthesized. The paintings benefit tremendously from the mix, as will your understanding of American art and history. A thrilling, illuminating show. (212) 535-7710, metmuseum.org. (Smith)20100114

METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART: ‘MASTERPIECES OF FRENCH ART DECO,’ ongoing. The largest exhibition of works from the Met’s extensive holdings in French Art Deco fills a large gallery to overflowing with nearly 150 examples of art, furniture, wallpaper, decorative objects and the odd painting. Far more aristocratic than its American counterpart, French Deco devoted exquisite craftsmanship, opulent materials and historicizing design to the creation one-of-a-kind luxury items. The desks and cabinets of the incomparable Jacques-Emile Ruhlmann quietly dominate, along with vases by Jacques Dunand and silver by Jean Puiforcat. (212) 535-7710, metmuseum.org. (Smith)20100114

METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART: ‘TIBETAN ARMS AND ARMOR FROM THE PERMANENT COLLECTION,’ through fall 2010.The paradox of militant Buddhism inspired the Metropolitan’s fascinating 2006 exhibition “Warriors of the Himalayas: Rediscovering the Arms and Armor of Tibet.” Now Donald LaRocca, the museum’s arms and armor curator, has created a follow-up installation of 35 objects from the Met’s collection (including 5 acquired in 2007). This time the focus is on defense rather than offense: examples of horse and body armor, dating from the 15th through the 20th centuries, outnumber swords, guns and spears. Most of these objects have seen more ceremonial than military action. All of them equate supreme craftsmanship with defense of the body and Buddhist principles. (212) 535-7710, metmuseum.org. (Rosenberg)20100114

METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART: ‘YOUNG ARCHER,’ continuing. Is it or is it not? “Young Archer,” a life-size marble carving of a naked boy drawing an arrow from his lion’s paw quiver might be the earliest known sculpture by Michelangelo. Scholars disagree. On a 10-year loan from France, the legless, armless boy with a yearning expression is in the Met’s bright and airy Velez Blanco Patio, where viewers may decide for themselves if it is the real thing. (212) 535-7710, metmuseum.org. (Johnson)20100114

★ METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART: ‘CINNABAR: THE CHINESE ART OF CARVED LACQUER,’ through Feb. 21. The exhibition’s approximately 50 carved lacquer dishes, platters, boxes, vases and other domestic objects — including a spectacular folding screen — are as engrossing for their formal beauty as for their amazing craftsmanship. The pigment cinnabar gives them a rich, dark red color, and intricate patterns, ornamental animals and pastoral vignettes carved and incised into their lustrous surfaces make every object a pleasure to behold. (212) 535-7710, metmuseum.org. (Johnson)20100114

EL MUSEO DEL BARRIO: ‘NEXUS NEW YORK: LATIN/AMERICAN ARTISTS IN THE MODERN METROPOLIS,’ through Feb. 28. A bright light at the top of Museum Mile, the city’s premier Latino art center has reopened after a makeover with a dense, episodic and absorbing visual history of the Latino art presence in New York in the first half of the 20th century. As much an archival display as a standard art show, it focuses on luminous, culture-changing personalities, from the Mexican-born art impresario Marius de Zayas, who teamed up with Alfred Stieglitz to bring the first Picasso show to New York; to Joaquín Torres-García, a Uruguayan who arrived in New York in 1920 and saw proto-Pop Art everywhere; to the Chilean Roberto Matta, a dynamic agent for Old World Surrealism in the New World. In addition, El Museo now has its first permanent-collection gallery, with an installation, “Voces y Visiones,” that reaffirms historical connections to the nearby East Harlem neighborhood. 1230 Fifth Avenue, at 104th Street, East Harlem , (212) 831-7272, elmuseo.org. (Cotter)20100114

MUSEUM OF MODERN ART: ‘GABRIEL OROZCO,’ through March 1. Gabriel Orozco’s delightful 1993 solo debut at the Museum of Modern Art was a barely-there affair of a few photographs, a ball of clay, a hammock and some fresh fruit. His one-man gallery show the next year was sparser still, with four plastic Dannon yogurt cups nailed to the walls of the gallery’s otherwise empty space. Mr. Orozco has since gotten considerable mileage out of this early thinking-light phase. Tapped by museums in Chicago, Paris, Berlin, Amsterdam and Mexico City, where he was raised, he has become an object of admiring critical scrutiny, and a hero to many young artists. Why? The question lingers, only half-answered, after seeing this retrospective, a taut, attractive but oddly conventional-looking survey, heavy on painting and sculpture. (212) 708-9400, moma.org. (Cotter)20100114

MUSEUM OF MODERN ART: ‘PAUL SIETSEMA,’ through Feb. 15. Acute self-consciousness is a salient feature of contemporary art, and few artists are more ingeniously self-reflective than this Los Angelino. Made with much technical finesse, his large drawings reproduce old photographs, newspapers and studio detritus. The grainy, black and white film meditates on apparently ancient artifacts that Mr. Sietsema actually made by hand. What is real, we are invited to wonder, what can we count on in these epistemologically slippery, hyper-mediated times? (212) 708-9400, moma.org. (Johnson)20100114

MUSEUM OF MODERN ART: ‘TIM BURTON,’ through April 26. Given the tremendous visual appeal of Mr. Burton’s movies, you would expect that this expansive retrospective of his nonmoving art would be equally exciting. Alas, it is a let-down. Focused mainly on hundreds of Victorian-Gothic-Expressionist cartoons dating from his teenage years to the present and including paintings, sculptures, photographs and a smattering of short films on flat screens, it is an entertaining show, but there is a sameness to all Mr. Burton’s two- and three-dimensional output that makes for a monotonous viewing experience. (212) 708-9400, moma.org. (Johnson)20100114

MUSEUM OF MODERN ART: ‘MONET’S WATER LILIES,’ through April 12. Devotees of late Monet can rejoice. The Museum of Modern Art has hung its three “Water Lilies” together for the first time since 2001, along with a relatively recent acquisition and two guests. That’s six waterlily paintings, all completed in the last dozen years before the artist’s death in 1926. In addition, these canvases are isolated from the onward march of Modernism according to MoMA, and their separateness emphasizes their meditative, immersive quality. But it also confirms their radical achievement. While bringing Monet’s Impressionism to a grand conclusion, they also open the path to Abstract Expressionism. It can still stop you in your tracks. (212) 708-9400, moma.org. (Smith)20100114

NEUE GALERIE: ‘FROM KLIMT TO KLEE: MASTERWORKS FROM THE SERGE SABARSKY COLLECTION,’ through Feb. 15. Serge Sabarsky, the art dealer and collector who founded the Neue Galerie with Ronald S. Lauder, never lived to see its 2001 opening. Most visitors to the museum know Mr. Sabarsky (1912-96) primarily through some of the Klimts and Schieles that rotate through the galleries and from the popular first-floor cafe that bears his name. They’ll be better acquainted with him after a visit to this exhibition, the first at the Neue Galerie to focus exclusively on Mr. Sabarsky’s acquisitions. The high point for many viewers will be the small second-floor room of drawings by Klimt, Schiele, Alfred Kubin and Oskar Kokoschka. 1048 Fifth Avenue, at 86th Street , (212) 628-6200, neuegalerie.org. (Rosenberg)20100114

★ NEW MUSEUM: ‘URS FISCHER: MARGUERITE DE PONTY,’ through Feb. 7. Naming his first American museum show after a fictional character from Mallarmé, the young Swiss sculptor Urs Fischer spreads himself thin, but brilliantly. Admirers of his gaping holes in walls may be disappointed by the sparseness, quiet and subtlety, but for the most part — despite too much Surrealism 101 — Mr. Fischer’s neo-Dada formalism wins out. Nose-thumbing irreverence colludes with Minimalist space, scale and perception. Take this three-floor show from the top and watch worlds collide: abstraction, found objects and trompe l’oeil; the actual-size, the large and the enlarged; images, reflections and volumes. Each floor is a kind of implosion, although it only becomes obvious on the lowest one. The perpetual twilight in the middle may be the most memorable moment. 235 Bowery, at Prince Street, Lower East Side , (212) 219-1222, newmuseum.org. (Smith)20100114

NOGUCHI MUSEUM: ‘NOGUCHI REINSTALLED,’ through Oct. 24, 2010. In anticipation of the museum’s 25th anniversary in 2010, this show presents about 200 of Isamu Noguchi’s works from the 1920s to the 1980s. They include sculptures in stone, wood, metal and clay; maquettes and models for parks and playgrounds; and props for Martha Graham dance productions. Though unfailingly suave, Noguchi’s polished abstraction no longer has the urgency it once had, but the museum as a whole is a wonderful time capsule, and the sculpture garden he designed is utterly blissful. Noguchi Museum, 9-01 33rd Road, at Vernon Boulevard, Long Island City, Queens , (718) 204-7088, noguchi.org. (Johnson)20100114

★ THE ONASSIS CULTURAL CENTER: ‘THE ORIGINS OF EL GRECO: ICON PAINTING IN VENETIAN CRETE,’ through Feb. 27. With its ensemble of some 40 religious images, including 6 by Domenikos Theotokopoulos, better known as El Greco, this glowing show is essentially a triple-layered visual essay. It roughs out the complex texture of a specific, cosmopolitan, East-meets-West island culture. It tells the story of a great artist who emerged from that culture, lived outside it and lastingly belonged to it. And it begins to suggest the variety and charisma of icons as a genre: spiritually instrumental objects that lie outside and beyond ordinary definitions of art. 645 Fifth Avenue, near 52nd Street , (212) 486-4448, onassisusa.org. (Cotter)20100114

P.S. 1 CONTEMPORARY ART CENTER: ‘LEANDRO ERLICH: SWIMMING POOL,’ through March 1. From P.S. 1’s first-floor hallway you step onto a wooden deck that surrounds a small, fully equipped swimming pool. Glowing lights built into the aqua walls light up watery depths below the gently churning surface. A flight of stairs leads to a lower level and a magical surprise. 22-25 Jackson Avenue, at 46th Avenue, Long Island City, Queens , (718) 784-2084, ps1.org. (Johnson)20100114

★ RUBIN MUSEUM OF ART: ‘VICTORIOUS ONES: JAIN IMAGES OF PERFECTION,’ through Feb. 15. Although Mohandas Gandhi learned from Jainism, an Indian religion that takes nonviolence as its first principle, in the West we still know little about it or its art. But now there are two exquisite shows of Jain art in New York. The smaller, at the Metropolitan Museum of Art through March 28, is made up mostly of tiny, jewel-like manuscript paintings depicting the lives of savior-heroes called jinas. The Rubin Museum has, in addition to large-scale paintings, three dozen splendid sculptures of the same figures. Together the exhibitions give an in-depth view of a great art tradition and a complex faith. 150 West 17th Street, Chelsea , (212) 620-5000, rmanyc.org. (Cotter)20100114

RUBIN MUSEUM OF ART: ‘VISIONS OF THE COSMOS: FROM THE MILKY OCEAN TO AN EVOLVING UNIVERSE,’ through May 10. This exhibition sets up a thought-provoking compare-contrast exercise between Western and Eastern ways of picturing the universe. On one side, myths about the cosmos are vividly imagined in Hindu, Buddhist and Jainist paintings and sculptures. On the other side, the West’s progressively more empirically based beliefs are represented mainly by illustrations in antique books, with astronomical photographs made by high-tech machines like the Hubble Space Telescope representing current scientific knowledge. 150 West 17th Street, Chelsea , (212) 620-5000, rmanyc.org. (Johnson)20100114

SMITHSONIAN NATIONAL MUSEUM OF THE AMERICAN INDIAN: ‘A SONG FOR THE HORSE NATION,’ through July 7, 2011. Including saddles, riding blankets, clothing and beaded bags and much more, this exhibition brings to light a fascinating and ultimately sad chapter in American history: the hundred-year period during which horses were central to the lives of the Plains Indians. A highly efficient form of transportation, horses enabled Navaho, Crow, Comanchee, Pawnee and others to expand their territories and flourish more than they otherwise would have. It also enabled them to make war more effectively, though ultimately not effectively enough. George Gustav Heye Center, 1 Bowling Green, Lower Manhattan , (212) 514-3700, americanindian.si.edu. (Johnson)20100114

STUDIO MUSEUM IN HARLEM: ‘30 SECONDS OFF AN INCH,’ through March 14. This exhibition of 60 disparate artworks by 42 contemporary artists from several generations might be called revisionist. It takes a refreshingly visual, even formal approach to art that is usually plumbed for political and social meanings based largely on the artist’s identity. Its curator, Naomi Beckwith, wants to emphasize the meanings — social and otherwise — embedded in the actual objects on view. Her success is mixed, partly because she doesn’t spend much time explaining the experiential nature of her selections in the catalog, and also because she includes too many familiar, established artists. But she makes many excellent choices, introduces some promising young talent and displays a visual intelligence that is often rare in her chosen profession. 144 West 125th Street , (212) 864-4500, studiomuseum.org. (Smith)20100114

★ WHITNEY MUSEUM OF AMERICAN ART: ‘OMER FAST: NOSTALGIA,’ through Feb. 14. Omer Fast — who has a powerful, discomforting solo show at the Whitney Museum and another at Postmasters gallery in Chelsea (through Feb. 13) — was born in Jerusalem in 1972, lives in Berlin and studied art in New York City. In past videos and films he has tackled subjects like the Holocaust, interracial machismo and the war in Iraq. In his new work he adds immigration and terrorism to the list, working a tense terrain that encompasses references to documentary, comedy and action films, and that leaves his own moral position ambiguous. He’s really good, and getting better. (212) 570-3600, whitney.org. (Cotter)20100114

WHITNEY MUSEUM OF AMERICAN ART : ‘RONI HORN A K A RONI HORN,’ through Jan. 24. Two floors is one floor too many for this late-phase Post Minimalist. She is best represented here by some exquisite cast-glass sculptures, photographs of Iceland’s extraordinary topography and several large drawings that combine aspects of carving, cartography and quilting. The catalog is even more elaborately stage-managed; the main take-away from the combination is that Ms. Horn’s ostensibly androgynous (but actually butch) persona is her most engaging creation and that self-portraiture and language are her truest modes of expression. (212) 570-3600, whitney.org. (Smith)20100114

Galleries: Uptown

★ FERNELL FRANCO, through Jan. 23. “Amarrados [Bound]” is the title of this somber exhibition of photographs by the Colombian artist Fernell Franco (1942-2006), who, as a self-taught artist and photojournalist, focused sustained attention, through half-abstract images, on the atmosphere of repression and violence that has plagued Latin America. The black-and-white photographs in this show, organized by Maria Iovino, were taken between 1978 and 1994 in streets and markets in Colombia, Ecuador and Peru. Each is a moody still life of a cloth-wrapped, rope-tied bundle, the kind made by street vendors to protect merchandise, though some of the forms suggest shrouded human bodies. This is Mr. Franco’s first solo museum exhibition in the United States; it is long overdue. Americas Society, 680 Park Avenue, at 68th Street , (212) 249-8950, americas-society.org. (Cotter)20100114

‘MEDIEVAL ART AND THE CONTEMPORARY SPIRIT,’ through Feb. 4. This outstanding selection of manuscripts, paintings and sculpture brought from London by the estimable dealer Sam Fogg includes several wonders. To name but two: a marble relief profile portrait of a Renaissance noblewoman by Mino da Fiesole and a large, miraculously intact (binding and all) choir book from 15th-century Lombardy known as the Olivetan Gradual. Its illuminations and foliate initials are surpassed only by a second kind of less usual initials enmeshed in extraordinarily fine, inventive calligraphic pen work. Richard L. Feigen, 34 East 69th Street , (212) 628-0700, rlfeigen.com. (Smith)20100114

‘KAZUO SHIRAGA: SIX DECADES,’ through Jan. 23. This is the first solo exhibition in this country devoted to Kazuo Shiraga (1924-2008), a Japanese artist and member of the radical Gutai group. Early on he achieved notoriety with performance pieces in which he wrestled with sizable amounts of mud. (There’s a grainy video of one in the show.) Then he settled down to making abstractions entirely with his feet. A novelty act, you might say, except that the works are extraordinarily powerful, rich in color and shocking in texture. They exude an intense, not quite human, energy. McCaffrey Fine Art, 23 East 67th Street , (212) 988-2200, mccaffreyfineart.com. (Smith)20100114

Galleries: Chelsea

RICHARD HAWKINS, through Jan. 23. This diverse and diverting solo exhibition includes messy collages revolving around pictures of pretty young men; slapdash abstract paintings; and garishly colorful, cartoonish paintings of young men in erotically suggestive situations. Most engaging are collages representing ancient Greek and Roman sculptures of athletic men accompanied by comical commentaries hand-printed in white on black that sound as if they had been written by a lascivious, homosexual art historian. Greene Naftali, 508 West 26th Street, Chelsea , (212) 463-7770, greenenaftaligallery.com. (Johnson)20100114

★ ‘THE PERPETUAL DIALOGUE,’ through Jan. 23. Those mammoth Chelsea spaces make sense after all, as galleries continue to do the work of museums. That’s what Andrea Rosen is doing in this group show of 31 artists organized by no fewer than 14 curators, including Ms. Rosen, who is celebrating her 20th year in business. With its large helping of young talent, the show recalls the New Museum’s “Generational,” though it’s really “intergenerational,” thanks to the inclusion of artists who have been on the job for some time, even as they remain unsung. Apart from art world networking — the perpetual dialogue of the title — there’s no theme, so just dive in. There’s a lot to think about here. Andrea Rosen Gallery, 525 West 24th Street , (212) 627-6000, andrearosengallery.com. (Cotter)20100114

ROBERT WILLIAMS: ‘CONCEPTUAL REALISM, IN THE SERVICE OF THE HYPOTHETICAL,’ through Jan. 23. A hero of the “Lowbrow” art movement, a genre of Pop-Surrealist illustration, Mr. Williams will be in this year’s Whitney Biennial. Though not a great painter, he has a trippy imagination. In “The Persuasion of Right Angles,” a square spiral made from sections of landscape fills the background, while in the foreground a sexy woman in a miniskirt interacts with midget humanoids with squared spirals for heads. Scary. Tony Shafrazi, 544 West 26th Street , (212) 274-9300, tonyshafrazigallery.com. (Johnson)20100114

ARTUR ZMIJEWSKI, through Jan. 30. This internationally acclaimed Polish video maker practices a form of relational aesthetics in which ordinary people are invited to participate in artificially constructed situations in order to reveal deep social problems. To say that he works with a heavy hand is to understate the case vastly. A weird, apparently unconscious sadism animates many of his pieces. In the most appalling one, he talks an elderly Holocaust survivor into having the faded number on his arm tattooed again. X Initiative, 548 West 22nd Street , x-initiative.org. (Johnson)20100114

Last Chance

BROOKLYN MUSEUM: JAMES TISSOT: ‘THE LIFE OF CHRIST’; closes on Sunday. Over the last two decades of his life, the successful French society painter Tissot (1836-1902) devoted himself to illustrating the Bible, beginning with a suite of 350 watercolors representing the life of Jesus. Sensationally popular in its day, the series ended up in the possession of the Brooklyn Museum. Now 124 of these small, finely painted gouaches have been resurrected for this surprisingly intriguing and moving exhibition. 200 Eastern Parkway, at Prospect Park , (718) 638-5000, brooklynmuseum.org. (Johnson)20100114

JAPAN SOCIETY: ‘SERIZAWA: MASTER OF JAPANESE TEXTILE DESIGN’; closes on Sunday. Serizawa Keisuke (1895-1984), one of the most prominent Japanese artisans of the 20th century, transformed paper and fabric with intricate, hand-cut stencils. His art updated, and some might say elevated, centuries-old crafts with allusions to Korean folk art, Chinese calligraphy, Aesop’s Fables, Indian miniatures and Western midcentury Modernism. This handsomely installed show includes an early artist’s-book adaptation of “Don Quixote,” as well as curtains, screens and kimonos from Serizawa’s mature and productive postwar years. 333 East 47th Street , (212) 832-1155, japansociety.org. (Karen Rosenberg)20100114

‘CLARA TICE: NAUGHTY OR NICE?’; closes on Friday. Tice (1888-1973), an overlooked artist and early feminist, frequented Dada circles in New York and supported herself making illustrations for “little” magazines, as well as for Vanity Fair and newspapers (including The New York Times). Her gracefully spare, often risqué images of women rev up Aubrey Beardsley; her portraits of men’s faces composed almost entirely of female nudes in different positions riff on Arcimboldo. Meredith Ward Fine Art, 44 East 74th Street , (212) 744-7306, meredithwardfineart.com. (Smith)20100114

WHITNEY MUSEUM OF AMERICAN ART: ‘GEORGIA O’KEEFFE: ABSTRACTION’; closes on Sunday. The focus of this surprisingly surprising show is the first two decades of O’Keeffe’s long career, beginning in 1915 when she made her first abstract drawings, which were also among the first fully abstract images by any American artist, and continuing into the 1930s, when she was spending increasing amounts of time alone in New Mexico. In between comes a body of bold, graphically punchy and sensuous painting that you tend to experience whole and fast, as you do a strong scent or a touch. (212) 570-3600, whitney.org. (Cotter)20100114
