Japanese Exports Rise for First Time Since Crisis Began

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By REUTERS
Published: January 26, 2010

TOKYO — Japan’s exports rose more than expected in December from a year earlier, the first increase since the global financial crisis blew up in late 2008 and a sign that solid growth in Asia could help Japan avoid another recession.

But export strength has been slow in spilling over to households and the yen’s rise to a one-month high this week could rekindle worries about the outlook, adding pressure on the Bank of Japan to maintain its ultra-easy monetary policy.

“Japan’s economic growth is likely to accelerate towards the end of this year, reducing the possibility of the BOJ taking further easing measures,” said Kyohei Morita, chief Japan economist at Barclays Capital. “But given that the yen is likely to appreciate again on such factors as China’s possible tightening, the BOJ may be prompted to take steps to respond to market volatility.”

Exports rose 12.1 percent in December from a year earlier, more than a median market forecast of a 7.6 percent rise and the first annual increase in 15 months, Ministry of Finance trade data showed on Wednesday.

The increase was predominantly due to solid demand for Japanese goods in Asia. Shipments to the region, which account for more than half of total Japanese exports, rose 31.2 percent from a year earlier.

Exports to China rose 42.8 percent on the back of strong growth in the country, which grew 10.7 percent from a year earlier in the three months to December.

But shipments to the United States fell 7.6 percent, a sign that improvements in the global economy remained uneven.

“Most countries around the world are seeing their economies recover but improvements in advanced economies remain fragile,” said Takeshi Minami, chief economist at Norinchukin Research Institute.

“We can’t rely too much on strong growth in those countries, so Japanese exports will continue to focus on shipments to Asia.”

While strong Asian growth is expected to underpin Japan’s economy, renewed rises in the yen, which hit a 14-year high against the dollar in November, could thwart the country’s fragile recovery.

The yen’s surge in November, coupled with government pressure for action to beat deflation, prompted the central bank to put in place a new financing operation in December.
