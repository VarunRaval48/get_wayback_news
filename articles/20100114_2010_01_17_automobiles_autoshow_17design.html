Detroit Auto Show: Finally, Some Electricity in the Air (January 17, 2010)

Enlarge This Image

The dash of the Audi A8. More Photos >

IT wasn’t a particular look or style that dominated the conversations of designers at the 2010 Detroit auto show, and it wasn’t a buzzword like retro or kinetic or flame-surfaced. It was the shadow of a number: 35.5.

That number is not the median age of the United States population, which is 36.7 years and rising, according to the government. Rather, 35.5 is miles per gallon, the required fleet average fuel economy that automakers must achieve in 2016 under the Obama administration’s rules.

But like the age number, the 35.5 figure is culturally, socially and economically important. It is a mandate that not just engineers, but designers as well, must deal with. The number reflects concerns over consumption and carbon.

It is also a number, the automakers’ displays suggest, that has spurred designers to look around the world for new ideas, looks, lessons, materials and philosophies. Designers, especially those at Detroit-based companies, are being pressed to join the global community and move away from creating cars tailored to a domestic market whose roads, distances and fuel prices kept engines and cars large. No longer can the United States be the odd country out.

The shift began long ago on other continents. European and Asian governments dealt with economy-crippling fuel crises by taxing engine displacement and fuel itself, affecting the looks and shapes of cars. Detroit shifted only grudgingly, first with the visual trick called downsizing, then with the rebadging of imports.

The input from abroad was evident all around Cobo Center, where two days of press introductions began last Monday. Ford, the most successful of the Detroit automakers at the moment, emphasized smaller cars. It brought the new Focus from Europe to join the Fiesta, a subcompact that will be in showrooms later this year.

General Motors introduced the Aveo RS concept, a look at the design future of the smallest car Chevrolet now offers, and it showed the Spark, an even smaller car it will sell. Both were based heavily on input from G.M.’s Korean studios.

The first Aveo, said Dave Lyon, G.M.’s executive director for North American interior and global cross-brand design, was actually an Italian design for a Korean client — specifically, the work of Giorgetto Giugiaro’s Italdesign firm for Daewoo — that was modified by Detroit. It was something of an afterthought.

Now, in the new global era of 35.5, the Aveo is being rethought and redesigned. The next Aveo, to be built in Michigan, must sell well for G.M. to meet the future fleet mileage requirements.

The Aveo and the Spark are hatchbacks, a shape that American buyers, in the era of 35.5, will get used to. The world’s automakers can offer lessons in building and selling the hatchbacks and compact cars that the auto industry categorizes as premium small, or small cars with the amenities of large ones.

“That is well established in Europe,” said Stefan Sielaff, head of design at Audi. The A1 small car his studios are planning now, he said, will accommodate many of the high tech elements of the A8 luxury sedan — a car with considerable dashboard real estate devoted to its telematics display screen — that won the Eyes on Design award here for the best new production car.

Another lesson of the Detroit show: in the era of 35.5, design innovation will happen inside the vehicle at least as much as outside. There were abundant signs that for all the innovations in automotive technology, the automobile is losing its once-central status in the world of tech.

The car, as a vehicle of mythology and dream, avatar of personal expression and symbol of social mobility, now has a rival in digital communication technology. Younger buyers, designers frequently note, now invest more of their personalities in their electronics than in their cars.

A natural response for car designers is to add more electronics to the car itself. But more subtle lessons for car designers may lie in the cultures of the digital world: in video games and interfaces.

The Focus exterior makes small look agile — it is a witty and subtle design. But Ford’s group vice president for design, J Mays, brushed over all that. He insisted on showing off the new Focus literally from inside the car. Seated in the passenger seat, he said that “the next big thing is H.M.I.,” using the abbreviation for human-machine interface.

Touch or voice, that interface to automotive electronics has become a focus of controversy as well as innovation and commercial competition.

It was significant that for Ford, the Detroit show figured almost as an afterthought to its appearance at the Consumer Electronics Show in Las Vegas this month, where Alan Mulally, the company’s chief executive, delivered the opening keynote address.
