Boldly, Boise State Moves the Question

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By PETE THAMEL
Published: January 5, 2010

GLENDALE, Ariz. — The fake punt known as Riddler will join Boise State’s historical repertory of trickery, alongside Statue Left and Circus from three years ago. Broncos Coach Chris Petersen’s daring call on fourth-and-9 from his own 33 in the Fiesta Bowl on Monday night was destined to be described as either fearless or insane.

Three years ago, Ian Johnson’s 2-point conversion on a trick play defeated Oklahoma in overtime.

With the score tied and less than 10 minutes remaining, Petersen’s daring won out and led to the winning touchdown. Now, that bold move could help further shift the evolving landscape of college football.

No. 6 Boise State’s 17-10 victory over third-ranked Texas Christian was a controversial milepost in the Bowl Championship Series era. The two teams were undefeated, leading to complaints that the matchup hurt the cause of teams outside college football’s power conferences who hope to someday crash the national title game.

“How can you beat the elite in college football if you can’t play them?” the argument went.

But after the confetti cleared at the Fiesta Bowl, it became obvious that Boise State did not have to beat an elite team from a B.C.S. conference to join the upper crust of college football next season. The legacy of Riddler could provide an answer to some of the questions surrounding the sport’s controversial system for picking a national champion.

Can a team from outside a B.C.S. Conference play for the national title? The answer should be a resounding yes. Boise State will return 21 of its 22 starters next season. (That does not include Kyle Brotzman, the placekicker and punter who moonlighted as a quarterback on Riddler, hitting tight end Kyle Efaw for 29 yards.)

The Broncos are scheduled to play Virginia Tech, a likely top-10 team, at a neutral site (perhaps on Labor Day weekend) and host perennially tough Oregon State.

The schedule puts Boise State in position, if it goes undefeated, to play for a spot in the B.C.S. title game.

“I think that slowly the nation is getting away from Boise being a cute little team on the blue turf and being a legitimate national team,” Western Athletic Conference Commissioner Karl Benson said.

Boise State will also have a defining star, something it lacked nationally when running back Ian Johnson left last year. The sophomore quarterback Kellen Moore did not play great against T.C.U., hitting 23 of 39 passes for 211 yards with no touchdowns or interceptions. But his season totals were astounding. He led the Broncos to only the second 14-0 season in modern college football history with 39 touchdown passes and 3 interceptions, and completed a little less than 65 percent of his passes.

Perception in college football is driven by star power, and Boise State now has it. It also has a gift that keeps on giving: the Fiesta Bowl victory over Oklahoma three years ago. That game allowed Boise State’s coaches to cast their recruiting net wider and attract a higher caliber of athlete. (From Johnson’s sophomore year to his senior year, he was relegated to a backup tailback and special-teams contributor because Boise State simply had better players.)

Did T.C.U. and Boise State prove they belong with the elite?

The power and speed of T.C.U.’s defense, and the caliber of skill on Boise State’s offense, are making believers out of more and more college football observers. Could Boise State beat Texas or Alabama? We’ll never know. Could the Broncos play with them? No question.

After the Fiesta Bowl, T.C.U. Coach Gary Patterson thanked the news media for changing their perception of the Horned Frogs program.

“At some point in time, you decided to change your mind and give us a chance as a program, give Boise a chance,” he said. He added, “What we tried to do was prove you right.”

And that notion leads to one of the bigger questions that will loom over college football this summer.

Where will Boise State (and to a lesser extent T.C.U.) be ranked next season?

After Boise State beat Oklahoma in the Fiesta Bowl in 2007, it lost the starting quarterback Jared Zabransky and a number of other stars. The Broncos finished that year undefeated and ranked No. 5 in the Associated Press poll and No. 6 in the coaches’ poll. Where did they start the next season? They were No. 24 in the A.P. poll and No. 23 in the coaches’ preseason poll.

“Like it or not, the preseason polls determine who plays in the national championship,” Benson said.

It would be shocking if Boise State were outside the top five at the outset of next season. The Broncos have gone 26-1 the past two seasons and return nearly everyone on the two-deep depth chart. Petersen did not want to pontificate on the importance of the polls, other than to say the Broncos will have a “decent” ranking. (T.C.U. should receive top-10 consideration because it returns all but six starters and plays Oregon State on a neutral field and also has what are usually tough conference games against Brigham Young and Utah.)

“Certainly, with the schedule we’re going to play next year,” Petersen said, “I think we could be ranked low, and if we beat the teams on our schedule, we would jump dramatically.”

The most significant jump Boise State can now make is from top-10 team to legitimate national championship contender. Because of Riddler, a once near-impossible leap is a legitimate question. Boise State, with its most daring of moves, has already made college football in 2010 a lot more interesting.
