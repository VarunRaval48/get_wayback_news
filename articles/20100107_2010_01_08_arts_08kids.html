FOR CHILDREN

‘ADVENTURES WITH DORA AND DIEGO’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) The young Latino stars of the Nickelodeon animated television series “Dora the Explorer” and “Go, Diego, Go!” have taken their expeditions into new territory at the Children’s Museum of Manhattan, whose exhibition has separate Dora and Diego environments, including Diego’s Animal Rescue Center and Dora’s Fiesta. Intended for ages 2 to 6, the interactive, bilingual displays are designed to teach language, science and music skills, as well as facts about Latin America. From 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; until 8 p.m. this Friday; Tisch Building, 212 West 83rd Street , (212) 721-1223, cmom.org; free with museum admission: $10; $7 for 65+; free for under 1 and members; free to all this Friday from 5 to 8 p.m.20100107

‘ARCHAEOLOGY ZONE: DISCOVERING TREASURES FROM PLAYGROUNDS TO PALACES’ (Friday, Sunday through Tuesday, and Thursday) Children will step into the shoes of an explorer like Indiana Jones in this permanent exhibition at the Jewish Museum, but the adventures will be purely scholarly. Still, there is plenty of excitement in analyzing artifacts like a jar handle, a clay jug and a bangle and figuring out the purpose behind ancient pieces like a Greek helmet and a bull-shaped vessel. This interactive show also includes a recreated room from the Ottoman period (about 1900), where young archaeologists can dress in costume. Hours: 11 a.m. to 5:45 p.m., except Fridays, open until 4 p.m., and Thursdays, open until 8 p.m.; 1109 Fifth Avenue, at 92nd Street. , (212) 423-3200, thejewishmuseum.org; free with admission: $12; $10 for 65+; $7.50 for students; free for under 12 and members.20100107

ASYLUM STREET SPANKERS (Sunday) They don’t have what you’d call a child-friendly name, but these musicians weren’t always a family-oriented group. The band used to be devoted to country blues, and it’s part of the crossover music crowd that the B.Y.O.K. (Bring Your Own Kid) series at 92YTriBeCa cultivates. (It’s made Nirvana’s “Sliver” into a kids’ song.) And no one will be spanked. At 11 a.m., 200 Hudson Street, at Canal Street , (212) 601-1000, 92Ytribeca.org/byok; $15.20100107

‘AUNT LEAF’ (Friday through Sunday) The Hudson River Valley has inspired its share of folklore — just ask Ichabod Crane. Now comes this tale by Barbara Wiechmann, which draws on the region’s stories and on two poems by Mary Oliver. Part of StartHere: Innovative Theater for Young People, an interdisciplinary series at Here Arts Center, the show, for ages 9 and older, features a longtime haunting, a girl who won’t speak and a mysterious old woman. (Through Jan. 24.) Previews on Friday at 7 p.m., Saturday at 2 and 7 p.m. and Sunday at 2 p.m.; opens on Sunday at 7 p.m.; 145 Avenue of the Americas, at Dominick Street, South Village , (212) 352-3101, here.org; $15; $10 for children.20100107

‘BABY LOVES SALSA’ (Saturday) It’s playing cats and dogs. To introduce children 2 to 6 to salsa, the singer-songwriter Jose Conde came up with a delightful conceit: a band of gatitos y perritos (kittens and puppies) from the streets of New York. His own group plays the bilingual tunes, like “Bailar Rapido” (“Dance Fast”), which everyone probably will. At noon (doors open at 11 a.m.), Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (866) 468-7619, ticketweb.com; $10; $30 for a family pack of four tickets.20100107

BROADWAY PLAYHOUSE: JULE STYNE (Sunday) “So who’s Jule Styne?” children may ask. They’ll learn at this presentation, the season opener in Merkin Concert Hall’s Broadway Playhouse series, which introduces great musicals to the next generation of theatergoers. Hosted by Sean Hartley and directed toward ages 4 through 11, the series includes singalongs, interactive games and a mini-staging of a work by the featured composer. At 11 a.m., 129 West 67th Street, Manhattan , (212) 501-3330, kaufman-center.org; $20.20100107

‘DEAR EDWINA’ (Friday through Sunday) What if Ann Landers had been a child — and she could sing? Then she might have been Edwina Spoonapple, the young heroine of this charming musical by Zina Goldrich and Marcy Heisler (“Junie B. Jones”). Edwina is an advice columnist who delivers her counsel tunefully, and this show, being revived after a run that ended in April, details her quest to be in a festival. (Through Feb. 15.) Friday at 7 p.m.; Saturday and Sunday at 11 a.m. and 1 and 4 p.m.; DR2 Theater, 103 East 15th Street, Manhattan , (212) 239-6200, dearedwina.com; $39.20100107

‘DR. NEBULA’S SUPER COLD ADVENTURE’ (Saturday) Dr. Nebula isn’t an absent-minded professor; he’s an absent professor. He’s always off on some scientific mission, but that doesn’t prevent him from having his own show at the American Museum of Natural History. This fictional doctor’s most recent inquiry concerns something topical: very cold temperatures. His assistant, Scooter, will lead children 4 and older on an exploration of how such an atmosphere affects matter. At 2 p.m., 79th Street and Central Park West , (212) 769-5100, amnh.org; $10; $8 for ages 3 to 12.20100107

‘FERDINAND THE BULL’ (Saturday) No one needs to worry about letting this bull loose in a theater; he’s that gentle fellow who likes to smell the flowers. In this production, based on Munro Leaf’s book, Ferdinand and his fellow players are brought to life by the troupe Hudson Vagabond Puppets, whose members use masks, mechanized figures and, of course, bunraku-style puppets. Encouraging all to be peaceful like Ferdinand, the show will conclude with a conflict-resolution demonstration involving the audience. At 11 a.m. and 1 p.m., Queens Theater in the Park, Flushing Meadows-Corona Park , (718) 760-0064, queenstheatre.org; $12.20100107

‘FOCUS’ (Friday, and Monday through Thursday) See the world of New York through a young artist’s lens. This exhibition, organized by Stephanie Pereira, features almost 40 photographs, as well as documentary videos and animations, by students from elementary through high school. Working with the nonprofit Magic Box Productions, which brings arts programs to schools, the young creators have dealt with both portraiture and the urban landscape. (Through mid-Feb. 2010.) From 8:30 a.m. to 5 p.m.; group tours available; the Gallery at 180 Maiden Lane, between Front and South Streets, Lower Manhattan , (914) 630-0256, magicboxproductions.org; free.20100107

FREE KIDS NIGHT AT GILBERT & SULLIVAN (Tuesday) Children will have the opportunity not only to follow the fates of love-struck officers, sailors and young beauties onstage, but also to follow them around backstage, thanks to the New York Gilbert & Sullivan Players. The company will award one free child’s ticket (for ages 12 and under) for every full-price adult ticket bought for its production of “H.M.S. Pinafore.” After the show young audience members can each choose a favorite costumed actor to lead them on a behind-the-scenes tour. (Children’s tickets are also half-price at all other performances, and weekend matinees through Jan. 17 feature Family Overtures, preshow introductions to the music and plots.) At 7 p.m., City Center, 131 West 55th Street, Manhattan , (212) 581-1212, nycitycenter.org; $40 to $96; free for children.20100107

‘THE FROG PRINCE’ (Saturday and Sunday) This isn’t the guy in the Disney film, but an onstage sufferer from Galli’s Fairytale Theater who would also like to give up the amphibian life for his true calling as royalty. Written and directed by Johannes Galli, the company founder, the show, for ages 3 and older, enlists the audience in helping the frog to break the spell. (Through Jan. 17.) At 3 p.m., 38 West 38th Street, third floor, Manhattan , (212) 810-6485, gallitheaterny.com; $20; $15 for children.20100107

‘HOUSE ABOUT IT!’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) This exhibition gives new meaning to playing house. Created by the Staten Island Children’s Museum, the show, whose centerpiece is a child-size house under construction, introduces young visitors to all aspects of the building trades. Activity stations include an architect’s office, a space to examine foundation materials and a workbench to practice sawing and drilling. The house offers opportunities for tiling and shingling, and a dollhouse lets young decorators ponder fabrics and furnishings. Hours: Friday, and Monday through Thursday, noon to 5 p.m.; Saturday and Sunday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; 1000 Richmond Terrace, Livingston , (718) 273-2060, statenislandkids.org; free with admission: $6; free for members and children under 1; free for grandparents on Wednesdays.20100107

HOUSE OF KIDS (Saturday) House of Kids is also the house of rock — and pop, reggae, country and other musical styles. In this new concert series from Live Nation, top children’s musicians perform at the Fillmore New York at Irving Plaza, along with other entertainers, including members of the Big Apple Circus. This event features Gustafer Yellowgold, the friendly space alien created by the illustrator and award-winning composer Morgan Taylor, who has returned for a multimedia show about his hero. At 10 a.m., 17 Irving Place, at 15th Street, Manhattan, , (212) 777-6800, irvingplaza.com; houseofkidsnyc.com; $20; free for under 2.20100107

KWANZAA SONGS WITH THE MUSEUM FOR AFRICAN ART (Friday) The holiday season is fast becoming a memory, but there’s still at least one more celebration to be had. This program, the first in Family Fridays, a three-month series from the Central Park Conservancy, is sponsored with the Museum for African Art. Michael Wimberly, a Harlem percussionist and composer, will lead children in a singalong and an exploration of Kwanzaa. From 2 to 4 p.m., Charles A. Dana Discovery Center, inside the park, at 110th Street between Fifth and Lenox Avenues, (212) 860-1370, centralparknyc.org; free.20100107

‘LISTEN TO THE WIND’ (Friday through Sunday) The title is the advice a village elder gave to Greg Mortenson when he was wondering how he could repay the people of Korphe, Pakistan, who had nursed him back to health after a disastrous attempt to climb K2 in 1993. He ultimately built them a school, a project that led to a wide-ranging program to further education in Pakistan and Afghanistan. This exhibition at the Children’s Museum of Manhattan features Susan Roth’s collage illustrations for Mr. Mortenson’s picture book about his journey, “Listen to the Wind: The Story of Dr. Greg and Three Cups of Tea,” along with photographs and Pakistani objects. This Friday, 10 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Saturday and Sunday, 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; the Tisch Building, 212 West 83rd Street , (212) 721-1223, cmom.org; free with museum admission: $10; $7 for 65+; free for members and under 1; free to all on Friday from 5 to 8 p.m.20100107

‘LITTLE RED RIDING HOOD’/’LA CAPERUCITA ROJA’ (Saturday and Sunday) The wolf suffers an identity crisis in this puppet musical version by Manuel Antonio Morán, presented in an easy-to- understand mix of Spanish and English by Teatro SEA, which specializes in Latino theater for children. It seems that Little Red can’t be persuaded that the wolf is really a wolf, and soon he begins to doubt himself too (with good reason: what wolf talks and sings?). (Through Jan. 23.) At 3 p.m., Los Kabayitos Puppet Theater, 107 Suffolk Street, near Rivington Street, Lower East Side , (212) 529-1545, sea-online.info; $15; $12.50 for 2 to 12; free for under 2.20100107

‘THE LITTLE MERMAID’ (Saturday and Sunday) Disney brought this Hans Christian Andersen tale to life with a large cast, but Manhattan Children’s Theater’s new production does it with only two actors. Here called Flotsam and Jetsam, they portray all the characters in the story of a mermaid who longs to join her human love. Adapted by Mike Kenny, the show unfolds against a set of underwater ruins. (Through Feb. 21.) At noon and 2 p.m., 52 White Street, near Church Street, TriBeCa , (212) 352-3101, mctny.org; theatermania.com; $20; $50 for priority tickets, which include front row seats, a signed program and a preshow cast meeting (must be bought by the preceding Wednesday).20100107

‘MAGIC AND ZONE: THE COMEDY MAGIC OF RICH MAROTTA AND TWILA ZONE’ (Wednesday) Some magic shows have all the deadly seriousness of Harry Potter navigating a really bad day at Hogwarts. But apparently not this one. Rich Marotta, a stand-up comedian, and his wife, Tamara Marotta, a k a the pink-haired and often bumbling Twila Zone, profess to combine laughter with illusions, creating a performance for ages 8 and older that offers magic and sends it up at the same time. At 8 p.m., Bleecker Street Theater, 45 Bleecker Street, at Lafayette Street, East Village , (212) 239-6200, magicandzone.com; $45; single tickets at $31.50 each or family tickets at a rate of $25 each or $100 for up to four tickets are available at the box office only.20100107

‘ONCE AND FOR ALL WE’RE GONNA TELL YOU WHO WE ARE SO SHUT UP AND LISTEN’ (Friday through Sunday, and Thursday) If you go to the Duke on 42nd Street and shut up and listen, you’ll find out just what this is: a play performed — and partly written by — a group of teenagers. Receiving its New York debut through the New Victory Theater as part of the Under the Radar Festival, this Belgian-British production for adolescent audiences may not be polite, but it is prizewinning: it earned three awards at the 2008 Edinburgh Festival Fringe. Friday and Thursday at 7:30 p.m.; Saturday at 2 and 7:30 p.m.; Sunday at 3 p.m.; Duke on 42nd Street Theater, 229 West 42nd Street, Manhattan , (646) 223-3010, newvictory.org; $25; $17.50 for New Victory members.20100107

‘PINKALICIOUS, THE MUSICAL’ (Saturday and Sunday) It’s time to think pink again. This show for ages 4 to 12, from Vital Children’s Theater, is in yet another revival, so if you missed it earlier you can catch it now at the Bleecker Street Theater. Elizabeth and Victoria Kann have adapted their children’s book, in which the pink-obsessed title character finds out that sometimes being in the pink can be too much of a good thing. (John Gregor wrote the score and some of the lyrics.) (Through February.) At 1 p.m., 45 Bleecker Street, at Lafayette Street, East Village , (212) 579-0528, pinkaliciousthemusical.com; $29.50; $25 for groups of 10 or more.20100107

PRESERVATION DETECTIVES’ FAMILY PROGRAM (Sunday) The Eldridge Street Synagogue, the landmark 1887 building where many Jewish immigrants worshiped, offers its “Preservation Detectives” tours every Sunday, when children 5 to 10 can view artifacts, investigate neighborhood lore and play roles of the past. This month the theme is “Treasure Trove,” and young participants will learn what Sabbath customs and recipes Eastern European immigrants brought with them, prepare braided challah and make their own mezuzot (decorative cases for prayers, which are affixed to doorposts). At 1 p.m., 12 Eldridge Street, between Canal and Division Streets, Lower East Side , (212) 219-0302, eldridgestreet.org; $15 a family.20100107

‘SESAME STREET: A CELEBRATION OF 40 YEARS OF LIFE ON THE STREET’ (Friday through Thursday) Sesame Street may not have an official location, but a good chunk of it is in Brooklyn for the next couple of months. This exhibition at the Brooklyn Public Library, commemorating the television show’s 40th anniversary, includes objects like animation cels, scripts, sheet music, props and even a few Muppets. (Through Feb. 21.) Hours: Friday and Monday, 9 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Saturday, 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Sunday, 1 to 5 p.m.; Tuesday through Thursday, 9 a.m. to 9 p.m.; Central Library, Grand Army Plaza, at Flatbush Avenue and Eastern Parkway , (718) 230-2100, brooklynpubliclibrary.org; free.20100107

‘SNOOPY AS THE WORLD WAR I FLYING ACE’ (Friday through Sunday, and Tuesday through Thursday) Most of the military life commemorated at the Intrepid Sea, Air & Space Museum is real, but for the next few months the institution will honor some imaginary battles: those waged by the “Peanuts” character Snoopy as a flying ace inside his Sopwith Camel (a k a his doghouse). Organized by the Charles M. Schulz Museum and Research Center, this exhibition features digital prints of Schulz’s original drawings. The museum will also display “Peanuts”-inspired artwork created by sailors aboard the U.S.S. Intrepid (the aircraft carrier that is now the museum’s home), who used the images to personalize their work and living spaces. (Through April 30.) From 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., Pier 86, 46th Street and 12th Avenue, Clinton , (212) 245-0072, intrepidmuseum.org; free with admission: $22; $18 for 65+ and college students; $17 for ages 3 through 17 and veterans; free for active military, retired military and children under 3.20100107

TAP DANCE OPEN HOUSES (Wednesday) You don’t need tap shoes or tap experience, just a willingness to try a brief class at these events from the American Tap Dance Foundation. Young hoofers from preschoolers through teenagers are invited to kick up their heels and sample the fun. (On selected days through Jan. 16.) From 2:30 to 4:30 p.m., American Tap Dance Center, 154 Christopher Street, near Greenwich Street, No. 2B, West Village , (646) 230-9564, atdf.org; free.20100107

TRIBECA CINEMAS KIDS CLUB LAUREL GRAEBER 49294 TRIBECA CINEMAS KIDS CLUB (Saturday) The creative minds behind the Tribeca Film Festival have created this series for children, showing the first and third Saturdays of every month. This program, “Oui! Oui!: Shorts From France,” presents the work of the director Albert Lamorisse. The morning screening, for ages 3 to 7, is Lamorisse’s nearly wordless classic “The Red Balloon,” which will be followed by a show with a balloon artist. The afternoon program, for older children, will feature not just “The Red Balloon” but also “White Mane,” about a seemingly untamable horse. Afterward the author and educator Bill Gordh will lead a storytelling workshop. At 10:30 a.m. and 1:30 p.m., Tribeca Cinemas, 54 Varick Street, at Laight Street , (212) 941-2001, tribecafilm.com/kidsclub; $10; $7 for under 14; $18 and $12 to attend both screenings. LAUREL GRAEBER 20100107
