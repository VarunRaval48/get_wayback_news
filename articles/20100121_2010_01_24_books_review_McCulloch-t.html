By ALISON McCULLOCH
Published: January 21, 2010

THE LISTENER
By Shira Nayman.
Scribner, $27.

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image

ONLY IN NEW YORK: Photographs From Look Magazine.
By Donald Albrecht and Thomas Mellins.
208 pp. Museum of the City of New York/Monacelli. $50.

New York City held a special allure for Look. The zestful, unabashedly voyeuristic pictures here, from the 1940s and ’50s, revel in diverse humanity. Above, Frank Bauman’s 1948 portrait of a knife thrower.

The madness of war, even after war is over, envelops everyone in this well-paced psychological thriller by the author of “Awake in the Dark,” a book of short stories about the Holocaust. Set just after World War II in a mental hospital north of New York, “The Listener” enters into the world of the shell-shocked, the battle-fatigued, the sufferer of post-traumatic stress disorder — call it what you will. Staff members and patients are afflicted in their own ways, particularly the hospital’s director, Henry Harrison, and his “most memorable” patient, a former soldier named Bertram Reiner who has a mysterious past that Harrison is determined to uncover. As Harrison, who himself endured the horrors of the Great War, grows increasingly obsessed with the enigma that is Reiner, his marriage begins to crumble. Lonely and on edge, he rediscovers an old opium habit and takes up with a nurse who is battling her own demons. Through these tormented characters, Nayman bares some of the era’s ghastly incoherencies, from America’s postwar embrace of Nazi scientists to the role of psychiatrists like Harrison, “called upon to fix people up mentally so they could be given another chance at getting killed.”

VICTOIRE
My Mother’s Mother.
By Maryse Condé.
Translated by Richard Philcox.
Atria International, $19.99.

The novelist Maryse Condé never knew her maternal grandmother, an illiterate mulatto born in the 1870s on the Caribbean island of Marie-Galante, a dependency of Guadeloupe, who died long before Condé was born. But ever since she was a child and fell under the spell of a photo that stood on the family piano, Condé wanted to know her and resolved that one day she would. The result is this beguiling mix of fact and fiction, officially billed as a novel but also as a work of “reconstitution.” At its heart, it is a story about mothers and daughters — first Victoire and her only child, Jeanne; then Jeanne and her own daughter, Maryse. Jeanne escaped the kind of poverty and travail that marked Victoire’s life — and that is so well described here — to become a teacher and join the elite group of Guadeloupe’s Grands Nègres, but she paid a price for it. “Jeanne was so keen to allay suspicion,” Condé writes, “and convince everyone that she was not ashamed of her mother, whom she paradoxically loved more than anything in the world, that she became tyrannical and cruel.”

LA’S ORCHESTRA SAVES THE WORLD
By Alexander McCall Smith.
Pantheon, $23.95.

Admirable women are a staple of McCall Smith’s novels, from Precious Ramotswe and her Botswanan detective agency to the Scottish philosopher Isabel Dalhousie. La, short for Lavender, differs from these series heroines in being (so far) a standalone character and one who shines with a lower wattage. Educated at Cambridge University in the early 1930s, when few women were, La is pressed into marriage by a determined suitor, despite her own doubts and the advice of her tutor. “It breaks my heart,” she tells La, “to see all these intelligent girls come to us and then leave, more or less promised to some man. . . . What a criminal waste.” Before long, La’s husband has run off to France for another woman, and war with Germany is imminent. La decides to quit her city life and moves to the country, where she does her part by tending hens, growing vegetables and starting an orchestra in the village. A wounded Polish airman whom La befriends becomes a mildly unsettling influence, but not enough to shatter the book’s easy calm. Surely only a writer like McCall Smith could make the war years feel like such a better, simpler time.

THE GLASS ROOM
By Simon Mawer.
Other Press, paper, $14.95.

It’s the late 1920s, and on a hillside in Czechoslovakia’s second city an architectural marvel is rising from the mud: a futuristic house composed of clean lines, steel pillars and glass that represents the dreams of the wealthy and cultured Liesel and Viktor Landauer. But next door in Germany something else altogether is starting to rise, and it’s clear from the outset that these worlds are about to collide. Viktor is Jewish and not only sees what is coming but has the means to do something about it, salting money away in Switzerland before eventually fleeing with both his family and his mistress. Among those left behind are Liesel’s best friend, Hana; Hana’s Jewish husband; and, of course, the house. Modeled on the real Villa Tugendhat, in the Czech city of Brno, the Landauer Haus — or, rather, its vast glass room — is a main character in this novel (a finalist for the 2009 Man Booker Prize) and a rather unsympathetic one at that. With its “cool, calm rationality,” its ability to liberate people “from the strictures and conventions of the ordinary,” its aura of detachment (“plain, balanced, perfect; and indifferent”), even its standing witness to a rape, “silent and reserved, observing impassively,” the haughty glass room strikes an irritating note in an otherwise satisfying tale.

MAP OF THE INVISIBLE WORLD
By Tash Aw.
Spiegel & Grau, $25.

A medley of ethnicities, nationalities and backgrounds come together in this story, set primarily in 1960s Indonesia, about an orphaned boy searching for his foster father, a Dutch-Indonesian artist who has vanished after a group of soldiers removed him from his island home. The boy, 16-year-old Adam, makes his way to Jakarta and enlists the help of an old friend of his father’s, an American teacher who, like almost everyone in this restless novel, isn’t entirely sure where she belongs: “I was conceived on one continent, born on another and raised on four — five if you count Australia,” she explains to an Indonesian colleague. Added to the mix are a disaffected Australian journalist, a cynical United States Embassy operative, an Indonesian subversive and Adam’s long-lost brother, Johan, now living with a wealthy family in Malaysia. Tash Aw juggles these varied lives with type font changes, flashbacks and parallel narratives, giving the story a disjointed feel rather like that of the turbulent society in which these disparate characters must find their way. “This country is falling apart,” the Australian journalist decides, “not just at the edges but at its core.”

Alison McCulloch, a former editor at the Book Review, divides her time between Ireland and New Zealand.
