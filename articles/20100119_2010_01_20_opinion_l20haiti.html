A Plea for the Stricken in Haiti

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
Published: January 19, 2010

To the Editor:

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Tim Gough

I was part of a group of students from the University of Miami who were in Haiti during this terrible tragedy, helping to establish community centers for youths in the slum of Cité Soleil in Port-au-Prince.

We were in downtown Port-au-Prince when the earthquake struck. Fortunately, we were safe inside a car and did not get hit by any buildings. I have never seen such destruction and chaos. There were people crying and running, and it was heartbreaking to hear the voices of trapped people pleading for help. At the same time, it was inspiring to see how the Haitian people helped one another.

After the earthquake, we had to walk several miles to reach higher ground for safety. Many people would stop us and ask if we could help them find some of their loved ones, but with so much chaos it was almost impossible.

The majority of Haitians we have worked to help in Cité Soleil are unaccounted for. More attention needs to be paid to the suffering of those in marginalized communities like Martissant and Cité Soleil where aid has been slow to arrive and most of the people trapped will probably die.

Kristina Rosales
Miami, Jan. 19, 2010

To the Editor:

Re “3 Presidents Unite in Campaign to Raise Money for Earthquake Recovery in Haiti” (news article, Jan. 17):

It is fantastic to know that two former presidents, Bill Clinton and George W. Bush, are uniting with President Obama in the United States’ effort to help relieve the devastated country of Haiti.

Mr. Clinton, Mr. Bush and President Obama have found a cause that has united them in their goals. Mr. Clinton and Mr. Bush both have powerful connections with people who will be able to help them with the United States’ effort to support Haiti; this is exactly what the people of the United States need to see.

Despite the fact that these three men have differences, they can still come under a single banner and work toward the greater good. We should be able to follow their example; let us set our differences aside and work toward the betterment of society and help those who are truly in need.

Uriel Rafael
Los Angeles, Jan. 17, 2010

•

To the Editor:

Re “A Helping Hand for Haiti,” by Bill Clinton and George W. Bush (Op-Ed, Jan. 17):

Both in your paper and on TV Sunday morning the two former presidents asked for donations to help Haiti. They mentioned building a modern economy there, creating jobs and confronting AIDS and yet were silent about the main problems, poverty and overpopulation.

After Katrina, the poor homeless of New Orleans were airlifted throughout the United States to begin new lives. Many Haitians had tried to make new lives in the United States and until the other day were deported back to poverty and hopelessness.

The planes that fly in with aid could fly out with refugees, but the poor, tired and hungry homeless Haitians are left behind. If an earthquake toppled Toronto, we would welcome our Canadian neighbors, wouldn’t we?

Jean Kathleen Ranallo
Englewood, Fla., Jan. 17, 2010

•

To the Editor:

Re “Boon for Red Cross via Texting” (front page, Jan. 19):

Could not the banks show that they share the priorities of everyday Americans — and a true sense of wanting to show appreciation for what their government has done to save them — by donating an amount equivalent to even 10 percent of this year’s bonuses to Haitian relief?

A wire transfer to the Red Cross would be their version of texting to 90999.

Alison Jones
Union, Conn., Jan. 19, 2010

•

To the Editor:

Re “Looting Flares Where Order Breaks Down” (front page, Jan. 17):

The action of securing essential food and water during the life-and-death chaos of a natural disaster is not “looting”; it is human survival. Finding a bag of rice in a collapsed building is reason for celebration, not condemnation.

Please use caution when describing these acts of basic human determination for survival and maybe even a moment of hope.

Barbara Goldfeder
Nahcotta, Wash., Jan. 17, 2010

•

To the Editor:

As a cultural anthropologist with experience in Haiti, I disagree with David Brooks’s analysis of why Haiti has experienced so much devastation from last week’s earthquake (“The Underlying Tragedy,” column, Jan. 15). Mr. Brooks blames Haiti’s culture.

Students of Haiti deeply admire the industriousness, courage, generosity, intelligence, resilience and creativity that characterize Haiti’s poor majority and the vibrant culture maintained under the most difficult conditions.
