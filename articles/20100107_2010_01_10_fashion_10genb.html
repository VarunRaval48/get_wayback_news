Generation B

Seeking Greener Pastures

Rob Bennett for The New York Times

Joseph Cesarelli, who is unemployed, volunteers to take care of three horses on Long Island. “Gives me a reason to wake up,” he said.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints
By MICHAEL WINERIP
Published: January 7, 2010

PERRI CESARELLI gets up at 5:30 a.m., takes the dog out, eats breakfast, showers, dresses, then, at 7:05 walks the 12 minutes to the train station in Little Neck, N.Y., leaving herself a five-minute buffer before catching the 7:22 into Manhattan.

She’s 46, and for a dozen years has worked for a hedge fund, where she’s now the office manager. It’s a job that pays a solid middle-class wage ($66,500 a year) and that she enjoys (“something new every day”). If she’s lucky she’s on the 6:24 home, and walks in the door by 7:15.

She expects her husband, Joseph, to have dinner ready — he’s unemployed and spends his days at home. But too often he hasn’t prepared anything, and though she’s not proud of this, she gets angry, or almost as bad, it breaks her heart.

Her husband feels terrible about it, too. He’s 56, has been looking for work for over two years and says some days he just can’t bear it. “Some days I’m so depressed I don’t want to do anything,” he said. “The hard part about this — I’ve always considered myself a survivor, always been able to find work I liked.”

His bachelor’s degree is in biology, and he spent six years as a research assistant at Columbia University and the American Museum of Natural History. He loves theater, and in the ’80s and ’90s worked on Broadway in the wardrobe union. He taught himself computer skills and became a programmer/analyst for Prudential Securities, making $56,000 a year by 2001.

But after 9/11, Prudential downsized, moving its back offices from lower Manhattan to New Jersey. In 2002, Mr. Cesarelli was let go. He had several jobs after that, but none that he much liked and none that paid close to his old salary. And since the recession, he’s had none at all. “I was a person who could pick himself up and do anything, but I’m not so sure I’m good at that anymore,” he said. “Sometimes I get so anxious and depressed.”

It has strained the marriage. “Two years without him working, it’s a shock,” said Ms. Cesarelli. “Both my parents worked and both Joseph’s parents. I never thought I’d be a sole breadwinner. Not what I thought life would be. Certainly not what Joseph wanted. Not what he was working towards.”

“Not what I wanted,” Mr. Cesarelli said.

It’s been called a mancession, but the misery is spread pretty evenly. Mr. Cesarelli is mired in a bad demographic. In December, 11 percent of men were out of work versus 8.8 percent of women, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics. In Mr. Cesarelli’s field, computers, 100,000 jobs have been lost in the last two years. And once a middle-aged person loses a job, it takes longer to be rehired. In December, jobless workers over 45 were unemployed an average of 33.4 weeks, versus 26.8 weeks for younger workers.

“Baby boomers grew up believing if we worked hard and applied ourselves, it would work out,” Mr. Cesarelli said. “Well, it didn’t. Everything collapsed around me.”

Mr. Cesarelli met his wife in 1993 at the Esquire court reporting company, where they occupied nearby cubicles. She had just ended a bad marriage; he was in the process of splitting up with his longtime live-in girlfriend.

Ms.: “After a divorce, I wasn’t sure how serious I wanted to be.”

Mr.: “We never went out on dates. We’d take long walks.”

Ms.: “Midtown to the Battery.”

Mr.: “Talk and talk. They say successful relationships are based on communication. Ever meet somebody that you just liked — just so many things about her?”

She asked him out.

Ms.: “He was shy. It was a couple of months before he kissed me. What I liked about Joseph — he was a nice guy, he was polite, good looking, older. Most guys I met my age were insufferable.” They enjoyed movies, theater, books, concerts. He belonged to a medieval re-enactment group and took lessons to learn to ride horses, care for them and joust at the fairs.

In 1997, they celebrated their wedding at a re-enactment fair. In 1999, they bought a small three-bedroom house in Queens for $225,000, taking out a 5.8-percent 30-year mortgage. “We never did that adjustable rate thing,” Ms. Cesarelli said. “We’re very careful people.”
