Vivien Schweitzer

The dynamic young violinist HILARY HAHN, left, came into the spotlight in her late teenage years with her debut album of solo Bach repertory. Bach has since been an important part of her performing and recording career. To celebrate the release of “Bach: Violin and Voice” — featuring Ms. Hahn, the soprano Christine Schäfer and the baritone Matthias Goerne in arias and duets with solo violin obbligato — Ms. Hahn will be joined by guest musicians for a Bach party on Tuesday at the Greenwich Village club Le Poisson Rouge. The free-form evening of performances and discussion will include Bach’s Chaconne in D minor for solo violin. At 7:30 p.m., 158 Bleecker Street, near Thompson Street, (212) 505-3474, lepoissonrouge.com; $20.

The young French countertenor PHILIPPE JAROUSSKY, who sings with a pure, sweet-toned voice and fine coloratura technique, has already recorded a substantial catalog of baroque repertory. For his New York recital debut at Carnegie Hall on Thursday, accompanied by Jérôme Ducros, he leaves traditional countertenor territory and offers a program of 19th- and 20th-century French songs, including works by Chausson, Saint-Saëns, Fauré, Hahn and Franck. At 7:30 p.m. on Thursday, Weill Recital Hall, Carnegie Hall, (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $40.

Theater

Jason Zinoman

I saw “ONCE AND FOR ALL WE’RE GONNA TELL YOU WHO WE ARE SO SHUT UP AND LISTEN” at the 2008 Edinburgh Festival Fringe when my wife was pregnant, which was just about the worst possible timing. This frenetic piece of physical theater fearlessly portrays childhood as it really is, especially the parts that parents (and those looking toward parenthood) would rather not see.

Produced by Ontroerend Goed, a Belgian production company that developed the play with 13 floppy-haired and dynamic actors, this new work, featured in the Under the Radar festival this year, isn’t a play so much as a series of carefully choreographed vignettes illustrating that kids on the verge of puberty can seem both younger and frighteningly older than their age, sexually curious, violent and insistent on pushing the limits even when there are none. There are few words, lots of loud music and always the chance that an actor might tumble into the audience. At one point a desperate-looking actress says, “I’m afraid that I’ll do the same as everyone before me, like my parents.” The ferocious energy of this cast hits its zenith in some clever group scenes that appear like playground chaos, but if you look closely, there is more order than there appears. Patterns emerge. Random acts begin to make sense. You see a little bit how these strange creatures approaching adults actually think. Through Jan. 17, the Duke, 229 West 42nd Street, Manhattan; (212) 967-7555, publictheater.org; $25.

Film

Neil Genzlinger

The 1943 film “MISSION TO MOSCOW” opens with a statement from Joseph E. Davies, the United States ambassador to the Soviet Union in the crucial years from 1936 to 1938, that is so ridiculously earnest it’s likely to draw laughs today. “While in Russia, I came to have a very high respect for the integrity and the honesty of the Soviet leaders,” he says. “I respected the honesty of their convictions, and they respected mine. I also came back with a firm conviction that these people were sincerely devoted to world peace.”

The movie, which depicts Davies’s time among Stalin and the rest of those Soviet peace lovers (Walter Huston portrays Davies), is now known to have been a piece of propaganda encouraged by the administration of President Franklin D. Roosevelt in an effort to build support for the Soviets as America’s wartime allies. Howard Koch wrote the extremely pro-Soviet screenplay; later, during the House Un-American Activities Committee investigations, he ended up on the Hollywood blacklist. BAMcinématek screens the film on Tuesday followed by a panel discussion featuring the film writers Glenn Kenny, Ed Hulse, Farran Smith Nehme and Lou Lumenick. 7 p.m., Brooklyn Academy of Music Rose Cinemas, 30 Lafayette Avenue, at Ashland Place, Fort Greene, bam.org; $12.

Television

Neil Genzlinger

Television has a fair number of interesting things to offer this week as it pulls out of the holiday-season doldrums: the well-regarded HBO series “Big Love” begins a new season on Sunday; “The Simpsons” marks its 450th episode Sunday on Fox; VH1 has the Critics’ Choice Movie Awards on Friday; Jesse Ventura wraps up the first season of his wacky “Conspiracy Theory” reality show on TruTV on Wednesday with an episode called “Apocalypse 2012.” But the attention, for several reasons, is going to the season premiere of “AMERICAN IDOL,” Tuesday and Wednesday at 8 on Fox.

Though the show is still a ratings juggernaut, there was much clucking in May when the Season 8 finale drew about three million fewer viewers than the previous year’s finale had. And then there are the off-season headlines. Paula Abdul vacated her judge’s chair. The producers decided to stick with a four-judge format despite complaints that it wasn’t as crisp as the original three-judge setup and hired Ellen DeGeneres to be that fourth judge.

Then, as 2009 ended, reports began to surface that the only judge who matters, the nasty Simon Cowell, was considering leaving the show when his contract expires at the end of this season. Ah, the intrigue! Let the off-key singing begin.

Dance

Roslyn Sulcas

It’s a balletic sort of week in New York City. Sunday is the last day to catch Pacific Northwest Ballet, performing pieces by Twyla Tharp and Benjamin Millepied, at the Joyce Theater before the company heads back to Seattle. But there is a great deal of classical dance to see this week — all of it from the NEW YORK CITY BALLET, which is presenting an unusual and compelling combination of two full-length works and two mixed programs. On Tuesday there is this season’s final performance of Balanchine’s witty and poetic “Midsummer Night’s Dream,” with Sara Mearns and Gonzalo Garcia in the roles of Titania and Oberon. On Wednesday comes Peter Martins’s “Romeo + Juliet” with its pared down, contemporary designs by the Danish artist Per Kirkeby (whose work was given a major exhibition last year at the Tate Modern in London). The wonderful Sterling Hyltin and Robert Fairchild dance the title roles that evening, and on Friday, Erica Pereira, recently promoted to soloist, and Allen Peiffer take those parts. And on Saturday a mixed-bill evening provides more reasons to head to Lincoln Center: namely Daniel Ulbricht, Tyler Angle and Mr. Fairchild as the blithe sailors in Jerome Robbins’s “Fancy Free”; Maria Kowroski and Joaquin De Luz in Balanchine’s “Prodigal Son”; and Ashley Bouder, who made a dazzling debut in this role at just 17, in the Balanchine-Robbins collaboration, “Firebird.” Tuesday and Wednesday at 7:30 p.m., Thursday and Friday at 8 p.m., Saturday at 2 p.m. and 8 p.m., David H. Koch Theater, Lincoln Center, (212) 870-5570, nycballet.com; $20 to $125.

Pop

Jon Pareles

The annual GLOBALFEST is something like a whirlwind tour captained by a cosmopolitan disc jockey, hopping from Guadeloupe to Transylvania, Senegal to the Louisiana bayou, Paris to Siberia. Pragmatically it’s an audition by a dozen world-music acts during the annual convention of the Association of Performing Arts Presenters. But it’s held in a club — the three rooms of Webster Hall, with overlapping sets — with an audience that’s not just there for business.

This year’s lineup includes four United States debuts: by Alif Naaba, a guitar-picking songwriter from Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso; Nguyên Lê’s Saiyuki, playing vigorous pan-Asian jazz; Caravan Palace from France, mixing Hot Club jazz with D.J. electronics; and Namgar, from Siberia, mingling Mongolian elements with hard rock. As that sampling suggests, Globalfest is full of fusions: local music reconfigured in a wired world.

There are traditionalists too. Cara Dillon, from Ireland, is a singer steeped in Celtic music. Cedric Watson, from Lafayette, La., represents zydeco’s younger preservationists; he plays fiddle and button accordion and sings in Cajun French. La Cumbiamba eNeYé, from Colombia, plays coastal music that long ago fused African, Spanish and Andean ingredients into propulsive grooves. François Ladrezo & Alka Omeka, from Guadeloupe, play a plugged-in version of gwo-ka music, which is among the roots of the island’s smoother modern dance beat, zouk. La Excelencia, from New York City, holds on to a more recent tradition: the hard-driving salsa dura of the 1960s and ’70s.

The lineup also includes Nightlosers, a band of Romanians who love both frenetic Gypsy music and the blues; Federico Aubele, an Argentine songwriter who draws on the languorous pulse of down-tempo club music, and Meta and the Cornerstones, a Senegalese-American reggae band. Exotic enough? Sunday at 7 p.m., Webster Hall, 125 East 11th Street, East Village, (212) 545-7536; $40.

Art

Randy Kennedy

In 1917, the year Franz Kafka found out that he had tuberculosis, he wrote a short parable about the mysterious interplay between form and change: “Leopards break into the temple and drink to the dregs what is in the sacrificial pitchers; this is repeated over and over again; finally it can be calculated in advance, and it becomes a part of the ceremony.”

Beginning Sunday at SCULPTURECENTER in Long Island City, Queens, a group show revolving around this haunting Kafkaism — which has had a durable afterlife in epigraph and essay — will present works by a number of up-and-coming European artists like Lucy Skaer, a nominee for the 2009 Turner Prize, and João Maria Gusmão and Pedro Paiva, who represented Portugal at last year’s Venice Biennale.

The works in the show, “Leopards in the Temple,” all share an interest in “protean moments where materials elide, transform and overlay,” as the exhibition’s curator, Fionn Meade, described them. Nina Hoffmann, a young artist from Berlin, presents a single slide projection of a photograph showing two young women transformed into one figurative abstraction as their bodies form a positive-negative silhouette. Latifa Echakhch uses tea glasses from her native Morocco as raw material, smashing them to create a multicolored scatter in which the glasses, by becoming broken and useless, are transformed in a gallery setting into a form instantly recognizable as post-Minimalist art.

“In the show,” Mr. Meade said, “the artists have returned to various formal vocabularies but always with the adjacent relationship of borrowing rather than a wholesale attempt at remaking or revivifying.” Through March 30, 44-19 Purves Street, (718) 361-1750, sculpture-center.org.
