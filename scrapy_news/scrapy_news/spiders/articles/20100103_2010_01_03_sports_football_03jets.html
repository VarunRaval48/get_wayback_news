For Sanchez, Comfort Level Has Increased With Games Importance

Joe Robbins/Getty Images

Jets quarterback Mark Sanchez, who is 7-7 as a rookie starter, has been using techniques from earlier in his career to play better.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By DAVE CALDWELL
Published: January 2, 2010

FLORHAM PARK, N.J. — By the middle of last week, Mark Sanchez, the rookie Jets quarterback, had determined that Sunday’s game against the Cincinnati Bengals would be grander in magnitude than the Rose Bowl. It would be bigger than 10 Rose Bowls, maybe 100, he said.

Until now, the Rose Bowl has served as his benchmark. As a quarterback at Southern California, Sanchez played in the 2009 Rose Bowl and passed for more than 400 yards in a victory over Penn State, certifying his value as a high draft pick.

The Jets traded up and picked him fifth over all, and the first-year coach Rex Ryan has stuck with Sanchez through an inconsistent season for him and the Jets. Now an American Football Conference playoff berth is at stake for the Jets (8-7), but Sanchez has said he will not be overwhelmed by the challenge.

Sanchez seems to understand that no other player at Giants Stadium on Sunday night will have more to lose — or gain — than he does. His record as a starter is 7-7, and he has 20 interceptions versus 12 touchdown passes. But now he is prepared, calmly, to carry the Jets.

“Maybe it came too late, but it was time to really make a jump as a rookie and start to not play as a rookie anymore,” Sanchez said Wednesday. “Have I arrived? Absolutely not. Do I feel totally comfortable and do I know absolutely every single thing that is going on? Am I trying? Absolutely.”

Even last week, Sanchez’s teammates said it would not be necessary for him to feel the need to carry the Jets on Sunday. The Bengals, who are 10-5 and guaranteed a first-round playoff game at home (perhaps against the Jets), may not have anything to play for Sunday.

Jets running back Thomas Jones said of Sanchez: “There is pressure, but there is not. All you can do is go out there and play your best and see what happens. I think that is his approach.”

Sanchez would like to think that he will be better at handling the pressure than he was even as recently as two weeks ago, when he threw three interceptions in a 10-7 loss to Atlanta at Giants Stadium that left Ryan thinking the Jets’ playoff chances were doomed.

Although Indianapolis Coach Jim Caldwell all but handed the Jets a 29-15 victory last Sunday by pulling many of his starters early, Sanchez said he generally played well, rebounding from the bitter loss to Atlanta with an unflashy game in which he made sound decisions.

Ryan said Wednesday: “Really, you go back five weeks. We thought we had to win out, so those were some pressure games as well. I think he knows the formula for our team’s success. He’s confident. He’s ready to go.”

Ryan and Brian Schottenheimer, the Jets’ offensive coordinator, have devised ways to make it easier for Sanchez. Probably their most well-known innovation is a color-coded system in which Sanchez has been asked to play with more freedom, or caution.

After the loss to Atlanta, Schottenheimer said Thursday, he asked Sanchez to think of things that he used earlier in his career to play better — mantras like, “focus in,” “repeat the down and distance,” “throw completions.” At Southern California, he would hold his hands over his eyes and tell himself to focus.

“It’s just more positive reinforcement that’s kept him locked in the game,” Schottenheimer said.

Progress comes incrementally. Against the Colts, Schottenheimer said, Sanchez made an errant call that resulted in Dwight Freeney, the defensive end, steaming in alone on Sanchez. But Sanchez knew to take the sack instead of hurrying a pass that could have been intercepted.

His teammates, Sanchez said, have been steadfast in their support. Jones told him that he would eventually grasp his responsibilities, that the game would slow down. It is a long season, Jones said, and the learning curve would flatten. His teammates would cover his back.

“At some point, he’s going to have to make a play,” linebacker Bart Scott said last week when asked about Sanchez’s role in Sunday’s game. “But he’s not going to have to make every play.”

Bengals Coach Marvin Lewis said in a conference call last week that Sanchez introduced himself at the N.F.L. Scouting Combine in February in Indianapolis even though he knew Lewis was set with another former U.S.C. star, Carson Palmer, as his quarterback.

“To go to New York and have to play with the scrutiny and everybody watching everything he did to become the opening day starter, it’s a very difficult thing,” Lewis said, adding: “Like any young player, anytime things don’t go as well for the team or for you, there’s going to be some rocky points. I think he’s done a nice job fighting out of them.”

At least one more test remains. Sanchez has tried to lighten the load by saying that it would be an accomplishment if he and the Jets made the playoffs, but that it would not be a crushing disappointment if they did not. Lots of rookie quarterbacks do not make the playoffs.

But he also said: “I want to be able to look back at this season as a real success. I think this game has a lot to do with it, not that it’s a failure if things don’t work out. I learned a lot. It’s going to be great, but we really want to get in this postseason, so we’ve just got to make the most of it.”
