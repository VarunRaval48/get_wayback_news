When Politicians Call It Quits

Luke Sharrett/The New York Times

STEPPING ASIDE Senator Christopher Dodd last week said he would not run for re-election, after looking hard at his chance of winning.

By SHERYL GAY STOLBERG
Published: January 9, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

WASHINGTON — There are many ways for a politician to make an exit.

There is the resignation-in-disgrace exit (Richard Nixon) and the sex scandal exit (Larry Craig, Eliot Spitzer) and the kicking-and-screaming exit (Rod Blagojevich, the former Illinois governor who declared, “I will fight, I will fight, I will fight,” as he was about to be impeached). Some politicians (Hillary Clinton, Jon Corzine) have no choice but to make an exit — the voters rejected them. Some make a dramatic exit (Sarah Palin) only to stoke questions about whether they are plotting a comeback.

The nation witnessed another sort of exit — a kind of political letting go — last week when, on a frigid afternoon, Christopher Dodd, the Connecticut Democrat, took a hard look at his electoral prospects, concluded he was in “the toughest political shape of my career” and announced he would not seek re-election after nearly 30 years in the United States Senate. With his 8-year-old daughter on one side of him and his wife cradling their 4-year-old daughter on the other, Mr. Dodd, 65, stood in front of his picturesque white clapboard house and reached for lofty rhetoric.

“In the long sweep of American history,” he said, as an American flag whipped in the bitter winter wind, “there are moments for each elected public official to step aside and let someone else step up. This is my moment to step aside.”

Mr. Dodd’s poignant departure points out the difficulty of leaving a life in politics, especially in Congress, where lawmakers often serve for decades and their identities become deeply entwined with their jobs. Mr. Dodd saw his father, Senator Thomas Dodd, run for re-election — and lose — after being censured by the Senate for financial misconduct. In leaving, the younger Mr. Dodd renewed an old Washington debate over when to call it quits, and how to do it gracefully.

“It’s hard to let go,” said Trent Lott, the Mississippi Republican who served in Congress for 35 years before leaving the Senate at the end of 2007 to become a lobbyist. Mr. Lott made a very calculated decision about it; he was forced out as Republican leader in 2002 after making racially insensitive remarks, but stuck around long enough to make a comeback into the leadership. He said he often worried about hanging on too long: “I’ve literally prayed, ‘Help me, Lord, to know when it’s time to go and not stay until I’m a drooling old man.’ ”

Anyone who has been in Washington for any length of time has watched lawmakers wrestle with such choices — and sometimes suffer indignities as a result. When Senator Arlen Specter of Pennsylvania concluded last year that he could not survive a Republican primary challenge this May, he switched parties and became a Democrat. The 79-year-old senator, once the powerful chairman of the Judiciary Committee, is now reduced to junior status; when the panel held Supreme Court confirmation hearings for Justice Sonia Sotomayor, Mr. Specter was seated with the freshman Senator Al Franken at the far end of the dais.

Strom Thurmond, the South Carolina Republican, served in the Senate until he was 100 — and spent his final years there being ferried about the Capitol in a wheelchair, with his aides whispering in his ear when it came time to vote. Senator Robert Byrd, the West Virginia Democrat, is 92 and increasingly frail, provoking hushed conversations among some fellow Democrats about whether he really should have run that last time around. Yet others find inspiration in Mr. Byrd’s infirmity, especially after he arrived at the Capitol in his wheelchair to cast middle-of-the-night health care votes.

“He can’t do a lot of work, but he’s there against all the physical odds,” said Bob Shrum, the Democratic strategist. “He is in some ways a moral leader in the Senate.”

The most graceful exits, people on both sides of the political aisle agree, are made by those who, like the very best prizefighters, walk away in top form. George Mitchell quit the Senate when he was Democratic leader, went on to become chairman of the Walt Disney Company, and currently is President Obama’s Middle East envoy. Howard Baker quit when he was Republican leader and went on to become ambassador to Japan.

“Politics is a lot like acting or sports,” said Charlie Cook, the editor of the nonpartisan Cook Political Report. “It’s hard to give up the limelight, and the key is to do it at or approaching the peak of your career, and not before you start heading downhill.”

By that standard, Mr. Dodd does not fare very well, in Mr. Cook’s eyes, because he appears to be leaving so he won’t be thrown out by his constituents. But Mr. Cook says another Democratic senator who announced his retirement last week, Byron Dorgan of North Dakota, did make a dignified departure; Mr. Dorgan is widely considered vulnerable to a challenge from Gov. John Hoeven of North Dakota, but the governor has not announced his candidacy, so Mr. Dorgan can say he is leaving on his own terms.

“There was not a perception that Dorgan was dead meat,” Mr. Cook said. “That was exquisite.”

But Mr. Shrum sees it exactly the opposite way, through a more partisan lens. To him, Mr. Dorgan’s is the less graceful exit, because the North Dakota senator is backing down from a fight and, without a Democratic heir apparent, his seat will almost certainly fall into Republican hands. But Mr. Dodd does have an heir apparent, Connecticut’s attorney general, Richard Blumenthal, which gives the party a chance to keep his seat.

“Chris’s exit was an honorable exit,” Mr. Shrum said.

Mr. Dodd is not the first senator to speak of a duty to step aside. John Warner, the Virginia Republican, was 80 when he announced in 2007 that he would retire after five terms. His electoral prospects were good, but he said he wanted to let go of the Senate with his health — and his personal dignity — intact. He still keeps his retirement speech, written in longhand, in his desk drawer, with its quote from another well-known Virginian, Thomas Jefferson:

“There is a fullness of time when men should go, and not occupy too long the ground to which others have the right to advance.”
