Obama Aide Gets Waiver to Investigate Airline Plot

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By PETER BAKER
Published: December 31, 2009

The White House has granted a special ethics waiver to allow President Obama’s top counterterrorism adviser to conduct a review of the intelligence and screening breakdown that preceded the failed Christmas Day bombing attempt on an American passenger plane over Detroit.

John O. Brennan, the deputy national security adviser for homeland security and counterterrorism, is in charge of diagnosing what Mr. Obama has already called the “human and systemic failures” that allowed a Nigerian man with known extremist views and explosives in his underwear to board the flight from Amsterdam to the United States. Mr. Brennan gave a preliminary report to Mr. Obama on Thursday.

Mr. Brennan, who was a longtime C.I.A. officer, needed the waiver because for more than three years before his current post he was chief executive of the Analysis Corporation, an intelligence firm that provides services to the government. Norm Eisen, the White House ethics counsel, wrote on the White House Web site on Wednesday that Mr. Brennan’s past ties to the company, were outweighed by his knowledge of the nation’s intelligence system.

“By virtue of his long experience in government and the private sector, John brings a unique mixture of know-how and understanding to this assignment,” Mr. Eisen wrote.

In the formal waiver, also posted on the White House site, Mr. Eisen noted that Mr. Brennan no longer had any financial ties to the company and that he would recuse himself from any part of the review dealing directly with services provided by his old firm.

Mr. Brennan brings intimate understanding of the watch-list system in question in the review. After the attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, he set up the government’s Terrorist Threat Integration Center, which was meant to coordinate various intelligence agencies, an organization that later evolved into the National Counterterrorism Center. Mr. Brennan served as the counterterrorism center’s interim director when it was created.
