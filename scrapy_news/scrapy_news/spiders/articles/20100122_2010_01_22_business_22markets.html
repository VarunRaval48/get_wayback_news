Obamas Bank Proposal Helps Erase the Months Gains

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JAVIER C. HERNANDEZ
Published: January 21, 2010

Concerns over President Obama’s plan to limit the activities of banks helped drag shares down on Thursday, erasing all the gains the stock market made in the first three weeks of 2010.

Skip to next paragraph

A trader at the New York Stock Exchange on Thursday. Investors were concerned about future earnings in the financial sector.

Shares endured a second day of heavy losses, pushing them back to December levels, after the president’s proposal to curb the size of banks and limit certain investment strategies. That stirred concern about future profits for the financial sector.

As a result, banking stocks tumbled: JPMorgan Chase fell 6.59 percent, Bank of America dropped 6.19 percent, Citigroup fell 5.49 percent, and Morgan Stanley declined 4.21 percent. Goldman Sachs, which reported profit Thursday that far outpaced expectations, was down 4.12 percent.

Shares of material and industrial stocks dropped in light of a weaker-than-expected snapshot of the job market and signs that the recovery for manufacturing would be slow. There were also lingering concerns that China would take steps to slow its economic growth in hopes of pre-empting inflation.

At the close of trading, the Dow Jones industrial average declined 213.27 points, or 2.01 percent, to 10,389.88. The broader Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index dropped 21.56 points, or 1.89 percent, to 1,116.48 and the Nasdaq declined 25.55 points, or 1.12 percent, to 2,265.70.

The Labor Department said initial jobless claims unexpectedly climbed to 482,000 last week — the highest level in two months — 36,000 more than the previous week. A spokesman for the department, however, said the rise “did not reflect economic reasons,” and he pointed to a backlog of filings during the holidays.

On Wall Street, where any hint of a worsening picture for the jobs market is greeted with alarm, shares opened tepidly. With the unemployment rate at 10 percent, investors are concerned by any sign that the pace of firing is increasing.

Adding to investors’ concerns, a barometer of manufacturing activity in the Philadelphia region fell more than expected in January, to 15.2 from 22.5 in December (readings above zero indicate growth). That number signaled the fifth consecutive month of expansion but fell short of forecasts.

Alan M. Gayle, senior investment strategist for RidgeWorth investments, said investors were worried about the costs of Mr. Obama’s bank plan, even though the proposal was still far from enactment.

“Anything that restricts” the size of banks “runs the risk of hurting the international competitive position,” Mr. Gayle said. “The concern is that these rule changes could restrict the long-term growth potential in financials.”

While Mr. Obama’s remarks startled Wall Street, investors were also responding to renewed worries about the strength of economic recovery. China said its economy grew by 10.7 percent in the fourth quarter compared with a year ago, leaving investors wondering whether China would take steps to curb inflation and bank lending, which could slow global growth.

Wall Street is in the middle of earnings season, when companies report quarterly profits. But this time, so many companies are exceeding expectations that stocks are holding steady or even declining in response to good numbers. That has shifted the focus to economic indicators like home construction, the trade deficit and consumer prices.

“There is a growing expectation now that we will not have a robust recovery,” said Joseph V. Battipaglia, a market strategist for Stifel Nicolaus. “The outlook has become more clouded. Any data point can shift a landscape.”

In addition to Goldman Sachs, several companies reported stronger-than-expected profits. EBay said Wednesday after the markets had closed that its earnings had tripled in the quarter, partly because of growth in the PayPal business and its sale of Skype. The company’s stock climbed 8.55 percent on Thursday.

Xerox said profit in the fourth quarter was 25 cents a share, beating expectations of 22 cents a share. The company’s stock rose 4.39 percent.

Xerox said it had achieved its earnings through cost-cutting, which investors have begun to frown on. Traders are expecting companies to show they have established lucrative revenue streams. In the near term, analysts say the focus for investors will be on profits. Several bellwether companies, including General Electric and McDonald’s, will release earnings on Friday.

Even as investors turned away from the stock market, the dollar — traditionally viewed as a safe haven — showed little change. It held steady against most world currencies and remained slightly below $1.41 against the euro.

Crude oil fell $1.66 to settle at $76.08 a barrel.

The Treasury’s 10-year note rose 15/32, to 98 8/32. The yield fell to 3.59 percent, from 3.65 percent late Wednesday.
