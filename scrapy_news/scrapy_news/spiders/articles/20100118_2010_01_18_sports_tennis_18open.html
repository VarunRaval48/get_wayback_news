Australian Open

In Melbourne, Sharapovas Comeback Ends Quickly

Clive Brunskill/Getty Images

Maria Sharapova lost in three sets to Maria Kirilenko in Round 1 of the Australian Open.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JOE DRAPE
Published: January 17, 2010

MELBOURNE, Australia — They are the best tennis players in the world, yet they still get butterflies, and all Kim Clijsters, Andy Roddick, Justine Henin and Rafael Nadal had to do was watch Maria Sharapova slink out of Rod Laver Arena to know why.

Sharapova, a three-time Grand Slam winner, was going home after the first match on center court of the Australian Open. Sharapova was beaten soundly by her fellow Russian Maria Kirilenko, 7-6, 3-6, 6-4. Sharapova had beaten her childhood friend from juniors to the professional tour.

But Kirilenko, No. 58 in the world, waltzed Sharapova, a former No. 1, around the court with an array of shots that made her look like a dancer with two left feet.

“She’s not really the type of player that makes you feel that good,” Sharapova said. “She changes the pace a lot. She just has a little bit of a different type of game. Today she was just able to execute many things really well.”

While the rain spit on the outdoor courts, suspending play and sending patrons seeking shelter, the rest of the world’s top players went about the business of calming their own nerves and sending opponents the way of Sharapova.

Henin’s comeback was easily the most anticipated; this is her second tournament since she announced her comeback in September after being away from the tour for nearly two years. She was 21 when she beat Clijsters here for the 2004 championship. In the 2006 final, however, she retired in the second set against Amélie Mauresmo because of stomach cramps.

In 2007, she was going through a divorce and did not play here. The Australian Open was her last Grand Slam event before she retired in May 2008 as the world’s top-ranked player.

Henin was relieved to turn back her fellow Belgian Kirsten Flipken. She has played down her chances to capture an eighth Grand Slam title, though she made it clear after her match that she was going to enjoy trying.

“It’s been a tough day,” she said. “First round in a Grand Slam is something that I hate, and usually the first week. But this week is going to be special, because I’m going to be pushed very early at a high level. It’s a different situation than in the past.

The men’s defending champion, Rafael Nadal, overcame a sluggish start to bounce the Australian Peter Luczak in three sets. He trailed, 5-3, in the first set, but after being visibly annoyed with himself he fought back to reach the second round in Melbourne.

The two Andys — fifth-seeded Murray, and seventh-seeded Roddick — dispatched their opponents in three sets in dominating fashion. But Roddick acknowledged that he was beating back his own doubts and anxiety as much as he was defeating the Dutchman Thiemo De Bakker.

“You can’t really control the way you feel,” he said, explaining that it’s the start of the season and players have been alone with their thoughts for too long. “It’s a week of buildup, a week of practice, then all of a sudden it seems like it’s here. You know, you don’t really know — you can’t really replicate the match conditions in practice. So it is about getting used to it all over again.

“Then once you kind of find a comfort zone, it’s fine.”

Clijsters defeated the Canadian Valerie Tetreault, 6-0, 6-4, making it clear her late-season comeback after two years away as a wife and mother was anything but a fluke. Suddenly, she says, what was old to her is new again.

“I have the experience from the past, but I haven’t been here for so long,” she said. “It doesn’t feel the same way as it did when I was 15 or 16 because, you know, I’ve been here before. The butterflies are there, but they’re not the same butterflies as, you know, the ones when I was 15 or where I couldn’t sleep the night before a match.”

The rain forced the cancellation of most of the evening matches, but one of the best contests of the day was an all-Chinese showdown between Zheng Jie and Peng Shuai. Zheng, who watched her ranking drop to 35 after leaving the Chinese state sports system to manage her own career, came from a set down to defeat her countrywoman.

While Henin and Nadal were waiting to play their openers, the United States Open winner Juan Martín del Potro took care of Michael Russell in four sets.

Kirilenko’s upset of Sharapova, however, was a career highlight for the young Russian. She came here with not only a depth of unrealized potential but, for the first time in more than year, fresh legs.

“I was confident, and knew I could play a whole match, not just the first set and fall apart,” said Kirilenko, 22, who has played through tender knees.

For Sharapova, it was hardly the kind of return to the upper echelons of women’s tennis that she had envisioned. Sharapova thought she had recovered from shoulder surgery in October 2008, which had her contemplating the end of her career.

She won in Tokyo last year and was a finalist eight other times. Nike believed she was better than ever, and last week extended her endorsement deal for another eight years for what was reported to be $70 million.

Sure, she looked glamorous in the sheer, forest green shift swirled with yellow, which fell over her aqua tennis dress. It was the briefest of fashion statements — 3 hours 22 minutes to be exact. Sharapova landed only 61 percent of her first serves. Her concentration appeared spotty as well, as she drilled 45 winners but alternated them with 66 unforced errors.

She did break Kirilenko’s serve in the final set to pull to 5-4 but could not hold her own. And when her forehand went wide at 40-30, Sharapova headed to the net with her head down and tried to muster a smile for her friend.

Sharapova said her shoulder felt fine, but acknowledged her focus might need some work.

“Well, I’m leaving on Monday of the first week,” she said with a shrug. “So that explains a lot.” she said, sounding as surprised as everyone who witnessed her defeat. Then she brightened, or at least tried to.

“I could be disappointed, or I could just take it as it is and just go back on the court and just keep working,” she said. “I choose Option 2.”
