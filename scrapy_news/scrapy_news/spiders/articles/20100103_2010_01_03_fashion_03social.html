A Taxing Estate

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By PHILIP GALANES
Published: December 31, 2009

My mother passed away recently. In the months before she died, she struck up a serious romance. After we distributed her things, her new boyfriend worked hard to place a beautiful quilt, which my mother had made, with a local museum. I greatly appreciated his effort.

Skip to next paragraph
Christoph Niemann

Now he’s asked if he can take a tax deduction for the donation. He never owned the quilt, so I think the deduction should go to my mother’s estate. Should I give him the deduction, for all his hard work? Anonymous, Denver

Sorry for your loss, Denver daughter.

At first blush, your mother’s beau sounds nervy. But think of it like this: You’ve both suffered a big blow, and chances are his won’t be much acknowledged. It was a relatively new relationship, without the trappings of marriage — or blood, like yours.

I bet one of the reasons he worked so hard to place the quilt with the museum was to keep his connection with your mother alive. Same with the tax deduction, which may be his last chance to formalize their relationship.

Still, you make an excellent point: If he didn’t own the quilt, he can’t take the tax deduction. Why not give him the coverlet retroactively so he can take it? Unless your mom was Richie Rich, the estate probably isn’t looking at a huge tax bill, anyway.

And if you still don’t want to (or if your accountant vetoes the idea), make sure that her beau receives something else of your mother’s. And maybe invite him to your next family get-together.

Midlife Mean Girls

A friend and I started a monthly discussion salon. My co-organizer did not invite my closest friend to the first gathering, which took place at her house. I suspect she feels a bit threatened by my friend, who was perplexed and hurt by her exclusion. This drama rings of the silly social cliques I avoided in high school. May I invite my friend to next month’s gathering, which I am hosting?Anonymous

You know the proverb: Give a woman an invitation to a hoity-toity party, and you entertain her for an evening; teach her to get along with the co-host, and you entertain her for life.

Of course you can invite your friend to the next salon. It’s your house! But as much as I admire your inclusiveness, that won’t solve your problem.

So how about hosting an easy lunch or glasses of Sancerre for the Three Amigos-to-Be before your next meeting? No need to make a federal case of it — or to even mention the tension. If you’re right, and your co-host feels needlessly threatened, the situation should resolve itself quickly.

And remember, they don’t need to turn into bosom buddies; just less like meanies from the cheerleading squad.

We Take No Boarders

My husband and I bought a lovely three-story house. We love to entertain — dinners, cocktail parties and such — but we aren’t big on overnight guests. Occasionally, when we bite the bullet for a college friend or a niece, they insist on bringing guests. How do we minimize the sleepovers and still remain within the confines of hospitality?Anonymous, New York City

And so it follows, as the night unto the day, that if there are no extra beds, there can be no overnight guests.

So take a hint from Candy Spelling — the inventor of the gift-wrapping room — and reprogram all those extra bedrooms. You’ll need an office, of course, and maybe a woodworking shop. A hair-ironing suite? A room for hamster wheels? Presto, you’re plum out of space.

It may also help to keep a list of affordable inns and hotels at the ready, for use at the first sign of overnight hinting. And for mandatory guests, who ask to bring guests of their own, just say, “You know, we’re looking forward to spending time with you on your own.”

Totally reasonable — and fewer beds to change.

Hey Mom, Say Thank You

For the holidays, I made a donation in my parents’ name to an organization that buys books for children in need. When told, my mother asked why I hadn’t made the gift to such-and-so organization instead. Then she said if I should make a donation for them again, I should ask to which charity they would like it made. Did I make a huge faux pas?A. R., San Jose, Calif.

Only if you knew that your parents were big advocates of children in need going bookless.

You did nothing wrong. It would be one thing if you’d chosen a hot-button charity that your parents don’t support — say, Pistols for Preteens. But the cause you picked sounds great, and your mother a little graceless. Most adults are pleased when cash goes to good causes in lieu of senseless gifts. (Bubble bath, anyone?)

Still, she’s your mother, and now you know she takes a different view. So make it easy on yourself next time and ask.
