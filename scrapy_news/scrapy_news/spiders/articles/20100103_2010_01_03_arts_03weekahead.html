Charles Isherwood

The sixth annual UNDER THE RADAR festival gets under way this week at the Public Theater and ancillary sites, waking up a sleepy postholiday period with a wide-ranging choice of challenging, exciting and sometimes just plain cockamamie new works.

This year’s selection includes well-established figures like Richard Maxwell, who presents a new show, “Ads” (a co-production with Performance Space 122) mixing video and live performance; the highly regarded Philadelphia troupe Pig Iron, reprising its ingenious meditation on the mind, “Chekhov Lizardbrain”; and a new production from the frequent collaborators Charles L. Mee and Anne Bogart, who together are re-creating Martha Graham’s “American Document.”

But the festival, produced by Mark Russell, also introduces some intriguing newer names into the mix. “Silver Stars,” written by Sean Millar and produced by the Brokentalkers, is a song cycle derived from the experiences of gay men from Dublin to New York. “The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle” is a multimedia adaptation of the Haruki Murakami novel, directed by Stephen Earnhart. In “Space Panorama” the British director and performer Andrew Dawson recreates a documentary of the Apollo 11 moon landing with a minimum of means: his hands, a black-draped table and a recording of Shostakovich’s 10th symphony. “John Cassavetes’ Husbands,” conceived and directed by Doris Mirescu, is a theatrical adaptation of the 1970 film about middle-age malaise. Performances begin Wednesday, (212) 967-7555, publictheater.org.

Film

Mike Hale

“SWEETGRASS” is a title redolent of cowboys and big skies, and Ilisa Barbash and Lucien Castaing-Taylor’s film has those, but it’s not your typical western. The cowboys are herding sheep, not cattle, and when they get lonely, they can pull out a cellphone and call their families. Also, they’re real. Ms. Barbash and Mr. Castaing-Taylor (a wife-and-husband team) spent three summers in the Absaroka-Beartooth Mountains in Montana filming an annual 200-mile sheep drive. When their documentary was shown at the New York Film Festival, Manohla Dargis wrote in The New York Times that it was “pleasantly unhurried” and made “with great attention to beauty.” Opens Wednesday, Film Forum, 209 West Houston Street, west of Avenue of the Americas, South Village, (212) 727-8110, filmforum.org; $12.

The programmers of the Tribeca Film Festival have chosen 6 films from the 15 on the short list for the best-documentary Oscar for TRIBECA CINEMAS PRESENTS: DOCS ON THE SHORTLIST. On Feb. 2 we’ll find out how many, if any, of their choices earn nominations. The dolphin-slaughter movie “The Cove” seems like a good bet, as does the HBO film “Which Way Home,” about children riding the rails across Central America and Mexico to get to the United States. Less familiar titles include “Living in Emergency,” about Doctors Without Borders volunteers in Africa, and “Soundtrack for a Revolution,” directed by Bill Guttentag and Dan Sturman (“Nanking”), about the role of music in the civil rights movement. Friday and Saturday, Tribeca Cinemas, 54 Varick Street, at Laight Street, (212) 941-2001, tribecafilm.com/docseries; $10.

Dance

Claudia La Rocco

Early January is rerun time in New York, as theaters hope to tantalize the herd of artistic directors at the annual Association of Performing Arts Presenters. If I were a presenter, I would be on my way to the New Museum’s ticket office right now to reserve a seat for TRAJAL HARRELL’S “TWENTY LOOKS OR PARIS IS BURNING AT THE JUDSON CHURCH (S),” which he originally performed at the museum in the fall. The “S” is for small, but this solo is grand in scope and rich in meaning, beginning with Mr. Harrell’s provocative question: “What would have happened in 1963 if someone from the vogueing ball scene in Harlem had come downtown to perform alongside the early postmoderns at Judson Church?”

Would the resulting collision have been a formalist spectacle, replete with cross-cultural echoes? We’ll never know, but Mr. Harrell’s strictly constructed catwalk, which unfolds in Franklin Evan’s elegantly evocative set, proposes some pretty compelling possibilities. If only all seductions were this irresistible and this smart. Thursday and Friday at 7 p.m., the New Museum of Contemporary Art, 235 Bowery, at Prince Street, Lower East Side, (212) 219-1222, newmuseum.org; $18.

Classical

Vivien Schweitzer

Since his inaugural concert in September as music director of the New York Philharmonic, ALAN GILBERT has shown his mettle in repertory from Mozart to Messiaen. Now he turns his attention to Russian works. On Wednesday through Friday at Avery Fisher Hall Mr. Gilbert will conduct Rachmaninoff’s sweeping Symphony No. 2, and on Thursday and Friday he will conduct the powerhouse pianist YEFIM BRONFMAN in Prokofiev’s Piano Concerto No. 2, a work well suited to Mr. Bronfman’s remarkable technique and musicality. Wednesday at 6:45 p.m., Thursday at 7:30 p.m. and Friday at 8 p.m., Lincoln Center, (212) 875-5656, nyphil.org, $37 to $119.

The ranks of genre-busting ensembles and composers are growing, with ever-increasing numbers of musicians experimenting with different aesthetics. On Wednesday at the West Village club Le Poisson Rouge, Gene Pritsker’s chamber band SOUND LIBERATION (based on what he calls “the philosophy of ending the segregation of sound vibration”) offers classically inspired works infused with hip-hop and rock rhythms. The band will be performing selections from its recent release, “Open Up Your Ears and Get Some,” and excerpts from “Money,” Mr. Pritsker’s chamber-hip-hop opera. At 10 p.m., 158 Bleecker Street, near Thompson Street, (212) 505-3474, lepoissonrouge.com; $10.

Pop

Nate Chinen

For New York City jazz fans the WINTER JAZZFEST has become a perennial postholiday splurge and a righteous kick-start to the new year. Timed to coincide with the annual conference of the Association of Performing Arts Presenters — the people who book concert halls and festivals all over the globe — it puts forth a profusion of available talent with an unspoken emphasis on cosmopolitan chic. The artists and bookers (and critics) are all on the job, but all others are free to come and go as they please, drifting among a handful of cozy Greenwich Village clubs well into the morning hours.

If you’ve never seen the Vijay Iyer Trio or Darcy James Argue’s Secret Society — state-of-the-art bands that recently landed on Top 10 lists in The New York Times and elsewhere — here’s your chance. The same goes for the Matt Wilson Quartet, the J. D. Allen Trio and Rudresh Mahanthappa’s Indo-Pak Coalition, all of which released fine albums last year, and all of which sound even better in person.

Maybe you’ve been hearing about some of the bright new contenders like the bassists Linda Oh and Ben Williams (who will feature original music for their groups) or the singers Sachal Vasandani and Gretchen Parlato (who will appear one after the other on Saturday night). Maybe you keep wondering about all the fuss over the pianist Jason Moran and the violinist Jenny Scheinman (who will perform as a duo).

Then again, maybe these names and the dozens of others mean nothing to you yet. Even better. Come hungry and graze, or binge, until you’re done. Friday and Saturday at various locations; $25, or $30 for a two-day pass. For tickets and a full schedule: winterjazzfest.com.

Art

Ken Johnson

At the start of the New Year the art world grinds almost to a halt, but at least three New York galleries are opening exhibitions this week. Choose from Column A: famous photographers; Column B: painter’s painter; or Column C: potentially intriguing group show of conceptual and performance art.

In Chelsea, Cheim & Read will mount two photography exhibitions. “IN THE ABSENCE OF OTHERS” brings together works by DIANE ARBUS that are missing the odd-looking people who were her trademark. “A Castle in Disneyland, Cal. 1962,” in which a fantasy building rises from a moat of still water, will be featured along with other haunting images of unpeopled places. In addition “21ST CENTURY” will present recent works by WILLIAM EGGLESTON, including pictures of unprepossessing subjects like bags of ice in a commercial dispenser. Thursday through Feb. 13, 547 West 25th Street, (212) 242-7727, cheimread.com.

In SoHo, Team Gallery presents “UNTITLED ’10,” an exhibition of new paintings by STANLEY WHITNEY. Though not a household name, the veteran Mr. Whitney is much admired by other painters for his sensuously brushy canvases bearing loosely gridded blocks of rich color. Thursday through Feb. 6, 83 Grand Street, (212) 279-9219, teamgal.com.

In TriBeCa the nonprofit gallery apexart offers “THE INCIDENTAL PERSON,” which, if nothing else, has an excellent title. It will display projects by more than two-dozen artists who ventured out of the usual art world confines to intervene in ordinary life situations. Wednesday through Feb. 20, 291 Church Street, (212) 431-5270, apexart.org.
