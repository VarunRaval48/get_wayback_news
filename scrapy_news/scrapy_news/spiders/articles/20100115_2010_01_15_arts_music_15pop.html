★ BON IVER (Thursday) Bon Iver is the alias of the singer-songwriter Justin Vernon, who famously recorded his debut album, “For Emma, Forever Ago” (Jagjaguwar), in an isolated Wisconsin cabin in deep winter. Unsurprisingly, the resultant folk songs are contemplative and heavy with longing, ideal for a frigid, lonely night. For this unusual show, part of the New York Guitar Festival, Mr. Vernon — along with Chris Rosenau of Collection of Colonies of Bees — will be performing a score for the Charlie Chaplin films “One A.M.” and “Easy Street.” With the guitarist Steve Kimock, who will perform a score to Buster Keaton’s “Cops.” At 8 p.m., Merkin Concert Hall, 129 West 67th Street, Manhattan , (212) 501-3330, kaufman-center.org; sold out. (Amanda Petrusich)20100114

★ CALYPSO ROSE (Friday) A Calypso legend from Tobago, Calypso Rose — who will celebrate her 70th birthday this year — is a charming, addictively energetic performer of native Caribbean music. At 8 p.m., Symphony Space, 2537 Broadway, at 95th Street , (212) 864-5400, symphonyspace.org; $25; $21 for World Music Institute members and children; $18 for students with valid ID. (Petrusich)20100114

JULIAN CASABLANCAS (Friday) The Strokes, once the heirs apparent to New York’s downtown guitar scene, have become more of a breeding ground for solo side projects than a rock band proper. The vocalist Julian Casablancas is the latest Stroke to go rogue: his solo debut, “Phrazes for the Young” (Cult Records/RCA), is a dark, dissonant and oddly hopeful record. At 9 p.m., Terminal 5, 610 West 56th Street,Clinton , (212) 582-6600, terminal5nyc.com; $31 in advance; $35 at the door. (Petrusich)20100114

DAS RACIST (Tuesday) Pop culture memes are frequently built on absurdities, and Das Racist’s “Combination Pizza Hut and Taco Bell” (“I’m at the Pizza Hut, I’m at the Taco Bell, I’m at the combination Pizza Hut and Taco Bell”) has become the stuff of Facebook updates, YouTube parodies and countless remixes (some quite expert). Still, this hip-hop duo isn’t all gimmick; its rhymes are smart and funny and delivered with a lazy loquaciousness that’s instantly appealing. With Freddie Gibbs, Mickey Factz, Tanlines and Gordon Voidwell. At 8 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; $10 in advance; $12 at the door. (Petrusich)20100114

DINOSAUR JR. (Saturday, Sunday and Monday) In the late 1980s Dinosaur Jr. — the guitarist J. Mascis, the bassist Lou Barlow and a drummer named Murph — gave new depth to deafening guitar squall, employing feedback and distortion to epic, glorious ends. The band broke up in 1997, but reunited in 2005; its new album, “Farm” (Jagjaguwar), demonstrates what the trio has always done best: moody, melodic cacophony. Saturday at 9 p.m., Bowery Ballroom, 6 Delancey Street, near the Bowery, Lower East Side, (212) 533-2111, boweryballroom.com; sold out. Sunday at 9 p.m., Music Hall of Williamsburg, 66 North Sixth Street, Brooklyn, (718) 486-5400, musichallofwilliamsburg.com; sold out. Monday at 9 p.m. Brooklyn Bowl, 61 Wythe Avenue, near North 11th Street, Williamsburg, Brooklyn , (718) 963-3369, brooklynbowl.com; $20. (Petrusich)20100114

ECSTATIC SUNSHINE (Monday) This guitar duo from Baltimore switches between shimmering, high-pitched clouds of noise and rough, chunky riffs. With McDonalds and Jason Urick. At 9 p.m., Cake Shop, 152 Ludlow Street, between Stanton and Rivington Streets, Lower East Side , (212) 253-0036, cake-shop.com; $7. (Ben Sisario)20100114

ELEFANT (Saturday) Elefant pours romantic yearning into its spare guitar-bass-drums arrangements. Over obsessive, steady-pulsing bass lines and choppy guitar that hint at the early Cure, Diego Garcia sings about “the smell of the ocean on her skin, the rags of her dress blowing in the wind.” At 9 p.m., Gramercy Theater, 127 East 23rd Street, Manhattan , (212) 777-6800, livenation.com; $18 in advance; $20 at the door. (Jon Pareles)20100114

GANG GANG DANCE (Friday) Like radio waves converging somewhere in outer space, Gang Gang Dance’s music — made from tribal rhythms, pulsating electronics and Bollywoodesque vocals — is a tangle of sounds that seem slightly out of sync, but in time develop hypnotic patterns. At 9 p.m., Music Hall of Williamsburg, 66 North Sixth Street, Brooklyn , (718) 486-5400, musichallofwilliamsburg.com; $15. (Sisario)20100114

HANDSOME FURS (Wednesday) This married duo — Dan Boeckner of the Canadian indie-rock band Wolf Parade and Alexei Perry — aren’t prone to nuzzling or giggling onstage. Their guitar-and-keyboard dirges are heavy, dark and desperate, although their new album, “Face Control” (Sub Pop), does have its moments of ecstatic levity: “Talking Hotel Arbat Blues” has a singalong chorus (“I don’t know, but I been told/Every little thing’s been bought and sold”) that evokes classic Bruce Springsteen. With Hannibal Buress. At 9 p.m., Maxwell’s, 1039 Washington Street, Hoboken, N.J. , (201) 653-1703, maxwellsnj.com; $16. (Petrusich)20100114

★ KID CUDI (Thursday) Kid Cudi, from Ohio, is an example of a new kind of pop star: the kind who enjoys considerable fame and acclaim — based, in part, on underground mixtapes and high-profile cameos — before a debut album is even released. Still, when “Man on the Moon: The End of Day” (Motown) finally arrived in late 2009, the hype felt justified; Kid Cudi is an expressive singer and gentle, melodic rapper, and his songs can be both hypnotic and energizing. At 9 p.m., Fillmore New York at Irving Plaza , 17 Irving Place, at 15th Street, Manhattan, (212) 777-6800, irvingplaza.com; sold out. (Petrusich)20100114

★ LADY GAGA Wednesday and Thursday) Lady Gaga, née Stefani Joanne Germanotta, has been driving the music press bonkers with her signature space-nymph style, which, at any given moment, may include thick blond bangs; hot pants (or no pants); dresses made from stuffed animals; fake blood; and copious makeup. Her debut album, “The Fame” (Interscope), has kicked up comparisons to both Madonna and David Bowie, and her glammy electronica (“Bad Romance,” “Paparazzi”) is wiggly and brash. With Jason Derulo and Semi Precious Weapons. At 7:30 p.m., Radio City Music Hall , (800) 745-3000, radiocity.com; $49.50 to $69.50. (Petrusich)20100114

CONSTANTINE MAROULIS (Wednesday) Against all odds and logic, “American Idol” has proved itself a genuine pop juggernaut: winners (Carrie Underwood, Kelly Clarkson) have gone on to forge respectable, profitable careers, while finalists (Jennifer Hudson, Clay Aiken) have enjoyed similar — if not greater — successes. Constantine Maroulis placed sixth on the fourth season of the show, and “Constantine,” his debut album on the appropriately named Sixth Place Records, is a collection of smarmy, cartwheeling rock songs. Mr. Maroulis is currently starring in the Broadway musical “Rock of Ages,” which features music by Styx, Journey, Bon Jovi and REO Speedwagon; in 2009 Mr. Maroulis was nominated for a Tony Award for the role. At 8 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; $20. (Petrusich)20100114

JOEY MCINTYRE (Saturday) It’s extraordinarily difficult to transcend teenage-idol status, and even more difficult when that adulation wasn’t for you alone: Joey McIntyre is the youngest member of the legendary boy band New Kids on the Block, and, like his compatriot Jordan Knight, he has since struck out on his own. At 7:30 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; sold out. (Petrusich)20100114

MOS DEF (Sunday) Though Hollywood jobs take up most of his time these days, Mos Def, once the great, socially conscious hope of New York hip-hop, has never walked away from his calling as a rapper. When he’s not feeling sluggish or preoccupied — or simply bored, as he says on his 2006 album, “True Magic” (Geffen) — he is a passionately literate and polemical performer. At 11:30 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea , (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; $45 in advance; $50 at the door. (Sisario)20100114

BETH ORTON (Sunday and Monday) Over a decade ago Ms. Orton started contributing clear, siren vocals over electronic music. On her own she has headed toward intimate, confessional folk, a journey completed on her 2006 album, “Comfort of Strangers” (Astralwerks). With Sam Amidon. Sunday at 8 p.m., City Winery, 155 Varick Street, near Spring Street, South Village, (212) 608-0555, citywinery.com; $25 for bar stools; $35 for reserved tables; $45 for reserved best tables; $45 for V.I.P. seating. Monday at 8 p.m., the Bell House, 149 Seventh Street, Gowanus, Brooklyn , (718) 643-6510, thebellhouseny.com; $22.50 in advance; $25 at the door. (Sisario)20100114

JOE PERNICE (Friday) Joe Pernice, from the Pernice Brothers, couches songs of heartache and melancholy in doleful melodies and lush arrangements. At 8:15 p.m., Mercury Lounge, 217 East Houston Street, at Ludlow Street, Lower East Side , (212) 260-4700, mercuryloungenyc.com; $15. (Pareles)20100114

THE SMITHEREENS (Saturday) Since the 1980s the Smithereens, from New Jersey, have played fuzzy, ’60s-inspired pop songs deeply indebted to the Beatles and the Kinks. Although its activity has waned slightly in recent years, the band recently released “The Smithereens Play Tommy” (Koch), its take on the Who’s 1969 classic. At 8 p.m., B. B. King Blues Club & Grill, 237 West 42nd Street, Manhattan , (212) 997-4144, bbkingblues.com; $27 in advance; $30 at the door. (Petrusich)20100114

THE SWELL SEASON (Tuesday) In 2006 the Irish musician Glen Hansard, also of the successful rock band the Frames, collaborated with Marketa Irglova, a Czech pianist and songwriter, on “The Swell Season” (Overcoat). The next year the pair — whose high, starchy voices are eerily compatible — starred in the independent film “Once” and earned an Academy Award for “Falling Slowly,” a lilting, desperate duet. They released an impressive follow-up, “Strict Joy” (Anti), in 2009. With Josh Ritter. At 8 p.m., Radio City Music Hall , (800) 745-3000, radiocity.com; $37.50 to $47.50. (Petrusich)20100114

TIMBALAND (Wednesday) Timbaland’s round, slyly grinning face pops up in music videos with considerable frequency, nodding alongside the pop stars (Justin Timberlake, the Pussycat Dolls, Nelly Furtado) whose songs he so expertly produced. “Shock Value II” (Blackground), released at the end of 2009, is the rapper’s third album of his own, and he (re)assumes the spotlight well. At 9 p.m., Fillmore New York at Irving Plaza, 17 Irving Place, at 15th Street, Manhattan , (212) 777-6800, irvingplaza.com; $37.50 in advance; $40 at the door. (Petrusich)20100114

★ VAMPIRE WEEKEND (Sunday through Tuesday) Vampire Weekend blends Ivy League preppiness with African roots music, its members mining Congolese soukous while wearing sweater vests. Its first album, “Vampire Weekend” (XL), is compelling and palatable in the heady, gleeful spirit of Paul Simon’s “Graceland,” and its follow-up — “Contra” (XL), released this month — is even better. Sunday at 8 p.m., United Palace Theater, 4140 Broadway, at 175th Street, Washington Heights, (800) 745-3000, ticketmaster.com, sold out. Monday at 8:30 p.m., Webster Hall, 125 East 11th Street, East Village, (212) 533-2111, ticketmaster.com; sold out. Tuesday at 9 p.m., Bowery Ballroom, 6 Delancey Street, near the Bowery, Lower East Side , (800) 745-3000, ticketmaster.com; sold out. (Petrusich)20100114

VETIVER (Thursday) Led by the singer and songwriter Andy Cabic, the California band Vetiver mines the British and American folk canons for inspiration, as in 2008’s “Thing of the Past” (Gnomonsong), a stunning collection of folk covers (including tracks by Biff Rose, Vashti Bunyan and Townes Van Zandt). Its new album, “Tight Knit” (Sub Pop), is a less explicit homage to folk’s past, but no less compelling. With Avi Buffalo. At 8 p.m., the Bell House, 149 Seventh Street, Gowanus, Brooklyn , (718) 643-6510, thebellhouseny.com; $15. (Petrusich)20100114

★ LOUDON WAINWRIGHT III, ROSANNE CASH (Friday) It’s not unusual for musicians — folk artists, in particular — to find themselves staggered (and, occasionally, preoccupied) by the work of another songwriter. The folk singer Loudon Wainwright III recently released “High, Wide and Handsome: The Charlie Poole Project” (2nd Story Sound Records), a loving homage to the great country singer Charlie Poole: the two-disc set includes covers of Mr. Poole’s songs, as well as original compositions inspired by his tumultuous life. With the country singer Rosanne Cash, whose most recent album, “The List” (Manhattan), is based on a list of 100 country songs her father — that’s Johnny Cash — gave her to study at 18. At 7 p.m., Rubin Museum of Art, 150 West 17th Street, Chelsea , (212) 620-5000, rmanyc.org; sold out. (Petrusich)20100114
