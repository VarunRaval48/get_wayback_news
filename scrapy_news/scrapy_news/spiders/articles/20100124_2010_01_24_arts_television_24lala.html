Guerrilla Comic Invades Los Angeles

Showtime

The British comedian Marc Wootton in character as, from left, Gary, Brendan and the psychic superstar Shirley Ghostman from “La La Land,” his new series on Showtime.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MARGY ROCHLIN
Published: January 22, 2010

FOR someone like Marc Wootton even the simplest query — “How did your new Showtime series come about?” — can be an excuse for theatrical digression.

“Well I just did what I do best, which is very sexy dancing,” a deadpan Mr. Wootton explained. Then he offered a complicated chain of events that somehow wove together a meeting with Robert Greenblatt, Showtime’s president for entertainment, ice-skating, a face full of glitter and an instant green-light callback from Showtime.

“I’m lying,” is how he finished the story. “What’s the question again?”

Such fantastical excursions should be expected from Mr. Wootton, a bearish 33-year-old British comedian who has made a name for himself by fusing prank comedy, performance art, improvisation and reality television. The more factual (but certainly less entertaining) reply is that Mr. Greenblatt is a fan of Mr. Wootton’s “High Spirits With Shirley Ghostman,” a television hit in Britain in 2005 that was broadcast on BBC America in 2006. In it Mr. Wootton, playing Shirley, a sashaying psychic superstar in a blond pageboy and a white satin suit, airily inflicted absurd predictions on real audience members who assumed that they were in the presence of a bona fide clairvoyant.

For “La La Land,” Mr. Wootton’s new series, he springs Shirley Ghostman on the people of Los Angeles, and for good measure he has created two new characters for himself: Brendan, a hapless documentary filmmaker, and Gary, a taxi driver with movie star aspirations.

Misha Manson-Smith, a longtime conspirator of Mr. Wootton’s and director of “La La Land,” said that Los Angeles turned out to be the perfect staging ground for Mr. Wootton’s specific brand of guerrilla theater because it is a place where so few seemed daunted by the trio of newly arrived pushy, over-confident Britons. “It’s a mecca for real people who would be as exciting as Marc’s characters,” said Mr. Manson-Smith, who also confessed that he and Mr. Wootton were at their happiest on days when a casting agent, professional head-shot photographer or hack director would be caught on camera muttering, “What an idiot” or putting the boorish Gary, Brendan or Shirley in his place.

“I much prefer the joke being on my characters,” Mr. Wootton said by speakerphone from his manager’s London office with Mr. Manson-Smith. “At the end of day the three are complete buffoons. I just love it.” As for the less-innocent targets — the self-professed exorcist hired to cleanse Shirley of his demons, for example, or a swaggering member of the anti-immigrant watch group known as the Minutemen Project — Mr. Wootton admitted: “There was a sort of satirical agent at work. We’re actually trying to undermine them and make them look pretty silly.” During these exchanges there is always a level of anxiety created by watching him press a subject’s buttons so relentlessly. But he insists that he has been mining this vein of comedy long enough to know when to advance and when to retreat.

“I’ve never been punched in the face,” he said. “Yet.”

Often “La La Land” can feel a bit like “Punk’d” without the on-camera ‘Just kidding!’ reveal. But for someone who isn’t familiar with Mr. Wootton the easiest comparison is Sacha Baron Cohen. How are Mr. Wootton and Mr. Baron Cohen similar, Mr. Manson-Smith was asked. Before he could answer, Mr. Wootton shouted, “I’m putting my hands over my ears and humming. Hmmmmmmm.”

The difference is “dramatic irony,” Mr. Manson-Smith said. “It’s the viewer knowing more about a situation than someone in it. I think maybe what’s a little different.” In Mr. Wootton’s comedy, he added, “there’s not so many jokes.”

Mr. Wootton interrupted. “No jokes? That sounds terrible.”

“It’s more about funny situations,” Mr. Manson-Smith continued, noting that while Mr. Baron Cohen prefers hit-and-run interactions with real people, Mr. Wootton favors an approach that can require him to keep up the charade for extended periods. That two of his “La La Land” characters had sidekicks who hung around from morning until night added another layer: Not only did Mr. Wootton have to stay in character, but so did the Mr. Manson-Smith and the crew. “Chico would turn up every morning to pick me up,” said Mr. Wootton of the sad-eyed, balding man who landed the job of Shirley’s driver from a Craigslist ad. “He really thought a documentary was being made about me.”

As much outrageous flair as this type of entertainment demands, Mr. Wootton remembers his Portsmouth, England, boyhood as “quite middle of the road,” distilling it into three phrases: “Keep my head down. Make sure that the bullies don’t get me. Make sure that I’m not one of the freaks.” He studied English literature and theater at Exeter University and put in his time after graduation with touring theater companies. But he didn’t discover his true calling until roughly 10 years ago, when he turned up as a fabricated persona on a popular morning radio show. After that came a string of television series, including a hidden-camera game show called “My New Best Friend” in which contestants had to con their pals and family members into believing that Mr. Wootton (disguised as all manner of oafs) had become their favorite person in the world.

It was in the early 2000s when a grieving friend asked him to accompany her on a visit to a medium. “I think it was the first time I got a bee in my bonnet about what this guy was doing, which was essentially charging her quite a deal of money and telling her some pretty awful things. I started researching and seeing other psychics performing live and thought about what a weird world it is,” Mr. Wootton said. The rest is cringe-comedy history.

Which isn’t to say that he isn’t surprised about where his chosen performance niche will take him. When he returned to London after filming “La La Land,” he was startled to discover that he had actually missed the town. “It was so strange coming back here and going, ‘Hi, how’re ya’ doin’?’ to someone on the street, and them just looking down and trudging to work in a kind of rude way. The people there are really, really nice,” he said of Los Angeles. “I really loved the sincerity.”
