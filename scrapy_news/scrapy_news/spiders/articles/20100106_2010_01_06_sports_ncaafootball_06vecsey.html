Texas-Alabama: Rematch 45 Years in the Making

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By GEORGE VECSEY
Published: January 5, 2010

There is only one thing wrong with this national championship game, Pete Lammons says. It is not being held in the Orange Bowl.

Namath, in his familiar No. 12 jersey, played against four future teammates on the Jets, including Texas tight end and linebacker Pete Lammons.

That would be hard to do, since they have torn down the historic old dump in Miami, where Lammons won two epic games: one for the University of Texas, one for the Jets, both involving his good buddy Joe Namath.

Lammons became Namath’s tight end in the 1969 Super Bowl III upset by the Jets. Lammons and three former Texas teammates would never miss a chance to remind Namath, who played for Alabama, of that 1965 Orange Bowl, won by Texas, 21-17.

“Heck, we’d rub it in,” Lammons said. “Never missed a chance.”

Now there is another Texas-Alabama game, this one for the national title, 45 years after Lammons and Namath became acquainted.

“Intercepted him twice,” Lammons noted early in a pleasant telephone interview Tuesday.

Lammons is traveling to this Bowl Championship Series game in the Rose Bowl in Pasadena, Calif., on Thursday night to root for his alma mater against Namath’s alma mater.

This championship game should only be as good as that game, which is remembered for the all-American quarterback, Namath, being stopped at the goal line by Tommy Nobis, the Texas all-American linebacker.

“I give Tommy credit,” Lammons said, “but we had two pretty good defensive tackles, Don Talbert and Tom Currie. They were right there, too.”

Alabama people, including Namath, still think he crossed the goal line, bad knee and all.

That play is often referred to as the final play of the game, but in fact there were seven minutes left, and Namath threw four straight incompletions in a later flurry.

Lammons, who played linebacker and tight end that night, was lined up only a few yards away from the ball, as a defensive end on a goal-line formation.

“They made a few yards on the first down, then tried two quarterback sneaks but didn’t get in,” Lammons said. “Ninety-nine times out of a hundred, with a yard to go, you figured they’d try another sneak.”

The YouTube version on the Web shows a dark-shirted Namath, wearing No. 12, lunging forward, his right shoulder dipping, and then the white-clad linemen converge on him, before the Texas players jump out of the scrum in glee.

Both coaches come off well in the retelling. Bear Bryant of Alabama had won the unofficial national title that season, with the final vote taking place before the bowl games. He refused to scapegoat the officials for a tough goal-line call, even though some of the officials in the two-conference split squad seemed to be itching to put their arms in their air.

Lammons said Texas Coach Darrell Royal told his defenders to play it straight with Namath, who had been hobbled all season with a bad knee. (The man known as Joe Willie was later so revered in pro football that giant linemen would stop him or sack him but lay him down as gently as they could. He was their meal ticket, and one cool dude.)

•

“We knew about his fortitude in playing hurt,” Lammons recalled from the preparations for his Orange Bowl. Alabama started Steve Sloan, who was so good, Lammons recalled, “We said, ‘Oh, man, the other guy is better than this?’ ” Namath hobbled onto the field in the second quarter and nearly won the game.

The Tide had two more chances after the failed sneak, but Lammons intercepted a pass by his future teammate, and then Namath threw four straight incompletions in a futile drive, giving the Longhorns, who had been ranked fifth in the final Associated Press poll, the victory over the reigning champions.

Lammons also caught two passes, one setting up a score, and recovered a fumble. In The New York Times on Jan. 2, Allison Danzig wrote that Lammons “had a big day.” Forty-five years later, it looks even bigger.

“Best game of my life, a career day,” Lammons said.

He would have another big day in that same Orange Bowl, 4 years 11 days later. By that time, Lammons and lineman John Elliott had joined Jim Hudson and George Sauer (who had collaborated for a 69-yard touchdown against Alabama) as teammates on the Jets with Namath and Paul Crane, a lineman who also played for Alabama, who would have finished undefeated had they beaten Texas.

“Highly unusual to have six players from the same game on one team,” Lammons said Tuesday.

The four Texans would bring up that Orange Bowl game whenever Namath was around, Lammons said. Perhaps that helped in the bonding on that boisterous young team. When the surprising Jets reached the third Super Bowl, as underdogs to the Baltimore Colts, “we had been there before,” Lammons said, meaning a big game, inside the Orange Bowl.

Calling himself “mostly retired,” Lammons lives in Houston and owns a few thoroughbreds. He and all Texas fans happen to know that Alabama has lost seven and tied Texas once in their eight meetings. If he runs into Joe Willie in Pasadena, Lammons just might mention this.
