Going Really Indie, With His Own Cash

Melissa Jo Peltier

In foreground in front of the camera, Stephen Lang, Karen Allen, Nick Thurston and Leslie Murphy on the set of John Gray’s film “White Irish Drinkers.”

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By JOHN ANDERSON
Published: January 22, 2010

INSIDE the Bay Ridge Manor catering hall in Brooklyn recently, preparations were under way for yet another rites-of-passage weekend: bar mitzvahs, weddings, sweet 16s. But in an upstairs office resembling a military headquarters a different kind of promise was being fulfilled: “ ‘White Irish Drinkers’!,” a young woman said, answering the phone. No, it wasn’t an A.A. meeting. It was a movie.

A few blocks away from the production office, “White Irish Drinkers,” a coming-of-age drama set in the 1970s, was being directed by John Gray, who grew up in a vastly different Bay Ridge from today’s polyglot neighborhood. (See: China One/Fresh Taco; Schnitzel Haus; La Maison de Couscous.) As his multicultural 20-something crew busied itself recreating the insular neighborhood of his youth, Mr. Gray seemed serenely in his element. At 52 he was making a movie like a 25-year-old, with all the inconvenience, frugality and uncertainty of any indie production. And though he was financing the film with his own $600,000, he appeared an oasis of calm, shooting inside a dilapidated brownstone that had recently been inhabited by an old woman and her 25 cats.

Mr. Gray’s lack of stress belied the fact that it’s never been a tougher climate for independent film, since they don’t usually feature seven-foot blue-skinned Na’vis or Meryl Streep. And doubly so for those involving small-scale personal stories set in ancient times, like the ’70s.

“I’ve never understood why directors shout and stomp and scream and make people uncomfortable,” Mr. Gray said. “I feel making movies is the greatest thing in the universe, and here are all these people helping me do it.”

He didn’t quite say “happy people.”

“We almost had a crew d’état the other night,” said one of the producers, Paul Bernard, gesturing toward his mostly very young team members, who were getting a little cranky as the production neared the end of a breakneck 16-day shoot. The generation gap was pronounced: although a relative newcomer, Nick Thurston, plays the central figure in Mr. Gray’s not-quite-autobiographical story, the production features a chain of ripened interrelationships. Mr. Bernard and Mr. Gray met a decade ago while working on a TNT film called “The Hunley,” and later on “Ghost Whisperer” (the CBS show that Mr. Gray created, and from which he made the money for “White Irish Drinkers”). Mr. Bernard also knew Peter Riegert, one of the film’s leads, from when they both worked on the television show “Leverage.” Mr. Riegert has known his co-star Karen Allen since they appeared in “Animal House,” and Ms. Allen had almost done a play with Stephen Lang, who portrays the alcoholic father of the boys at the heart of the film. Melissa Jo Peltier, who is married to Mr. Gray, is a producer and director of second-unit photography.

The moral of the story: If you’re going to make an independent film, it helps to have connections and a track record.

“It didn’t hurt that Peter and Stephen had already signed on to it,” Ms. Allen said, explaining why she was sitting on a milk crate in an unheated room in Brooklyn on a Friday night while the tail end of a dying hurricane was whipping the streets outside. As usual, she said, it was the writing that got her attention, although “agents are always afraid of things being done on this scale, and this is about as pared down as you can be and still be working with the unions.”

Ms. Allen, who teaches, acts onstage and appeared in the last “Indiana Jones” sequel, said that she had done a lot of independent films, and that a lot of them never got finished. “You can put your heart and soul into something, and it never sees the light of day,” she said. Which is why it’s so difficult, she said, for young directors to get scripts to established actors. And where Mr. Gray had a decided advantage.

“John is not 23,” Mr. Lang said by phone as he was leaving for the world press tour for “Avatar,” in which he plays the villain. “He’s been around the block, has had a whole career, and he’s in a position where he said, ‘If I don’t make this now, when am I going to make it?’ ”

Mr. Gray agreed that “White Irish Drinkers,” a story about choosing the direction one takes in life, was all about personal passion. “And there’s another way to look at it,” he said. “Like: ‘What a vanity production. The guy wants to make his own movie.’ But I’ve been very lucky in television and had a lot of success, and there comes a moment where you have to be careful that your career’s not leading you instead of you leading your career.”

Financing the movie himself required a personal risk. But he made an initial decision about how much he could afford to lose. And he wanted to work without the controls that a studio, financier or outside producer would impose. “Besides,” he said, “if I’m not willing to take this step, how can I really expect anybody else to do it, a studio or an independent company or anybody?”

He laughed, then added, “I fully expect never to see any of this money again.”

Mr. Gray’s attitude is probably healthy given the shifting landscape in the movie industry and the appetites of the moviegoing public: A period piece with middle-aged actors? And no vampires? It seems, at best, high-risk, something to which other filmmakers can attest.

“I was rather naïve when I began writing ‘Adventureland’ years back,” said the director Greg Mottola (“Superbad”). “I assumed one could market a nostalgic college-, high-school-age movie to a young audience and the generation that lived through the decade the movie was set, but no studio I met with had any desire to do that. It was all about the under-25 audience. The response I usually got was, ‘We’ll make this movie, if you rewrite it to make it contemporary.’ ” (The movie was eventually released, with young actors including Kristen Stewart, cast before “Twilight,” but flopped.)

At the same time, there may be other forces that could work in Mr. Gray’s favor. “Everyone in the independent sector has been talking about the glut of product that’s hit the independent film world the last five years, with all the hedge-fund money that came in,” said James Schamus, CEO of Focus Features and a longtime figure on the New York independent scene. “That money, obviously, has disappeared, but we’re still feeling the glut. It’ll take another year to clear through the system.”

What’s exciting, Mr. Schamus continued, is that “the films that are going to be made in the low-budget arena are going to be made out of pure passion.” He added: “And that’s really cool. I can’t say if this film is going to succeed, but the films you’re going to see at Sundance this year, and Sundance next year, are films that are going to be made by people who had to make them. Not by people who got a check from Uncle Moe, who works for a hedge fund in Connecticut.”

Mr. Gray wrote his own check, wrote his own script, directed his own film (it’s in postproduction now, being edited at night and on weekends by a friend from “Ghost Whisperer”) and will be taking all the credit. Or blame.

“Whether it succeeds or it fails, I can’t say, ‘They made me do it this way,’ ” he said. “It’s a scary place to be. But it’s O.K.”
