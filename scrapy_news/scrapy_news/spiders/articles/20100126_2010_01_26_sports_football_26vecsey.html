Ryans Gospel of Green Softens Sting of Defeat

Richard Perry/The New York Times

Mark Sanchez talking to reporters at the Meadowlands on Monday. The Jets' players seemed united around the words of their coach, Rex Ryan.

The home game. The playoff in January. The new stadium in the Meadowlands, shimmering in green.

Before the Jets scattered on Monday, Rex Ryan made the point that he wanted to win the division next season so they could hold a playoff game in their new home. That would be the next step after this season ended so flatly Sunday with the Jets being picked apart by Peyton Manning, 30-17, in Indianapolis.

Ryan must have done a good job addressing his troops Monday morning because they were bristling with optimism as they stuffed their belongings into the ubiquitous plastic bags.

The big, blustery coach was a fresh breeze all season. The Jets went from a 4-6 record to winning two playoff games. But next year, fans will expect them to win the Super Bowl.

It just doesn’t work that way for outsider teams that had their sweet little moments — George Mason making the Final Four of college basketball in 2006, the Tampa Bay Rays advancing to the World Series of 2008. The record book says the Nets actually reached the N.B.A. finals in 2002 and 2003, but that must be a misprint.

On Monday morning, Ryan wanted to prod his players out of thinking they were one-year wonders, but at the same time douse them with reality.

He told the players: “You can’t make an assumption that we are going to get back. It’s 0-0, and we have to get away from it a little bit, but let’s come back hungrier than ever. Like: ‘Let’s go take this thing. Let’s try to get a home game.’ ”

He liked the idea of dominating the new palace nearing completion in the Meadowlands. The new stadium has no corporate name yet, and Ryan insisted it would be called “the new Jets Stadium” whenever the Jets play there.

“I can’t wait,” Ryan went on. “We get our stadium and we are not the visitors in our stadium; this is our stadium. We are the biggest show in town, and that’s what it’s going to be.”

He praised the fans, talked about victories over New England and Cincinnati, and ultimately squeaking into the playoffs.

“It was awesome,” Ryan continued. “And I want to have that stadium. I want it to be a rocket and I expect it to be like that. And our fans should be wearing Jets stuff, too. Don’t have to wear it every day like I am, but at least two or three times a week would be fine.”

The big man, who has set a style of green, floppy chic, again joked about the XXXX green sweatsuits he seems to wear all the time. Some of us who watched the big man rage at an official on Sunday feared he might pop an artery right there on national television. Ryan would do himself a favor by getting down to XXX sweatsuits next season. Jets fans like to think the Rex Ryan dynasty is just beginning.

Certainly, the players feel they are in on something special, and have since Ryan’s first enlightened wisecracks on the first day of camp.

“I’ve bought it — and threw away the receipt — since Rex got here,” said Siona Pouha, a defensive lineman with a feel for language. “And I’m not refunding it,” Pouha added.

Ryan’s address to the troops echoed in the spacious locker room.

“We became a team nobody wanted to play,” said D’Brickashaw Ferguson, an offensive tackle.

“It doesn’t start with the conference championship,” wide receiver Jerricho Cotchery said. “It takes a lot of work.”

•

Mark Sanchez, a rookie quarterback, repeated another talking point of his coach: “Change the culture,” he said. When asked if that was a reflection on the 41-year Super Bowl drought, Sanchez said it was not that sweepingly historical.

“No, we become an all-weather team,” Sanchez said, meaning the Jets had become a team that could win on turf or grass, in September or January.

The Jets were fun in the past month because the fans had no expectations. Ryan said the players must now improve or fall backward. It is a law of football physics.

Just about everything will be different next season. On Monday, Ryan revealed that five assistants — Doug Plank, Brian Smith, Kevin O’Dea, John DeFilippo and Chuck Smith — would not be back. This was less than 24 hours after the final whistle in Indianapolis.

Things will continue to change. The Giants and the Jets are tempting fate by gouging the fans for the obnoxious personal seat licenses. If Sanchez starts throwing Richard Todd knuckleballs, it could be a repeat of David Wright’s dropping from 33 homers to 10 last year when the Mets moved into their spacious stadium named after a bank we are all subsidizing.

The two New Jersey teams are leaving a perfectly good stadium to move into their new palace, with more seats and more luxury boxes in order to sell more wine and shrimp to customers behind glass. This effete clientele does not match Ryan’s profile of lusty fans cheering in the nastiest weather. But if Ryan tells fans to make noise, expect them to make noise. In his first season, Ryan has done everything else right.
