Exclusive!!! Gossip Has a History!

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By JANET MASLIN
Published: January 24, 2010

“No one speaks of the good we’ve done,” the magazine mogul Robert Harrison complained. Perhaps he was referring to Confidential magazine’s occasional public-service article about the dangers of cigarette smoking or air travel. But in 1957 readers could be pardoned for overlooking Mr. Harrison’s good deeds; he was much better known for the innuendo mongering that had turned Confidential into America’s most dangerous publication and created its legacy. Ask John Edwards whether the magazine’s tactics are still operative today.

Henry E. Scott

SHOCKING TRUE STORY

The Rise and Fall of Confidential, Americas Most Scandalous Scandal Magazine

By Henry E. Scott

Illustrated. 222 pages. Pantheon Books. $26.

“Shocking True Story,” Henry E. Scott’s history of the magazine’s rise and fall, was not prompted by a fan’s voyeuristic interest. Not directly, anyhow. Mr. Scott took his inspiration from James Ellroy’s novel “L.A. Confidential,” and he has traced the magazine’s trajectory with a faintly academic zeal. He winds up with a short book, a long bibliography and a cautionary tale about the wisdom of flagrant tell-all tactics. He does not refute Mr. Harrison’s faith in the idea that “the basic vehicle of the story-behind-the-story will be here long after we are all dead.”

Mr. Scott’s book finds space to quote Camille Paglia about how tabloid publications, “half-fictionalized as they are,” use sex and violence to tell “the lurid pagan truth about life.” More notable, it reprints numerous photographs, headlines and screaming article excerpts to help capture Confidential’s distinctive storytelling. “It was the start of the nastiest, most widely circulated hearsay in the annals of rumor-mongering,” the magazine once wrote about an Adlai Stevenson smear that it planned to spread. “By phone, on planes and trains, from the racket of factory assembly lines to the quiet of hospital rooms, from the big-town sharpies to unsophisticated villagers, it burned the ears of a nation.”

A swipe of that magnitude was not a casual effort. “Shocking True Story” examines the way Confidential created and refined its tactics in response to the popularity of movie stars, the public’s taste for scandal and the movie studios’ initial fear of fighting back. After an early career publishing cheesecake magazines, Mr. Harrison founded Confidential at the end of 1952. By 1953 his publication was leering about Joe DiMaggio’s courtship of Marilyn Monroe, wondering why “the bosomy beauty” had not yet been “asked for her hand and what came with it.” And Mr. Harrison was delighted to find that the owner of one swanky New York nightclub was buying the magazine at its 25-cent cover price and charging his patrons $1 to read it.

By 1955, encouraged by its smash-hit success, the magazine was daring to peer into some of Hollywood’s most sacrosanct keyholes. In an article about Desi Arnaz’s cheating on his wife, Lucille Ball, when the “I Love Lucy” stars were America’s sweethearts, it dredged up this quotation: “ ‘I knew Desi was inviting me for more than a drink,’ said the babe.”

Never mind that the story was old, that Arnaz and his wife had been separated at the time it purportedly happened, and that “the babe” it quoted was a prostitute on Confidential’s payroll. It was still both safe and lucrative to run a story titled “Does Desi Really Love Lucy?”

As it moved beyond home wrecking, Confidential tattled about interracial romances or even friendships (at a time when such relationships could cause white stars’ films to be banned in parts of the country), outed homosexual people (“for the most part, history has confirmed its revelations,” Mr. Scott says, and he’s right) and otherwise torpedoed carefully built show-business images. The trick lay in publishing slightly less than the magazine actually knew, to the point at which those it exposed were afraid to fight back. A favorite blackmailer’s gambit of the era was this: “Confidential is working on a story about you, but I can get it killed for $500.”

Confidential eventually inflicted damage expensive enough to prompt retribution. “Today’s headlines, tomorrow’s toilet paper,” the studio chief Jack Warner reportedly told his golden-boy star, Tab Hunter, who in his 2005 autobiography would say that Confidential’s insinuations about his keeping “all-male” company were “as serious as a heart attack.”

And the fact-checking got sloppy enough for Confidential to be caught with its own pants down, as when Maureen O’Hara could use her passport as proof that she had not been canoodling in Grauman’s Chinese Theater as the magazine had reported. She sued for libel; so did other high-profile plaintiffs. By 1958 the heyday of headlines like “It Was the Hottest Show in Town When Maureen O’Hara Cuddled in Row 35” was over.

Mr. Scott can be sloppy too; he mixes up Frank O’Hara and John O’Hara in citing “Appointment in Samarra” (actually by John O’Hara) as one of the books that Confidential’s lawyer cited in a California court, claiming the magazine was not as obscene as “Samarra” and other works of fiction. And “Shocking True Story” is skimpy enough to use and reuse the same Confidential stories.

But Mr. Scott means to be serious. And he traces the connection, already well described by Mr. Ellroy, between the magazine’s snooping and the lurid world of Los Angeles gumshoes.

Mr. Scott also fashions a memorable portrait of Howard Rushmore, the onetime film critic for The Daily Worker (fired for praising “Gone With the Wind”). Mr. Rushmore became Confidential’s Red-hunting right-wing editor before turning himself into the magazine’s worst enemy. In a book filled with comeuppances and poetic justice, the single most tawdry image is a crime-scene photo of Mr. Rushmore and his wife after her murder and his suicide in a New York taxi. Confidential’s countless imitators would happily post that kind of image today.
