Mets Acquire Gary Matthews Jr., an Itinerant Outfielder

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By TYLER KEPNER
Published: January 22, 2010

Gary Matthews Jr. has been a Met before. At the start of the 2002 season, he pinch-hit for Al Leiter and pinch-ran for Mo Vaughn. Then he was traded to Baltimore, the fifth stop in a seven-team, 11-year major league career.

Only his paycheck separates Matthews, who returned to the Mets in a trade with the Los Angeles Angels on Friday, from being just another journeyman outfielder. He is signed to a five-year, $50 million contract that ranks as one of the worst deals in recent history.

The Mets are not responsible for much of it; the Angels agreed to pay $21 million of the $23 million remaining on Matthews’s contract through 2011. In exchange, the Angels received the right-hander Brian Stokes, who had a 3.97 earned run average in 69 games for the Mets last season.

Mets General Manager Omar Minaya said Matthews would compete with Angel Pagan for the starting center-field job in place of Carlos Beltran, who had knee surgery last week and is not expected to return until May.

“It’s going to be an open competition,” Minaya said. “That’s the same thing that I told Gary Matthews Jr. when he asked. Nobody’s going to be given the job.”

Matthews, a 35-year-old switch-hitter, batted .250 with 4 home runs and 50 runs batted in in more than 360 plate appearances for the Angels last season. He has lost roughly 100 plate appearances a year since 2006, when he made the All-Star team with Texas and hit .313 with 19 homers and 79 R.B.I.

“I think he could be a change-of-scenery guy,” Minaya said. “It’s an opportunity for him to come to New York and be able to hopefully get more playing time. And we have him for the next two years at a very reasonable price. The key for him is going to be playing time. He’s had some years of very good production, and he’s had some years of low production.”

Matthews’s career year persuaded the Angels, normally shrewd judges of talent, to give him the lucrative contract. But in February 2007, Matthews was one of about 15 current and former players tied to Internet shipments of human growth hormone and other drugs.

Matthews insisted in a statement that he had never used H.G.H., but his Angels career seemed doomed from the start. After his first season in Anaheim, the Angels essentially replaced him with another free agent, Torii Hunter. In three years with the Angels, Matthews hit .248 with a .325 on-base percentage and a .383 slugging percentage.

“He just didn’t have the bat speed; that’s what was most alarming,” said a West Coast scout who was granted anonymity so he could speak candidly about another team’s player. “When you’ve got a long swing like he does and you’re not all that quick, holes start to present themselves. When you’re losing at-bats, you get anxious and it’s hard to find a groove.”

Matthews had knee surgery after the 2008 season, and he is not considered the defensive standout he was in Texas. But the scout said he was still a good defender who could handle center field, and Minaya said Matthews could play any outfield position.

The Mets tried to add other players this week, but catcher Bengie Molina re-signed with the San Francisco Giants for less than the Mets offered, and starter Joel Pineiro accepted a higher offer from the Angels.

Minaya said that the Mets would continue to explore the market for catchers and starting pitchers but that he was happy with the players he has. The Mets’ catchers include Omir Santos, Henry Blanco, Chris Coste and the prospect Josh Thole.

“I’m comfortable that if we started the season right now, we’re going to be O.K. with the catching,” Minaya said. “Santos, I think, is going to get better. I do believe that the kid Thole, we’ll see how he plays in camp, and I feel very good about having those two veterans available in Coste and Blanco. If we have to go with these guys, we will.”

As for free-agent starting pitchers, the Mets attended Ben Sheets’s workout this week and have also shown interest in Jon Garland. For now, the Mets have Johan Santana, Mike Pelfrey, John Maine and Oliver Perez in their rotation, with Jon Niese and Fernando Nieve as candidates for the fifth spot.

“We expect our guys to be healthy,” Minaya said. “And if our guys are healthy, it’s the same staff we went out there with last year and expected to compete, and a lot of people expected us to compete. We’re hoping that some of the young guys are able to go out there and pitch and give us quality innings.”
