Fresh Powder Meets Fine Dining at Cortina dAmpezzo

Chris Warde-Jones for The New York Times

From left, the Refugio Pomedes, and Venison steak with trimmings at Rifugio Averau.

By EVAN RAIL
Published: January 24, 2010

CONSIDERING the elegant bottle of sangiovese he was about to pour, the waiter’s ragged stocking cap and well-worn black sweater might have been casual to the point of gauche. The diners weren’t exactly in black tie either, with many dressed in stretchy sportswear and poofy coveralls. But the dishes were without flaw: an unusual beet ravioli, startlingly rich and sweet, and a creamy bowl of polenta that was cheesy and velvety.

Skiing From Meal to Meal in Italy

It was a refined meal that one might expect from any high-end restaurant in northern Italy. But afterward, the patrons didn’t call for a taxi or ask the valet to bring the BMW. Instead, they simply strapped on their skis and set off, carving down the mountain through oceans of glorious packed powder.

Skiing — or snowboarding — to a great meal might sound unusual to anyone who has ever endured a bland lunch of chili bowls and pepperoni pizza at a ski lodge, which pretty much sums up the cafeteria food at most North American ski resorts. But several snowy destinations in Europe have a reputation for excellent cuisine that is almost as alluring as the first-rate pistes, from farmhouse cheeses in the Haute-Savoie in France to caviar and Champagne in Zermatt, Switzerland.

But for a heady combination of low-key glamour, accessibility and the ability to ski right into some of its best restaurants, perhaps nothing quite compares with the resort of Cortina d’Ampezzo, in the jagged Dolomites of northern Italy.

Set in a teacup-like Alpine valley and surrounded on all sides by slopes, Cortina has been attracting visitors for much of the past two centuries: one of its main hotels, Ancora, was built in 1826, and many others date from the 19th century. It became a hot spot for celebrities like Brigitte Bardot, Sophia Loren and Audrey Hepburn during and after the Dolce Vita era, earning the nickname “salotto dei famosi,” or “the celebrities’ living room.”

Today, its clientele seems to come straight from the Italian gossip pages: actresses, models, Azzurri soccer players and superstars like George Clooney. With such rarefied company, perhaps it’s not surprising that the town of Cortina itself has several high-end restaurants, like the Michelin-starred Tivoli and a branch of the high-end El Toulà mini-chain.

The real culinary action, however, seems to take place not in town, but on the surrounding slopes, where a network of old mountain huts known as rifugi cook up what some food-minded skiers say is some of the heartiest and homiest cuisine in the Alps.

Built over the past century or so to shelter shepherds and hikers, many rifugi have since been turned into pubs, restaurants and even modest hotels. Today, there are 56 rifugi peppering the ring of mountains surrounding Cortina, according to the town’s tourism board. Many have large wooden terraces overlooking the slopes, with rustic, wood-furnished interiors, like Rifugio Pomedes, a family-run lodge at an aerie-like elevation that is shared by eagles and chamois.

But the most appealing feature may be their ski-to-eat arrangement: diners can ski right up to the front door for a rich and warming meal that shows off the ingredients and traditions of northern Italy.

Visiting the region over several days this winter, I got my first taste of the marriage of great powder and great meals on a sunny and not-too-cold Friday morning in December, as we made our way to the top of the Faloria ski area, one of the largest pistes facing Cortina from the west.

Waking early, my friend Rob and I walked five minutes from our hotel, Oasi, a modest but charming lodge, to the Faloria gondola, which appeared to leave from the very center of town, just a few steps from Cortina’s bus station. As the gondola rose, we got a glimpse of Cortina from above: a small but sprawling collection of houses, dominated by the Santi Filippo and Giacomo Church on the main pedestrian walk, with gleaming white mountaintops above the town on just about every side.

The scene was less monumental and more intimate than the huge peaks of Switzerland and Austria, with jutting rocks zooming by close to our gondola. The glare of the sun against the white snow was blinding. As rough as the topography seemed, the slopes turned out to be well groomed, though often narrow and occasionally very steep, even on the runs labeled easy. Even though it was early in the season, there had been plenty of snowfall, and the pine trees lining the trails were almost completely covered with a blanket of white.

We made several loops before realizing we had built up an appetite. From the top of the Vitelli chairlift, we made a hard right and skied a few minutes down a narrow track until we pulled up to the Rifugio Capanna Tondi, which resembled an American ranch home: a squat, white stucco-and-wood lodge with long, sloping roof lines. On a giant wooden deck overlooking the wide downhill piste, we stopped and unclipped our boots.

We avoided the garrulous Italian families in the bustling front room and took a table in a quiet dining room in the back. The décor felt more Austrian than Italian, with pine-paneled walls, forest-green doors and hardwood floors.
