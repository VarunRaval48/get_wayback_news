Opera

★ ‘CARMEN’ (Saturday and Thursday) Though it is difficult to make an opera as familiar as “Carmen” seem startling and fresh, the British director Richard Eyre does it in his gripping new production. Other than updating the Seville setting from the 1830s to the 1930s, to evoke the repressive era of the Spanish Civil War, Mr. Eyre presents a fairly traditional take on Bizet’s masterpiece. But by teasing out details that show the characters bucking against civic and sexual constraints, he uncovers the rawness at the opera’s core. And he draws courageous performances from a willing cast, led by the Latvian mezzo-soprano Elina Garanca, who makes a sultry, sexy and impetuous Carmen. As Don José, the tenor Roberto Alagna blends rough-hewn ardor and French refinement. The robust baritone Mariusz Kwiecien utterly looks the part of the suave and cocky matador Escamillo, and the rich-voiced soprano Barbara Frittoli is a vulnerable Micaëla. In his Met debut, the young Canadian conductor Yannick Nézet-Séguin draws a compelling account of this ingenious score from the Met orchestra. (Through May 1.) Saturday at 1 p.m.; Thursday at 8 p.m.; Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; sold out. (Anthony Tommasini)20100114

‘DIE DREI PINTOS’ (Saturday and Sunday) The Bronx Opera is presenting what is billed as the first New York staged performances of Weber’s comic opera “Die Drei Pintos,” left incomplete at his death in 1826 and finished in 1888 by Mahler. The company presents the work (which Weber intended as a comic counterpart to his Faustian opera, “Der Freischütz”) in a new English edition by Benjamin Spierman, who directs the production. His father, Michael Spierman, conducts. Saturday at 8 p.m.; Sunday at 2 p.m.; a preperformance talk begins an hour before each show; Kaye Playhouse, 68th Street between Lexington and Park Avenues , (212) 772-4448, kayeplayhouse.hunter.cuny.edu; $25 to $40. (Vivien Schweitzer)20100114

‘IL MONDO DELLA LUNA’ (Tuesday and Wednesday) Neal Goren’s enterprising young Gotham Chamber Opera mounts what might be its most ambitious project to date, as the director Diane Paulus stages Haydn’s whimsical 1777 opera buffa, about a strict father tricked by his daughters into believing that he has been transported to the moon, in the Hayden Planetarium’s dome, using new digital video projections provided by NASA. (Through Jan. 28.) Tuesday at 7:30 p.m.; Wednesday at 8 p.m.; Hayden Planetarium, Rose Center for Earth and Space, American Museum of Natural History, Central Park West and 79th Street , (212) 279-4200, gothamchamberopera.com; $30 to $125. (Steve Smith)20100114

★ ‘DER ROSENKAVALIER’ (Friday) After nearly a decade away from the role, the soprano Renée Fleming is singing the Marschallin in the Met’s revival of Strauss’s “Rosenkavalier.” She is joined by her colleague from her last run in the opera, Susan Graham, singing Octavian, one of this essential mezzo-soprano’s signature achievements. Ms. Fleming may have lost some of the vocal sumptuousness she brought to the Marschallin before. But she sings the role splendidly. In ways that matter, she gives a richer and more nuanced and poignant performance as this middle-aged princess, who seeks delight and escape from the inexorable passage of time in an affair with her smitten 17-year-old cousin. Ms. Graham remains nearly ideal as the impetuous young male lover. The appealing soprano Christine Schäfer takes over the role of Sophie for the final four performances. The bass Kristinn Sigmundssonis a suitably boorish and vocally booming Baron Ochs. The veteran maestro Edo de Waartconducts a lithe account of Strauss’s most beloved opera. At 7:30 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; sold out. (Tommasini)20100114

★ ‘SIMON BOCCANEGRA’ (Monday) As a young man, Plácido Domingo was a baritone. He built his top range through hard work, note by note, to become a tenor, a project that turned out extremely well. He has sung some 124 roles so far. Now, for the first time at the Metropolitan Opera, he performs his newest role, for baritone, the title role of Verdi’s “Simon Boccanegra.” For this back-to-the-future adventure, Mr. Domingo will have the support of James Levine in the pit, and a cast including the tenor Marcello Giordani and the soprano Adrianne Pieczonka. Though the plot of “Simon Boccanegra” is nearly impenetrable, in its revised version, which is offered here, the opera is rich with first-class Verdi music. (Through Feb. 6.) At 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 262-6000, metopera.org; $20 to $275. (Tommasini)20100114

★ ‘STIFFELIO’ (Tuesday) In 1993 the Metropolitan Opera presented a company premiere production of Verdi’s little-known opera “Stiffelio” as a vehicle for Plácido Domingo, who compellingly sang the title role of a 19th-century Evangelical Protestant minister facing a shattering spiritual crisis over the infidelity of his wife. Mr. Domingo is back for the revival of “Stiffelio,” the first Met performances since 1998. But this time he is in the pit, conducting. The title role is sung by José Cura, who reins in his burnished, sometimes wild tenor voice to give an intelligent and effective performance. As Lina, Stiffelio’s guilt-ridden wife, the soprano Julianna Di Giacomo takes over for Sondra Radvanovsky, who sang it with affecting expressivity on opening night (and returns at the end of the run). Mr. Domingo conducts capably. The real star, though, is Verdi’s inexplicably neglected 1850 opera. At 8 p.m., Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; $20 to $375. (Tommasini)20100114

‘TURANDOT’ (Saturday and Wednesday) The tenor Salvatore Licitra has taken over the role of Calàf in the Met’s revival of Franco Zeffirelli’s opulent production of Puccini’s “Turandot.” When Mr. Licitra made his Met debut in 2002, a last-minute substitute for Luciano Pavarotti in “Tosca,” he had an undisciplined but muscular and vibrant voice. In recent years his singing has been erratic. In this production he is singing with more control and evenness, but he still sounds constrained and leathery. He had his moments, though. The soprano Maria Guleghina slices through the orchestra with her steely voice as Turandot. The soprano Maija Kovalevska is a rich-voiced, deeply expressive Liù. The exciting work of the conductor Andris Nelsons continues to be the news of this revival. (Through Jan. 28.) Saturday at 8:30 p.m.; Wednesday at 8 p.m.; Metropolitan Opera House, Lincoln Center , (212) 362-6000, metopera.org; $20 to $375. (Tommasini)20100114

Classical Music

BARGEMUSIC (Friday through Sunday) The violinist Curtis Macomber presides over a trio that includes Michael Kannen, cellist, and Marija Stroke, pianist, at this intimate floating concert hall on the East River on Friday and Saturday. Their program is comfortably standard: Haydn’s Piano Trio in D minor (Hob. XV:23), Schumann’s Trio No. 1 in D minor (Op. 63) and Beethoven’s Trio No. 6 in E flat (Op. 70, No. 2). On Sunday a piano quintet — with Vivian Weilerstein, pianist; Don Weilerstein and Jennifer Curtis, violinists; Nicholas Mann, violist; and Claire Bryant, cellist — takes over to play selections from Bach’s “Art of Fugue” and works by Enescu, among them, the Violin Sonata No. 3, the Piano Quintet in A minor (Op. 29) and a movement from the Symphonie Concertante, arranged for solo violin by Sherban Lupu. Friday and Saturday at 8 p.m.; Sunday at 3 p.m.; Bargemusic, Fulton Ferry Landing next to the Brooklyn Bridge, Brooklyn , (718) 624-2083, bargemusic.org. On Friday and Saturday, $35; $30 for 65+; $15 for students. On Sunday, $40; $35 for 65+; $20 for students. (Allan Kozinn)20100114

★ YEFIM BRONFMAN (Sunday) This splendid pianist, who just completed a run of concerts with the New York Philharmonic and will tour Europe with the orchestra starting next week, is joined by a stellar group of Philharmonic principal string players in Brahms’s Piano Quartet in F minor. The program also includes Mozart’s Duo in G (K. 423) and Ravel’s String Quartet in F. At 2:30 p.m., Grace Rainey Rogers Auditorium, Metropolitan Museum of Art , (212) 570-3949, metmuseum.org; $45. (Smith)20100114

ENSEMBLE ACJW (Tuesday) This student group, drawn from participants in the Academy — an educational collaboration among Carnegie Hall, the Juilliard School and the Weill Music Institute (hence the acronym in its name) — offers a free program that touches on the friendship between Robert and Clara Schumann and the young Brahms. The works are Robert Schumann’s “Märchenerzählungen” (Op. 132), Clara Schumann’s Piano Trio in G minor (Op. 17) and Brahms’s Serenade No. 1 in D (Op. 11). At 8 p.m., Paul Hall, Juilliard School, 155 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center , (212) 769-7406, juilliard.edu; free. (Kozinn)20100114

★ EUROPA GALANTE (Thursday) The scintillating Italian Baroque violinist Fabio Biondi leads his vital period-instrument ensemble in works by Telemann, Sammartini, Nardini, Corelli and Locatelli. At 7:30 p.m., Zankel Hall, Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $58 and $64. (Smith)20100114

★ RUSTEM HAYROUDINOFF (Sunday) This exciting Russian pianist makes his New York debut with Franck’s Prelude, Fugue and Variations; Chopin’s Ballade No. 4; Shostakovich’s Three Preludes and Fugues; and the Preludes (Op. 32) by Rachmaninoff, whose music is one of Mr. Hayroudinoff’s specialties. At 5 p.m., Frick Collection, 1 East 70th Street, Manhattan , (212) 547-0715, frick.org; sold out. (Schweitzer)20100114

★ MARILYN HORNE FOUNDATION (Tuesday through Thursday) James Levine, Warren Jones and Marilyn Horne lead master classes next week as part of “The Song Continues,” an annual celebration of the song recital. Recitals will be presented by foundation vocalists, including the baritone Edward Parks and the soprano Nadine Sierra on Tuesday, and the mezzo Sasha Cooke on Wednesday. (Through Jan. 22.) Recitals: Tuesday and Wednesday at 5:30 p.m., Weill Recital Hall. Master classes: Tuesday through Thursday at 7:30 p.m., Zankel Hall, Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $5 to $20. (Schweitzer)20100114

MONTEVERDI ‘VESPERS’ (Wednesday) Artek, the period-instrument and vocal ensemble, has long specialized in the music of Monteverdi, particularly the madrigals and his first opera, “Orfeo.” To celebrate the 400th anniversary of the publication of his “Vespers” of 1610, Artek is joining forces with three other groups: Piffaro, a wind band; Parthenia, a viol consort; and the National Gallery of Art Vocal Ensemble. At 8 p.m., Church of St. Ignatius Loyola, 980 Park Avenue, at 84th Street , (212) 866-0468, artekearlymusic.org; $10 and $20 tickets remaining. (Kozinn)20100114

NEW YORK PHILHARMONIC (Friday and Saturday) Alan Gilbert is about to take the Philharmonic on a European tour, but before the members leave, they finish off a subscription series with the baritone Thomas Hampson as the soloist in “The Wound Dresser,” John Adams’s setting of the Walt Whitman poem. The program also includes Schubert’s “Unfinished” Symphony, Haydn’s Symphony No. 49 (“La Passione”) and Berg’s Three Orchestral Pieces. Friday at 2 p.m.; Saturday at 8 p.m.; Avery Fisher Hall, Lincoln Center , (212) 875-5656, nyphil.org; $29 to $112. (Kozinn)20100114

★ PACIFICA QUARTET (Saturday) This excellent young ensemble, which took over as the quartet in residence at the Metropolitan Museum in October, offers a characteristically wide-ranging program that opens with Mendelssohn’s String Quartet No. 1 and ends with Beethoven’s String Quartet in B flat (Op. 130), with its original ending, the “Grosse Fuge.” In between, the group plays a work composed for it, Jennifer Higdon’s “Voices.” At 7 p.m., Grace Rainey Rogers Auditorium, Metropolitan Museum of Art , (212) 570-3949, metmuseum.org; $45. (Smith)20100114

MENAHEM PRESSLER (Friday) This distinguished pianist is joined by the violinist Alexander Kerr, the violist Kim Kashkashian and the cellist Eric Kim for Mozart’s Piano Quartet No. 2 in E flat and Dvorak’s Piano Quartet No. 2 in E flat. At 7 p.m., Metropolitan Museum of Art , (212) 570-3949, metmuseum.org; $45. (Schweitzer)20100114

★ CHRISTINE SCHÄFER (Wednesday) After performing her last Sophie for the Met’s current “Rosenkavalier,” this German soprano turns her attention to an unorthodox yet fascinating juxtaposition of songs by Purcell and George Crumb, which she and the pianist Eric Schneider have performed widely and issued on a haunting 2007 CD, “Apparition.” At 7:30 p.m., Zankel Hall, Carnegie Hall , (212) 247-7800, carnegiehall.org; $44 and $52. (Smith)20100114

SIX SATURDAYS WITH VIERNE (Saturday) John Scott, the organist and director of music at St. Thomas Church Fifth Avenue, offers a rare chance to hear the complete organ symphonies of the French composer Louis Vierne. This concert, the second in the six-part series, features the Symphony No. 2 in E minor. (Through Feb. 13.) At 4 p.m., St. Thomas Church Fifth Avenue, 1 West 53rd Street, Manhattan , (212) 664-9360, saintthomaschurch.org; free, but donations are suggested. (Schweitzer)20100114

★ VIENNA PHILHARMONIC (Friday through Sunday) The mighty Viennese return to Carnegie Hall with two distinguished maestros at their helm. On Friday Daniel Barenboim conducts Beethoven’s “Pastoral” Symphony, Schoenberg’s Variations for Orchestra and Wagner’s Prelude and Liebestod from “Tristan and Isolde,” an ideal vehicle to showcase the orchestra’s fabled string sound. Saturday is the beginning of Carnegie’s celebration of Pierre Boulez’s 85th birthday. He conducts Schoenberg’s Chamber Symphony No. 2, Webern’s Six Pieces, the Adagio from Mahler’s Symphony No. 10 and Schoenberg’s Piano Concerto, with Mr. Barenboim as soloist. On Sunday Mr. Barenboim takes the podium again, leading Schoenberg’s Five Pieces for Orchestra, Mr. Boulez’s “Notations I, II, III, IV and VII” and Beethoven’s Symphony No. 5. Friday and Saturday at 8 p.m.; Sunday at 2 p.m.; 20100114
