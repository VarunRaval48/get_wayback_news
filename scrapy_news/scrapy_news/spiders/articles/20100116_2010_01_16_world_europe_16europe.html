Europe Sends Aid and Pledges Money for Haiti

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MATTHEW SALTMARSH
Published: January 15, 2010

PARIS — The effort to aid Haitian earthquake victims is presenting Europe with the first test of its newly created foreign policy structure and prompting France to call on other nations to speed the forgiveness of the nation’s international debt.

In the European Union’s headquarters in Brussels, Catherine Ashton, who has taken over as the new foreign affairs’ chief, has been bringing together the humanitarian aid, development and civil protection officials of the European Commission, the bloc’s executive arm, as well as experts from the European Council, which represents individual governments.

“It is the first time that all these actors have been brought together,” said Lutz Güllner, spokesman for Ms. Ashton, “and that such a coordination was done pulling together all the strands making sure that all available instruments can be used.”

By Friday, European authorities had pledged millions in aid, both through Brussels and individually. The pledges include 6 million pounds from Britain, 3 million euros from Spain, 3 million euros from the European Commission, 2 million euros from the Netherlands and 1.5 million euros from Germany.

Brussels has sent a team of experts to assess the situation in Haiti, while a number of governments have joined the search-and-rescue effort, sending in searchers and sniffer dogs.

On Monday, European development ministers will hold an emergency meeting in Brussels to consider how to start reconstruction once humanitarian aid has reached Haiti.

“It is important to tell the people of Haiti that we stand ready to help them as much as we can in this tragedy,” Ms. Ashton said Thursday. “They can count on Europe.”

Her new job was created by the Lisbon Treaty, which entered into force late last year.

Ms. Ashton’s appointment has not yet formally been approved by the European Parliament and she has yet to build her own diplomatic corps, one of the chief responsibilities of the job. Yet she has already been acting as the European Union’s main coordinator, speaking about how to address the crisis in Haiti with the United Nations’ chief, Ban Ki-moon, and Secretary of State Hillary Rodham Clinton.

European nations are also acting individually to help Haiti.

France, the former colonial ruler of Haiti, has attempted to take a lead in announcing initiatives for the country. France has a large community of Haitians, estimated at 50,000 or more, and is also host to numerous Haitian exiled political figures and intellectuals.

Christine Lagarde, the French economy minister, said on Friday that she had asked members of the Paris Club, a grouping of creditor nations, to speed debt relief for the country.

The Paris Club agreed in July to clear Haiti’s $62.7 million in debt under the International Monetary Fund and World Bank’s initiative for heavily indebted poor countries. Creditors also committed on a bilateral and voluntary basis to cancel an additional $152 million.

France has asked that nations clarify how much had been canceled already and how much is still outstanding.

President Nicolas Sarkozy has proposed holding a international conference on aid and reconstruction and pledged to visit the island in the coming weeks. Foreign Minister Bernard Kouchner told RTL radio that the reconstruction conference could take place in March.

In addition, on Thursday, the French immigration minister Eric Besson, said France had suspended expelling illegal immigrants from Haiti in the wake of the earthquake.

Italy, itself a victim of a powerful earthquake in the Abruzzo region last April, has focused its efforts on sending medical teams.

On top of the official efforts, a number of nongovernmental relief efforts are also being coordinated from Europe

Doctors Without Borders, founded in 1971 by a group of French doctors, said Friday that it had treated at least 1,500 patients in Haiti.

It had 800 staff in the country before the quake and expects 70 extra staff to arrive within days. It is sending supplies to set up a 100-bed hospital with a surgical unit.

The Geneva-based International Committee of the Red Cross said Friday that one of its teams — comprising 11 emergency experts — had arrived in Port-au-Prince, while a cargo plane carrying 40 tons of medical supplies was in the air and expected to reach the area late Friday.

On Friday, Red Cross workers in Port-au-Prince distributed medical assistance to five major hospitals and clinics, as well as to smaller facilities set up by local doctors in areas with a high concentration of earthquake survivors.

In Britain, the actor John Hurt led a joint appeal by the Disasters Emergency Committee, which brings together a number of aid agencies, asking the public for help.

“It is clear after the immediate rescue attempt is completed we will be left with ongoing humanitarian challenges,” said the committee’s chief, Brendan Gormley.

Britons have donated more than 2 million pounds to the appeal in 36 hours, according to the committee.

A number of companies in Europe have also pledged assistance.

E.D.F., the French electricity giant, said that it was ready to send technicians to Haiti from the neighboring French islands of Martinique and Guadeloupe to help assess the damage to the electricity network. The company said that it was also prepared to send in teams to help provide emergency power to help the rescue and medical efforts.

The company has been working with the non-profit group Electriciens sans Frontières.

The Irish telecommunications company Digicel said it would donate $5 million and help repair the phone network, the Associated Press said.

Stephen Castle contributed reporting from Brussels.
