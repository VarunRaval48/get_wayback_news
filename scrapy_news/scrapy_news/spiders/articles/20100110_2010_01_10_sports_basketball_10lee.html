Lee Secures the Perimeter by Perfecting His Jump Shot

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By HOWARD BECK
Published: January 9, 2010

Sometimes a single play reveals infinitely more than its outcome. On a recent evening at Madison Square Garden, David Lee stood 21 feet from the basket, with the ball in his hands and Tim Duncan, one of the N.B.A.’s premier defenders, in front of him. Lee pump-faked, Duncan lurched and Lee darted around him.

The Knicks assistant Phil Weber used to be “a yanker” on his jump shots, taking his wrist back too far and shooting line drives.

It was a simple, unremarkable basketball play. But there was meaning in the moment: Duncan left his feet, with an impulse to contest the shot.

The scene would have been unimaginable two years ago, and probably even two months ago. But then Lee, the Knicks’ erstwhile rebounding specialist, is not the same player he was on opening night.

Meet David Lee, ace jump shooter.

For the previous 15 games before Saturday, Lee had been as brilliant on the perimeter as he had been at the rim, his rapid evolution on full display. Since Dec. 6, Lee had converted 53 percent of his jumpers from 16 to 23 feet, and 75 percent of his shots from 10 to 15 feet, according to an analysis provided by HoopData.com.

In 35 games, Lee had converted more perimeter shots (86) than he did all of last season (82). He had hit 66 shots in the 16-to-23-foot range, compared with 51 last season and only 18 in 2007-8.

Clearly, this is not the same player who in team marketing materials was once labeled, plainly, the Rebounder.

“You wouldn’t believe the things people have said to me, like, ‘What are you doing shooting that one?’ ” Lee said before a recent game.

He chuckled at the thought. Lee is undoubtedly enjoying his newfound skills, as are the Knicks. Through Friday, they had won 12 of their last 18 games, a trend mostly attributed to stickier defense, more deliberate offense, a tighter rotation and more defined roles. Lee’s sudden, surprising emergence as a jump shooter has gone almost unnoticed.

Almost.

“I think he has improved that tremendously,” Duncan said on Dec. 27, after the San Antonio Spurs held off the Knicks for a 95-88 victory. “Every year I see him, he gets better.”

Lee outplayed Duncan that night and made him look silly twice on pump fakes. The first time, Duncan had to chase Lee from behind and fouled him. The second time, Lee missed a tough shot in traffic.

Those plays were set up by Lee’s early success. He hit a 21-footer in the first minute of the game, and an 18-footer two minutes later.

There was a time when a Lee jumper was as likely to make Knicks fans cringe as, say, a Jared Jeffries jumper.

When Lee was drafted out of Florida, with the 30th pick in 2005, he was regarded as merely a hustle player, albeit a good one. He was athletic, strong around the basket and able to finish with either hand. At 6 feet 9 inches, he was a little short for a center, and not skilled enough to be a reliable power forward. As a senior, Lee averaged 13.6 points, but “most of his stuff was dunks,” said Atlanta Hawks center Al Horford, a former teammate at Florida.

The Knicks loved Lee for his lunch-pail persona and promoted it to the hilt. But Lee knew he had to expand his game to stay on the court. He took 53 long-range jumpers in his second season, about one per game, sometimes just to test it or because he was wide open when his defender had left to double-team someone else.

“My shot was pretty screwed up before,” Lee said. “My whole thought was not to miss it bad when I shot it, so it wouldn’t be embarrassing.”

Lee’s accuracy rate from 16 to 23 feet was in the low 30s every season until this one. He was shooting 45 percent (66 of 146) in that range this season, well above the league average for power forwards (40.5 percent) and for centers (40.4), according to HoopData.com.

The change has been gradual, nudged along by the usual forces: practice and confidence. Lee has always worked on his jump shot, but he became fanatical about it over the last two summers, after Mike D’Antoni became the Knicks’ coach.

D’Antoni values big men who can shoot, and he was skeptical about Lee’s ability to play in his spread-the-floor offense.

“I thought he was very limited offensively,” D’Antoni said.

Phil Weber, D’Antoni’s top assistant, saw something else: Lee was “a yanker” — meaning he yanked his wrist back too far on his windup, shooting line drives instead of parabolas.

“Throwing darts,” said Weber, who helped Amar’e Stoudemire and Boris Diaw develop their jump shots when he worked in Phoenix.

Every assistant has helped Lee tweak his form over the last few years. Then it became a matter of repetition and confidence. Weber said Lee’s hard work “is paramount.” But Weber also credited D’Antoni, who encourages his players to shoot freely and does not bench them for missing, as long as they are taking good shots.

“As a player, that’s the greatest thing in the world,” Weber said.

So when Lee opened nearly every preseason game with a 19-footer, just to see if it went in, there was no hesitation or worry. In October and November, many of them missed. But the persistence started to pay off in early December.

Lee went 4 of 8 on deep jumpers in a Dec. 6 victory over the Nets and has not stopped shooting since then. He sealed a late-December victory over Chicago with a 17-footer with five seconds left. There is no greater test of a shooter’s steadiness than putting up a critical shot in the closing seconds of a tight game.

“He’s got confidence,” D’Antoni said.

By expanding his game, Lee has diversified the Knicks’ offense. In the past, defenses could play off Lee and throttle him on the pick-and-roll. Now they have to play him tightly to respect the jumper. That means more room for Wilson Chandler to drive and for Danilo Gallinari to shoot.

The Knicks now routinely deploy Lee as a playmaker, in a set called “delay.” Lee holds the ball at the top of the key and can pass, drive or shoot. A point guard early in high school, Lee is also among the Knicks’ best passers. He was two assists short of a triple-double in a Dec. 30 loss to the Nets.

“He’s got a great sense of timing on the offensive end,” D’Antoni said. “He’s become a point guard in the way we play.”

As a jump-shooting big man, Lee may soon rival Duncan and Chris Bosh for his effectiveness on the perimeter. Statistically, he is now among the best bigs in the league, averaging a team-leading 18.9 points and 10.9 rebounds through Friday.

Lee is still a bruiser at heart, more at home trading elbows inside than floating around the 3-point arc. But he now talks the language of jump shooters, speaking of balance, release point and control, and the expectation that all shooters have: that every shot is destined to swish through.

“Occasionally, I still surprise myself,” he said. “But it’s gotten to the point now that I trust it. And I’m not going to stop shooting it.”
