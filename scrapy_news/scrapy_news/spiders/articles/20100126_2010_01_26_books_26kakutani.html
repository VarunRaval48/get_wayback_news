When Impulse Control Goes Awry

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By MICHIKO KAKUTANI
Published: January 25, 2010

The title of Robert Stone’s new collection of stories, “Fun With Problems,” is clearly meant to be read in an ironic sort of way. Mr. Stone’s characters do not have fun. They may get high, get drunk or get loaded, but they are almost always lost, disaffected and desperate. They may visit exotic parts of the world and go on all sorts of wild adventures, but in doing so, they are always courting danger or falling prey to bad faith, bad karma and bad vibes. Even scuba diving or bungee jumping becomes for them existential confrontation with mortality, raising primal questions about courage and angst, life and death, God or his absence.

FUN WITH PROBLEMS

Stories

By Robert Stone

195 pages. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt. $24.

Mr. Stone tends to be at his best — in his novels “A Hall of Mirrors,” “Dog Soldiers” and “Damascus Gate” — when he’s using his reportorial skills to conjure a strange or alien land, and relying on his narrative gifts to create harrowing action sequences that reveal his characters’ innermost needs and fears. He’s at his worst — in, say, the abysmal “Bay of Souls” — when he’s pontificating (or allowing his heroes to pontificate) about the meaning of life, and stuffing his story lines full of predictable, prepackaged B-movie tropes.

Unfortunately for the reader, “Fun With Problems” is a grab-bag collection that’s full of Mr. Stone’s liabilities as a writer, with only a glimpse here and there of his strengths. “Honeymoon” is a ridiculous little tale about a man who talks to his former wife while on his honeymoon with a new young bride, and who later commits suicide during a dive. “Charm City” is an equally preposterous story about a grifter named Margaret who works with her meth-head daughter and the daughter’s boyfriend. Margaret picks up a mark — a middle-aged, married writer — at a classical music recital, cases his country home for things to steal and then ransacks the house with her gang.

As for “High Wire,” it reads like a wobbly variation on Mr. Stone’s 1986 Hollywood novel, “Children of Light.” Once again, we’re given a portrait of a burned-out screenwriter with a taste for coke and ’ludes and alcohol, and his onetime lover, a troubled actress who specializes in “the old nothingness-and-grief routine” and who’s on the fast track to dissolution and despair.

This time the screenwriter, a guy named Tom Loving, keeps putting himself in romantic triangles with an actress named Lucy and her other boyfriends, as drugs and AIDS decimate the ranks of their lovers and friends.

“We could not know it, but death was coming big time,” Tom portentously intones, adding that when he learned of the death of one of Lucy’s exes, he “became drunk and depressed, as if a fellow circus performer had fallen from a high wire. All of us worked without a net.”

Of course it doesn’t take death or the fear of AIDS to send one of Mr. Stone’s heroes into a drunken, drug-fueled tailspin. They’re quite capable of doing that out of simple boredom or self-absorption. Eric Floss, the hero of “The Wine-Dark Sea,” is a freelance journalist with a “tendency to overlook deadlines and even entire assignments.”

A few weeks out of rehab, Eric “had started smoking the odd joint and getting drunk by six”: he had “occasional blackouts” and “woke up with strange troublesome women,” yet allowed himself “to believe that things were working out.” His love of drink, combined with his penchant for macho “antler-rattling,” will put him on a collision course with a potential source for a story he’s doing in a small island town, where the secretary of defense is holding a conference.

Poor impulse control also drives the plot of “The Archer,” a story about Duffy, an artist and university professor who is said to have “threatened his wife and her lover with a crossbow,” while dressed in Jockey shorts and announcing himself as Cupid. Duffy insists that the story is “pure invention” but actually isn’t sure what happened, since he can remember only bits and pieces of that fateful evening. Later in the story, Duffy gets drunk while visiting a Southern university and makes an awful scene in a restaurant, insisting that the crab he’s been served isn’t crab but “some rotten thing out of a tube.”

Whereas Duffy tries to make amends for this outburst, Matthews, the hard-drinking hero of the title story, is more of a hard-boiled case. He picks up a woman named Amy, persuades her to drink with him (even though she’s a “drunk farm” alumna who’s trying to stay sober), takes her to bed and abruptly walks out to her tears.

He was the man, Mr. Stone writes, whose ex-wife had once said that he didn’t care whether he even had sex, as long as he could make some woman unhappy.

Matthews wears his hardheartedness proudly like a badge: “Spite had taught him detachment. The trick was to carry on indifferent to his own feelings and without pity for things like Amy’s ditsy vagueness or the neediness she was beginning to display.”

Similar cynics populate Mr. Stone’s novels, of course, but in the most persuasive of those books, he not only maps the sources of his heroes’ malaise and the fallout it has on their lives but also dramatizes their flailing efforts to grab after one last chance at a big score or even a whiff of love and salvation. In the stories in this volume we are not given the full arc of his people’s lives; we get only snapshots of their drunken nihilism and puerile self-pity. It’s certainly not enough to make us care, not even enough to engage our voyeuristic curiosity; it’s simply dismal and depressing.
