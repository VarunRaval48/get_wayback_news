The Haggler

When Canceling a Contract Is a Workout

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By DAVID SEGAL
Published: January 9, 2010

LET’S start with an update. In our last episode, we met Jagoda Walczak, a Polish immigrant who spent $17,600 to become a franchisee with Coverall Cleaning, a janitorial services franchiser based in Boca Raton, Fla. Ms. Walczak said she was unable to earn anything close to the sums she had expected and wanted her money back.

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Christoph Hitz

The column ended with a representative from Coverall promising to “work toward a solution” with Ms. Walczak. And the Haggler is pleased to say that the company followed through. Terms of the arrangement are confidential — that’s the way these deals often work — but a Coverall rep called the refund “substantial” and Ms. Walczak said she was very pleased with the outcome.

Undoubtedly, the spirit of comity and good will on display here will inspire a healing and oneness throughout Consumerland, and the Haggler will soon have nothing to do except frolic in fields of lilies where pixies dance in midair, the sound of a harp wafting from a hill.

Until then ...

Q. When I moved to Los Angeles a few months ago, I tried to cancel my month-to-month gym contract with Bally Total Fitness. Signing up with the company had been a breeze. Canceling turned out to be a nightmare.

Bally doesn’t let you cancel with a branch, but requires you to contact its central administration, which then stonewalls you until you think hard about giving up. I sat on hold for more than 30 minutes on three occasions — including one hourlong wait. Even finding an e-mail address on its Web site requires archaeological digging.

I finally enlisted my credit card company to cancel my membership by simply stopping electronic payments.

That worked, but to my amazement Bally sent what looks like a bill, filled with numbers, dollar signs and phrases like “current month dues” and “total dues amount.” When I miraculously got a rep on the phone, he said that this wasn’t a bill — it’s a statement of how much I “would” need to pay if I were still a member of the gym.

Huh? If I didn’t keep close track of my financial transactions, I would have assumed that I owed money to the gym for a nonexistent balance. Bally claims that it is not required to advise clients of this strategy, and the rep said I could expect more in the future. Can you get the company to stop issuing these notices to me? I don’t want to hear from it and I certainly will never become a member again.

Anurima Banerji

Los Angeles

A. O.K., this is just a theory, but maybe Bally thinks that if you quit the gym, you need a new workout. And if the company assumes you’ve sworn off exercise, maybe deeply enraging hold time is the only way it can force you to burn calories.

Ever think about that? Maybe Bally’s phone tree is basically an aerobics class masquerading as a hassle. Boom. Step to the left, press 1. Boom. Add a bicep curl, press 2. Boom.

C’mon, people! Feel the burn.

And hold.

Yes, this sounds crazy, but for years a lot of former and current Bally’s members have been strenuously venting about the company’s enrollment policies — complaints about heavy-handed sales tactics, hard-to-cancel memberships and collection agency nightmares.

The company, based in Chicago, has endured more than its share of lousy headlines, as well an investigation by Eliot Spitzer, then the attorney general of New York, whose office received more than 600 complaints about the company over a five-year period. The investigation ended in 2004 with an agreement by the company to reform a number of sales and advertising practices and hand over $200,000, the cost of the investigation.

Bally emerged from its second bankruptcy in August and is now owned by JPMorgan Securities and Anchorage Advisors, an investment management fund. A spokesman for Bally said that it was now trying hard to change its image by revamping its enrollment policies and becoming a more “customer centric” company.

“The new Bally is committed to becoming the most trusted name in fitness,” said the spokesman, Peter Marino, by e-mail. “We know we are not perfect; however, we take all member concerns very seriously, including Ms. Banerji’s.”

If the company is serious about reform, Ms. Banerji’s experience suggests that it has a way to go. She forwarded to the Haggler the bill-like statement, which she received in December, and she’s right — it sure looks like a bill. But there’s this somewhat mysterious sentence under “total amount due: $112”: “It’s time to protect your investment and save hundreds of dollars of the price of a new membership.”

Now, the implication is that this is an offer to rejoin for less money than a new customer would pay — perhaps by forgoing the initiation fee. In other words, this isn’t a fake bill. It’s an offer of a really good deal that will “save hundreds of dollars.”

Just one problem. The Bally’s closest to Ms. Banerji is currently offering a month-to-month membership, with no enrollment fee, at $31 a month. Given that she was spending $28 a month at her former gym, she would not save a dime by coughing up the “total dues amount” of $112 until she’d been a member at the new gym for three years. Not exactly a great deal, and a long way from “hundreds of dollars” in savings.

But all of this is speculation. Mr. Marino would not say much about the statement received by Ms. Banerji, beyond describing it as a “renewal piece” that every former month-to-month member is sent once, a couple of months after canceling.

Well, that was his first explanation. When the Haggler pressed for more information on this “renewal piece” — which, by the way, is the Haggler’s favorite new phrase — Mr. Marino sent an e-mail message the next day to say that, on second thought, it was sent to Ms. Banerji “in error.” He added that “we have taken action to rectify the situation and ensure that other members will not be similarly inconvenienced.”

Bally’s own New Year’s resolution!
