Delay in Trial for Abortion Doctor Killing

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MONICA DAVEY
Published: January 11, 2010

The trial of the man accused of murdering a Kansas doctor nationally known for performing late-term abortions was delayed on Monday, as lawyers disagreed about how far the defense should be permitted to go in presenting opposition to abortion as a reason for the killing.

Prosecutors in the case against Scott Roeder, an abortion opponent charged with shooting Dr. George R. Tiller at his Wichita church last May, asked a judge to bar Mr. Roeder’s public defenders from portraying the crime as voluntary manslaughter as opposed to first-degree murder.

Mr. Roeder, of Kansas City, Mo., has acknowledged in legal documents to having carried out the shooting, but he has pressed to be allowed to show that he believed he was saving unborn children in the process.

Late last week, Judge Warren Wilbert of Sedgwick County District Court in Wichita suggested he might consider allowing evidence that could support a theory of voluntary manslaughter, which can be defined, under state law, as “an unreasonable but honest belief that circumstances existed that justified deadly force.”

That possibility has outraged prosecutors and some national abortion rights groups, which say that consideration of such a lesser charge could encourage more violence against abortion providers. Mr. Roeder’s supporters, who profess that violence against abortion providers can be justified by the lives it saves, took Judge Wilbert’s remarks as a clear and surprising victory.

But on Monday, as jury selection was to begin, court officials announced a delay until Wednesday, after another hearing on the entire question.

Joe Stumpe contributed reporting from Wichita.
