Sunday Routine | Oscar Padilla

Watching It All, Walking the Beat

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By SUSAN SWARD
Published: January 23, 2010

Oscar Padilla, 38, the San Francisco-born son of Salvadoran immigrants, joined the San Francisco Police Department 11 years ago. For the last eight years, he has worked at the Ingleside Station in the city’s south-central area. Last year, he volunteered for a foot patrol beat on Mission Street. He is on duty on Sundays. (His words have been edited and condensed.) SUSAN SWARD

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Heidi Schumann for The New York Times

Oscar Padilla’s beat is on Mission from Persia to Geneva.

QUIET TIME With fewer businesses open, I know it’s usually going to be quieter. You’d think more people would be out because it’s Sunday, but most of the time people don’t start coming out until about noon or 1.

DAY’S BEGINNING I wake up about 8. I make coffee. I have oatmeal or toast or cereal. I shower and leave from home in the East Bay at 9. I put on my uniform at the station, and I start work at 10.

LINEUP Lineup is in the lieutenant’s office. The lieutenant tells us the wanted people, new crimes in the area, everything going on in the district.

FIVE-BLOCK WALK At 10:45 I catch a bus or walk to my beat. On Sundays and every other day, my beat is the five blocks of Mission from Persia to Geneva. There are a lot of small businesses, apartments, bars, restaurants, drugstores.

LUNCH ON THE ROAD I usually walk alone because my partner is on maternity leave. At 2 or 3 I stop and eat at a restaurant on my beat — there’s all types.

PLAY TIME Some people know me because on Saturdays I play soccer in the Latin League. The merchants get to know you, too.

UP AGAINST IT A lot of juveniles are hanging out in the parking lots, and with it comes the juveniles who want to be involved in gangs — guns and fights come with that. Half my job is juvenile-related. Another 25 percent of the job involves quality of life offenses — drinking, urinating in public. The rest is miscellaneous.

FANS AND FOES Some look on us as the bad guys because we wear a uniform. They might say we stopped them because of whatever race they are. But others say: “God bless you. There’s not too many problems now that we see you more often.”

COMMUNITY POLICING I applied for this beat because I wanted a change. Before, in a patrol car, you’d answer a call, drive away and usually not do followup.

CLEANING UP We try to get rid of the problems, but when you get rid of one, another will take its place. For me personally, I take that as a reality.

GOING HOME As soon as I get home, I’m more relaxed, and I try to put the day of work behind me.
