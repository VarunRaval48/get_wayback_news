Cowell Says He Will Leave Idol

Danny Moloshok/Reuters

Simon Cowell and Paula Abdul last season on “American Idol.” Mr. Cowell says he expects to work with Ms. Abdul again.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By EDWARD WYATT
Published: January 11, 2010

PASADENA, Calif. — Simon Cowell, the acerbic British music producer whose caustic commentary as a judge on “American Idol” has guided the program to the top of the television ratings, announced on Monday that he would leave the program after this season to begin producing and judging a new talent competition, “The X Factor,” for the Fox network in the fall of 2011.

The move is guided in part by the fact that Mr. Cowell owns “The X Factor,” a version of which has been broadcast in Britain since 2004, while he is simply an employee on “American Idol.”

While the American version of “The X Factor” will be broadcast in the fall and “American Idol” will remain in the winter and spring, Mr. Cowell’s other talent-show judging responsibilities, on the British “X Factor” and “Britain’s Got Talent,” will effectively preclude him from continuing to work as a judge on “Idol.” Mr. Cowell is also an executive producer and part owner of “America’s Got Talent,” which is one of NBC’s biggest summer hits.

The deal raises several important questions for Mr. Cowell and for Fox, which is losing the most recognizable face on its biggest hit. Fox executives sought on Monday to play down that aspect of the announcement, saying that “Idol” is bigger than any one person. They noted that “American Idol” had been the most-watched show on television for several years and had stretched its lead over the No. 2 show in each season.

Not everyone agrees, however, about the effect of Mr. Cowell’s decision to leave the show. Fred Silverman, the longtime television executive who at different times headed the programming divisions of NBC, ABC and CBS, said in an interview that he thought Mr. Cowell’s departure would undoubtedly “have a negative effect on the show,” especially in light of the program’s slide in the ratings in each of the last three years.

“He’s the voice everybody wants to hear,” Mr. Silverman said. “It doesn’t mean the show will fail without him. But it will be nowhere near as successful as it was in its heyday.”

Mr. Cowell joined Fox executives at a meeting of the Television Critics Association here to announce the move, which had been widely expected. Peter Rice, the chairman of entertainment for Fox Broadcasting, said the agreement was completed about an hour before it was announced, and he had Mr. Cowell sign a contract for the deal onstage at the meeting.

“We did talk about me staying on both shows,” Mr. Cowell said, adding that he was offered a lot of money to do so. “When we looked at the practicalities of that, it was just about impossible.”

Nevertheless, Mr. Cowell said, “American Idol” is bigger than its biggest star, and he said he believed it would continue to thrive. “I have said all along this show could last 10 to 20 years,” he said. “I’m very comfortable that it will continue to be the No. 1 show.”

It is uncertain, however, whether viewers will turn out in the same strength for two similar talent competitions in one year. Although “Idol” and “X Factor” will not compete directly against each other, they face no shortage of reality competition from other broadcast networks as well as an army of cable channels, all of which seem to be adding to their reality-competition lineups almost daily.

“To expect any new show to come in and attract the 25 million viewers that ‘American Idol’ has is not realistic,” said Brad Adgate, a senior vice president for research at Horizon Media. “The landscape has changed so quickly since 2002,” when “Idol” began, he added. “And I think the show does as well as it does because of him.”

Mr. Adgate noted that when production issues caused the show last season to limit Mr. Cowell’s comments on some performances, many fans howled in protest that they wanted to hear Mr. Cowell’s assessments over those of other judges.

“The X Factor” will differ from “Idol” in several ways, including being open to groups and having no upper age limit on competitors.

Mr. Cowell declined to speculate on who might join him as judges on “X Factor.” He also declined to comment on earlier news reports that he had approached Paula Abdul for a spot on the new show, although he added, “whatever happens, I will be working with her in some capacity.”

Ms. Abdul was in negotiations with Fox and the producers of “Idol” over a new contract last summer when she suddenly announced she was leaving the show. Executives at Fox said privately at the time that the network was not eager for her to continue. She was replaced by Ellen DeGeneres.

Asked on Monday if Ms. Abdul would be welcomed back to Fox on Mr. Cowell’s new show, Mr. Rice said she would.

He declined, however, to speculate on how the network would go about replacing Mr. Cowell on “Idol,” whether through an overhaul of the program’s entire format or by, as it did in replacing Ms. Abdul, looking for someone to fill the fourth spot at the table

“We are announcing it in time that we can investigate how to energize the show,” Mr. Rice said. “We’re not going to make rash decisions.” Neither, he added, will the network be looking for “a Simon clone.”

With “X Factor” scheduled to have its debut for the fall of 2011, Fox will be able to show a full season of “Idol” without Mr. Cowell in the winter and spring of 2011. Mr. Silverman said he believed that the network and Mr. Cowell would realize that going 16 months without Mr. Cowell on the air would hurt both “Idol” and “X Factor,” perhaps producing some compromise to bring Mr. Cowell back for a final “Idol” season next year.
