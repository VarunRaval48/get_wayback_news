Trying to Stop Four Spades, With a Hint and Without

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By PHILLIP ALDER
Published: January 20, 2010

The most successful players at the District 3 Winter Regional in Rye Brook, N.Y., held Jan. 12 to 18, were R. Jay Becker of New York and Michael Kamil of Holmdel, N.J.

Skip to next paragraph

They captured two knockout team titles with Michael Rosenberg of New Rochelle, N.Y., and Richard Zeckhauser of Cambridge, Mass. The diagramed deal occurred during the final of the Friday-Saturday Knockout Teams against Judith Bianco, Win Allegaert, Lapt Chan and Justin Lall of New York.

In the given auction Bianco (South) bid four clubs to help Allegaert (North) judge what to do if East-West went to five hearts. At the other table Lall (East) jumped to four hearts over two spades, and Becker (South) rebid four spades. So the defenders did not know about declarer’s club length.

Against Becker, Chan (West) led the heart ace. Declarer ruffed and played a low trump, East winning with his king and shifting to the club six. South took that and played another trump, cruising to 10 tricks.

Zeckhauser (West) and Rosenberg (East) defended perfectly against Bianco. West also led the heart ace, East playing his ten as a suit-preference signal for diamonds. South ruffed and played a low spade, but Zeckhauser, disregarding the usually reliable “second hand low,” won with his ace. He expected to defeat the contract if his partner had either the ace-queen of diamonds (they would get one spade, two diamonds and a diamond ruff) or the spade king and diamond ace (they would get two spades, one diamond and a diamond ruff). Then West shifted to the diamond seven.

When declarer played low from the dummy, East did his part, ducking the trick to South. (If East had won this trick and returned a diamond, declarer would have taken it in the dummy and discarded her last diamond on the heart king.)

South played another trump, but East won with his king, cashed the diamond ace and gave his partner a diamond ruff for down one.

Plus 420 and plus 50 gave the winners 10 international match points on the board.

The play is interesting if East wins the second trick with his spade king and shifts to a low diamond. South wins with dummy’s king and discards a diamond on the heart king to give this position:

If South now leads either of dummy’s trumps, West wins and plays a diamond to his partner’s ace. Then another diamond promotes the spade nine as the setting trick.

To make the contract declarer must either come to her hand with a club to lead a trump toward dummy’s ten or, much more artistically, lead dummy’s last heart and discard her remaining diamond. Then, if East takes the trick and plays another diamond, South ruffs in her hand and leads a low trump.
