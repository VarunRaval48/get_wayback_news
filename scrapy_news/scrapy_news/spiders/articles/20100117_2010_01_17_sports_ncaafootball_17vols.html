Tennessee Tries to Give Its Fans a Reason to Believe

By RAY GLIER
Published: January 16, 2010

KNOXVILLE, Tenn. — When someone pulls up in a car to take him to the store or to the movies or to go bowling, Bobby Maze, a senior guard on Tennessee’s basketball team, does not immediately jump in. First, he has to ask the big question.

Bobby Maze says Tennessee athletes need to be careful about who they associate with after recent incidents.

“Anything in this car I should know about?” Maze asks. “Tell me now.”

The driver, who could be a friend or an acquaintance, should understand what Maze is talking about. Alcohol. Guns. Drugs. Where is this ride headed, really?

Maze shook his head about the reality of being an athlete at Tennessee in light of what has happened in the past two months.

“You have to be cautious, you have to ask questions,” he said. “You better know who you are getting in the car with.”

On Nov. 12, three football players were charged with armed robbery two blocks from campus. Charges against Janzen Jackson, a star safety, were dropped, but the two others, who were dismissed from the team, were in court last week and made deals on lesser charges.

On Tuesday, wide receiver Nu’Keese Richardson pleaded guilty to attempted robbery and was placed on probation for three years. Two days later, defensive back Michael Edwards pleaded guilty to reckless endangerment and received probation.

On Jan. 1, Tyler Smith, a star basketball forward, was arrested with three other players near campus after a traffic stop. The four were charged with breaking misdemeanor gun, drug and alcohol laws.

Smith was dismissed from the team Jan. 8. Cameron Tatum, a sophomore guard, and Melvin Goins, a junior guard, were suspended for two games and will be reinstated Sunday. Brian Williams, a junior center, remains on indefinite suspension.

The players’ troubles come on the heels of an N.C.A.A. investigation of the Tennessee football program. The inquiry followed the disclosure that female students had gone to South Carolina to help recruit high school players, a possible violation of N.C.A.A. rules.

Under Lane Kiffin, who quit last week to accept the Southern California job, the Volunteers’ football team committed a string of secondary N.C.A.A. violations. The players Kiffin left behind were stunned, but some of their anxiety was relieved Friday night when Louisiana Tech’s Derek Dooley was named the coach.

“This has not been a fun stretch, obviously, when you have these kinds of things,” Athletic Director Mike Hamilton said. “It’s been disheartening and embarrassing, but it does give you the opportunity to re-examine what you are about and address your core values.”

Many felt Kiffin did not hold some of Tennessee’s core values regarding longtime traditions. The acrimony boiled over when he resigned by reading a one-minute statement. Kiffin left with a police escort. Students protested and set fire to a mattress.

It was easier for fans to portray Kiffin as a mercenary because he had no Tennessee ties, but Bruce Pearl, the men’s basketball coach, said it was not necessary to have Tennessee ties to be beloved by the Big Orange faithful.

“I’m passionate, I’m appreciative, I was midmajor my whole career, so this is my first high-major coaching position and I want to show my appreciation,” said Pearl, who is from Boston. “That’s how you assimilate into it. I’m real comfortable with the family.”

The Tennessee football camp seemed split over Kiffin.

“I liked his oomph, liked that energy,” said Nigel Mitchell-Thornton, a linebacker from Stone Mountain, Ga. “I know what people said about him and the traditions, and the traditions are important, but there are other ways of doing things, too.

“I didn’t think things were that bad. He definitely threw you out there on the field and let you compete, which a lot of us liked.”

Pearl said the fans who were angered by the athletes’ arrests would soon remember whom they were rooting for. After the suspensions, Tennessee, with just six scholarship players, stunned No. 1 and unbeaten Kansas, 76-68.

“When things like this happen, your family is the first to criticize you, but they are also the first to forgive you and rally around you,” Pearl said. “The fans have rallied around our basketball team, and they are going to rally around those football student-athletes.”
