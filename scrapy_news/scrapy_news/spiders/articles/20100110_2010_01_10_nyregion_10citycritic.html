Getting There: First, Journey by Keyboard

  • comments
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By ARIEL KAMINER
Published: January 8, 2010

In New York, the shortest distance between two points is not a straight line, it’s an express line. Those bus and subway routes can feel as much a part of the city’s geography as the rivers that divide it and the bridges that span them. But the Metropolitan Transportation Authority’s recent announcement that it plans to eliminate two subway lines and dozens of bus routes will force a lot of New Yorkers to redraw their mental maps, and rethink how they get from point A to point B.

One terrific way to do that is hopstop.com. Wherever you’re going, this site can calculate the most efficient mass-transit route, predict its duration, compare it with the cost and duration of a taxi ride, adjust for time of day, and offer a 360-degree view of the corner where you’ll land. If you don’t like buses, say, or if you will be carrying too much for the subway, you can tailor the request accordingly. Hopstop operates in 16 cities, but it started in New York. Which means that after five years hopping trains and running for buses, local users have left a long data trail.

To help me understand how the proposed cuts would affect riders, the staff reached deep into Hopstop’s big, googly brain and felt around.

Authority officials have been adamant that the cuts, set to begin in June, would have only minimal effects, and in large part, Hopstop’s searches bore that out. For example, of the last 3,000 trips Hopstop users plotted involving the W, one of the two subway lines facing elimination, fewer than 1 percent would increase by 10 minutes or more.

Similar story for the X25 bus, another on the endangered species list.

With the X32 bus, the effect would be more noticeable. The X32 is a weird line, mostly designed to ferry some Bronx Science students back and forth to their homes in Queens. It runs four times a day — two early-morning journeys to the Bronx, two early-afternoon rides back to Queens. If it disappears, those students might switch to a private school bus. But Hopstop says more than 1,000 people requested directions that included the X32 bus in just the past 90 days, clearly not to find out how to get to school. For those people, the cuts would add an average of 23 minutes each way, Hopstop says.

I asked Joe Meyer, the company’s chief executive, to find me the journey that would suffer most without the X32. He came up with a doozy, from DeKalb Avenue in the Bronx, up near Woodland Cemetery, to 26th Avenue in Queens, not far from Fort Totten and Little Neck Bay. Try rerouting that one. I had a city map, a subway map, a bus map and all the commuter rail timetables, but I was still lost. No mere human — not even a Bronx Science student — can optimize all those options.

Hopstop, of course, found me a new route in an instant. It just happened to be a terrible one.

What used to be a 1 hour, 14-minute, one-transfer trip would now take 2 hours and 16 minutes — and would involve a local bus, a Metro-North train, an express subway, a Long Island Rail Road train, another bus, a donkey cart, a hula hoop and a single rusty roller skate. O.K., not the roller skate. But still.

So I tried Trip Planner, the authority’s online tool. This site tracks only subways and buses, and doesn’t have any neat 360-degree camera. Still, it’s surprisingly useful. At the hours the express bus now runs, Trip Planner suggested an alternate that clocked in at 1 hour, 52 minutes.

Google has its own route planner, called Google Transit — useful in many cities, but hard to customize. Still, Google promised to get me to my destination in less than 1 hour, 50 minutes. All the sites used slightly different data sets and allowed for different travel preferences. Hopstop’s route was the most laborious, but also the most adventurous. And by far the most difficult to construct without an algorithm. I decided to give it a test run.

My journey began at 3573 DeKalb Avenue, a modest building on a not-bad block displaying ancient “apartments for rent” signs in flowery mid-century cursive. I walked to the B16, which rolls past the cemetery — especially picturesque with a light dusting of snow. At the Woodlawn Metro-North stop, I waited a cold half-hour, then caught the train to Grand Central Terminal. Most of those miles are strangely removed from time. At any point along the way it could be 2010 or 1950 — except the graffiti confirms it as 1985. I rode past the New York Botanical Garden, past United Pickle and Imperial Damper and Louver, past East Tremont Avenue, and eventually into Midtown.

The No. 7 express was not running, alas, so I took the local, which was decorated with those ironic-heroic portraits of people soldiering forth despite stuffy noses. Fourteen minutes later, the train arrived at 61st Street in Woodside, Queens. I’d missed my Long Island Rail Road train, though, so it was 30 more minutes before I could trundle along 44th Avenue, past the Archie Bunker houses and the halal butchers, of which he would probably not have approved. After 13 more minutes I got off and walked to the B28 bus. It came immediately, and took me, along with some freezing Chinese women, through Auburndale to Bayside and finally — finally! — to the corner of 26th Avenue and Corporal Kennedy Street.

There, behind a hedge the size of a minivan, stood the Ear, Nose and Throat Associates of New York. “Hear better, breathe better, feel better,” the sign said. I did feel better, now that my journey had ended, 2 hours and 45 minutes after it began. I’d gotten to see a lot more of the city and its varied transit options than if I had traveled express. But I was tired and cold, so I began my hourlong journey home.

By cab. I’m not that stupid.
