Nurturing Sanchez Leads Jets to Rely on Him

Barton Silverman/The New York Times

Quarterback Mark Sanchez has no touchdowns or interceptions in the Jets' last two victories.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By DAVE CALDWELL
Published: January 5, 2010

FLORHAM PARK, N.J. — Mark Sanchez’s rookie season has included a series of upgrades, some more noticeable than others. The latest sat at the foot of his locker Tuesday, propped just so in front of a black loose-leaf notebook.

It is a black band that Sanchez can cinch around his forearm while he plays quarterback for the Jets. It has a clear plastic window with a list of plays, arranged by numbers. Rather than call a full play to him over his headset — a mouthful that can be misunderstood — the Jets’ coaching staff calls a number.

Sanchez relays the play to the Jets’ offense, and away they go. The system was used for the first time in last Sunday’s 37-0 victory over the Cincinnati Bengals. Although a playoff berth was no sure thing at the time, Coach Rex Ryan wanted Sanchez to be ready for the postseason.

And now, they are here. The Jets, winners of five of their last six games, are to play the Bengals in a first-round playoff game Saturday in Cincinnati, where it will be freezing, the crowd will be hostile and the Bengals will probably play much harder than they did last weekend.

Although Ryan is a rookie N.F.L. head coach with a deep background as a defensive assistant, he is aware that Sanchez needs nurturing more than pushing.

After practice Tuesday, Ryan said: “He’s probably been better than I’d thought he’d be coming in. He’s smart. He’s competitive. We knew all these things, but I guess just seeing it played out, the sky is the limit for him.

“It’s going to be funny watching how this thing works, because in a year — next year, probably — we are going to be all talking about how he’s the strength of our team and not looking at it as a negative. And I truly believe it’s going to be a matter of time before that happens.”

Ryan is a throwback. He believes that a punishing running game and a suffocating defense wins games, and the Jets are the best in the league at both of those things. And yet he knows that his success in the long run depends on Sanchez’s development.

Sanchez, 23, told a story Tuesday about how distraught he was after the Jets lost to Atlanta, 10-7, on Dec. 20. Sanchez threw three interceptions in a game he mistakenly thought — as did Ryan — that the Jets had to win to stay alive for a playoff berth.

After he showered and dressed but before he attended a news conference, Sanchez said, he walked into the coaches’ locker room at Giants Stadium. Shaking his head, he began a blistering self-critique of his play that day. Sanchez said he did not want to attend the news conference, because he knew he would blame himself. Ryan interrupted him.

“He said: ‘Look, man, this is what we signed up for — you and me,’ ” Sanchez said. “ ‘Ten-plus years — we’re going to be doing this a long time. There are going to be losses, but there are going to be way more wins.’ To hear that from him, he almost gives you the pep talk before you got to face the music. It’s like that after wins, it’s like that after losses. You want to play for this guy.”

The Jets won their next two games, albeit against teams that had already clinched playoff berths, to earn one of two American Football Conference wild-card berths. Sanchez did not throw a touchdown pass in either game, but he did not throw an interception, either.

In the last six games, one of which Sanchez missed with a knee injury, his statistics were not much better than they were in the first 10 games, six of which the Jets lost. But teammates say Sanchez runs the offense with more confidence and precision.

“It’s a pretty complicated offense, but he’s absorbed it really well, and at this point of the season, he knows it inside-out,” tight end Dustin Keller said. “It’s been huge for him. He knows it’s the second season, and he has to play like a veteran now.”

The veteran running back Thomas Jones said Tuesday that he had continued to remind Sanchez that the regular season, as long as it is, served as an opportunity to extend the season. “All that stuff,” Jones said of the regular season, “is for now, for this opportunity.”

Ryan was asked Tuesday if he had been tempted to bench Sanchez when the Jets lost six of seven games after opening the season with three victories. Sanchez threw 14 interceptions in that stretch, including five in an overtime loss to Buffalo on Oct. 18.

“If you got Brett Favre sitting on the bench, you would make that trade,” Ryan said. “We would do that. But you know, we got a lot of confidence in Mark, we always have, and we always felt it gave us the best opportunity to succeed. One of our missions is obviously to give him the playoffs, and he must have done pretty well, because here we are.”

Now the Jets have unfurled a Wildcat package with Brad Smith, the former quarterback at Missouri, running the offense. It has lightened the load even more on Sanchez.

Sanchez seems to realize he was lucky. The Jets have not had to count on him, until now. His rookie season, he said Tuesday, feels as if it has lasted a lifetime. But now, he feels as if all of the missteps along the way have prepared him for this weekend.

“Just making the playoffs, it’s like a new life,” Sanchez said. “It’s like a breath of fresh air, really.”
