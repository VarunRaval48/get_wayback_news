U.S.C. Welcomes Kiffin, and Deals With Questions

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By BILLY WITZ
Published: January 13, 2010

LOS ANGELES — When Lane Kiffin left Tennessee, students burned mattresses and threw debris at cars. He received a much more welcoming reception Wednesday when he was introduced as the new coach of Southern California.

About 150 students packed Heritage Hall, the athletic department building, awaiting Kiffin’s entrance to his news conference, along with the former U.S.C. star Keyshawn Johnson and the current quarterback Matt Barkley.

Instead of torches, the students carried a welcome mat, exchanging high-fives with the brash, 34-year-old Kiffin and greeting him with an off-color chant as he descended a flight of stairs to replace Pete Carroll, who left to coach the N.F.L.’s Seattle Seahawks.

The different scenes on the two campuses served Kiffin well, as upon his introduction he chose to present himself as a different coach than the rulebook-flaunting, smack-talking character who jousted with Florida Coach Urban Meyer and earned himself a tribute in a Lil’ Wayne rap song.

Asked if he had any words for Stanford Coach Jim Harbaugh or U.C.L.A. Coach Rick Neuheisel, Kiffin demurred.

“We don’t need to go out and grab attention, because we have it,” said Kiffin, who agreed to a five-year, $20 million contract, according to The Los Angeles Daily News. When Kiffin outlined his priorities, he insisted he would play by the rules. “No. 1, we will run an extremely clean, disciplined program,” Kiffin said.

Beyond campus, Kiffin’s hiring is being widely viewed as a thumb in the eye of the N.C.A.A. In Kiffin’s less than 14 months at Tennessee, the football program committed six secondary violations and is under investigation for its use of student hostesses in recruiting. Three of Kiffin’s recruits were dismissed from the team after they were arrested for armed robbery.

Now, Kiffin assumes control at a university that is under N.C.A.A. scrutiny. U.S.C. has a hearing before the N.C.A.A. Committee on Infractions on Feb. 19-21, when it will learn the findings of investigations into its football and basketball programs. Coincidence or not, fliers were taped to the doors of the athletic department building warning that there was currently a dead period in recruiting and listing certain activities that were prohibited. But Athletic Director Mike Garrett scoffed at the idea that the N.C.A.A. would view Kiffin’s hiring as a sign that U.S.C. was not taking compliance seriously.

“Oh stop,” Garrett said. “We’ve talked about that. We took sanctions in basketball. Football, we think we have a case. We’ll fight the case and then we’ll see.”

Right now, that is probably less of a concern for U.S.C. fans than this: Can Kiffin coach?

Kiffin acknowledged that as “a very fair” question after his 5-15 record as coach of the Oakland Raiders and a 7-6 mark at Tennessee. But he said it was important to note that he took over two downtrodden programs.

Kiffin’s previous U.S.C. tenure, when he climbed from graduate assistant to offensive coordinator, was marked by his ability to recruit and coach on the teams that won at least a share of two straight national championships.

But he is most remembered for having the Heisman Trophy winner Reggie Bush standing on the sideline on a fourth-and-2 play against Texas in the 2006 Rose Bowl. If successful, the Trojans could have run out the clock, stretched their winning streak to 35 games and won another title. But LenDale White was stopped short and Texas rallied for a 41-38 victory. U.S.C. has not played for a championship since.

Garrett, who would not confirm reports that he went after Oregon State Coach Mike Riley or Jacksonville Jaguars Coach Jack Del Rio, said that Carroll alerted him two weeks ago that he was speaking with Seattle.

“I try to catch people right at the point they’re going to burst out,” Garrett said of Kiffin. “I think he’s right at the cusp of becoming a great coach. That’s what turned me on. When he was here, we knew he was very talented, we knew he was very smart. The question was how would he season? How would he handle it?”

And how did he conclude that Kiffin was ready?

“He got beat up,” Garrett said. “I wanted to know how he got off the mat, how does he perform.”

One of Kiffin’s strongest assets is the staff he has assembled. He will bring with him from Tennessee his father, Monte Kiffin, who was long regarded as one of the N.F.L.’s best defensive coordinators, and Ed Orgeron, who was the defensive line coach and recruiting coordinator at U.S.C. until he left after the 2004 season to become the coach at Mississippi. Also expected to return to U.S.C. is the offensive line coach Tim Davis, who left in 2004 to go to the Miami Dolphins. Davis comes from the University of Minnesota. But the big plum would be the U.C.L.A. offensive coordinator Norm Chow, who is considering an offer that would reportedly double his salary.

The architect of U.S.C.’s offense on its championship teams, Chow has a reputation as a developer of quarterbacks — including the Heisman Trophy winners Ty Detmer, Carson Palmer and Matt Leinart — and an adroit play-caller. Chow has patched up his relationship with Carroll and Kiffin, and was returning Wednesday from the national coaches’ convention in Florida. He and Kiffin had not yet spoken.

Orgeron was already busy calling U.S.C. recruits on Tuesday night. Receiver Kyle Prater, from Hillside, Ill., who had planned to enroll Monday when classes began, opened up his recruitment when Carroll left. Now he plans to enroll at U.S.C. on Monday.

Markeith Ambles, a receiver from McDonough, Ga., who had committed to Tennessee, said he might go to U.S.C. But Kiffin made a point of saying that he would not recruit players who had committed to Tennessee, unless they requested he do so.

That, in itself, was a drastic change from Kiffin’s tack at Tennessee, where he accused Meyer of breaking rules, and was himself accused of telling a South Carolina recruit that he would pump gas if he went there.

“This guy isn’t arrogant,” Monte Kiffin said of his son. “There were some things that were said that were taken out of context. If he was arrogant, I’d slap him upside the head. If he was, I wouldn’t come with him to Tennessee. I wouldn’t be here.”
