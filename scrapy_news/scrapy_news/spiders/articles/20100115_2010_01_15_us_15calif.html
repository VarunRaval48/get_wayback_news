If California Owes You, It Would Like to Pay You

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JESSE McKINLEY
Published: January 14, 2010

SAN FRANCISCO — Somewhere high in the snowy, mountainous wilds of Alpine County, Calif., there is a Californian with a piece of paper worth $1,305.

This person is not alone. Across the state, 89,000 residents and businesses — including 2,315 here in San Francisco alone — are sitting on some $50 million in uncashed i.o.u.’s from the state, a souvenir of California’s most recent, but by no means its last, budget crisis.

John Chiang, the state controller, promised Thursday to contact every one of the nonredeemers in an effort to “close an ugly chapter in California fiscal history.” Mr. Chiang practically begged them to drain the treasury.

“We have your money,” he said. “Ask for it. We want to give it back to you.”

Such sudden largess was greeted happily by money-crunched officials like Pamela Knorr, the administrative officer in Alpine County, who volunteered to hunt down her county’s one mysterious resident.

“We’re so small, I probably know who it is,” Ms. Knorr said. “I’d love to be the bearer of good news for once.”

All told, the state issued about 450,000 i.o.u.’s valued at more than $2.6 billion to taxpayers, vendors and contractors in July after it failed to close a budget gap on time and started to run out of money.

The state started redeeming the i.o.u.’s, with interest, in September after a budget was passed, but some people apparently did not get the message.

Mr. Chiang speculated that some residents might have been confused by the i.o.u.’s, which were labeled as registered warrants, the bureaucratic parlance for the notes. Or, he said, “they might have other things on their mind.”

Nowhere is the problem more acute than in Los Angeles County, where there are some 23,000 i.o.u.’s lying around, worth almost $10 million.

The announcement about the neglected i.o.u.’s came as the state continued to weather grim economic times. A report on Thursday found that some 630,000 homes went into foreclosure in California in 2009, more than in any other state in the nation, according to RealtyTrac, an online real estate company that specializes in foreclosures.

Unemployment also continues to run high in California. It was more than 12 percent in November.

And the state, despite wanting to make good on its markers, is also facing more trouble down the line, including projections showing a $19 billion to $21 billion deficit in the next year and a half.

“We’re going to have plenty of cash challenges,” Mr. Chiang said.

Residents have until September to cash the i.o.u.’s. After that, the state will not honor them and the money will remain in the treasury.

The slow-poke redeemers come from up and down California, including Modoc County — on the Oregon border where six rural residents are owed $182 — and sun-dappled San Diego, where some 6,000 people could claim more than $2.6 million.

In Alpine County, south of Lake Tahoe, there is one outstanding i.o.u. in the county of 1,180 full-time residents. Like many counties, Alpine has been hit hard by cutbacks from the state, Ms. Knorr said, including major reductions in financing for social programs and road maintenance, a vital service in an area where heavy snow can fall from October to May.

Ms. Knorr thought perhaps the Alpine i.o.u. might have been thrown away by one of the county’s many retirees or just a citizen skeptical that anything good could have been coming from the government.

“Maybe they thought,” Ms. Knorr said, “the state was going to sock it to them again.”
