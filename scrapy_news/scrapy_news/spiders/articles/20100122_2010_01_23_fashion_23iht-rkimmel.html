    Putting Clothes in Context

    By SUZY MENKESJAN. 22, 2010

    Inside

      PARIS — Prosthetic masks molded into expressions of greed, gaiety, lasciviousness and fear were worn by figures trying their luck at the casino tables. Yet the rich glamour of their velvet suits in spring green, russet and dusty pink still stood out.

      The American designer Adam Kimmel’s first show on live models, inspired by and in collaboration with the artist George Condo, was a great kickoff to the Paris menswear season. The vitality and originality of the men’s shows is all about embedding the clothes in a context.

      The Comme des Garçons show on Friday reflected the fearful world we live in — hence the bulky bulletproof vests worn under suits and the open-front culottes, sometimes buckled like kilts, on top of the pants.

      “Protection,” said the enigmatic Rei Kawakubo. And though this show did not have the punch of Comme’s army blanket theme of a year ago, there still was something powerfully contemporary in the use of layering and of sturdy fabrics like corduroy and dark plaids. Thick textures also included a wrap skirt in fleece. The kilt and the skirt are re-emerging since their 1980s popularity. But this Comme collection was resolutely masculine, even with pink socks lining the hefty shoes.

      The standout shows are often the simple ones, like Viktor & Rolf’s “Paint it Black,” with the Rolling Stones lyric accompanying a parade of clothes that had darkened textures but never a feeling that they were fancy or artsy. The design duo has come a long way since they first launched Viktor & Rolf Monsieur in Florence in 2003, with themselves as models. For the autumn 2010 season the seamless mix of formal and casual; the knits in neat shapes; the tailored shirts, perhaps with brush strokes of black, produced a sophisticated and classy collection.

      Junya Watanabereinterpreted the Jazz Age and though that idea did not make an exceptional collection, this was a fine show. The skill was in giving a click of interest to the tailoring — double-breasted suits illuminated with red stitching — and then melding formal and casual, which has become the season’s major trend. Mr. Watanabe made that mix more than just putting a nylon parka over a suit or teaming a tailored jacket with jeans. He showed inserts of leather in suits and made the sportswear as powerful as the more formal pieces. The designer also homed in on another strong story: the revival of the camel coat, streamlined for the 21st century.

      The designer Dai Fujiwara literally woke up and smelled the coffee for the Issey Miyake show, where Antologia, his local Tokyo cafe, took center stage. In the program notes its owner described how imagination became reality, as a vat of coffee dye, streaked with foaming milk, turned into swirling patterns. But Mr. Fujiwara never let the theme drown the clothes, showing instead outfits that reflected relaxed daytime customers, chatty intellectuals and streamlined waiters. And, as ever with this house, the backstory was in the complexity of weaving and dyeing that enriches seemingly simple clothes.

      The boxing ring in the center of Jean Paul Gaultier’s runway, where two women in molded bras and silk shorts slugged it out, marked the fault line of the show: the theme overwhelmed the clothes. By the time he came out in a stylish black-hooded terry robe, artistic blood painted on his face, the boxing idea seemed like a gimmick. Close up, and minus the distractions, the JPG clothes were strong and of-the-moment: newly fashionable jersey, perfectly tailored; thick cabled knits; and excellent outerwear.

      Even in the showroom, Yohji Yamamoto got his message across: a graphic take on his signature great coats and roomy jackets, making them in ultra-fine patchworks of tweed or with an interesting twist, like an attached scarf or a single origami of folds on the lapel. Black and white shirts took on eye-popping patterns, as for Op Art. The focus was on the attention to detail, from polka dot linings to brightly stitched button holes.

      Alexis Mabillehas the courage of his convictions: to bring a whimsical romance to menswear. He grounded the show in the slate mansard roofs of Paris, its gray stone walls and today’s leaden skies, but made the clothes malleable, from jersey suits to soft pants or a top draped across the chest.
