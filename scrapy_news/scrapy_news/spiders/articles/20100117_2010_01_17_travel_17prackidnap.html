Practical Traveler

Stroller, Diapers, Paperwork

Joe Raeadle/Getty Images

To travel with a minor across borders, you may need to show documents like a birth or marriage certificate for proof of guardianship.
  • comments
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MICHELLE HIGGINS
Published: January 17, 2010

THE recent return to the United States of a 9-year-old boy who was at the center of an international abduction and custody dispute once again called attention to the issues that can complicate international travel with children, especially when one parent is staying behind.

The boy, Sean Goldman, was turned over to his father on Dec. 24 after a five-year custody battle that began when his mother took the boy to Brazil for what was supposed to be a two-week vacation, but then told Sean’s father that she was staying in Brazil.

The case, while extreme, underscores the complexities of taking a minor abroad. Travel and legal experts say a variety of factors need be considered, ranging from paperwork requirements to dealing with different surnames for the parent and child.

In the Goldman case, Sean’s mother, Bruna Bianchi, requested that his father, David Goldman, sign a general permission statement for travel from June to July of 2004, according to Mr. Goldman’s lawyer, Patricia E. Apy. Later, Ms. Bianchi, who died in 2008, declared her intention to keep their son and filed for divorce. The case dragged on in both the American and Brazilian courts until Brazil’s chief Supreme Court justice ordered the boy returned to his father late last month.

The case represents every parent’s nightmare. Parental child abductions, though relatively rare, have been rising. During the 2008 fiscal year, through Sept. 30, the State Department was notified of 1,082 new outgoing international parental child abductions, with the majority pertaining to Mexico, Canada, Britain, Japan and India. That’s up from 794 in fiscal year 2007 and 642 in 2006.

Carefully considering which travel documents to apply for is particularly important for multicultural or divorced or separated families, Ms. Apy said. This is true even if that couple is happily married. For example, parents from different countries sometimes agree to have their children registered as citizens of both. “Later, when there is conflict,” she said, “those registrations serve to complicate the legal issues, or even prevent the timely return of children.”

Parents in a contentious relationship with an ex-spouse may have similar concerns, even if abduction isn’t a fear. If you are worried that the other parent may try to take your child out of the country against your will, or if your views on international travel for your child simply differ from that of your ex, it may be advisable to include in a custody agreement a statement prohibiting your child from traveling abroad without your permission or that of the court.

Once the child has a passport, however, it can be difficult to prevent the other parent from removing the child to another country without your permission, according to the State Department. Strict rules help prevent a passport from being issued to your child without your consent. For children under 16, both parents must appear together with the child to sign the application. Otherwise, written and notarized permission from an absent parent or another documented explanation — like proof of sole custody of a child, an adoption decree or the death certificate of a deceased parent — must be supplied.

You may also ask that your child’s name be entered into the State Department’s Children’s Passport Issuance Alert Program, which alerts parents if anyone applies for a United States passport for the child. Once out of the country, however, American court custody decisions may not be recognized if one of the parents is a citizen of the other country. For example, almost all children born to a Japanese parent and a United States citizen may travel on Japanese passports issued in the United States even if the left-behind parent in the United States does not agree, according to the State Department. Some countries, including Mexico and Canada, require written permission for a child traveling with one parent, primarily to combat abductions and to comply with the Hague Abduction Convention, designed to help participating countries resolve disputes.

Even when nothing as drastic as abduction is involved, parents are advised to know the rules concerning travel with children. You can check the State Department’s consular information sheets (travel.state.gov/travel) for details on entry and exit requirements.

Being prepared to prove that your child is yours may be the last thing on your mind when checking to see if the car seat, stroller and diaper bag are packed, but in these times of heightened security, it can avoid hassles that might disrupt your trip.

Jane Dunham, 50, of Westerville, Ohio, was caught off guard a few years ago when traveling with her two daughters, who go by their father’s last name. Delta stopped the trio on their way to Cancún because they didn’t have a notarized letter of consent for the trip from the father. “I scrambled to call him and get him to fax a letter to the airline that had a copy of his driver’s license on it,” Ms. Dunham said. “We nearly missed the flight.”

That experience prompted her to carry documentation every time she travels, including the death certificate for her husband, who has since died.

It’s best to be prepared even if the place you’re visiting doesn’t explicitly say it requires such forms. Individual customs or immigration agents have a certain amount of discretion as to when documentation must be checked, and airlines and cruise companies have been known to ask for documentation even if the country requiring it doesn’t.

To cover all your bases, consider drafting a notarized permission letter from both birth parents or legal guardians for any time your child travels with someone else, including grandparents, neighbors, the babysitter or the other parent. If that’s not possible, another documented explanation, like proof of sole custody of a child, an adoption decree or the death certificate of the absent parent, can be presented instead.

If you have a different last name from the child’s, bring a copy of the birth certificate for proof of guardianship. If you have remarried and have a new last name, a marriage certificate or other legal paperwork that links your name back to the one listed on the birth certificate can be helpful.

FamilyTravelForum.com, a travel planning site that offers consulting services including information on documents parents might need when traveling abroad, provides free permission forms to registered users. Parents can also draft their own, acknowledging whom their child will be traveling with and citing the dates of the trip. A witness and notary public seal are recommended. For something more comprehensive, Forms4Travel.com offers legal forms a traveling parent or child might need, from $12.95.

Sure, all this paperwork may seem like overkill. There are plenty of parents who pass through immigration without ever being asked for any of it.

And some parts of the procedure may be out of the parents’ hands. Children are more frequently being asked questions by immigration or security officials about whom they are traveling with. That’s what happened when Jim Fahey took his two toddlers on a vacation to Mexico last February. When returning to the States, a customs official in Houston greeted the children and playfully pointed to Mr. Fahey and asked who he was. “I was relieved that they said ‘Daddy’ and not, ‘I don’t know,’ ” Mr. Fahey said.
