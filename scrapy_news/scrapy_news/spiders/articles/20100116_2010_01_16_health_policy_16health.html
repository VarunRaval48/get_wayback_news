In Health Talks, President Is Hands-Off No More

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By SHERYL GAY STOLBERG and DAVID M. HERSZENHORN
Published: January 15, 2010

WASHINGTON — President Obama has taken full control of the health care negotiations, casting himself for the first time in the role of mediator between the House and Senate during a 72-hour marathon of talks that have turned his White House into a de facto Congressional conference.

Mr. Obama spent more than six hours with Democratic leaders on Wednesday, slipping in and out of health care talks to coordinate a response to the earthquake in Haiti. He spent much of Thursday in health care talks, ducking out in the evening to address House Democrats and attend his daughter Malia’s band recital (she plays flute) and then returning to the negotiating table until 1 a.m., shortly before the group recessed for the night.

He was back at it Friday afternoon, holed up once again in the Cabinet Room from 1 p.m. to 3:45 p.m., at which point the White House announced that Mr. Obama was done holding or attending health care meetings, at least for now — a signal that officials are waiting for cost analysis from the Congressional Budget Office.

“It was always our sense that once the bills were passed that he would have to play a much more direct role in helping work out the details,” his senior adviser, David Axelrod, said in an interview, as the talks went on down the hall from his West Wing office. “This has been a prodigious task from start to finish; we knew it would be.”

Mr. Obama has always had political ownership of the bill, and with Republican attacks on it mounting and public opinion moving against him, he has become more assertive about its benefits, telling House Democrats that if Republicans want to campaign against the measure, “that is a fight I want to have.”

But Mr. Obama has not been consistently involved in hashing out details of the legislation until now. Lawmakers and Congressional aides said his personal involvement was crucial in forging a deal on a proposed excise tax on high-cost, employer-sponsored insurance policies, which had loomed over the negotiations as one of the sharpest differences between the House and Senate versions of the health care legislation.

“All of us are indebted to him for the incredible time and his attention to the details of the legislation that he has invested,” the House speaker, Nancy Pelosi, said.

Senator Dianne Feinstein, Democrat of California, in a telephone interview from her home state, said, “I think the president has to do this or there won’t be a bill, candidly.”

By any measure, the level of presidential involvement is extraordinary. Tom Daschle, the former Senate majority leader, said the last time he could remember such round-the-clock talks was 15 years ago, during the government shutdown in early 1995, when leaders closeted themselves in the Oval Office with President Bill Clinton and Vice President Al Gore.

“It certainly demonstrates — not that it needed to be demonstrated — the president’s commitment,” Mr. Daschle said.

But the longer the talks go on, the more nervous Democrats appear. Mrs. Feinstein said calls to her office were running four to one against the measure, and warned that she would wait until she saw the final product of the negotiations before committing her vote.

Representative Frank Pallone Jr., Democrat of New Jersey and chairman of the Energy and Commerce Subcommittee on Health, said he was “reserving judgment on the agreement to tax high-cost plans, adding, “I am not saying that I or anybody else has totally bought into it.”

And even as they tried to resolve their disagreements, Congressional negotiators and the president stirred up a tempest on Friday by reopening an issue on which the House and Senate agreed — establishing procedures for federal approval of generic versions of expensive biotechnology drugs. The House and Senate bills give brand-name companies 12 years to market drugs without fear of generic competition; Mr. Obama wants generics to get to market sooner.

Mr. Obama had said from the outset that he was going to leave the details of health legislation to Congress — and for months he did just that. But in recent weeks he has been stepping up his involvement.

He met personally with top House leaders last week and laid the groundwork for a compromise by telling them he was committed to including the excise tax in the bill — even though many rank-and-file House Democrats oppose it.

Mr. Obama also told the House leaders that he favored a Senate provision to create an independent agency to recommend Medicare savings, and that he preferred the Senate approach on taxes and fees. But he also told the House leaders that he was committed to brokering fair agreements on countless other differences.

Representative Charles B. Rangel, Democrat of New York, and chairman of the tax-writing Ways and Means Committee, said lawmakers appreciated the president’s involvement — even when they disagreed. In a recent interview, Mr. Rangel described how he preferred the House proposals to help pay for the legislation.

As for Mr. Obama, he was trying to maintain some good humor. Earlier in the week, after a long day of juggling health care and the Haiti earthquake, the president walked out of the White House Situation Room after a briefing on search and rescue operations, Mr. Axelrod said, “and said, sort of wryly, ‘I guess we can take some solace that the H1N1 flu is under better control.’ ”

Robert Pear contributed reporting.
