A Night Out With | Jay Harrington

Finally, All the Beer Is His

Stephanie Diani for The New York Times

PLAYFUL The actor Jay Harrington, No. 5, with teammate Harold Augenstein in a Hollywood basketball league game.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JAMIE DIAMOND
Published: January 8, 2010

Santa Monica, Calif.

JAY HARRINGTON was lying on the floor. Not because he’d been knocked down — that would come later. He was warming up before going to play in the Entertainment League, a Hollywood basketball group.

His uniform was green with white stripes; his face was red with exertion. “Oh, I’m getting old,” he said.

Mr. Harrington, 38, is trim and boyish. Along with Portia de Rossi, he stars in “Better Off Ted” on ABC, a deft satire about a deceitful multinational corporation trying to make a profit through weird science. Mr. Harrington plays Ted Crisp, the head of a research department that concocts, among other things, a tooth whitener that kills all the world’s fish and unbreakable plates that can withstand gun blasts but weigh 27 pounds each.

Inside the gym: the squeak of shoes against the wooden floor, the thunk of the basketball, the smell of steamy socks. Mr. Harrington ran backward with an intent look on his face, occasionally colliding with people and falling down. Just before the buzzer sounded to end the first half, a player on his team swooped up and shot the ball into the basket. “We did that on purpose,” Mr. Harrington said grandly. Nonetheless, the opposing team won, 46-39.

Game over, Mr. Harrington met up with some former roommates and his younger brother, Adam, also an actor, at N.Y. & C., a pizza place here. The talk turned to travel plans. The Harrington brothers were about to fly home to Boston — on separate flights, within a half-hour of each other. “We’re like the president and the vice president,” Jay Harrington said. “We take separate flights for the good of the country.”

Perhaps because of his clean-cut looks and strong hairline, Mr. Harrington has been compared to Jon Hamm and George Clooney. But he doesn’t buy the idea that he embodies a kind of old-fashioned male glamour. “It’s because I wear suits on the show,” he said, “and I walk calmly in the midst of chaos, which makes me seem manly.”

He started acting early, at a children’s theater company near his family’s home on Cape Cod, but it took some time to land a starring role on a well-regarded series. “Better Off Ted” is now in its second season, but only last year did Mr. Harrington move into his own place, without roommates. “When I look into the refrigerator,” he said, “I know the beer’s all mine.”

And now, when he’s out in public, people recognize him — sort of. “They usually say, ‘Didn’t I go to high school with you?,’ ” he said, trying in vain to cut a slice of pizza with a fork. “Luckily, that worked out really well at my 20th high school reunion, because then I could say, ‘That’s right, I did go to school with you.’ ”

Giving up on the pizza, Mr. Harrington started to fidget. He needed to go home and pack. Very lightly. He hates carrying suitcases, so three days earlier, he had sent a box of his clothes home by FedEx. None of his pals seemed impressed by this level of planning. “It’s guy packing,” said Scott Windhauser, a former roommate. “You stuff your clothes into a box. Your mom washes them for you.”
