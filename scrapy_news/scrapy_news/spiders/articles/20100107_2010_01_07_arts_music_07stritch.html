Music Review | Elaine Stritch

Sondheims Wisdom, With Wistful Fragility

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By STEPHEN HOLDEN
Published: January 6, 2010

One reason any singing actor with a modicum of talent can’t go wrong interpreting the songs of Stephen Sondheim is that they make whoever sings them sound like a genius and a sage. In a fusion of sensibilities, the composer’s complicated thoughts become the singer’s, so that the performer’s best possible self — a more intuitive, smarter self (Mr. Sondheim doesn’t do stupid) — comes to the fore.

That’s why an all-Sondheim program like Elaine Stritch’s new show, “Elaine Stritch Singin’ Sondheim ... One Song at a Time,” at the Café Carlyle is so touching. The selections — a baker’s dozen — constitute a handpicked, personal anthology of the composer’s observations on life, time, relationships and show business, expressed by supremely articulate stage characters, whose thoughts are indelibly stamped with Ms. Stritch’s outsize personality.

Ms. Stritch, who turns 85 on Feb. 2, has always been a loudmouth life-of-the party type with a big streak of wistful show biz sentimentality; she is an old hand at blinking back tears while putting on a rueful smile. Even now she conveys the childlike wonder of a stage-struck teenager who can’t believe her good fortune to have entered the magic kingdom.

Long ago she refined the mask of a clown who can stare you down with a withering glare then take it all back the next moment with a sly grin. At Tuesday’s opening-night show, songs were delivered as half-spoken, half-sung dramatic monologues illustrated with incisive, sometimes mimelike body language. Jonathan Tunick’s dry, efficient arrangements for a six-member band led by her pianist, Rob Bowman, stayed out of her way.

The new element was Ms. Stritch’s discernible fragility. Either she no longer feels the need to sustain the world-conquering bravado of an emotional bombardier, or she lacks the stamina. The show is wistful, reflective, funny and overwhelmingly retrospective in mood. An awareness of impending mortality seeps into several songs. “Broadway Baby,” a number to which she triumphantly laid claim 25 years ago at Lincoln Center’s “Follies in Concert” was bisected on Tuesday into a before-and-after interpretation, the first half quietly ruminative, the second half aggressive.

The evening began with a lightly self-mocking “I Feel Pretty,” in which she embodied a happy-sad clown striking comic poses. She immediately plunged into a version of “Rose’s Turn” (from “Gypsy”) that made you wish she had played Mama Rose onstage 30 years ago when the time was right. Only roughly following the melody, she turned the repeated exclamation “for me” into a hard-boiled growl of raw id.

Depending on who is singing it and how, that Rorschach of a standard, “Send In the Clowns,” can be a rueful reflection on career choices and romantic second chances or the end of life. Ms. Stritch’s version turned it into a stoic, good-natured acceptance of mortality.

“The Road You Didn’t Take” (from “Follies”) was appended to Ms. Stritch’s story of how she chose to appear onstage in Los Angeles rather than on Broadway in the original production of “Anyone Can Whistle.” “Everybody Says Don’t” rang as a personal credo about the need to take chances and break rules.

The show’s most remarkable moment was Ms. Stritch’s dry-eyed, spoken recitation of the lyrics from “Every Day a Little Death” (from “A Little Night Music”). Although the song is about the steady erosion of a relationship through repeated betrayals, Ms. Stritch made it about much, much more. It became a song about time eating away at everything. As she spoke, you could almost hear the clock ticking.

Elaine Stritch performs through Jan. 30 at the Caf Carlyle, at the Carlyle Hotel, 35 East 76th Street, Manhattan; (212) 744-1600, thecarlyle.com.
