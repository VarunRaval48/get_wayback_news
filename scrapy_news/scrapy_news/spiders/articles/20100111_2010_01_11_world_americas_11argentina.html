Argentine President and Central Bank in Standoff

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By ALEXEI BARRIONUEVO
Published: January 10, 2010

RIO DE JANEIRO — A disputed plan by President Cristina Fernández de Kirchner of Argentina to use billions of dollars in foreign currency reserves to repay debt has inflamed political tensions in that country, Latin America’s third-largest economy.

Mrs. Kirchner’s cash-poor government is seeking to use $6.5 billion of Argentina’s nearly $48 billion in central bank reserves to help cover $13 billion in international debt that is due this year. She has not explained in detail how the central bank reserves would be replenished.

But after her central bank president, Martín Redrado, refused to support the plan, Mrs. Kirchner fired him by decree on Thursday. One day later a federal judge, María José Sarmiento, blocked Mrs. Kirchner’s plan to tap the reserves and ordered Mr. Redrado reinstated, saying that only Congress could remove him.

On Saturday the government appealed the two court rulings, Cabinet Chief Aníbal Fernández said on television.

The standoff between the president and her central bank has shaken Argentina’s financial markets, and opposition politicians have charged that the government is seeking to undermine the bank’s autonomy. It has also highlighted what critics call Mrs. Kirchner’s tendency to try to govern by decree, and her willingness to seize seemingly autonomous funds to try to maintain populist economic policies and increase her flagging popularity, political analysts said.

The latest move suggests that the president is unwilling to adjust her governing style, even after her wing of the Peronist Party lost control of Congress in elections last June.

“This is the beginning of what will probably be a long and complicated battle between the government and Congress and, potentially, the Supreme Court,” said Daniel Kerner, analyst with the Eurasia Group, a political risk consulting firm in New York.

Argentina’s Congress is in recess until March, but opposition lawmakers, including Vice President Julio Cobos, called on lawmakers last week to return early for an emergency session of the lower house on Jan. 20.

Mr. Fernández, the cabinet chief, said in a radio interview that Mr. Cobos was behind Mr. Redrado’s decision to not resign after Mrs. Kirchner requested it. Mr. Cobos, who has said he would run for president in 2011, has been at odds with Mrs. Kirchner since voting against a plan to increase export taxes on soybeans.

Mr. Redrado said Friday that he was convinced that he had fulfilled his mission “to protect the savings of all Argentines” by rejecting the government’s call to dismiss him. He noted that the central bank had had 55 presidents in its 75-year history. “That makes the Argentine system unstable,” he said.

Argentina’s economic policies, including heavy subsidies of food and fuel, and outside criticism of its reporting of economic statistics have hampered its ability to lure foreign investment and contributed to heavy capital flight over the past two years.

Still, financial markets responded positively in recent weeks to the government’s announcements that it would restructure $20 billion in foreign debt from the 2001-2 default.

The decision to use central bank reserves echoes Mrs. Kirchner’s moves to retake control of the private airline Aerolíneas Argentinas last year and to seize $30 billion in private pension funds.

On Friday, she defended her move to tap central bank reserves, saying in a televised appearance that it would keep Argentina from paying “fat profits” to speculators. “It is much better to use the reserves than to borrow at interest rates of 15 or 14 percent, when the reserves are barely earning 0.5 or 1 percent,” she said. “A person with just an elementary school education would understand this.”

Judge Sarmiento refused to hear appeals over the weekend. She complained that a police vehicle had been posted outside her home as a means of apparent government pressure. “This doesn’t seem reasonable,” she said in an interview with TeleNoticias, a TV channel. “There is a patrol car at my house and I have never asked for a guard at any moment. I don’t need one.”

Mr. Kerner of the Eurasia Group said that if the government successfully appealed the court’s suspension of its latest decree, it is possible it would be able to spend central bank reserves before Congress or the Supreme Court had a chance to declare the decree unconstitutional.
