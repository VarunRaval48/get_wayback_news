Sorting Out Legislation

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MICHELLE QUINN
Published: January 8, 2010

The California State Legislature convened this week to begin dealing with a projected $19.9 billion budget deficit over the next 18 months, a situation Gov. Arnold Schwarzenegger described as “our Katrina.” Beyond the budget, state legislators have other issues to contend with, including these three legislative proposals, all of which arise from local concerns or incidents.

AB 1601 | D.U.I. Assemblyman Jerry Hill, Democrat of San Mateo, said he was moved to change state law after reading about a San Mateo County man who was still driving with a valid license after his eighth drunken driving conviction. Mr. Hill’s legislation would authorize judges handling drunken driving cases to consider a person’s entire record beyond the current 10-year limit.

It would also allow judges to permanently suspend a person’s license after three or more drunken driving convictions. Currently, judges can permanently take away the license of a person found to be under the influence only if someone has been hurt or killed.

From 1983 to 2006, 310,971 people in California were convicted of D.U.I. or alcohol-related reckless driving convictions at least three times, according to the state’s office of traffic safety.

AB 984 | WITNESSES News coverage of the October gang rape of a student at Richmond High School prompted Assemblyman Pedro Nava, Democrat of Santa Barbara, a former prosecutor, to introduce a bill that would make those who observe a rape or murder of a person of any age liable for not reporting it.

The Richmond attack went on for more than two hours with multiple bystanders, the police have said. If the victim had been 13, rather than 16, those who witnessed the crime could be prosecuted for failing to report it. Six people have been arrested in relation to the attack.

Under Mr. Nava’s bill, observers who fail to report could face misdemeanor charges and punishment of up to six months in jail and a $1,500 fine.

“The fact that this girl’s episode could have been brought to a close almost immediately is too much to bear,” he said. “It isn’t as if I am asking people to intervene to put themselves in harm’s way.”

Senator Leland Yee, Democrat of San Francisco, is planning to introduce a related bill that would make an observer legally obligated to report a felony level sexual assault against a victim 18 or younger, a spokesman said.

AB 390 | LEGAL MARIJUANA When Assemblyman Tom Ammiano, Democrat of San Francisco, introduced AB390 last February, he said he wanted to provide the state some economic relief, to the tune of $1.5 billion, and start a conversation about the effectiveness of drug enforcement.

The bill would allow adults 21 and older to legally possess, grow and sell marijuana. The state would charge a $50 per ounce fee and a 9 percent tax on retail sales.

The bill has no strong support in the legislature, and since it was introduced a ballot initiative that mirrors it may go to voters in November. Mr. Ammiano said that if his bill does not proceed quickly in the next month, he will hold it to await the results of the vote.
