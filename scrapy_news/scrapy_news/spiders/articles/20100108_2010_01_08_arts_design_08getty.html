Getty Museum Director to Leave Post This Month

Monica Almeida/The New York Times

Michael Brand, the Getty Museum director, on the grounds of the Getty Villa in 2006.

  • Print
  • Reprints
By RANDY KENNEDY and CAROL VOGEL
Published: January 7, 2010

The J. Paul Getty Museum in Los Angeles said on Thursday that Michael Brand, who took over as its director at the end of 2005, is stepping down at the end of the month.

In a brief telephone interview Mr. Brand declined to explain his departure. Several museum officials and others in the art world with knowledge of the situation said that Mr. Brand and James N. Wood, the president and chief executive of the Getty Trust, the umbrella organization that oversees the museum, had sharply different ideas about the direction the museum should take, though the specifics of their disagreement were unclear.

Mr. Brand, 51, said that his decision to resign had been made recently and that the events that led to the decision had “been brewing since before Christmas.” He said he had no immediate plans to go to another institution and would continue to live in the museum director’s residence through the year and receive his salary until the end of January 2011, past the expiration of his five-year contract in December 2010.

“I obviously leave with great pride about what we’ve been able to do here in four years,” Mr. Brand said. “If you think back to December 2005, it was a very different museum.”

He took over at a troubled time for the museum and its parent, the Getty Trust. Less than two months after Mr. Brand was named to his position the trust’s president, Barry Munitz, resigned under pressure amid questions about his use of the trust’s money. Mr. Munitz, whose travel and expense spending were being investigated by the California attorney general’s office, was required to repay the trust $250,000 and was not given a severance package. Deborah Gribbon, the museum’s director before Mr. Brand, resigned in 2004 after disputes with Mr. Munitz.

David Bomford, the associate director for collections at the museum, is to serve as interim director until Mr. Brand’s successor is named.

After Mr. Brand took over the Getty Museum, and Mr. Wood, the former longtime director of the Art Institute of Chicago, was named to run the Getty Trust at the end of 2006, the perception in the museum world was that the Getty has begun healing from its wounds.

But historically, museums with more than one tier of leadership like the Getty have been troubled by power struggles. In the 1980s and 1990s the Metropolitan Museum of Art was run by Philippe de Montebello as its director, who oversaw curatorial affairs, and William H. Luers as its president, who oversaw the museum’s administration. Mr. de Montebello and Mr. Luers were said to have had a strained partnership, one that was dissolved in 1999, when Mr. de Montebello was given full charge of the museum, and Mr. Luers retired.

For 14 years the Philadelphia Museum of Art had the same two-tiered system and that too was dissolved in 1997 in favor of a single leader.

The Getty Trust, which oversees the museum, a research wing, a conservation wing and a foundation, has often struggled with competing interests among its branches and the relationship of those branches’ leaders to the leadership of the trust.

During Mr. Brand’s tenure he led negotiations that resulted in more than 40 antiquities in the Getty’s collection being returned to Italy and Greece, which had asserted that the pieces had been looted.

With the reopening of the Getty Villa in 2006 he oversaw two campuses for the museum, and he opened the museum’s Center for Photographs, one of the largest spaces in the country dedicated to the display of photography.

“Dr. Brand not only led efforts to settle issues impacting the museum’s antiquities collection, he worked to frame a new acquisitions policy for the Getty that squarely addresses provenance issues, and he has been a leader within the profession in this regard,” Mr. Wood said in a statement. “I have every confidence that he will excel in whatever he chooses to do in the years ahead.”

A native of Australia, Mr. Brand was a rising star in the museum world and had been on short lists for several other museum directorships when he made a huge leap to the Getty from the Virginia Museum of Fine Arts in Richmond, which he had run for four years.

He was the assistant director of the Queensland Art Gallery in Australia at 38, and took the director’s job four years later in Richmond, where he embarked on an aggressive mission to build the museum’s holdings, shepherd donations of major collections and raise $100 million for an expansion.
