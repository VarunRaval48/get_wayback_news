To-Do List Grows for Leadership Group

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By DANIEL WEINTRAUB
Published: January 2, 2010

The Silicon Valley Leadership Group represents business interests in a region known for being on the leading edge of innovation. But when it comes to politics, the group is decidedly retro, a throwback to the days when there was a viable center in California’s civic affairs.

With nearly 300 companies on its roster, from Accenture to Yahoo, the group stands out as a small island of ideological diversity in a sea of partisan polarization.

The group is for taxes or against them, depending on how they are structured; for more regulation or less of it, depending on what is being regulated; and for affordable housing, transportation financing and education reform.

A cynic might say the group’s guiding philosophy is a paraphrase of that old General Motors line: What’s good for Silicon Valley is good for California. And the group’s agenda for 2010 certainly reflects its narrow self-interest. But it goes deeper than that.

“There are several issues where the traditional Democrat or the traditional Republican positions are a fit for the innovation economy, and several where they are left scratching their heads,” Carl Guardino, the group’s president and chief executive officer, said in an interview late last month.

“We are not just immediately out-of-the-gate antitax,” Mr. Guardino said. “If you are going to have a good education system, quality transportation and water systems, you have to pay for those things. So sometimes our position on infrastructure issues leaves Republicans scratching their heads. But often our position on tax and regulatory issues can leave Democrats scratching their heads.”

The group’s members supported billions of dollars in general tax increases that were part of a budget package last February. But they also lobbied hard to win a tax cut that some critics described as a needless giveaway to corporations at a time when the state is desperate for cash.

That tax break will let companies choose whether to have their income tax based on the proportion of their total sales occurring in California or on a combination of their sales and their operations here, including payrolls and property. The new rule will mean a tax cut for firms that have a big presence in California but sell most of their products in other states and countries.

Lenny Goldberg, who runs the California Tax Reform Association with support from organized labor, hopes to repeal the tax break with a ballot measure next year. Mr. Goldberg said it was an embarrassment when Silicon Valley — including the leadership group — lobbied to give its companies a choice of the old income tax system or the new one, allowing them to use whichever method yielded a smaller tax bill in any given year.

Defeating Mr. Goldberg’s initiative will be a priority for the leadership group in 2010. But the group’s to-do list is topped by two other measures already scheduled for next year’s ballots.

One is a June measure that would create open primaries in California elections. If the proposal passes, voters would be able to choose among all candidates in the primaries without regard to party affiliation. The top two finishers would advance to a run-off election.

“The open primary,” Mr. Guardino said, would help “free well-meaning legislators from the grips of the party caucuses.” The result might be a Legislature that looks a little more like Silicon Valley and less like Orange County or Marin County.

The leadership group’s other major priority for 2010 will be passing an $11 billion bond measure to finance a historic overhaul of the state’s water management system. The proposal seeks to safeguard the water supply while repairing and preserving the environmentally fragile Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta. With much of Silicon Valley’s water supply coming from the delta, the group sees passage of the bond as crucial to the region’s future.

Between the June and November elections, the group will be fighting in the Legislature to expand financing for affordable housing, protect transportation funding from possible budget cuts and overhaul the management of California’s public schools.

Allies in these battles will come from the left, the right and the center, depending on the issue. The leadership group, in other words, will be in the middle of everything.

Daniel Weintraub has reported on California politics and policy for more than 20 years.
