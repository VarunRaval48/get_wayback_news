Giants Have Lots of Time Off Ahead, but the Vikings Are Hoping for a Week

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By PAT BORZI
Published: January 2, 2010

EDEN PRAIRIE, Minn. — At the south end of the Minnesota Vikings’ chilly Winter Park field house Thursday, defensive players broke into position groups to start practice. Quickly, assistants offered telling signs of how the suddenly vulnerable Vikings defense might deal with two critical injuries in Sunday’s game against the Giants at the Metrodome.

The massive run-stopping tackle Pat Williams, his right elbow in a brace, stood to the side while the defensive line coach Karl Dunbar walked his starters through the finer points of the Giants’ zone-blocking techniques. And in the middle of the field, the veteran cornerback Antoine Winfield — playing through a right foot injury — took an inside position in the Vikings’ package of six defensive backs, instead of lining up on an outside receiver as he usually does.

Williams, who sat out Monday night’s 36-30 overtime loss at Chicago, is listed as questionable for Sunday, and Winfield is probable. The game is meaningless for the Giants, who are out of playoffs for the first time since 2004, but it holds significance for the Vikings, the National Football Conference North champions. Minnesota needs a victory and a loss or a tie by Philadelphia to clinch a first-round bye.

Sunday’s game could turn on whether either of the two shaky, injury-depleted defenses can right themselves. Minnesota’s defensive coordinator, Leslie Frazier, in his third season, is not facing the same job scrutiny as his Giants counterpart, Bill Sheridan. Frazier has a successful three-year track record, and some Vikings fans automatically blame Coach Brad Childress anytime something goes wrong.

Still, Frazier, who is often mentioned as a future head coach, must coax more sacks and takeaways from a group with championship aspirations that has not played its best in almost a month.

“It’s important for our defense to finish strong,” Frazier said.

The Vikings (11-4), who are 7-0 at home, had hoped to clinch the No. 2 seed by now. But a stretch of three losses in four games took away the option of resting starters for the playoffs, as the Giants did in last year’s regular-season finale against Minnesota.

“We’re not even considering that,” Childress said. “That’s a luxury.”

Most of the national focus on the Vikings in recent weeks involved the offense — disagreements between Childress and quarterback Brett Favre on play calling, and the star running back Adrian Peterson’s drop-off in production (no 100-yard games since Nov. 15) and penchant for fumbling (he has lost six, the most of any N.F.L. runner).

But the defense has slipped, too. Beginning with a 30-17 loss at Arizona on Dec. 6, when middle linebacker E. J. Henderson sustained a season-ending broken leg, the Vikings gave up more than 360 yards of offense three times in four games.

The Cardinals double-teamed the All-Pro end Jared Allen and held the Vikings’ N.F.L.-leading pass rush without a sack, which allowed Larry Fitzgerald and Anquan Boldin to exploit weaknesses in the Minnesota secondary. Quarterback Kurt Warner threw for 285 yards and 3 touchdowns as Fitzgerald and Boldin combined for 21 catches.

“We were just sluggish,” defensive end Ray Edwards said. “I don’t think they did anything spectacular.”

Winfield, recovering from a broken foot, missed a game for the sixth time this season. The next week, he led the Vikings with nine tackles in a 30-10 home victory over Cincinnati. But on Monday, he struggled in one-on-one coverage. Devin Aromashodu beat him on Jay Cutler’s winning 39-yard touchdown pass. Winfield said he was amenable to shifting to the nickel, or anywhere else.

“I want to be out there,” Winfield said.

Pass coverage is not the only problem. The Vikings, who have had the N.F.L.’s No. 1 run defense the last three seasons, have permitted four straight opponents to surpass 100 yards. More quarterback pressure is vital. Only five of the Vikings’ N.F.L.-best 45 sacks have come the last four weeks.

“We’re all grown men,” Edwards said. “We all know what our job is. We need to do our jobs better than what we’ve been doing.”

For Allen, the solution is simple: stuff the Giants’ run game on first and second downs.

“If we can do some things to get them down in the score, or in third and longs, we’ll get some one-on-ones and some good opportunities,” he said.
