Charles Kleibacker, Fashion Designer, Dies at 88

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By ERIC WILSON
Published: January 7, 2010

Charles Kleibacker, a former fashion designer whose taste for intricately constructed gowns gave him the nickname Master of the Bias in the 1960s, died Sunday in Columbus, Ohio. He was 88.

Designs by Mr. Kleibacker, who was an adjunct curator in Ohio.

The cause was pneumonia, said the Columbus Museum of Art, where Mr. Kleibacker had been an adjunct curator of design, overseeing several influential exhibitions, since retiring.

His most recent show, “Kleibacker’s Class Act,” opened last April and included clothing worn by fashion plates like Brooke Astor, Nan Kempner and Nancy Reagan, much of which he personally collected for the museum. Some of his own designs are featured in a new survey of 20th-century American fashion, “American Beauty: Aesthetics and Innovation in Fashion,” on view at the Museum at the Fashion Institute of Technology in New York through April 10.

“He is one of the only curators I have ever known who also really understood how to design a garment,” said Patricia Mears, an F.I.T. curator who worked with Mr. Kleibacker on that show. “Because he was a couturier who became a curator, he had a very different eye.”

At earlier points in his career Mr. Kleibacker was also a journalist, a copywriter and a driver for the cabaret singer Hildegarde, jobs that may have also influenced his work as a designer and showman. Though he described himself as reserved, he was not often shy about repeating his nickname, which refers to cutting and sewing pattern pieces on the bias of the fabric, diagonally across the weave, a technique that requires superior skills from the designer.

Diana Vreeland, then the editor of Vogue, was an early champion of Mr. Kleibacker, and his work was featured regularly in Harper’s Bazaar and Town & Country, but it was never clear who first called him the master of the bias cut.

Charles John Kleibacker was born on Nov. 20, 1921, in Cullman, Ala., where his family owned a department store, and where an older sister who survives him, Ruth Richard, still lives.

In the 1940s Mr. Kleibacker studied journalism at the University of Notre Dame and worked briefly as a newspaper reporter in Alabama before moving to New York. While pursuing a graduate degree at New York University, he wrote advertisements for De Pinna, a bygone clothing store on Fifth Avenue and 52nd Street, and a store in San Francisco, where he happened to meet Hildegarde and her manager, the songwriter Anna Sosenko, in his hotel.

“She traveled with a big entourage and I had a station wagon, and I think partly that’s why they hired me, actually,” he once said.

While driving Hildegarde on tour, and later accompanying her through Europe, he began to meet her numerous dressmakers and realized, while waiting in the atelier of Christian Dior, that he wanted to become a designer. In 1954 he took a portfolio of sketches to Paris and landed a position as assistant to Antonio del Castillo, then the head designer of Lanvin.

Mr. Kleibacker returned to New York in 1957 and worked for the dressmaker Nettie Rosenstein; three years later he started his own collection, laboring over the choice of fabrics and construction, sometimes meticulously tearing threads from fabrics to slightly change their color. His gowns, favored by a roster of private clients that included Pat Nixon, Diahann Carroll and Mary Travers, typically cost more than $1,500 and took hours to make.

After retiring he taught fashion design at several universities, initially as designer in residence at Ohio State University, and served as curator for more than a dozen exhibitions. The most prominent, in 1992, was called “In Black and White: Dress From the 1920s to Today,” using only those colors to show the evolution of style. In 2003 Mr. Kleibacker devoted an entire show at the Columbus Museum of Art to black dresses, “Not-So-Basic Black.”

“It got across to women how they are really at their best in black,” he told The Columbus Dispatch. “In color — no matter how beautiful you are — you walk into a room and you’re competing with the draperies, the carpet, the art on the wall. You become just another piece of furniture.”

This article has been revised to reflect the following correction:

Correction: January 11, 2010
An obituary on Friday about the fashion designer and museum curator Charles Kleibacker misidentified the Paris atelier where, Mr. Kleibacker said, in the 1950s he realized he wanted to become a designer. It was Christian Dior’s — not that of Yves Saint Laurent, who did not start his own atelier until 1962.
