A Pillow-Talk Portrait of a Hollywood Survivor

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By JANET MASLIN
Published: January 6, 2010

In writing his exhaustingly detailed biography of Warren Beatty, Peter Biskind relied on strange rules of decorum. He decided that Mr. Beatty’s relationship with his wife, Annette Bening, “was off limits, because I didn’t want to be in the position of writing anything that might embarrass them or their four children.” He chose to respect the privacy of the Beatty household. Less sacrosanct: Mr. Beatty’s pants.

STAR

How Warren Beatty Seduced America

By Peter Biskind

Illustrated. 627 pages. Simon & Schuster. $30.

Related

Elizabeth Hess

Peter Biskind

So “Star” has its salacious moments, most of them startlingly crude even in the context of a book eager to gossip. But the awkward sexual asides in “Star” seem oddly irrelevant to Mr. Biskind’s own idea of hot stuff. He sounds far more excited by trying to guess the true budget of “Ishtar” than by quoting a description of how Mr. Beatty looks in a steam room. And “Star” lets the budget details and other petty bickering take up far more space.

This book depicts Mr. Beatty as a master manipulator with shrewd survival instincts. Sure enough, he has borne out that image by sending forth his lawyer, Bertram Fields, in take-charge mode. Mr. Fields has been quick to say that the book contains “baloney” and, more damning, that it’s dull. The first claim may be debatable. The second is not.

In fact “Star” conveys the previously unthinkable idea that there may not be an interesting Beatty biography to be written. Only one guy knows what makes Mr. Beatty tick, and he’s not talking. Or not speaking in an authorial voice anyhow: Mr. Beatty let himself be interviewed by Mr. Biskind on and off since 1989, but he stopped short of allowing “Star” to present itself as authorized. As for the interview material, some of it is reasonably sharp. But much more of it is long-winded and petty. Not for nothing has Mr. Beatty had the reputation of someone who can speak at great length without saying anything at all.

Nor is his reputation for seducing the press undeserved. Having told Mr. Biskind that Mr. Biskind reminded him of Leon Trotsky (hey, whatever works), he has somehow co-opted the ordinarily tough author of the classic “Easy Riders, Raging Bulls.” Mr. Biskind winds up full of such remarks as, “Of course, Beatty was right” to undercut his sometimes keen insights. (“He was the Paris Hilton of the late 1950s.”) And he applauds Mr. Beatty’s unstoppable way with women. “If you looked like him and were gifted with the talent for seduction that was his, why not?” Mr. Biskind asks. “He did it because he could, thank you very much, Dr. Freud!”

He also makes remarkable claims for the Beatty oeuvre. “Dick Tracy,” “Reds” and “Bulworth” variously make Mr. Biskind think of Orson Welles, Jean Renoir, Francis Ford Coppola, Charles Dickens and Bertolt Brecht. As for the duds like “Town & Country,” Mr. Biskind finds somebody who’ll say this: “Had Warren directed it, it would have been one of the truly memorable American romantic comedies.” Squabbles over Mr. Beatty’s maddeningly dilatory tactics on movie sets, his reluctance to share credit and his tight-fisted financial dealings usually wind up with Mr. Biskind’s seeing the wisdom of Mr. Beatty’s ways.

“Star” uses running commentary from several Beatty associates, like Buck Henry and Dick Sylbert, who are caustic, talented and insightful enough to warrant books of their own. But a lot of this material is very stale, and Mr. Biskind dissembles about its timeliness by relying heavily on the present tense. The hopeful note on which the book ends, with Mr. Sylbert saying, “He may still pull it off,” about Mr. Beatty’s capacity for doing great work, is weakened if you look up the page note stating that Mr. Sylbert said it in 1995. It’s further diminished by the fact that Mr. Sylbert has been dead for nearly eight years.

The smartest observers quoted in “Star” are weighed down by countless other, less astute ones, who are far more numerous. And some of the subjects that are covered exhaustively by Mr. Biskind have already been well covered. No one who has read Mark Harris’s terrific “Pictures at a Revolution” will want to wade through another long account of how “Bonnie and Clyde” was made.

The problem is partly demographic: older readers who care about film already know these stories. And younger ones aren’t apt to be impressed with material culled from the “making of” documentary that comes with the “Bonnie and Clyde” DVD. Mr. Biskind, who did persuade some of Mr. Beatty’s best known ex-flames to talk to him (among them Diane Keaton and Michelle Phillips), is reduced to drawing upon what he himself calls the “bimbographies” of other actresses to fill in gaps.

“Star” provides a clear sense that assembling this book became a wearying task for its author. Mr. Biskind sleepwalks through the “Shampoo” and “McCabe and Mrs. Miller” years, sometimes hobbled by the fact that his earlier, mean-spirited writing estranged him from knowledgeable figures like Robert Towne. By the time “Star” picks up steam, in its long slog through the making of “Reds” and a relatively fresh discussion of the underappreciated “Bulworth,” this book’s tediousness has taken root.

What wakes it up is malice. The book goes out of its way to savage the critic Pauline Kael, who is said by Buck Henry to have been manipulated by Mr. Beatty “on a level unknown to man.” Mr. Henry continues, “Even I can’t believe it, except that it was Warren.”

In the mid-90s, Mr. Biskind says, Mr. Beatty told him that he hadn’t spoken to Ms. Kael, whom he had briefly tried to mold into a studio executive, in 10 years. “And do you know what’s worse?” he asked. “She doesn’t know it!” Mr. Beatty was galled when his own behavior had a tree-falling-in-a-forest effect. If the same thing happens to “Star,” he’s less likely to complain.
