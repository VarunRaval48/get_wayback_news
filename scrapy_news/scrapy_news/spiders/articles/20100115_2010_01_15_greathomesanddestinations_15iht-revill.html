Athletes' Village Getting New Life After Winter Games

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By JOANNE BLAIN
Published: January 14, 2010

VANCOUVER, BRITISH COLUMBIA — When Vancouver starts to return to normal after the 2010 Winter Olympics, another transformation will begin — the athletes’ village will reinvent itself as a mixed-use residential community on the city’s waterfront.

The apartments and townhouses built to shelter about 2,800 Olympic and Paralympic athletes will get a few quick touch-ups before they are put on the market in May as Millennium Water, a 1,097-unit mix of high-end condominiums, rental suites and public housing just across a bridge over False Creek from downtown Vancouver.

More than a third of the 737 housing units tagged for private ownership were snapped up during the initial pre-sales in 2007, before the worldwide housing slump took hold and developers decided to delay a second round of sales.

Since then, the Vancouver housing market has rebounded strongly, fueling high hopes for post-Olympic sales at prices ranging from 499,000 Canadian dollars to more than 10 million dollars, or $485,000 to $9.7 million.

Prospects haven’t always been so positive for the project, which initially was mired in controversy over funding, construction delays and cost overruns.

The city, which had guaranteed Olympic organizers that it would build the athletes’ village in time for the Games, was forced to provide funding after the U.S. hedge fund initially behind the project refused to advance any more money to the private-sector developer the city had chosen, citing cost overruns and the uncertain market at the time.

So Vancouver taxpayers are now on the hook for at least 850 million dollars, as well as another 320 million dollars for public projects like roads, public housing and a community center.

But the athletes’ village was delivered on time to the Olympic organizers in early November, and now fingers are crossed that the developer, Millennium Southeast False Creek Properties, will be able to capitalize on the hot housing market after the Games and pay back the city’s loan.

“We’re very confident, given the current real estate market, that the city is in a position to fully recoup its investment in this project,” said Bill Aujla, the city’s project manager for the Olympic village.

Bob Rennie of Rennie Marketing Systems, which is selling Millennium Water, thinks there may even be a positive side to the initial criticism and controversy over the project. “If you can underpromise and overachieve, it doesn’t get much better,” he said.

On a walking tour of the village, as workers put the finishing touches on the seven concrete residential buildings and the grounds, Mr. Rennie pointed out features like the waterfront community center, a central plaza that will be flanked by an upscale supermarket and drugstore, and a restored heritage building that will house a restaurant and shops.

Inside, the highest-priced condos will feature amenities like remote-controlled exterior sunshades, Sub-Zero and Miele appliances, and limestone floors in the bathrooms.

The look is sleek and modern, with hardwood floors and wood veneer kitchen cabinets, quartz countertops, floor-to-ceiling windows in many units, a minimum ceiling height of nine feet, or 2.7 meters, and a choice of light, neutral or dark color schemes.

Most of the top-priced suites are adjacent to False Creek, which separates the central downtown area from the rest of the city to the south, and have decks with sweeping views of the city skyline flanked by water and mountains.

The rental units are at the center of the complex, and the public housing units — which the city still may convert into rentals or even sell to offset costs — are closest to the arterial road that borders the development.

Prices will not be set until the remaining 474 units go on sale in May, but Mr. Rennie says the largest suite in the complex — a 3,900-square-foot, or 362-square-meter, waterfront penthouse — will be priced at around 10 million dollars. The smallest units, at 450 square feet, will cost about 499,000 dollars, he said, while the majority will be priced between 800,000 and 1.2 million dollars for 900 to 1,200 square feet.

Millennium Water is being marketed as one of the greenest condominium developments in the city. Taking advantage of Vancouver’s abundant rainfall, the complex will use recycled rainwater to irrigate landscaping and flush its toilets. A system that recovers heat from the city’s sewage network will substantially offset heating costs. And the city’s newest subway line, which started running between the airport and the downtown core in time for the Olympics, is just a couple of blocks away.

“What you see in the Olympic Village is on the cutting edge of what will need to be done to sustain our environment,” Mr. Aujla said.

Mr. Rennie said he expected the green features and the proximity of transit to appeal to many buyers.

That was one of the attractions for Feroz Abdulla, who started looking for his first condo in 2007 in downtown Vancouver. At Millennium Water, he discovered he could get a more open floor plan in a quieter setting without adding much time to his commute downtown.

In the first round of sales, the 28-year-old business manager and part-time D.J. ended up buying a 736-square-foot, one-bedroom apartment overlooking the central plaza for $545,000, which he will move into next summer. “The bonus was an 88-square-foot patio, which the other buildings downtown weren’t offering,” he said.

In a city where many condo towers are selling out before construction and the moving trucks start unloading when the paint is barely dry, Millennium Water is unusual because the majority of its condos will not go on the market until the entire development is completed.

But once the Olympics are over and prospective buyers get their first look at what it offers, Mr. Rennie is convinced his job will be easy.

“You’ve got the brand of the Olympic Village, you’ve got the green elements, and you’ve got the location on the water,” he said. “It’s pretty unique.”
