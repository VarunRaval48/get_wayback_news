Theater Listings: Jan. 1 7

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By THE NEW YORK TIMES
Published: December 31, 2009

Approximate running times are in parentheses. Theaters are in Manhattan unless otherwise noted. Full reviews of current shows, additional listings, showtimes and tickets: nytimes.com/theater.

From left, Elsa David, Darren Pettie and Glenn Fitzgerald in “This,” a comedy by Melissa James Gibson about a group of close friends entering the choppy waters of middle age, closing on Sunday at Playwrights Horizons.

Previews and Openings

‘A VIEW FROM THE BRIDGE’ In previews; opens on Jan. 24. Scarlett Johansson and Liev Schreiber should make this Arthur Miller revival a hot ticket. Cort Theater, 138 West 48th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com.

Broadway

‘BYE BYE BIRDIE’ Robert Longbottom’s color-coded revival of this 1960 musical satire is a painful example of misapplied talent. The unlucky cast members include John Stamos, Gina Gershon and Bill Irwin (2:20). Henry Miller’s Theater, 124 West 43rd Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Ben Brantley)

★ ‘FELA!’ This transporting musical biography of a Nigerian revolutionary of song doesn’t so much tell a story as soak an audience to, and through, the skin with the style and sensibility called Afrobeat. There has never been anything on Broadway like Bill T. Jones’s kinetic production, which stars the charismatic Sahr Ngaujah and Kevin Mambo (2:30). Eugene O’Neill Theater, 230 West 49th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

★ ‘FINIAN’S RAINBOW’ A glorious cast restores the luster of this oddball 1947 musical with a first-rate score by E. Y. Harburg and Burton Lane. Kate Baldwin and Cheyenne Jackson are the frisky lovers, Jim Norton the wily Irishman with a purloined pot of gold, and Christopher Fitzgerald the leprechaun on the lam. Daft? Yes, but heaven for musical theater lovers (2:25) St. James Theater, 246 West 44th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Charles Isherwood)

‘GOD OF CARNAGE’ Christine Lahti, Annie Potts, Jimmy Smits and Ken Stott are the able new combatants in Yasmina Reza’s shallow but entertaining comedy about the beastly ways of husbands and wives (1:30). Bernard Jacobs Theater, 242 West 45th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Isherwood)

★ ‘HAIR’ (Tony Award for best musical revival.) Diane Paulus’s thrilling, emotionally rich revival of the 1967 rock musical delivers what Broadway otherwise hasn’t felt this season: the intense, unadulterated joy and anguish of that bipolar state called youth. A production that finds the complexity and deep ambivalence in a show people tend to remember as a feel-good free-for-all (2:20). Al Hirschfeld Theater, 302 West 45th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

★ ‘IN THE NEXT ROOM, OR THE VIBRATOR PLAY’ Sarah Ruhl’s delightful new play is set in the late 19th century in the adjoining parlor and consulting room of a doctor who specializes in treating “hysteria” in women with the use of a newfangled electrified aid. Michael Cerveris is the kindly doctor, Laura Benanti his curious wife. A real novelty: a sex comedy written for adults (2:30). Lyceum Theater, 149 West 45th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Isherwood)

‘A LITTLE NIGHT MUSIC’ Trevor Nunn’s nudgingly acted, lugubriously slow revival of Stephen Sondheim and Hugh Wheeler’s erotic waltz of a musical is most notable for the participation of the lustrous Catherine Zeta-Jones, in a lively (if miscast) Broadway debut, and of the indomitable (and invaluable) Angela Lansbury (2:50). Walter Kerr Theater, 219 West 48th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘MEMPHIS’ In the city of the title in the 1950s, a pioneering white radio D.J. (the endearing Chad Kimball) falls in love with R&B; music and one of its comely practitioners (the lovely Montego Glover) in this slick but essentially soulless musical written by David Bryan and Joe DiPietro (“The Toxic Avenger”) and fleetly directed by Christopher Ashley. Shubert Theater, 225 West 44th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Isherwood)

★ ‘NEXT TO NORMAL’ No show on Broadway makes as direct a grab for the heart — or wrings it as thoroughly — as this brave, breathtaking musical. Tom Kitt and Brian Yorkey’s portrait of a manic-depressive suburban mom (the astounding Alice Ripley) and her family, directed by Michael Greif, isn’t a feel-good musical. It’s a feel-everything musical (2:20). Booth Theater, 222 West 45th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘RACE’ David Mamet’s surprisingly slack examination of a hot-button topic registers well within the average Broadway audience’s comfort zone. There are glimmers of the author’s scalpel-edge intelligence, and James Spader is excellent as a white lawyer representing a white man accused of raping a black woman. David Alan Grier, Richard Thomas and Kerry Washington round out the able cast (1:40). Ethel Barrymore Theater, 243 West 47th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘RAGTIME’ Marcia Milgrom Dodge’s appealingly modest interpretation of this 1998 musical about early-20th-century American growing pains, adapted from E. L. Doctorow’s novel, finds an affecting, uneasy human soul within the work’s panoramic sweep. This production doesn’t disguise its hearty preachiness, but it elicits glimmers of delicate beauty (2:45). Neil Simon Theater, 250 West 52nd Street, (212) 307-4100, ticketmaster.com. (Brantley)

★ ‘ROCK OF AGES’ The Sunset Strip in the 1980s is the setting for this absurdly enjoyable jukebox musical weaving more than two dozen arenarock hits from the glory years of MTV — from Journey and Pat Benatar to Poison and Whitesnake — into a story of heartbreak and warped-vinyl dreams. Big hair, songs that are trashy fun, fabulous cast (2:25). Brooks Atkinson Theater, 256 West 47th Street, (212) 307-4100, ticketmaster.com. (Isherwood)

★ ‘WEST SIDE STORY’ In a startlingly sweet revival of this epochal musical, Arthur Laurents trades insolence for innocence, with dividends and losses. If the Jets and the Sharks seem too cute to be true, the show’s doomed lovers (played by Josefina Scaglione and Matt Cavenaugh) have rarely been more affecting. And as Anita, Karen Olivo is a stunner (2:35). Palace Theater, 1564 Broadway, near 46th Street, (212) 307-4100, ticketmaster.com. (Brantley)

★ ‘WISHFUL DRINKING’ Carrie Fisher’s brut-dry, deeply funny one-woman show allows you to see the world through the self-dissociating gaze of a child of Hollywood who has always lived in a fishbowl (2:20). Studio 54, 254 West 54th Street, (212) 719-1300, roundabouttheatre.org. (Brantley)

Off Broadway

★ ‘AVENUE Q’ Even without legs of their own, the singing hand puppets of this Tony-winning musical are still standing tall in their new Off Broadway digs. This “Sesame Street”-style show about being young and jobless in New York remains fresh, in all senses of the word (2:15) New World Stages, 340 West 50th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

★ ‘BRIEF ENCOUNTER’ This captivating adaptation of the 1945 film, a British import from the Kneehigh Theater Company, may traffic in the antics of classic stage spoofery, but its real raison d’tre is to love, honor and obey the spirit of the movie that inspired it. Emma Rice is the inventive director (1:35). St. Ann’s Warehouse, 38 Water Street, at Dock Street, Dumbo, Brooklyn, (718) 254-8779, stannswarehouse.org. (Brantley)

‘CIRCLE MIRROR TRANSFORMATION’ Annie Baker’s absorbing, unblinking and sharply funny one-act takes place in a “creative drama” class in Vermont, where relationships form and fold, secrets are revealed, and everyone gets in touch with his or her inner child (1:45). Playwrights Horizons Peter J. Sharp Theater, 416 West 42nd Street, (212) 279-4200, ticketcentral.com. (Anita Gates)

‘CIRCUMCISE ME’ Yisrael Campbell’s highly entertaining account of his conversion to Judaism — not once but three times — is elevated beyond shtick by the recognition that, aside from the jokes, this is Mr. Campbell’s life, and that his quest for a “relationship with God” continues even after the house lights rise (1:30). Bleecker Street Theater, 45 Bleecker Street, East Village, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Daniel M. Gold)

‘DO NOT GO GENTLE’ This one-man show about the poet Dylan Thomas forgoes plot to focus on the writer’s beautiful words (1:35). Clurman Theater, 410 West 42nd Street, (212) 279-4200, ticketcentral.com. (Ken Jaworowski)

★ ‘THE EMPEROR JONES’ Ciaran O’Reilly’s inspired, poetic production of Eugene O’Neill’s short, brutal drama — starring a wondrous John Douglas Thompson — finds Jungian echoes of universal resonance in a work often perceived as a dated portrait of the black man’s burden (1:10). SoHo Playhouse, 15 Vandam Street, (212) 691-1555, sohoplayhouse.com. (Brantley)

‘LOVE, LOSS, AND WHAT I WORE’ Or, shoes, bags and heartaches. A stuffed closet of comic or poignant stories, or both, collected by Nora and Delia Ephron, about women and their relationship to clothes. Featuring a rotating cast of five comics and actors. Westside Downstairs, 407 West 43rd Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Isherwood)

★ ‘MISALLIANCE’ A delightful revival of one of Shaw’s breeziest comedies. If it is one of his talkier plays, it is also very witty, and this Pearl production gives full voice to Shaw’s litany of sins committed in the name of English propriety (2:30). City Center, 131 West 55th Street, (212) 581-1212, nycitycenter.org. (Wilborn Hampton)

‘NEWSICAL THE MUSICAL’ The four performers in this topical revue — Christina Bianco, Rory O’Malley, Christine Pedi and Michael West — are skilled mimics, but the format sure seems tired, especially when the show delves into politics, where jokes have a shelf life measured in hours. Even the Tiger Woods jokes, relatively freshly installed, seem wearying. But some of the less time-bound material works nicely, especially Ms. Bianco’s Celine Dion impression (1:30). 47th Street Theater, 304 West 47th Street, Clinton, (212) 279-4200, ticketcentral.com. (Neil Genzlinger)

★ ‘THE ORPHANS’ HOME CYCLE’: PART ONE: THE STORY OF A CHILDHOOD On the basis of the three-work production that begins this New York premiere, Horton Foote’s heart-piercing, nine-play family album about growing up lonely in early-20th-century Texas should be the great adventure of the theater season. Directed with cinematic fluidity and novelistic detail by Michael Wilson (2:50). Signature Theater at Peter Norton Space, 555 West 42nd Street, Clinton, (212) 244-7529, signaturetheatre.org. (Brantley)

★ ‘OUR TOWN’ David Cromer strips away the folksy warmth in which Thornton Wilder’s play is often saturated to reveal this classic’s quietly mournful depths. This wonderfully intimate production puts a small audience smack in the middle of Grover’s Corners (2:00). Barrow Street Theater, 27 Barrow Street, West Village, (212) 868-4444, smarttix.com. (Isherwood)

‘THE UNDERSTUDY’ Julie White fabricates bountiful laughs from a sumptuous display of strained nerves in Theresa Rebeck’s scattershot backstage comedy, which also stars Justin Kirk (“Weeds”) and Mark-Paul Gosselaar (“Saved by the Bell”) as actors on far different levels of the showbiz totem pole (1:30). Laura Pels Theater, 111 West 46th Street, (212) 719-1300, roundabouttheatre.org. (Isherwood)

‘ZERO HOUR’ Jim Brochu does an excellent imitation of Zero Mostel in his standard-issue bio-play (1:50). Theater at Saint Clement’s, 423 West 46th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Jason Zinoman)

Off Off Broadway

‘RED NOIR’ An elaborate, abstract and scattershot play by the poet Anne Waldman, directed by the inveterate avant-gardist Judith Malina, “Red Noir” is participatory theater of the old Lower East Side school. It’s confusing and exhausting but sometimes exhilarating, and greatly aided by its largely youthful and idealistic cast (1:25). The Living Theater, 21 Clinton Street, Lower East Side, (212) 352-3101, theatermania.com. (Andy Webster)

★ ‘ROMEO AND JULIET’ The Nature Theater of Oklahoma’s inspired riff on Shakespeare’s classic consists of verbatim monologues in which people recount all they know about the star-crossed lovers. These variously informed, confused, chaotic and plain crazy soliloquies are performed with fake-histrionic brilliance by Anne Gridley and Robert M. Johanson (1:30). The Kitchen, 512 West 19th Street, Chelsea, (212) 255-5793, thekitchen.org. (Isherwood)

Extravaganzas

★ ‘BIG APPLE CIRCUS: BELLO IS BACK!’ The high-haired clown Bello Nock returns to the big top, along with an astonishingly talented group of exciting performers (1:50). Damrosch Park at Lincoln Center, (888) 541-3750, bigapplecircus.org. (Jaworowski)

Long-Running Shows

‘BILLY ELLIOT THE MUSICAL’ Ballet dreams in coal country(2:50). Imperial Theater, 249 West 45th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘CHICAGO’ Jazz Age sex, murder and razzle-dazzle (2:25). Ambassador Theater, 219 West 49th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘FUERZA BRUTA: LOOK UP’ A sensory bath aimed at clubgoing college kids in search of cultural diversion (1:05). Daryl Roth Theater, 20 Union Square East, at 15th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Isherwood)

‘IN THE HEIGHTS’ A musical panorama of a neighborhood in Washington Heights undergoing change (2:20). Richard Rodgers Theater, 226 West 46th Street, (212) 307-4100, ticketmaster.com. (Isherwood)

‘JERSEY BOYS’ The biomusical that walks like a man (2:30). August Wilson Theater, 245 West 52nd Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘MAMMA MIA!’ The jukebox musical set to the disco throb of Abba (2:20). Cadillac Winter Garden Theater, 1634 Broadway, at 50th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘MARY POPPINS’ Supernanny (2:30). New Amsterdam Theater, 214 West 42nd Street, (212) 307-4747, ticketmaster.com. (Brantley)

‘THE PHANTOM OF THE OPERA’ Who was that masked man, anyway? (2:30). Majestic Theater, 247 West 44th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘SOUTH PACIFIC’ A 1949 tale of men and women unmoored by war (2:50). Vivian Beaumont Theater, 150 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

‘WICKED’ Oz revisited (2:45). Gershwin Theater, 222 West 51st Street, (212) 307-4100, ticketmaster.com. (Brantley)

Last Chance

‘A BRITISH SUBJECT’; closes on Sunday. This fast-paced but formulaic play traces the real-life story of a journalist who campaigns to free a man unjustly imprisoned in Pakistan (1:15). 59E59 Street Theaters, 59 East 59th Street, (212) 279-4200, 59e59.org. (Jaworowski)

‘FLAHOOLEY’; closes on Sunday. A revival of a little-seen 1951 musical by E. Y. Harburg, better known as Yip, the gifted lyricist behind “Finian’s Rainbow,” among many other memorable shows. A strong ensemble production directed by Keith Lee Grant can’t hide the flaws in this satirical fable about a toy company looking for the next big Christmas thing — it scatters shots at everything from capitalism to corporatism to consumerism to McCarthyism — but it still makes intriguing theatrical archaeology (1:30). Theater for the New City, 155 First Avenue, at Ninth Street, East Village, (212) 254-1109, theaterforthenewcity.net. (Gold)

‘IRVING BERLIN’S WHITE CHRISTMAS’; closes on Sunday. This seasonal offering, a stage version of the 1950s movie with some additional songs drafted for the occasion, features new faces in the main roles this year, but it’s just as lukewarm as ever. The most interesting addition is David Ogden Stiers as the retired general at whose Vermont inn most of the action takes place (2:30). Marquis Theater, 1535 Broadway, at 45th Street, (212) 307-4100, ticketmaster.com. (Genzlinger)

★ ‘LET ME DOWN EASY’; closes on Sunday. Anna Deavere Smith’s engrossing new show, drawing as usual on verbatim excerpts from interviews with a wide variety of subjects, addresses the power and the fragility of the human body and its susceptibility to disease. Most moving are the many voices discussing the harsh realities of death, and the possibility of finding grace in its shadow (1:30). Second Stage Theater, 305 West 43rd Street, Clinton, (212) 246-4422, 2ST.com. (Isherwood)

★ ‘LOVE CHILD’; closes on Sunday. Daniel Jenkins and Robert Stanton star in this rollicking two-hander, which they also wrote. It’s a backstage story in which the two men play all the characters: a group of actors staging a ridiculous updating of Euripides, as well as a couple of overly loud audience members. No wigs or costume changes here: it’s all in the tics and accents, and half the fun is seeing if you can keep up (1:20). New World Stages, 340 West 50th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Genzlinger)

‘THE MARVELOUS WONDERETTES’; closes on Sunday. At a 1958 prom, this jukebox musical’s title characters sing hits of the era from a female perspective; they return at their 10-year reunion, weathered and wiser. For fanciers of the girl-group sound, the production is an utter charm bomb (2:00). Westside Theater, 407 West 43rd Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Webster)

‘RADIO CITY CHRISTMAS SPECTACULAR’; closes on Sunday. With its idealized celebrations of Christmas, New York and Radio City itself, the Spectacular is a curio, a throwback to a golden era of stage shows, complete with Santa, camels and the Rockettes. Corny? Sure. But that’s a huge part of its appeal (1:30). Radio City Music Hall, (212) 307-1000, ticketmaster.com. (Gold)

‘SHREK THE MUSICAL’; closes on Sunday. This musical cavalcade of storybook effigies, adapted from the animated film of the same title, can feel like 40 blocks’ worth of a Macy’s Thanksgiving Day Parade, accompanied by an exhaustingly jokey commentary. But Brian d’Arcy James and the hypertalented Sutton Foster find a sweet, goofy chemistry as an ogre and a princess (2:30). Broadway Theater, 1681 Broadway, at 53rd Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Brantley)

★ ‘SUPERIOR DONUTS’; closes on Sunday. The new play from the “August: Osage County” author Tracy Letts is a warm-hearted, bile-free comedy about the friendship between an emotionally stunted ex-radical (Michael McKean) and the brash young man he hires to help out in the establishment of the title (Jon Michael Hill, in an exciting Broadway debut). Music Box Theater, 239 West 45th Street, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Isherwood)

★ ‘THIS’; closes on Sunday. Melissa James Gibson’s tart, melancholy comedy about four close friends entering the choppy waters of middle age turns on a familiar marital calamity — a straying husband — but does so with freshness, distinctive verbal wit and emotional insight (2:00). Playwrights Horizons, 416 West 42nd Street, Clinton, (212) 279-4200, ticketcentral.com. (Isherwood)

‘THE TOXIC AVENGER’; closes on Sunday. A geek-turned-monster in New Jersey tears the limbs off evildoers while singing pop-rock anthems. The cast is fun, but Joe DiPietro’s book and David Bryan’s music are mostly mediocre (1:40). New World Stages, 340 West 50th Street, Clinton, (212) 239-6200, telecharge.com. (Isherwood)

‘WINTUK’; closes on Sunday. This family-oriented holiday extravaganza from Cirque du Soleil is high on action, if short on emotion (1:30). WaMu Theater at Madison Square Garden, (800) 745-3000, ticketmaster.com. (Jaworowski)
