Spain's Breakout Talent

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
By DALE FUCHS
Published: January 4, 2010

BARCELONA — On first meeting, Josep Font does not seem the sort of designer who would be especially fond of lace-trimmed collars and velvet bows.

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Courtesy of Josep Font

A design from the haute couture spring/summer 09 collection.

He favors a street aesthetic for his everyday attire — sneakers, baggy jeans and a hooded sweatshirt. He rarely smiles. On the runway, the dark, rough-shaven Spaniard might be expected to produce an underground look, or something at least a bit naughty.

But Mr. Font’s spirit — nourished by Spanish folk verse and nostalgic Charles Trenet songs — is clearly romantic. In Spain his clothing is often described as “dreamlike,” and indeed, his naïf, somewhat gothic style would easily flatter a Tim Burton character like the Corpse Bride, whose life-size image is painted on one wall of Mr. Font’s studio in Barcelona.

“My idea of sexy is more interior, more sensual,” said Mr. Font, 43, in a recent interview at the studio, filled with his ethereal, Tim Burton-esque sketches.

Mr. Font’s brooding style has managed nonetheless to keep afloat four boutiques — in Madrid, Barcelona, Bilbao and Paris — and fuel a strong export business with Japan. Most recently, it has taken him to a place few of his countrymen have trod: the French couture runway.

Mr. Font is the only Spaniard to share billing with established maisons in the glittery line-up of the French Chambre Syndicale de la Haute Couture. He is one of 15 guest members — not-yet-household names such as Jean-Paul Knott, Alexis Mabille and Richard René — invited by the chamber in an effort to inject fresh talent into that extravagant and unprofitable enterprise. Since 2008, Mr. Font has presented four couture collections of 12 to 18 pieces each, set off by sculptural headpieces and live Flamenco music.

“He has that deep, dark Baroque mood that works well with the eccentricity couture loves,” said Donald Potard, the former CEO of Jean-Paul Gaultier, who now represents Mr. Font at his own talent-nurturing firm, Agent de Luxe.

But what does it mean to do couture without the financial prowess of Dior?

For Mr. Font, it means suddenly hiring more than 15 new embroiderers and cutters, and budgeting €120,000, or nearly $175,000, per season on the trappings of the shows themselves.

It means pondering obscure inspirational themes, such as “Frank Stella and Death in México,” Mr. Font’s pick for this year’s winter collection, which included a gray Mohair knit dress as poufy as a nest of feathers.

And it means hours of excruciating handiwork. Four seamstresses toiled an entire month to create the wing-like sleeves of a siren-inspired gown in last spring’s collection, in which each “feather” was composed of a Malibu plume and two organza petals. It took another month’s labor to sculpt yellow organza flowers into a flamenco-inspired cascade in his 2008 “My Spain” show, and at least 12 pairs of hands embroidered Swarovski crystals onto the traditional black mantón de Manila shawl, transformed into a long-fringed jacket.

Mr. Font’s passion for his country’s traditional craft-making techniques reached its logical extreme in that collection. Blue-and-white floral prints inspired by Valencian porcelain swirled up stockings and sleeves. The simple lace of the austere Castilian plains undercut the sweetness of a ballerina skirt. The wool braids of mountainous León swelled into a billowing blue vest.

“Font’s invitation is a very important event for Spanish fashion,” said Pedro Mansilla, a fashion specialist at Barcelona’s Pompeu Fabra University. “It is one of the few examples of the French recognizing Spanish talent. There has always been a bit of reserve towards Spain on the part of the French, like a superiority complex.”

In the early 1980s, during the cultural awakening that followed the death of the dictator Francisco Franco, the fashion world briefly turned its attention to Spanish talent, and a few designers, such as Agatha Ruíz de la Prada and Sybilla, managed to conquer international markets. Then the spotlight dimmed. The rest toiled in darkness.

“Spain wasn’t prepared to produce, to translate the boom into a commercial success,” Mr. Font said. “There wasn’t a fashion culture.”
