Square Feet | The 30-Minute Interview

Jonathan F. P. Rose

By VIVIAN MARINO
Published: January 14, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints

Mr. Rose, 58, is the founder and president of the Jonathan Rose Companies, which specializes in sustainable development. The company is involved in both nonprofit and for-profit projects and is one of the largest developers of affordable housing in New York City.

Tina Fineberg for The New York Times

Mr. Rose also runs the Rose Smart Growth Real Estate Investment Fund.

Q. Tell me about your business.

A. We have five areas of work: public policy, planning, project management, development and investment.

On the policy side, we work to create government programs that will make healthier cities. For example, we have a consulting agreement with the E.P.A. to enhance smart growth.

Our planning work is focused on environmental issues and on low-income housing and neighborhoods. We wrote a plan for the South Bronx Greenway to make it economically viable, and we’re doing work with Newark on the Passaic River waterfront.

On the project-management side, we partner with cities and not-for-profits. We’re working with N.Y.U. Polytechnic in Brooklyn to re-envision that campus.

As developers, half our work is with not-for-profits. We also do projects for our own account. We’re co-developers of an apartment building called the Tapestry, on 124th Street and Second Avenue. And in our investment fund, we’re buying buildings all across the country, including New York.

Q. Do you make much money on nonprofit projects?

A. Yes. As a company we have this very diversified strategy. The policy work is the part that makes very, very little money. The planning work makes a little more money. The development work is even more profitable, and the investment fund really provides us a bigger asset base.

Q. What are the returns for your investors?

A. We’re currently distributing between 2 and 3 percent — that’s the check we’re writing every quarter. For a variety of reasons that will go up in the next year.

Q. What is the status of Metro Green, a green affordable housing complex in Stamford that you are developing with Malkin Holdings?

A. Metro Green has three buildings — right next to the Stamford train station. We completed the first one, which is 50 units, in September and it’s 100 percent leased. The second Metro Green project begins next summer, then the third one sometime later. We’ll probably be a LEED gold apartment building.

Q. What types of green features are you putting into your buildings?

A. One of the things we love to do is buy undermanaged buildings, which very often have poor temperature controls. So we’re not only putting in digital control systems, we’re adding a control valve to every radiator where one may be missing, We’re using much lower electricity.

We’ll use low-V.O.C. paints, bigger windows and much more energy-efficient heating and air-conditioning systems. We’ll put ceiling fans in most of our units.

Sometimes, too, it’s not just what you install but how you use it. We’ll use integrated pest management that lowers toxicity for residents, and green cleaning materials. We also recycle our construction and building waste.

Q. Anything new you might be considering?

A. We’re beginning to look at feedback systems to help people better understand their energy use. The very newest thing I heard about was literally just yesterday — it’s a technology for putting an IP address on every outlet in apartments so that you could actually track energy use, and you feed that back to a resident. A resident can see the impact, for example, of leaving the large-screen TV on stand-by all the time. I’d love to start experimenting with this.

Q. Is sustainable building more expensive?

A. No. We spend about 1 percent more to make a building between LEED silver and gold. And there’s just a huge amount of data that shows that we can typically reduce energy use by about 30 percent simply by very inexpensive ways, and in some ways by paying better attention. Typically we’re focusing on four- to five-year paybacks.

Q. To what do you attribute your interest in smart growth and sustainable development?

A. I am very lucky to have come from a family of real estate developers, and so I grew up learning the craft from my father.

My family is also very philanthropic and I really grew up understanding the great joy one gets from giving back and also the many ways that you could help a community.

I started this company 20 years ago with me and my secretary, and we’re now 70 people and managing $1.5 billion of work.

Interview conducted and condensed by Vivian Marino.
A version of this interview appeared in print on January 17, 2010, on page RE9 of the New York edition.
