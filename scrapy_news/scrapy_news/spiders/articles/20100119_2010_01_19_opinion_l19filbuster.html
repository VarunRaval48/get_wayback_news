The Uses and Misuses of the Filibuster

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
Published: January 18, 2010

To the Editor:

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
James Lawrence Hughes

Re “Mr. Smith Rewrites the Constitution” (Op-Ed, Jan. 11):

Thomas Geoghegan’s article is right on the mark. The 60-vote requirement currently disabling the functioning of the Senate is a relatively recent development and a nefarious one in American politics. It threatens to immobilize action in the face of crisis and should be rejected by both parties.

A democratic society can survive and flourish only if it can act. Inaction at such times of crisis leads to despair and threatens to encourage the rise of extremist movements, both of the left and of the right. It should be rejected by both parties, but alas the Republicans have become so power-hungry that they will use any method to regain power.

Let the elected party rule, within constitutional limits, and be judged by the electorate at the appropriate time and place. Herbert D. Saltzstein

Scarsdale, N.Y., Jan. 11, 2010

The writer is a professor of psychology at the CUNY Graduate School.

•

To the Editor:

Thomas Geoghegan argues that the filibuster rule, which requires 60 senators to end debate, is inconsistent with the Constitution, because the Constitution expressly provides for supermajority rules in specific cases and because the vice president is given a casting vote in cases of a tie. Both arguments are wrong.

Each house of Congress has the right under Article I to make rules for its own proceedings. That the Constitution sometimes requires a supermajority rule does not negate a house’s authority to impose such a voting rule at other times any more than the requirement that the president report to Congress about the “State of the Union” precludes the president’s legislative communications on other matters.

The casting provision tells how to resolve ties, but does not suggest that all votes in the Senate must be capable of being equally divided, particularly because the Constitution itself, as in cases of impeachment, contemplates votes that cannot be so resolved.

John O. McGinnis

Chicago, Jan. 11, 2010

The writer is a professor at Northwestern Law School.

•

To the Editor:

The problem with the filibuster is not constitutional; it is political. The rule doesn’t need to be abolished or changed. Sometimes minorities do deserve to be heard. But the minority has power only if the majority cannot organize itself. The Democrats need to think politically and use this tool to their advantage.

My memory of filibusters is of the great civil rights filibusters in the 1960s. I learned then that when fundamental rights are at stake, the discussion has to pass through a crisis.

So let’s face it: this is a parallel with those great debates. A powerful economic minority in this country wants to deny the people a basic human right: health care. Make the minority stand up and say it opposes extending a basic right to the people. Let this issue reach a boiling point.

After the people watch a filibuster for a few days, perhaps some political elbow grease can move things along. The younger generation also needs to realize that there is political work to be done. This one really matters for the people.

Joan C. Tronto

Minneapolis, Jan. 11, 2010

The writer is a professor of political science at the University of Minnesota.

•

To the Editor:

Thomas Geoghegan raises legitimate constitutional questions about the antimajoritarian implications of the Senate’s filibuster rules. Curiously, he ignores a more egregious example of antimajoritarianism in the Senate’s health care bill, which he supports: “[I]t shall not be in order in the Senate or the House of Representatives to consider any bill, resolution, amendment or conference report that would repeal or otherwise change” the provision establishing a Medicare Advisory Board (which is charged with cutting costs or, some might say, rationing).

Today’s Democratic majority, with Mr. Geoghegan’s tacit acceptance, trivializes the notion of democracy by barring future majorities in perpetuity from fulfilling their legislative duties. If Mr. Geoghegan truly wants to preserve majority rule, he should protest its most flagrant abuse rather than rules, whatever their infirmities, that have been used in their current form by both political parties for 35 years. Robert A. Levy

Naples, Fla., Jan. 11, 2010

The writer is chairman of the Cato Institute.

To the Editor:

As demonstrated by Thomas Geoghegan, the filibuster rule is unconstitutional. Why, then, is this antidemocratic outrage allowed to continue? Because it is cherished — not by just a few but by most if not all senators, for the potential leverage it may afford any of them on some future issue.

It is all very well to be angry these days with, say, Senator Joseph I. Lieberman. But any single senator has standing to raise a point of order challenging the unconstitutional 60-vote rule, and every single senator choosing not to is complicit in the continuing hijacking of our democracy.

Senators take an oath to the Constitution. Who among them will honor that oath? Patrick A. McGraw

Cleveland Heights, Ohio, Jan. 11, 2010

The writer is a lawyer.

•

To the Editor:

Thomas Geoghegan rightly decries the rise of the filibuster and makes a good argument that the current dysfunctional Senate is at least inconsistent with the intent of our founding fathers. But a Supreme Court case declaring the filibuster unconstitutional may not be necessary. There is a much simpler, albeit much more difficult solution — get senators to do their job.

The reason filibusters are now so easy is that senators have come to define their job as going to the Senate floor only when they want to speak or when there is a vote. Real debate has become a thing of the past. If senators were actually present on the Senate floor — and there are ways to force that if the public were actually to demand it — filibusters would become much more difficult, and probably much more rare. Bill Mattea

Glenview, Ill., Jan. 11, 2010

The writer spent 25 years working for Democratic senators and committees in the 1970s, ’80s and ’90s.
