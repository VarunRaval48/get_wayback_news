Strike Killed Hijacking Suspect, Pakistan Officials Say

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By PIR ZUBAIR SHAH
Published: January 15, 2010

ISLAMABAD, Pakistan — A militant of Palestinian origin who had been on the F.B.I.’s most-wanted list was killed in an American airstrike, two Pakistani intelligence officials said Friday.

But an American intelligence official in Washington said that he could not yet confirm the Pakistani claim. The official spoke on the condition of anonymity because he was not authorized to speak publicly on the strike.

In the past, American officials have been more cautious than their Pakistani counterparts in declaring that American drone strikes had killed militants.

American officials believe that the militant, Jamal Saeed Abdul Rahim, was connected with the Sept. 5, 1986, hijacking of Pan Am Flight 73 in Karachi, in which at least 20 passengers were killed; the American government had offered a $5 million reward for his capture.

The Pakistani officials said that Mr. Rahim was killed in a strike last Saturday with three other militants in the Datta Khel area of North Waziristan.

The Associated Press reported that Mr. Rahim had been tried and convicted in Pakistan, but that he and three other suspects were apparently released in January 2008. All four were added to the F.B.I. list late last year, the agency reported.

Americans have identified Mr. Rahim as a Lebanese-born Palestinian belonging to the Abu Nidal organization. The Pakistani officials said he was with Al Qaeda.

The attack was part of a spate of drone strikes conducted by the United States this month in an attempt to kill top leaders of the Taliban and Al Qaeda in their sanctuaries in the mountains of western Pakistan. Last August, the No. 1 Taliban commander, Baitullah Mehsud, was killed in such a strike.

Pakistani security officials said a strike on Thursday was directed at Mr. Mehsud’s successor, Hakimullah Mehsud.

On Friday, there were conflicting reports about what might have happened to Mr. Mehsud. A militant said by phone that Mr. Mehsud was wounded.

But The Associated Press quoted a commander, Omar Khatab, saying that Mr. Mehsud had not been wounded and that he was leading fighters in South Waziristan.

An audiotape of a man claiming to be Mr. Mehsud surfaced on Friday, in which he denied claims that he had died, although The Associated Press reported that the recording made no mention of the strike on Thursday, an indication that it could have been recorded earlier.

The drone strikes, which sometimes inadvertently kill civilians, have been publicly condemned by the government of Pakistan as a violation of national sovereignty.

Eric Schmitt contributed reporting from Washington.
