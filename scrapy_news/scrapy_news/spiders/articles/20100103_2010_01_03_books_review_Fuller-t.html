The World Made Flesh

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By ALEXANDRA FULLER
Published: December 31, 2009

It’s hard to shake the feeling that Will Self’s peculiar brand of participatory journalism — manifest in his 2007 essay collection “Pyschogeography” and now in this electrifying follow-up, “Psycho Too” — is part of the very machinery that creates both our modern landscapes and our contemporary angst. His work is hyper-reactive, peeling hot off the page in real time, showing us that the more modern we are (jittery with jet lag, poisoned by pollution, attacked by our savaged environment), the more likely we are to sound pre-Victorian. “I was nervy and racked by caffeine and nicotine — one morning I even overdosed on coffee, no mean achievement,” Self confesses in his opening essay, “Walking to The World.” “I had a writerly girlfriend who was more advanced than me — she’d actually completed a novel, and in due course it was published. I found it difficult to get at her: after sweaty midnights, then throughout those cold dawns I struggled to prise apart her thin and resistant white limbs. . . . She turned away from my carefully crafted caresses and I saw peculiar spiral markings on her bare back and stubby neck. Ringworm. We both had it — the vermiculation of our short-let accommodation had bored through the plaster and into our flesh.”

Thus it is with all of Self’s work — so personal it is not always easy to know where the man ends and the words begin. Place always bores itself into his flesh and soul. There is no separation, he is telling us, between what we do to our surroundings and what our surroundings (in turn) do to us. And in order to best understand the relationship between mind and place, Self seems almost pathologically determined to walk almost everywhere. Hundreds of miles of trekking clock up in the course of these essays. Without the distancing, hermetically sealed comfort of a vehicle, Self experiences the world with shocking physicality: sweat, heat, cold, insects.

Like “Psychogeography,” this new collection takes us on a fresh tour of the planet — beginning with a lengthy account of Self’s walk to “The World,” a bizarre, faltering luxury development on the coast of the United Arab Emirates, where he finds himself profoundly depressed, stranded by sand and heat and traffic. “Personally, I think if you buy a house on a man-made 25-kilometer-square palm-shaped peninsula you’ve got the wrath of God coming to you.”

Thereafter, more than 50 short essays take us to places like a hermit’s cottage in Suffolk, England (only Self could write a line that assumes the casual acquaintance of more than one hermit: “The hermit who most influenced my own life was called Peter Buxton”), and on a drive around the countryside surrounding Los Angeles (“I listen to the Phil Collins station: WMCPHIL on the car radio, back-to-back plays of Phil’s greatest hits, segued with advertisements for colonic irrigation and cryogenic preservation”). Self visits the Green Zone — “the former mosh pit of the vile Baathist regime” — and, on a trip to Bavaria, reveals that “I always feel just a little bit gay when I’m in Germany; it’s probably a way of dodging the fact that I’m half Jewish.” All this travel leads him to recall an adolescent classroom: “We had a massive geography teacher when I was in my early teens, and by massive I mean physically huge. I’ve got some kind of a mental block about his name, probably because we were cruel to the poor fellow in a way that only 30 13-year-olds can be: ritualistically, gleefully, unstintingly, acting with the unfettered sadism of many concentration camp guards with but a single inmate.”

To add to the otherworldly brilliance of this densely written, vividly explored collection, each essay is accompanied by a witty, occasionally shocking and always visceral Ralph Steadman illustration. I was particularly struck and amused by the art that accompanied the essay about Self’s drive in the Los Angeles countryside. Here, a glaring, bespectacled palm tree puffs away at an ash-tipped cigarette. The words “Gonzo was here — 1971” have been etched onto its trunk. On the ground near the tree is a cow’s skull (wearing dark glasses). Zooming around the tree in a shark-toothed sports car, smoke bellowing from a pipe clutched between his lips, is Self, perfectly captured in all his manically gloomy glory. It’s hard to imagine that Self’s picturesque words need any illustration, but in a world so flattened by recession and dreary retrenchment, it is refreshing to be rewarded with such extravagant fare.

Self, the author of six novels, four story collections, three books of novellas and five works of nonfiction, is almost as notorious for his rakish behavior as he is for his writing. Although now sober for 11 years or so, Self was well known for his occasionally bleary appearances and all-night binges in London nightclubs. In 1994 he was busted for possession of hashish in the Orkney Islands (where he was living at the time), and in 1997 he was caught snorting heroin in the bathroom of John Major’s jet while covering the British electoral campaign for The Observer. Such scandals seem to have done nothing to hurt his career, perhaps because his immoderation is so wonderfully rendered. “I read, ate too much, asked the stewardess for more chocolate,” Self writes of his flight to the United Arab Emirates in “Walking to The World.” When this excess caused him to pass gas under his thin blanket, he says, “it felt as if, far from digesting, the food was reconstituting itself into small, hard, foil-lidded oblong trays.”

Alexandra Fuller is the author of Dont Lets Go to the Dogs Tonight: An African Childhood and, most recently, The Legend of Colton H. Bryant.
