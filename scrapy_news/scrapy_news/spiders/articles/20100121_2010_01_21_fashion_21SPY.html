Wife/Mother/Worker/Spy

As Different as Night and Day

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MICHELLE SLATALLA
Published: January 19, 2010

WHEN my two older daughters arrived home from college for winter break, they suddenly behaved in all the ways that I once believed would make me the happiest mother in the world.

Skip to next paragraph
Hadley Hooper

They cooked their own meals. They loaded the dishwasher afterward.

They washed their clothes. They even noticed, once in a while, that the dogs needed to be walked.

It was all I always dreamed of, except for one tiny problem: my daughters did it all in the middle of the night.

“They’re ruining my life,” I whispered to myself one night last week as I lay in bed, listening to them move around in the kitchen downstairs.

“Mine, too,” a voice said, right beside me in the dark.

Thank God my husband is a notoriously light sleeper. Otherwise there would be no one to keep me company at — I rolled over and looked at the bedside clock — 3 a.m. as I listened to the distant sounds of my offspring watching television. With all their friends.

I sat up. “Do you think they keep these same hours when they’re at school?” I asked. “How do they make it to their morning classes?”

“I don’t think they take morning classes,” my husband mumbled from beneath a pillow that covered his head. “Or even any early afternoon classes.”

Probably not. These days, it seems as if my husband and I live in a completely different time zone from our children even when we inhabit the same house. It started predictably a few years back when they hit adolescence, a phase when melatonin levels peak later in the day and circadian clocks set themselves to stay up later.

The differences in our schedules escalated during my children’s teenage years, when they and their friends became as nocturnal as hamsters. These days, they stay up all night texting one another, making phone calls, playing video games and watching reruns of sitcoms on their computers. And researchers who study the sleep patterns of adolescents say that these activities may exacerbate the situation.

“Nowadays there are lots of environmental issues that play a role in altering people’s sleep patterns, and the most obvious would be the computer,” said Dr. Nancy Collop, medical director of the Johns Hopkins Sleep Disorders Center. “It emits a blue light, which is one of the most stimulating lights to the little receptors we all have in the back of our eyeballs, which send messages to the brain to say whether it’s day or night and whether we should be awake or go to sleep.”

In other words, your receptors become confused if you stare into a computer screen late at night. Do it often enough and you may get to a point where, if left to your own devices during winter break, you will stay up into the wee hours, sleep through the morning and then emerge from your bedroom as a grump at around 2 p.m. to interrupt your mother, who is trying to write, by asking if there is anything to eat “for breakfast.”

What if this strange new 21st-century schedule also becomes the new normal for the rest of us? After all, sometimes I use a computer at night, right before bed. What if the blue light starts to affect me?

I don’t think I could take being nocturnal. But maybe it’s happening already. Why am I awake at 3 a.m., unable to go back to sleep?

I poked my husband.

“What?” he mumbled.

“What if they stay nocturnal all their lives?” I asked. “What if they graduate and instead of taking 9-to-5 jobs, their generation makes the rest of us adopt their weird schedules, and I have to start working from 9 p.m. to 5 a.m., and then I fall asleep on the job, and I get fired?”

“Go to sleep,” he said.

“It could happen,” I insisted. “And what if it gets to the point where vampires are forced to suck blood during daylight hours to stay under society’s radar?”

He sat up and considered.

“Would that be so bad?” he asked. Then he lay back down and put his head under his pillow, and I heard him mumble, “You’ll adapt.”

But will I?

The next day, I phoned Mary Carskadon, a Brown University professor whose research focuses on adolescent sleep behavior.

“Someone’s got to change, or I’ll never see my children again,” I told her. “Already I feel like they’ve moved to another continent.”

“Well, you could always start taking a nap so you can stay awake long enough to see them when they’re up,” she said.

A chill went down my spine. It was happening! I said goodbye to sunshine. And to morning glories in bloom. And to lunch, a meal my generation used to consume at an hour when, apparently, I’ll soon be asleep.

“So all is lost?” I asked.

“You mean, is the societal norm changing?” Professor Carskadon responded, adding cheerfully: “Potentially, it could. We’ve already seen a lot of things change in the last 10 years or so. And compare my adolescence, when we had a telephone in the house and a rule that said no calls in and out after 9, to theirs, where they’re living in a global society and their friends who are doing junior year abroad, in Australia, want to be texting and Skyping at odd hours.”

But whatever environmental factors are skewing my children’s perspective on sleep will have to contend with biology.

Their brains are still developing, after all. About five years ago, sleep researchers in Germany interviewed thousands of adolescents and in the process discovered what they described as “the first biological marker of the end of adolescence,” which was a desire that kicked in around age 20 to start going to bed earlier.

“The brain tends to correct itself,” Professor Carskadon said.

Which sounded good. Until I started wondering whose brain she was talking about.
