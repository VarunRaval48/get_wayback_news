On the London Stage

New Varnish for the Boards: British Theater Undergoing a Renewal

Sasha Gusov

Ian McKellen in “Waiting for Godot” at the Haymarket Theater in London.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
By MATT WOLF
Published: January 12, 2010

LONDON — A funny thing has happened imperceptibly yet unmistakably to the British theater of late: an entire generation of stage actor has pretty well passed from view.

By that, I don’t mean the swathe of actor-knights (and, on the distaff side, dames) who were already making their theatrical farewells when I first moved to London a quarter-century or more ago: The likes of Olivier and Ashcroft, whom I never got to see live, and John Gielgud and Wendy Hiller, both of whom I was fortunate enough to catch on stage on several occasions.

I’m referring instead to these legendary figures’ inheritors: Albert Finney, say, and Maggie Smith, Joan Plowright — who, of course, is Lord Olivier’s widow — and Michael Gambon. Whereas there was a time not very long ago when a single season might well have brought stage sightings of all these talents, this venerable array is itself disappearing from sight, for reasons having to do with health, temperament and personal predilection.

Mr. Finney, for instance, has long made clear the same preference for celluloid that is shared these days by Anthony Hopkins, the latter an actor whose Lear for the National Theatre in 1986 made the front pages of the British press at the time. Then came “The Silence of the Lambs” (1991) and Hannibal Lecter’s pride of place within the collective filmgoing psyche, since which time Mr. Hopkins has become a Hollywood kinda guy.

Dame Maggie’s case is more poignant, if only because here was a film star with two Oscars to her name by the late 1970s who nonetheless seemed identifiable first and foremost with the British theater, moving between new plays and classics with equal command. But that was before her West End run almost three years ago in Edward Albee’s “The Lady From Dubuque,” a difficult play brilliantly served by this actress. It was also the first of her stage ventures that anyone can remember not to pay back its costs. (I still think that same production would have fared better on Broadway, where it would have been more of an occasion.)

More to the point was the fight against breast cancer from which the performer, now 75, has made a welcome recovery. As a result, she told a London interviewer last fall, “I’m frightened to work in theater now. I feel very uncertain.” The stamina needed for the stage isn’t easy at any time, and it is that much more difficult when your confidence has been shaken, for whatever reason.

At the same time, Ms. Plowright, long a friend and colleague of Ms. Smith’s, hasn’t appeared in the theater since “Absolutely! (perhaps),” a strangely titled Pirandello revival back in 2003. As I think back on the two women headlining a 1984 West End run of the Restoration-era vintage comedy, “The Way of the World,” that seems entirely a product of a now-vanished age, not least because a commercial run of a play of that sort these days would seem impossible unless it were cast from reality TV. (That, by the way, is not a suggestion.)

Luckily, some of that same cluster of luminaries still happily ply their trade. Ian McKellen is due back this month at the Haymarket to reprise his acclaimed performance in “Waiting For Godot,” which was one of the hot tickets of the past year. Judi Dench — no stranger herself to the Haymarket — will open mid-February in “A Midsummer Night’s Dream” at the Rose Theatre in the London suburb of Kingston. Expect devotees of the grand tradition of British acting to be scouring commuter train timetables in keen anticipation. Derek Jacobi, seen this time last year in a sellout “Twelfth Night” on the West End for which his Malvolio was extensively laureled, has spoken of giving us his King Lear; whether that would be by way of a swan song one cannot yet say. Vanessa Redgrave and Eileen Atkins remain very much players, the sticking point being the growing paucity of roles for women of a certain age.
