Television Review | 'The Simpsons 20th Anniversary Special: In 3-D! On Ice!'

Ah, Homer, It Seems Just Like Yesterday

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By ALESSANDRA STANLEY
Published: January 7, 2010

“The Simpsons” has been on the air longer than “Gunsmoke,” but that’s not a reason to watch the show’s 20th-anniversary special on Fox this Sunday night. Tributes to even the best shows can smother them in pomposity. “The Simpsons 20th Anniversary Special: In 3-D! On Ice!” is worth a look because — as the self-mocking title suggests — it doesn’t make too big a deal of one of the most innovative, influential and irresistibly irreverent shows in the history of television.

The event opens with a new episode, “Once Upon a Time in Springfield,” followed by an hourlong documentary by Morgan Spurlock, the director and star of “Super Size Me.” The writers make a few smirking references to the show’s longevity — Bart at the blackboard writes over and over, “The world may end in 2012, but this show won’t.” Mostly it’s a typical half-hour of animated lunacy and a reminder to apostates of why they loved the show in the first place.

And that dose of unadulterated “Simpsons” is helpful because it’s so hard for even a well-made documentary to convey how deftly the writers blend highbrow humor into slapstick cartoon antics, not to mention social satire, irony, parody, self-mockery, political commentary and an unceasing sendup of television, particularly its home network.

Earlier animated series, notably “The Rocky and Bullwinkle Show” and “The Flintstones,” worked on two levels, but “The Simpsons” works on one more: children, adults, and adults who read a lot. And that sophistication, lightly worn, has schooled viewers over two decades. Not every show slips in references to “The Music Man” or Edgar Allan Poe, but those that do find an audience for it, thanks in large part to “The Simpsons.” (It’s not just “South Park” or “Family Guy.” Even “Gossip Girl” has sardonic allusions to “Breakfast at Tiffany’s” and Eliot Spitzer.)

In this episode Krusty the Clown’s ratings with girls are in the tank, and station executives force him to take on a female co-star, a frilly, pink, singing Princess Penelope (Anne Hathaway), who trills about her “princessness” and kisses unicorns.

Lisa is delighted, but Bart and Homer are as disgusted as Krusty himself. When Bart complains to his father that girls ruin everything, Homer replies, “Oh, Bart, you say that now, but when you’re grown up, you’ll just think it.”

The cartoonist Gary Larson (“The Far Side”) has a cameo. There are quirky references to Audrey Hepburn movies, as well as to Tintin and Captain Haddock, and at the end a small, sweet and self-deprecating note to fans: “Thanks for 20 wonderful years. Now stay turned for three Seth MacFarlane shows.”

Purists may want to stop there, but Mr. Spurlock’s retrospective is interesting, even if he is an odd choice to act as spokesman for a series that speaks so eloquently for itself. Mr. Spurlock’s trademark is to insert himself into the story as a have-not George Plimpton. On his FX series, “30 Days,” he spent a month surviving (barely) on minimum wage, and another month doing time in jail.

Here Mr. Spurlock roams the world, talking to the show’s creators, fans and detractors, including nuclear energy executives and Brazilians who hate the way “The Simpsons” mocked their country. In one horrifying scene Mr. Spurlock walks the beaches of Rio in a tiny tight swimsuit — Speedo Size Me.

There is a lot of “I” in this narrator, but what his first-person tour does offer is a light, breezy look at a comedy that sometimes is studied too ponderously by academics and pop-culture critics. He takes on the show’s staggering worldwide popularity and commercial success obliquely. A devotee in Britain has crammed his house, attic to basement, with Simpsons memorabilia. His wife is tolerant, partly because her room is crowded with a vast collection of stuffed bears.

“I’m just as sad as him,” she explains. “Everybody says so.” Perhaps because Mr. Spurlock treats even the weirder fanatics with good humor, not ridicule, they respond in kind.

Accolades from guest stars could be endless (Rupert Murdoch made a cameo; the physicist Stephen Hawking has made three), so the film taps only a chosen few visitors (Dan Rather, Sting) as well as actors like Julie Kavner, the voice of Marge, and alumni writers and producers like Conan O’Brien.

Homage is paid by some of those who owe their own success to “The Simpsons,” like Trey Parker, one of the creators of “South Park,” who says, “It was, at the time, insanely edgy.”

Serendipity is also given its due. The cartoonist Matt Groening explains how he created Homer and his family in 10 minutes while in a sudden panic, waiting to meet the director James L. Brooks (“Broadcast News,” “Terms of Endearment”), who is cheekily identified as a “Hollywood legend.” Mr. Brooks, who was then pulling together “The Tracey Ullman Show,” on which the Simpsons made their debut in 1987, had decided to recruit Mr. Groening because a friend had given him a panel from Mr. Groening’s comic strip, “Life in Hell.”

With the show now in its 21st season, it has become fashionable for bloggers to grouse that “The Simpsons” used to be a lot funnier a decade ago and boast that they’ve stopped watching new episodes. That canard is dispatched most efficiently by one of the current writers.

“I think the Internet message boards used to be a lot funnier 10 years ago,” Matt Warburton says deadpan. “And I’ve sort of stopped reading their new posts.”

At one point Mr. Spurlock asks people what the world would look like if “The Simpsons” had never existed. The answers are mostly facetious — one writer says he wouldn’t live in Malibu; another figures he would have an uglier wife — but it’s a valid question. The series has so often parodied “It’s a Wonderful Life” that it’s tempting to apply the Frank Capra conceit to the show itself.

If “The Simpsons” had never been born, even good sitcoms would have laugh tracks; there would have been no “King of the Hill” or “South Park”; Conan O’Brien would have ended up in law school; mugs, T-shirts and bedsheets would be decorated only with Big Bird; and as he staggers through Springfield searching for Homer and Marge, Matt Groening would see signs welcoming him to “Murdochsville.”

THE SIMPSONS

Once Upon a Time in Springfield

Fox, Sunday night at 8, Eastern and Pacific times; 7, Central time.

Directed by Matthew Nastuk; written by Stephanie Gillis; James L. Brooks, Matt Groening and Al Jean, executive producers. Produced by Gracie Films in association with 20th Century Fox Television.

WITH: the voices of Dan Castellaneta (Homer Simpson), Julie Kavner (Marge Simpson), Yeardley Smith (Lisa Simpson), Nancy Cartwright (Bart Simpson), Hank Azaria (Chief Wiggum), Harry Shearer (Ned Flanders) and Anne Hathaway (Princess Penelope).

Fox, Sunday night at 8:30, Eastern and Pacific times; 7:30, Central time.
