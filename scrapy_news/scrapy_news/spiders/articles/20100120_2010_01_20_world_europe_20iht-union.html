Bulgarian Drops Candidacy for European Commission

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By STEPHEN CASTLE
Published: January 19, 2010

BRUSSELS — After fierce criticism in the European Parliament, Bulgaria’s nominee for the European Commission abandoned her candidacy Tuesday, a move that delayed confirmation of all 27 new commission representatives and threatened further parliamentary infighting.

Rumiana Jeleva, who is Bulgaria’s foreign minister, finally gave way to her political opponents, who had grilled her last week during a stormy hearing in the European Parliament. They had questioned both the transparency of her financial declarations and her competence to be a commissioner for humanitarian aid.

The government in Sofia, which had initially resisted efforts to replace Ms. Jeleva, on Tuesday nominated an alternative candidate, Kristalina Georgieva, who has been vice president of the World Bank since 2008.

The dramatic development means that plans for a vote in the European Parliament to confirm the whole new European Commission in office will now not take place next week as scheduled. At least a further week will be needed to arrange a hearing for Ms. Georgieva, with the final vote expected Feb. 9.

But the delay could be longer if Ms. Jeleva’s political allies seek revenge by trying to block nominees from center or center-left parties, or force them to switch portfolios. The start of the new team has already been delayed once because of difficulties in ratifying the E.U.’s new Lisbon Treaty.

The episode is also a personal setback for the commission president, José Manuel Barroso, who had approved Bulgaria’s nomination of Ms. Jeleva and had sent a letter to the Parliament declaring that he was satisfied by her assurances.

The withdrawal has echoes of a similar setback for Mr. Barroso five years ago when Italy’s nominee, Rocco Buttiglione, withdrew his candidacy for a commission job after describing homosexuality as a sin. On that occasion, deputies also managed to force Mr. Barroso to switch the portfolios of some would-be commissioners. On Tuesday it remained unclear whether other nominees would be similarly singled out.

Ms. Jeleva issued a letter on Tuesday rejecting all the accusations against her but concluding that she had no hope “of an objective attitude and an objective assessment” from the Parliament.

Though Ms. Jeleva offered to resign from all her posts, Prime Minister Boiko M. Borisov did not accept her resignation as Bulgaria’s foreign minister.

In a statement, Mr. Barroso said he “took note of Mrs. Jeleva’s withdrawal as commissioner-designate” and “fully respects this personal decision.”

Daniel Cohn-Bendit, co-president of the European Parliament’s Green group, said that Mr. Barroso should have taken note of concerns raised about Ms. Jeleva’s candidacy in December.

“This debacle shows that Barroso does not have a firm grip on things,” Mr. Cohn-Bendit said in a statement. “Jeleva’s withdrawal is the right decision and offers a way out of the current impasse.”

Andrew Duff, a Liberal Democrat deputy and spokesman on the hearings, described the outcome as excellent. “The Parliament is trying to put in place a top-class commission that hasn’t got question marks over probity or competence,” he said. “I don’t think she had a chance of passing on those counts.”

But Joseph Daul, leader of the center-right group to which Ms. Jeleva belongs, attacked her critics, saying they “fired at Ms. Jeleva without scruple, they accused her without any proof and, on realizing that their personal attacks were without foundation, questioned her competence.”

Charles Grant, director of the Center for European Reform in London, said that Ms. Jeleva’s departure proves that the Parliament “has a real role to play for weeding out unacceptable commissioners.”

He added that Mr. Barroso could not be blamed for accepting her candidacy, despite warnings that she faced a difficult confirmation hearing. “He doesn’t have an intelligence service at his disposal,” he said. “He has to accept what national capitals tell him. But it is very embarrassing for Bulgaria and for its prime minister.”

Officials say that Mr. Barroso felt in no position to challenge Ms. Jeleva’s candidacy since she had served as a European lawmaker and is Bulgaria’s foreign minister. Ms. Jeleva is a prominent personality in Bulgaria and made a guest appearance on a Bulgarian television dance show last year.

Attention will now focus on the fate of other nominees to the commission who were criticized in hearings. They include two Liberal Democrats: Olli Rehn of Finland, who is to take up the economic and monetary affairs portfolio, and Neelie Kroes of the Netherlands, who is to take charge of the digital agenda.

After a private meeting between Ms. Kroes and senior parliamentarians, Pilar Del Castillo, a center-right deputy, said on Twitter that Ms. Kroes “gave more solid opinions and proposals,” indicating that opposition to her is receding.

Meanwhile, Maros Sefcovic, the Slovak nominee, thought to be vulnerable, gave a fluent performance in his hearing Tuesday. Algirdas Semata of Lithuania faced criticism after his appearance but, since he comes from the center-right, Ms. Jeleva’s departure may ease his way.
