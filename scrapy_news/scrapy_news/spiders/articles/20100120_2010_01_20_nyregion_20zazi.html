Queens Man Is Accused in Plot to Kill U.S. Soldiers

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By WILLIAM K. RASHBAUM
Published: January 19, 2010

A Queens College graduate who traveled to Pakistan in 2008 to attend a Qaeda training camp conspired to kill American service members in Afghanistan, a federal prosecutor said in court on Tuesday.

The prosecutor, Assistant United States Attorney James P. Loonam, also said his office expected to seek additional charges against the Queens College graduate, Adis Medunjanin, 25, and might consolidate Mr. Medunjanin’s case with that of Najibullah Zazi, 24, one of two other men who prosecutors say attended the camp. Mr. Zazi was charged in September in a separate indictment alleging a Qaeda bomb plot.

Mr. Loonam made the comments during a hearing in Mr. Medunjanin’s case before Judge Raymond J. Dearie of United States District Court in Brooklyn. Mr. Medunjanin was charged with conspiring to commit murder in a foreign country and receiving military-type training from Al Qaeda in a two-count indictment unsealed Jan. 9.

The bare-bones, two-page indictment had provided almost no details about the alleged conspiracy, and while Mr. Loonam’s comments in court were far from expansive, they constituted the most detailed description of it.

“In August 2008, the defendant traveled with others from the United States to Pakistan, with the intent of killing U.S. service members in Afghanistan,” Mr. Loonam told Judge Dearie.

Mr. Medunjanin, who has been held without bail since his arraignment, sat quietly at the defense table beside his lawyer, Robert C. Gottlieb. A bushy-bearded man wearing a navy blue smock over an orange prison T-shirt and baggy trousers, Mr. Medunjanin, a building superintendent, nodded in response when Judge Dearie greeted him at the beginning of the hearing.

Mr. Gottlieb had complained that his client was kept incommunicado from his family and lawyer for more than 36 hours after he was taken into custody Jan. 7, and he contended that Mr. Medunjanin’s constitutional rights were violated. He said any interrogation of his client during that time was illegal.

In court papers filed Monday, Mr. Gottlieb asked Judge Dearie to order that documents relating to his client’s detention be preserved, and to sign a series of subpoenas so he could obtain those records. He would not say what his client had told him about the interrogation.

Mr. Loonam defended the government’s conduct.

“Suffice to say that when the evidence comes forth in the proper procedural context,” he said, it will show that “the defendant’s constitutional rights were not violated in any way.”

The judge asked Mr. Loonam to ensure that the records Mr. Gottlieb sought be segregated and secured.

Mr. Medunjanin, Mr. Zazi and a third man, Zarein Ahmedzay, 24, who has also been charged, came under intense scrutiny by the federal authorities after the investigation became public in September.

The inquiry, which had been intense but secret in the previous weeks, was revealed after what federal law enforcement officials described as missteps by the Police Department’s Intelligence Division, which was not directly involved in the investigation.

Prosecutors have said that Mr. Zazi, who was indicted on a single bombing conspiracy count on Sept. 24, traveled to Pakistan and Afghanistan on Aug. 28, 2008, with the other two men.

Mr. Zazi, who was born in Afghanistan and grew up in Pakistan before his family moved to Queens when he was a teenager, moved to Denver in January 2009. According to court papers filed in his case, he admitted receiving training in weapons and explosives from Al Qaeda.

He has pleaded not guilty.

Mr. Ahmedzay, a taxi driver who was charged with lying to F.B.I. agents about the details of his trip to Pakistan and about whether he knew that another man had attended the training camp, also pleaded not guilty.

At a hearing in Mr. Ahmedzay’s case on Tuesday, another prosecutor, Berit W. Berger, said the government might bring additional charges in that case.
