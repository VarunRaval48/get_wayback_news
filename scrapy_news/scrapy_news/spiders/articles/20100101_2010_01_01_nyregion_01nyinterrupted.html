New York, Interrupted

Justice Takes No Holiday in Room 129 at a Manhattan Court

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By MANNY FERNANDEZ
Published: December 31, 2009

Joseph, a teenager, spent part of New Year’s Eve at 100 Centre Street, Room 129, Manhattan Criminal Court. He held his blue baseball cap in his hands as he stood before the judge.

New York City this holiday week has been running at half-speed, with shuttered offices, empty schools and plenty of seats on rush-hour trains. But there are some eternals that seem to never take a holiday, regardless of the date, and Room 129 is one of them.

It is one of the rooms on the main floor of the courthouse where many of the men and women arrested in Manhattan are arraigned, and though the number of defendants this week has been a fraction of what it normally is, there were 32 names on the calendar Thursday morning.

There were Jonathan Pena, Rile Johnson, Calvin Savage, Cindy Gomez, Lisa Mitchell, Malcolm Mock, Xiaohua Qiam. And there was Joseph, who is 16.

It all started with a trip to McDonald’s. Joseph and his friends were walking to get some food on Broadway near 157th Street in Washington Heights about 8 p.m. on Wednesday. Police officers observed him carrying an uncapped bottle of DonQ Cristal rum, according to court documents, and found two knives in his jacket.

Joseph said he had been jumped recently a few blocks away, and the knives were for protection. “I don’t want to get jumped again,” he said outside the courtroom after he was released.

One knife, Joseph explained, was his. The other belonged to his cousin, who was wearing a lighter coat, Joseph said. The knife would have made a bulge in his cousin’s coat, Joseph said, so he carried both knives.

This is how the day went in Room 129 and in the hallway outside: swirling with the crime-blotter details — who carried the knives where and why — that keep the criminal justice system busy the day before New Year’s.

Joseph, who lives in the Bronx and attends Harry S Truman High School, was charged with criminal possession of a weapon, criminal possession of knives or instruments and consumption of alcohol in a public place. He was told to return for another court appearance in February.

Joseph was hungry and tired as he walked out of the room — he had spent a sleepless night in custody at a police precinct station house, had been taken to the courthouse at 6 a.m. and had appeared before the judge about 11:30 a.m. He said he was happy to be out, but he had other things on his mind. He had not yet spoken to his mother.

“She’s going to be mad,” he said. “Every mother’s going to be mad. She’s going to bark at me.”

Room 129 is the kind of place where no New Yorker who is not paid to be there wants to end up. It is a stately, imposing place, with white-painted and wood-paneled walls, a high ceiling and two rows of windows, one of which offered a view of the bare snow-dusted trees outside and the other a less-scenic view of a wall.

A metal chain separated the gallery from the front of the courtroom. Other courtrooms upstairs have a velvet rope for that purpose, but Room 129 has a simple chain. One woman who unhooked it used a napkin to protect her hand as she did so.

The rows of wooden benches in the gallery, divided in the center by an aisle, are scuffed and worn, like the pews in an old church. The relatives of those arrested, the prosecutors, the defense lawyers and public defenders, the court reporters, the clerks, the court officers all did on New Year’s Eve what they would do on any other day in Room 129: They waited. It is what people do there.

On the backs of the benches, New Yorkers have had the time, the patience and the disrespect to carve their names and other messages into the wood (“Jay and Erica was here!”).

Some men and women were escorted in handcuffs down the center aisle by police officers. It was not how it was on television. No one put up a fight, and those in custody and those who put them there talked with one another quietly as they walked, as if old friends.

At 3:30 p.m., less than nine hours before the ball dropped in Times Square, Hasani Powell, 29, was called before the judge. His crime against the people of the State of New York: urinating in the West Fourth Street subway station about 3 a.m.

“I had to go really bad,” Mr. Powell said later. “I tried to hold it.”

Mr. Powell said he was issued a disorderly conduct violation and was arrested for an outstanding warrant for marijuana possession. The judge sentenced him to perform three days of community service.

“Worst experience of my life,” Mr. Powell said. “You never realize how good freedom is until it’s taken away from you.”

He checked his watch as he stood outside Room 129. It was New Year’s Eve in New York City, and he was a free man. “I still got time,” he said. “I’m still going to go out and celebrate.”
