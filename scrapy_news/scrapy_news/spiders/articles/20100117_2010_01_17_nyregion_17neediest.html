Waylaid by Illness, Now Standing on Her Own

Michelle V. Agins/The New York Times

Beatriz Hodge is a clerical aide at the Bronx Leadership Academy, where her son is a student.

For Beatriz Hodge, the difference between a life in Puerto Rico and a life in New York was a trip to the zoo.

In 1987, Ms. Hodge, who is from San Juan, was three days into what was supposed to be an eight-day visit with her sister when she contracted a bacterial infection that left her paralyzed.

“I went to bed, and when I tried to wake up in the morning, I couldn’t move,” said Ms. Hodge, now 42.

“Only my hands and head” were mobile, she added.

She spent nine months in the hospital because of the infection, which she said was a result of going barefoot at the Bronx Zoo. But she refused to abandon hope. “They told me I wasn’t going to walk again or have kids, but I said, ‘They’re not God,’ ” she recalled. When she finally left the hospital, it was with the help of a cane, not a wheelchair.

But her victory was tinged with sadness: Because of her illness, she was forced to abandon her studies at the Inter-American University of Puerto Rico.

“Going back to Puerto Rico wasn’t going to be comfortable,” she said, as she was now incontinent and faced constant lower-body stiffness.

So Ms. Hodge stayed, and made the most of her new life here, transferring to Hostos Community College and graduating with a diploma in public administration.

She eventually sent for her mother and brother. And she proved the doctors wrong on another count, giving birth to a son, Hector Herrand, in 1992.

The boy’s father was largely out of the picture. “My mother’s always been my baby sitter,” Ms. Hodge said, an arrangement made easier by the fact that mother and daughter live in the same neighborhood.

For several years after the birth of her son she supported herself with a patchwork of public assistance and Social Security disability payments, and bunked with family. Twelve years ago she found an apartment management company in Hunts Point in the Bronx and “came in every day and cried until they gave me this apartment,” she recalled.

“This apartment” is a warm two-bedroom unit, sparsely furnished and cinnamon scented. Thanks to a rent subsidy, she pays $612 a month, half of the market rent. Until a year ago she shared the space with her husband, a messenger for a photo company. When their five-year marriage ended, she fell behind on the rent, and by November she owed $1,823.

A clerical aide at the Bronx Leadership Academy, where her son is a student, Ms. Hodge earns $18 an hour during the school year, which is enough to pay the rent. But as an hourly employee, she is not paid during the summer and relies solely on $697-a-month disability payments, which kick in every year from August to October.

“I was going nuts,” Ms. Hodge said. “I was going to have to go to Housing Court — what was I going to tell them?”

A co-worker suggested she approach the Phipps Community Development Corporation, a member agency of the Federation of Protestant Welfare Agencies, one of the seven beneficiary agencies of The New York Times Neediest Cases Fund. She secured $1,500 for back rent, a development for which she is grateful.

“We need programs like Phipps for people who really, really need help,” she said. “They were very, very good.”

Ms. Hodge sometimes reflects on the life she could have lived in Puerto Rico if she had never come to New York for a visit. “I’d probably be a pediatrician,” she said. But then, she might never have had her son, who is set to graduate from high school in June and plans to become an electrical engineer. Either way, she does not spend a lot of time on what-ifs.

“I’ve had a lot of difficulties, but I’m moving forward,” she said.
