Disaster Touches a Haitian Enclave, but Comfort Is Just as Close

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By PETER APPLEBOME
Published: January 17, 2010

SPRING VALLEY, N.Y.

Columnist Page

“It’s Jacmel,” gasped Gertrude Nozin, to her one co-worker in the almost empty Restaurant Tropical on Friday afternoon.

She watched transfixed as CNN for the first time was showing video from the port city in southern Haiti, long known for its New Orleans-style cast-iron pillars and balconies, its papier-mâché masks, rich art scene and craft galleries, and now a horrific jungle of rubble in the wake of the earthquake. Somewhere amid the rubble were her mother and husband and other relatives who survived the quake.

“They’re sleeping in the streets, but they’re O.K.,” Ms. Nozin said, and turned back to watch more bodies being pulled from more debris, the repeating loop of the disaster back home.

Jacmel, it turns out, has a special place in the history of this Rockland County village, where Haitians make up about half the population of more than 25,000. It was the home of many of the first Haitians to settle here in the 1950s and ’60s, a good number of them middle-class business people and professionals fleeing the brutal dictatorship and death squads of Francois Duvalier, known as Papa Doc.

They were drawn to the rolling hills and Hudson River views of Rockland County, which, though colder and far away from what they knew, still reminded them of home. And a half century on, they have made this community their own. The mayor, Noramie Jasmin, and the deputy mayor, Joseph Anthony DesMaret, are Haitian, as is the county legislator, Jacques O. D’I. Michel.

They gather at the Konbit Nèg Lakay community service center. They worship at Haitian churches like the French Speaking Baptist Church, St. Joseph Catholic Church, Église de Dieu Spring Valley and about two dozen others in and around town, where the ministers have names like Yves Latouche, Moise Beauvilaire and Jean Duthene Joseph. If you want a lawyer or a doctor or a teacher who speaks Creole, you can find one.

On Friday and all weekend, residents gathered at the churches and community centers and on street corners to share information, to strategize and mourn, to share this moment at once sadly familiar and like no other in their shared tragic history.

“You feel as if you’re not in the world right now,” said Marie S. Brevil, an artist and writer who is working on a Ph.D. in public health. “You can understand some things. If there’s rain, you can run to a house to get dry. But this, three million people displaced? Who can understand that? Who can know what to do? Everyone goes to bed restless, they’re thinking all the time about what they can do.”

Ms. Brevil was at a meeting of perhaps 100 government officials, community leaders, church members, ad hoc relief organizers and ordinary folks at Konbit Nèg Lakay, where the walls are filled with art and metal sculptures of Haitian scenes and the meeting room is dominated by a vivid tableau of an idealized Haiti — blue skies, dazzling splashes of yellow and red, palm trees and people at work in fecund fields. It was painted in a day in 2008 by visiting artists from Haiti as both art and affirmation.

At the meeting, everyone had a thought, a question, an agenda, a strategy, large or small — finding as many Creole speakers as possible for the county mental health hot line; the names of Web sites connecting victims in Haiti and relatives far away; the florists planning to provide flowers and ribbons in blue and red, the colors of Haiti’s flag, as a sign of solidarity; how to get local doctors and nurses and rescue workers to Haiti as quickly as possible; what supplies are needed and what are not.

There is some fortune in Haiti’s history of misfortune. Already in place are groups like the Ramapo Haitian Task Force and the Rockland County Haiti Relief Campaign, formed in recent years to respond to devastating storms that ravaged the nation. And with so much need even in good times, people who left Haiti long ago have never really left.

“We always have that bond, even when we’re away,” said Jean Elie Porchette, a car salesman and computer technician who has been working nonstop trying to raise relief aid and using his Facebook page as a networking site since the disaster. “All you hear is about the poverty, but Haitians are good people. I tell you Haitians care. We have heart. We have this sense of togetherness, and despite all the negative things, we’ll get through this together.”

Still, all that history can help only so much this time, when every Haitian here, from the mayor on down, has been affected in some way. Ms. Jasmin received word Thursday that her husband, who had been traveling in Haiti, survived the earthquake. And so, every encounter and greeting is really about only one thing.

“You O.K.?” a woman asked a friend Friday at Konbit Nèg Lakay.

“I’m O.K., darling.”

“I’ll keep you in my prayers.”
