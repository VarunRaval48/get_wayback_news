ESPN Positions Itself to Take On Europe

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
By SAMUEL ABT
Published: January 24, 2010

PARIS — Now a word from the sponsor: When American football’s Super Bowl is played next month, it will include pauses for 62 commercials, some of nearly two minutes, according to CBS, the network that will carry the game.

During a regular-season National Football League game, the ratio is not quite that lopsided. There are 20 commercial breaks, 10 in each half, lasting usually from one to two minutes each. The game itself consists of 60 minutes of actual play, but usually three hours in elapsed time because of interruptions for injuries, play stoppage, instant replay and, of course, pauses for commercials.

As the sports commentator Lyle Fargas at Helium.com has complained, “More and more it seems that N.F.L. games are becoming commercials with a little football action occasionally mixed in.”

But what if, in a parallel sports universe, there were all those pauses but no paid commercials to fill them?

That is a question at ESPN America as the U.S. television sports network in Europe and the Middle East celebrates its first anniversary since it rebranded the North American Sports Network. Owned by Walt Disney, ESPN America carries all the major North American professional and college sports, and some minor ones, on cable television.

“We’re in more than 40 countries now, mainly in the European Union,” said Paul Melvin, the incoming — to offices in London in February — director of communications for ESPN International, which oversees operations for ESPN channels around the world. “And we’re reaching 16 million homes, an increase of 15 percent in the year since rebranding.”

He said that since acquiring NASN in 2007, “we’ve had 167 percent growth” in terms of homes reached.

Mr. Melvin declined to discuss the network’s balance sheet, but he acknowledged that most commercials shown during sports programs in the United States did not make it across the Atlantic.

“Ads need to be relevant,” he said. “Some multinationals might be interested in appearing on our programs, but we sell the network as such — for an audience in Europe.”

Time is sold to local and regional advertisers as well.

In the United States, major advertisers include beer and automobile makers, fast-food chains and, increasingly it seems, the producers of the impotence treatments Viagra and Cialis.

“Thirty-six points in the second half on the road,” exclaimed the Sports Illustrated writer Peter King after a New Orleans Saints game this autumn. “Did you get that? Thirty-six. That’s almost as many Viagra/Cialis commercials you see in an average Sunday.”

Nobody in Europe sees those ads. ESPN America carries only a handful of income-producing commercials as it selects games from the five U.S. networks that broadcast football games: CBS, NBC, Fox, NFL Network and ESPN.

In real time — 2:30 a.m. Central European Time for an 8:30 p.m. Monday Night Football kickoff on the U.S. East Coast, 7 p.m. C.E.T. for a 1 p.m. kickoff on Sunday — ESPN America covers all 17 weeks of the regular schedule, plus playoffs. It also shows innumerable Major League Baseball games, National Hockey League games and college football and basketball games, plus filler like lacrosse when nothing big is going on.

Before there was North American Sports Network — it began in Europe in 2002 — and especially before there was ESPN America, a fan of football who lived in Europe had almost nowhere to go. Football meant soccer, Inter Milan against Juventus, not the Carolina Panthers against the Chicago Bears.

Inter-Juventus is generally nonstop action with commercials limited to halftime and blinking panels alongside the field. In the N.F.L., the rules call for 10 commercial breaks in each half of a game: The placement of two ad breaks is mandatory — at the end of a quarter and at the two-minute warning before the end of a half — and eight are optional.
