The seed list for this crawl was a list of Alexa?s top 1 million web sites, retrieved close to the crawl start date. We used Heritrix (3.1.1-SNAPSHOT) crawler software and respected robots.txt directives. The scope of the crawl was not limited except for a few manually excluded sites.

However this was a somewhat experimental crawl for us, as we were using newly minted software to feed URLs to the crawlers, and we know there were some operational issues with it. For example, in many cases we may not have crawled all of the embedded and linked objects in a page since the URLs for these resources were added into queues that quickly grew bigger than the intended size of the crawl (and therefore we never got to them). We also included repeated crawls of some Argentinian government sites, so looking at results by country will be somewhat skewed.

We have made many changes to how we do these wide crawls since this particular example, but we wanted to make the data available ?warts and all? for people to experiment with. We have also done some further analysis of the content.

Reaching Out to a Devastated Haiti

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
Published: January 14, 2010

To the Editor:

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Edel Rodriguez

With thousands feared dead and millions reported homeless in the earthquake in Haiti on Tuesday (The New York Times on the Web, Jan. 13), America must act. This sad and tragic event requires a nonpartisan moment on the part of all Americans.

I applaud President Obama for coordinating the nation’s various agencies to immediately help and bring comfort to millions.

Major buildings were damaged, roads were wiped out and infrastructure was destroyed in the poorest nation of the Western Hemisphere. The United States can make a major difference in alleviating Haiti’s suffering.

It’s time to suspend for the moment the strong partisanship on issues that dominate our airways. This is the moment to renew the American spirit of helping those in tragic need. I ask, as an American, that we all pull together and demonstrate what American compassion can accomplish.

Alan Phillips
Bloomington, Ill., Jan. 13, 2010

•

To the Editor:

Last week I was in Haiti’s Central Plateau with a group of doctors and medical students providing health care for the impoverished under the guidance of Project Medishare, a Miami-based nonprofit.

The cases we saw reinforce what we read about Haiti: it is a country deeply mired in poverty and with a long and unfortunate history. We saw a child who was on the edge of death due to malnutrition, a woman who was blind because of a disease that could have been cured by a single dose of antibiotics and many others suffering from diseases that are simple to treat and virtually unheard of in the United States.

The side of Haiti you don’t hear about is that this is a nation richer than most in love, spirit and resilience. It is the warm nature of the people that draws anyone who visits Haiti back time and again.

After visiting one old woman at her small wooden hut, she insisted that we take a few grapefruits as a gift. As we left to hugs and thanks, I thought about how selfless this woman was as her grandson handed us her only things of value.

Despite the economic downturn, few of us in the United States are in as desperate a position as the Haitians. Let us all follow their example of love and charity and help with whatever we can, even if it’s only enough for them to buy a few pieces of fruit.

Christopher Cory Gibson
Salt Lake City, Jan. 13, 2010

•

To the Editor:

I write to express my sympathy for the people of neighboring Haiti. Catastrophes like this one remind us of the fragility of all life on a still geologically active planet and of the need to work together and help one another in times of need.

We must also support more research aimed at predicting earthquakes and improving construction and retrofitting methods to make old and new buildings better able to withstand the incredible forces experienced during an earthquake.

Though earthquakes are a fact of nature, we can do much to reduce the tremendous suffering and loss of life that are typically associated with these events.

Michael Pravica
Henderson, Nev., Jan. 13, 2010

The writer is an associate professor of physics at the University of Nevada, Las Vegas.

•

To the Editor:

Even before this calamity, Haiti was already coping with an incredible rate of unemployment, hunger and child slavery (known as restaveks, more than 200,000 children in Haiti survive as unpaid domestic servants) and a dilapidated natural, social and economic infrastructure.

Plagued by successive natural disasters, Haiti is also afflicted with one of the highest fertility rates in the hemisphere. The average Haitian mother has five children; half of Haitian children never attend school.

The Inter-American Development Bank, the World Bank and other aid agencies must coordinate their efforts and act urgently to help Haiti.

Getúlio P. Carvalho
Alexandria, Va., Jan. 13, 2010

An earlier version of this letter appeared as a comment on nytimes.com.

•

To the Editor:

It’s time to expedite the move of the detainees from Guantánamo Bay in Cuba to prisons in the United States. With the current crisis in Haiti, we could use the prison instead as a staging base for aid.

Charlie Patrick
Longmont, Colo., Jan. 13, 2010
