Connecticut Attorney General Says Hell Seek Senate Seat, and Is Quickly Embraced

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By RAYMOND HERNANDEZ and ALISON LEIGH COWAN
Published: January 6, 2010

Richard Blumenthal, the popular Connecticut attorney general, announced on Wednesday that he would seek the United States Senate seat being vacated by Christopher J. Dodd, suddenly altering the dynamic of an already volatile race.

Mr. Blumenthal’s move came just hours after Mr. Dodd, a fellow Democrat, ended his bid for re-election amid growing signs that his recent political troubles might cost the party a seat it has held for nearly half a century.

Mr. Blumenthal, 63, was quickly endorsed by Democratic leaders from Hartford to Washington, who hope that his high name recognition, untarnished reputation and popularity among voters will allow them to defend the seat in November against a Republican Party that had aggressively worked to defeat Mr. Dodd. Senator Robert Menendez of New Jersey, the chairman of the Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee, said the party embraced Mr. Blumenthal’s candidacy.

In fact, many senior Democrats had been saying privately for weeks that the party would be better off with Mr. Blumenthal on the ticket instead of Mr. Dodd. With the developments on Wednesday, those sentiments became public.

George Jepsen, a former Connecticut Senate majority leader and former Democratic state chairman, said Mr. Blumenthal’s candidacy instantly transformed a race that Republicans had stood a “significant chance” of winning while Mr. Dodd was the candidate.

“I think he is very likely to beat any Republican,” he said of Mr. Blumenthal.

Mr. Blumenthal, a five-term attorney general, is regarded as strong-willed and ambitious, a man who has plotted a jump to higher office for years. But since he was elected attorney general in 1990, he has never been truly tested in a statewide campaign, having cruised to re-election without needing to raise the large sums of money that candidates in more competitive races do.

With a wiry frame and hurried manner, Mr. Blumenthal is widely known in Connecticut as a hard-working official with an eye for hot-button issues, taking on drug makers and credit card companies. He was also one of the architects of the multibillion-dollar settlement that several states reached with tobacco companies in the late 1990s.

Announcing his candidacy at Democratic Party headquarters in Hartford, Mr. Blumenthal said he would “fight energetically and tenaciously for the people of Connecticut in the U.S. Senate,” as he did in the attorney general’s office.

For all his ambition, Mr. Blumenthal has earned a reputation as something of a political Hamlet. He passed up the opportunity to run for governor several times and once struggled with whether to pursue an appointment to a federal judgeship, before finally withdrawing his name from consideration.

But his allies take issue with the notion that he has been excessively cautious.

“People have always ascribed various motives as to why he didn’t run for governor,” said Roy Occhiogrosso, a Democratic consultant. “But I think it was never all that complicated. He really enjoys being attorney general. And the only job that has ever interested him enough to leave the job he loves is being a United States senator.”

The belief in Democratic circles that Mr. Blumenthal would be a stronger candidate than Mr. Dodd followed widespread public criticism of the senator about his ties to pharmaceutical and banking interests, including Countrywide Financial, a major subprime lender. It intensified in the fall as Mr. Dodd continued to struggle in the polls, even after having spent hundreds of thousands of dollars on television advertising over the summer.

Mr. Dodd’s shaky standing was underscored by a parade of Republicans who came forward to challenge him, including two potentially formidable candidates: Linda McMahon, the former chief executive of World Wrestling Entertainment, and Rob Simmons, a former congressman. In her first few months in the race, Ms. McMahon has spent more than $5 million of her own money running television and radio advertisements, assembling a campaign staff, and traveling around the state.

Republicans moved quickly to label Mr. Blumenthal as a Dodd protégé.

“As attorney general, Blumenthal looked the other way on Senator Dodd’s unethical indiscretions,” said Amber Wilkerson Marchand, a spokeswoman for the National Republican Senatorial Committee. “But voters won’t look the other way when they cast their ballots to restore checks and balances in Washington this November. The last thing the people of Connecticut need is another 20-year member of the old boys’ club to represent them in the U.S. Senate.”

In an interview Wednesday night, Mr. Blumenthal dismissed the attack, noting that his office sued Countrywide over predatory lending and other practices, and recovered hundreds of millions of dollars for consumers.

“Far from being anyone’s protégé, I have a record of 20 years as an activist attorney general fighting corporate wrongdoing and law-breaking,” he said.

Mr. Dodd’s political advisers expect him to have $1 million left in his campaign treasury when his operation shuts down. Jay Howser, Mr. Dodd’s campaign manager, said the senator plans to be “as helpful as humanly possible” to Mr. Blumenthal and other Democratic candidates, though some senior Democrats would like him to transfer the excess funds to the national party.

As for Mr. Blumenthal, Democrats say they are confident he will prove to be a strong fund-raiser. Moreover, some party officials are counting on Mr. Blumenthal, who lives with his wife and four children in Greenwich, to tap his family’s wealth to finance the race.

But Mr. Blumenthal, in the interview, ruled that out, saying he would rely on donations from supporters.

“My campaign will be a grass-roots, bottom-up effort,” he said.

Robert Davey contributed reporting.
