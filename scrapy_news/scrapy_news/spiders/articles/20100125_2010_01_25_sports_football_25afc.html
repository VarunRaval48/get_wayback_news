Colts 30, Jets 17

Colts Put End to Jets Surprising Run

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By GREG BISHOP
Published: January 24, 2010

INDIANAPOLIS — The Jets trudged toward their locker room in silence as confetti swirled behind them on the field. The N.F.L.’s most talkative team had finally been muted, muzzled by quarterback Peyton Manning and the explosive hometown Colts in the American Football Conference championship game.

The family of Coach Rex Ryan leaned against a railing. Several members appeared near tears. Ryan had turned a team, an organization and a league into believers, yet the Colts were the ones headed to the Super Bowl while the Jets only added to their 41-year drought.

After the Colts completed their 30-17 triumph Sunday in front of the 67,650 who blanketed Lucas Oil Stadium in blue, Jets receiver Braylon Edwards embraced Rob Ryan, Rex’s twin, along the railing.

“We’ll be back next year,” Edwards said.

He sounded convinced and devastated. A month ago, even the Jets believed their season was finished; even Rex Ryan declared their playoff chances dead.

What happened afterward seemed like divine intervention to the players. The Colts and the Cincinnati Bengals rested starters in the final two regular-season games, the Jets won two road playoff games, and the Jets’ bandwagon filled to capacity.

Receiver Jerricho Cotchery was a believer. Despite Manning’s mastery, despite his 377 yards passing and 3 touchdowns, Cotchery was convinced until he glanced at the scoreboard and watched the final seconds tick away. He was certain this season would last another two weeks until the very moment that it ended.

That the Jets’ roster is stocked with talented young players offered little solace for many.

“You never know when an opportunity like this is going to come again,” safety Jim Leonhard said. “Maybe this football team needed to get here and have this experience in order to take the next step. We thought we were ready this year. Maybe we weren’t.”

In the back corner of the visitors’ locker room, tackle D’Brickashaw Ferguson reflected and dressed at the same time. He predicted before this season that he would make the Pro Bowl and that the Jets would win the Super Bowl. He got one call right.

Ferguson tried to peddle positives. He pointed to the growth of quarterback Mark Sanchez over the final month. He talked about how the team stuck together when it lost six of seven games and fell to 4-6. In two weeks, Ferguson claimed he would feel better about this season, more proud. Told that he spit out the positives like bad broccoli, Ferguson relented.

“I definitely don’t feel that way right now,” he said.

The Jets played the first 28 minutes about as well as they could have hoped. Sanchez dropped an 80-yard play-action touchdown pass into Edwards’s waiting arms. Brad Smith, the college quarterback the Jets converted to wide receiver, threw a 45-yard strike to Cotchery that set up Sanchez’s second touchdown toss.

On that play, Sanchez found tight end Dustin Keller for a 9-yard score. Keller is a Purdue graduate and Indiana native whose uncle Tony Kendall died last week. Kendall left Keller a voice mail message Tuesday, told him to give everything he had. Keller responded with his third touchdown this postseason, one dedicated to Uncle Tony.

Linebacker David Harris had already sacked Manning twice, and the Jets had built a 17-6 advantage at the two-minute warning. Then Manning found Austin Collie for a 46-yard gain, setting up a 16-yard touchdown pass to Collie on the next play.

The Jets still led, 17-13, but the game changed there, at that moment. Ryan said the score “took the wind out of our sails.” Players went a step further. In the locker room at halftime, they said there was palpable frustration, a sense that the lead they held was tenuous, that the score before the half would come back to haunt them, somehow.

“We were 30 minutes away, 30 minutes from the Super Bowl,” tackle Damien Woody said.

More blows came in the third quarter. Running back Shonn Greene, the first rookie in N.F.L. history to gain at least 125 rushing yards in his first two postseason games, left for good with a rib injury. Jay Feely missed a second field-goal attempt, this time from 52 yards out.

Manning cut the Jets’ defense with the ease and precision befitting his status among the greatest quarterbacks in N.F.L. history. Ryan said the Jets tried man coverage, two-man coverage, zone coverage — “We tried you-name-it coverage,” he said — but Manning continued to exploit the Jets’ overmatched secondary. He passed for two of his touchdowns in the second half, when the Colts ensured their trip to Super Bowl XLIV on Feb. 7 against the New Orleans Saints.

“If you can’t disrupt his rhythm, he’s going to kill you,” Ryan said.

Regaining life fell to Sanchez, who was attempting to become the first rookie quarterback since the N.F.L.-A.F.L. merger to win a conference title game. Sanchez surpassed 200 passing yards in only 4 of the 17 games he started before this one. But he passed for 257 yards here. He scrambled away from pressure and converted third downs, his football growth over the last month as evident as the full beard on his face.

“That bodes well for next year,” fullback Tony Richardson said. “He was poised. He got us out of bad situations. He played as well as anyone could have expected.”

Afterward, Richardson told his younger teammates to remember how this felt, to store the pain for future motivation. As the players stalked into the locker room, their owner, Woody Johnson, thanked them and told them he was proud.

Of this season, Johnson said he had never “seen anything like it.”

Johnson was as conflicted as the Jets, maybe more so. His daughter Casey was found dead at the start of this playoff run. Even wins gave Johnson little solace, and he deflected that question afterward, saying only that the Jets tried to win the game.

As he left, the Jets gathered in the tunnel with their families. They spoke in hushed tones, noting both the talent that remains for next season and the difficulty teams have in returning to title games, talented or not.

“All my emotions are pointing toward disappointment,” Cotchery said. “I’m not even thinking about next year. I’m disappointed all the way around.”

He paused, conflicted still, then added, “We feel like we’re building something special around here.”
