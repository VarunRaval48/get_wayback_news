Oscar Race Begins at Golden Globes

Monica Almeida/The New York Times

From left, Sam Worthington, James Cameron, Zoe Saldana, Jon Landau and Sigourney Weaver of “Avatar.” More Photos >

  • Sign In to E-Mail

By BROOKS BARNES and MICHAEL CIEPLY
Published: January 17, 2010

BEVERLY HILLS, Calif. — A rain-drenched Hollywood paused in its efforts to raise money for Haitian earthquake victims on Sunday long enough to focus on the Golden Globes, perhaps the most frivolous of the high-profile stops on the entertainment industry’s awards trail. The big winner: James Cameron’s “Avatar,” which came away with awards for best dramatic picture and best director.

As usual, the 67th annual Golden Globe Awards — decided by the fewer than 90 members of the Hollywood Foreign Press Association — spread the love around, with no one television series or film dominating. “The Hangover,” about a Las Vegas bachelor party gone terribly wrong, won best comedy or musical, while Sandra Bullock, receiving a boost heading into Oscar nominations, won for her acting in the sports drama “The Blind Side.” “Glee,” about a lovable group of teenage losers, won best TV comedy.

“This is for anybody and everybody who got a wedgie in high school,” said Ryan Murphy, a creator of the program, as he accepted the trophy.

Several high-profile Oscar pictures were shut out or virtually ignored, including “Up in the Air,” which led the nominations with six heading into the night. It only won for screenwriting — a disappointment judging from the subsequent sour looks on the face of Jason Reitman, a co-writer and director of the picture. Another awards darling, “The Hurt Locker,” walked away empty handed, as did the musical “Nine.”

Meryl Streep, competing against herself in the category of best comedy or musical, won for playing an indomitable Julia Child in “Julie & Julia.” Accepting the award, Ms. Streep, whose other nomination was for “It’s Complicated,” first joked that she wanted to change her name to T-Bone, a reference to T-Bone Burnett, who had just won for best original song for “Crazy Heart.”

But then she struck a humble tone, saying that she had “played so many extraordinary women that I’m getting mistaken for one.” She said she was conflicted about putting on her “happy movie self” in the face of darker events unfolding in the world.

Jeff Bridges took the award for best actor in a drama for his role as a washed-up country singer in “Crazy Heart,” while Robert Downey Jr. won best actor in a comedy for “Sherlock Holmes.”

The ceremony kicked off with Mo’Nique collecting the trophy for best supporting actress in a motion picture for her monster of a mother in the tough urban drama “Precious: Based on the Novel ‘Push’ by Sapphire.” “I am in the midst of my dream,” said Mo’Nique, considered a lock for the award going into the ceremony.

The Austrian actor Christoph Waltz won for best supporting actor in a motion picture for playing a suave Nazi in Quentin Tarantino’s “Inglourious Basterds.”

A scattered, breathless Drew Barrymore won best actress in a television movie for “Grey Gardens,” while Julianna Margulies — in a surprise — won best actress in a television drama for “The Good Wife.” Showtime picked up three honors during the first half of the ceremony, including trophies for Michael C. Hall and John Lithgow (“Dexter”) and Toni Collette (“United States of Tara”). But HBO, as has become typical, walked away with the most television trophies: four.

For the third year in a row, AMC’s “Mad Men” took the award for best dramatic television series.

Best animated feature film went to Pixar’s “Up,” which also won for its score.

Rain turned the red-carpet spectacle preceding the ceremony into more of a circus than usual, with nominees navigating enormous puddles; golf umbrellas were the accessory of choice. It was hard to look glamorous in the process (especially for the bedraggled assistants), but most stars tried to keep a sense of humor. When an interviewer asked Sandra Bullock, a double nominee, what would happen to her hair if it got wet, she responded, “Chia Pet.”

Inside the ballroom, in the not-so-subtle pecking order of the Globes, ringside seats were reserved for movie royalty, while television powers sat mostly one flight up. Down front, one power point was Table 4, which had places set for Leonardo DiCaprio, Martin Scorsese, George Lucas, Robert De Niro, Cameron Diaz and Tom Hanks.

Another sizable presence was Mr. Cameron. Although his 3-D blockbuster, which neared $500 million at the North American box office over the weekend, had just four nominations — and none in the acting categories, as its impact owed more to its visual wizardry — Mr. Cameron was a center of attention in the ballroom, partly because his front-and-center table included California’s governor, Arnold Schwarzenegger, whose acting career took off with his performance as a cyborg in Mr. Cameron’s “Terminator” films.

Picking up his trophy for best director, Mr. Cameron struck a demure note. “Frankly, I thought Kathryn was going to get this,” he said, referring to his ex-wife, Kathryn Bigelow, a directing nominee for “The Hurt Locker.”

Released by 20th Century Fox in mid-December, “Avatar” — long advertised as a potential game-changer for the film industry because of its immersive, 3-D technology — quickly picked up award nominations from the directors’, writers’ and producers’ guilds, suddenly making the film a focal point for a Hollywood prize season that for months had seemed to be without a front-runner.

Days before the show, Hollywood’s celebrities, not unaware of the contrast between its glitter and the suffering around the world, had begun pushing an effort to relieve Haitian earthquake victims with a telethon scheduled for broadcast on about a dozen networks on Jan. 22. George Clooney, among the telethon’s organizers, was on hand Sunday as nominee for his performance in “Up in the Air,” about a corporate executioner in search of himself.

“I’m going to use it as a recruiting tool, because there’s a lot of people here we want to work on the telethon on Friday night,” Mr. Clooney said in an interview. “I’m going to go table to table with a pocketful of phone numbers.”

On the lighter side, Julia Roberts took her few minutes with “Access Hollywood” to mock NBC for its low ratings and late-night troubles. “NBC — I mean, you guys are in the toilet right now,” Ms. Roberts said to a stunned interviewer. Tina Fey cracked: “It’s not rain. It’s just God crying for NBC.”

This year, the Globes broke with past practice by hiring a host — the British comedian Ricky Gervais — and scheduling its three-hour broadcast on NBC as a live event across the country, rather than delaying the show in some time zones. Given the immediacy of the Internet, audiences on the West Coast had less incentive to watch a delayed broadcast because they already knew the winners.

Mr. Gervais’s banter — relatively tame at the opening of the show, aside from some off-color patter about his private parts — grew increasingly cheeky. Appearing on stage with a beer, Mr. Gervais introduced Mel Gibson, who has struggled with alcoholism, by saying: “I love a drink as much as the next man. Unless that man is Mel Gibson.”

About 15 million people watched the telecast last year, a big improvement from 2008, when the gala was replaced by a glorified news conference because of a writers’ strike and only six million tuned in. But the audience for last year’s show was still down sharply from 2007, when over 20 million watched.

The real-time broadcast eased the way for viewing parties in Los Angeles at restaurants like Craft, where Fox executives gathered to cheer its slate of contenders — movies that included “Avatar,” “(500) Days of Summer,” “Crazy Heart” and “Fantastic Mr. Fox,” and the television shows “Damages,” “Glee,” “House,” “How I Met Your Mother” and “Modern Family.”

If the Globes ceremony has sometimes been derided as a bit of an empty exercise — its prizes are awarded by so few people, and even they have been courted lavishly for months by aspiring winners — this year saw its populist approach spread elsewhere.

The Globes have long handed out a combined 10 nominations in its two best picture categories — best dramatic film and best comedy or musical — in a clear bid to attract stars and spread the glory. This year, the Academy of Motion Pictures Arts and Sciences will expand its nominees for the best picture Oscar to 10 from 5, though without splitting them into separate dramatic and comedy categories.

Because the Globes are awarded while Academy Awards voters still have their nominating ballots in hand, they exert considerable gravitational pull over the Oscar process. The best picture Oscar has mirrored the association’s choice for best drama or best comedy-musical in 15 of the last 22 years.

Still, the celebrity presence that makes the Golden Globes an annual Hollywood party for attendees and viewers alike owes much to its enormous slate of nominees in the television categories.

This year, Glenn Close, Jessica Lange, Drew Barrymore, Ms. Collette — all known for their past work in movies — donned jewels and evening wear and graced tables in the hotel’s ballroom, as they were honored for their work in television.

Similarly, Mr. Gervais has done his share of films, including “Night at the Museum” and “Ghost Town,” but the audience probably knows him best for his role as the self-embarrassing boss on the BBC version of “The Office.”

Melena Ryzik contributed reporting.

This article has been revised to reflect the following correction:

Correction: January 19, 2010
An article on Monday about the Golden Globe Awards, where the AMC show “Mad Men” was named best dramatic television series, misstated, in some editions, the number of consecutive years it has won that honor. This was the third consecutive year, not the second.
