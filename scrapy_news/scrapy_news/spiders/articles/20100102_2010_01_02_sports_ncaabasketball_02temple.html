Owl Who Took Unusual Path Has the Shot to Match It

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By KEVIN ARMSTRONG
Published: January 1, 2010

PHILADELPHIA — Of the six flights that Temple guard Juan Fernandez took during his round-trip journey home to Argentina and back to campus last week, one was postponed by snow and another because of engine problems. As he prepared to board for the last leg out of Miami International Airport, he was detained for four hours. He had forgotten part of his paperwork.

“Delays have kind of become my thing,” he said.

Fernandez, 19, whose family followed his father, Gustavo, around Argentina to six cities during a 17-year professional basketball career, was unsure of his itinerary two years ago. Several teams in Spain’s B or C leagues had made him offers to play, but his interest was in the United States. To gauge his son’s value there, the father called the national team member Pepe Sanchez, a former Temple star, about the college route.

“Most go the easy path of making the European money,” Sanchez said in a phone interview from his hometown, Bahia Blanca. “To go to school is a sacrifice for them.”

After deciding on Temple, Fernandez, a low-key sophomore with a disheveled hairdo, finished high school last December and made his debut on his third day in the United States. Fully adjusted now, Fernandez leads the No. 18 Owls (9-2) in assists per game (3.5) and is second in scoring (14.3). When they host No. 1 Kansas (11-0) on Saturday, his two-handed jumper and expansive vision will be on display.

“If he’s killing with that jump shot, it gives them that big-time weapon down the stretch,” said Siena Coach Fran McCaffery, whose Saints saw Fernandez score 20 points on 4-for-6 shooting from behind the arc in November.

Fernandez’s follow-through is far from textbook. He gets little elevation on jumpers, but the release includes a snapping motion by both wrists. After he hyperextended his left thumb in a collision with his teammate Michael Eric during a recent practice, Fernandez listened to his father joke about the positive that could come from it.

“Maybe it will make you more conventional,” he said.

Three weeks later, few questioned the motion during the Owls’ upset of third-ranked Villanova. Fernandez, who is 6 feet 4 inches, was 7 for 9 from 3-point range and scored a career-high 33 points.

“He’s really not a scorer,” said one N.B.A. scout, who spoke on condition of anonymity so as not violate league rules about commenting on underclassmen. “Passing and smarts are his strengths. He lacks N.B.A. quickness.”

Fernandez’s game conforms to the team-oriented model of his countrymen. Like San Antonio Spurs guard Manu Ginobili, whom Fernandez was introduced to and had dinner with last winter, and other Argentines, Fernandez focused on skill development when he was young. One-on-one play was limited to nonpractice time, and strength and conditioning were rarely, if ever, stressed.

“There are times when we’re going to need him to go outside the structure of the offense or another team will isolate a quicker guard against him,” the Temple assistant Matt Langel said. “It’s important he knows who he is and plays the angles.”

Lifting weights was a foreign concept to Fernandez before last winter. He wore gloves to his first session, which drew laughs from teammates.

“None of us were on that level,” the freshman T. J. DiLeo said.

As a 6-year-old, Fernandez asked his father to teach him how to play basketball. Playing then for the Boca Juniors, the elder Fernandez brought his son to a youth practice before his game. On the first possession, the boy did the unexpected.

“He just picked up the ball and looked like an American football player running the other way,” said Fernandez’s mother, Nancy Fiandrino.

Gustavo Fernandez, a pass-first point guard, straightened out his son and related locker room lessons. Each season, an American played on his team. The elder Fernandez would listen to the expatriates’ music and learn English from the lyrics.

“He was the brain,” Sanchez said of the father. “He left the game a legend here.”

The United States has embraced Fernandez, too. Each time he connected on a 3-point attempt last week against Bowling Green, the student section in McGonigle Hall chanted, “Ole, ole, ole!”

Some of the soccer chants have been lost in translation. Two weeks ago, Seton Hall fans repeatedly yelled, “Messi, Messi,” in a taunting tone.

“They did not know that Messi is one of my favorite soccer stars,” Fernandez said, referring to the Argentine striker Lionel Messi, who has been compared to Diego Maradona. “It was a compliment to me and served as motivation.”

In Fernandez’s hometown, Rio Tercero, a farming and textile area of 50,000 residents, his family projects Temple games on a wall in their restaurant, Pino Grigio, and locals follow games on finicky Internet feeds.

“Everyone knows you when you leave your town,” Juan Fernandez said.

Fans have begun to follow Fernandez into the airports. In line before his first return flight near his home last week, one man, whom he had never met, approached Fernandez and congratulated him on the Villanova outburst.

“Everyone kind of knows,” Fernandez said. “TV brings it everywhere.”

For those tuning in, it was clearest sign that he had arrived.
