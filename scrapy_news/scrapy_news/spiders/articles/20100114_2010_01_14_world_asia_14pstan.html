Deaths at Hands of Militants Rise in Pakistan

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By SABRINA TAVERNISE
Published: January 13, 2010

ISLAMABAD, Pakistan — The number of Pakistani civilians killed in militant attacks rose by a third in 2009 over the previous year, according to a new research report, a toll that exceeded even the number of civilian deaths in Afghanistan.

The report, released this week by the Pakistan Institute for Peace Studies, an independent research group based in Islamabad that tracks security issues, found that 3,021 Pakistanis were killed in insurgent attacks, 33 percent more than in 2008.

A United Nations report released Wednesday said that 2,412 civilians were killed in Afghanistan in 2009, attributing most of those deaths to militant attacks. The higher toll in Pakistan was driven by a surge of suicide bombings by militants against civilian targets, including mosques, a university and public markets.

Pakistan’s Interior Ministry said the number of deaths totaled 1,674, lower than the institute’s count. But the institute’s director, Muhammad Amir Rana, argued that his group’s data was more comprehensive, drawing on local media reports, which its employees double check, as well as official sources.

Militants carried out 87 suicide attacks in 2009, up from 63 the previous year, according to the institute. The bombings, which tend to inflict harm over a wide area, also helped account for a 60 percent rise in injuries, to 7,334.

The institute began issuing the reports in 2006, Mr. Rana said, to give a clearer picture of militancy in Pakistan. Since then, the number of Pakistanis killed in militant attacks has more than tripled.

While Pakistan has long had problems with violence, including sectarian fighting throughout the 1990s, it was not until after the Sept. 11, 2001, attacks in the United States that major terrorist attacks began to intensify. In 2005, attacks increased significantly, and have risen every year since, with their numbers doubling between 2005 and 2007, Mr. Rana said. The overwhelming majority of the suicide bombings last year were in North-West Frontier Province, a populous area that borders Afghanistan and Pakistan’s tribal areas, where the militants are strong.

In a countertrend, there was a drop in bombings from militants in tribal areas, where the military has begun an operation against them — 7, compared with 16 the previous year.

Punjab, the most populous province and the military and political heart of the country, suffered 15 suicide attacks, the second-highest number in the country, up from 8 in 2008, but far below the 52 that militants conducted in North-West Frontier Province. Punjab, ethnically distinct from the border regions in the mountainous west, had been relatively untouched by violence until recently. The surge in bombings last year shocked Pakistanis and helped turn public opinion and media coverage against the Taliban, though anger at the government and the United States also spiked.

The number of sectarian attacks jumped by 86 percent compared with 2008, according to the report, with the highest concentration in Dera Ismail Khan, a city in North-West Frontier Province whose Shiite minority has been made a target by militants.

In all, researchers counted 12,632 deaths from violence in 2009, of which about half were deaths associated with the Pakistani military’s campaigns against Taliban militants throughout 2009. The military keeps specific tallies of its own dead, though militant casualties are more difficult to track and are often based on estimates and not body counts in the field. Battlefields are rarely accessible to journalists.

Also included in the tally were 667 people — mostly civilians — killed in American drone strikes, the report said. An additional 2,000 were killed in nonmilitant violence, including political violence and tribal fighting.
