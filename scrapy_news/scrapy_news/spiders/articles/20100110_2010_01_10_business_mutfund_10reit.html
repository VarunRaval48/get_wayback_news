Just How Much Steam Do REITs Have Left?

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By ROBERT D. HERSHEY Jr.
Published: January 9, 2010

INVESTORS thinking of putting fresh money into real estate investment trusts might easily be dissuaded by unremitting news of distressed commercial property — and predictions that the situation will become even worse.

What’s more, REIT shares have already doubled from their low point in March, handily outpacing the 65 percent rebound of the Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index through the end of last year. That suggests that REITs are no longer bargains.

“We went from a near-death experience to a shocking investment recovery,” said Mike Kirby, chairman and research director of Green Street Advisors, a REIT specialist.

For all of 2009, mutual funds that invest in American real estate, a category that includes REIT funds, gained 30.35 percent, after a 39.7 percent battering in 2008, according to Lipper.

Despite the stocks’ recovery and the persistently grim headlines of default and bankruptcy, careful investors may still find opportunities if they are seeking diversified exposure to real estate — and without having to worry about tenants with wee-hours plumbing crises.

“Yields are still handsome, and there is room for continued growth,” said Tom Roseen, a senior research manager for Lipper. “People are able to get into these funds” at prices still little changed from 2003, the middle of seven consecutive years of positive performance, the longest stretch for any sizable asset class.

Mr. Kirby said that while the shares were no longer in the bargain bin, “our thinking is that REITs should be part of every portfolio — always.”

Apart from paying dividends that average a tad under 4 percent — not nearly as juicy as they were last spring but still twice that of the S.& P. 500 — many publicly traded REITs are planning to scoop up distressed properties that banks and other lenders are expected to toss onto the market. (Publicly traded REITs hold 10 percent of the nation’s commercial real estate.)

Among the likely sellers are many private REITs and other investors that bought at the height of the market in mid-decade with huge amounts of borrowed money — great waves of which will be due for repayment and will not be able to be refinanced.

Many sellers will be sacrificing certain property to hold on to other parts of their portfolios, said Brad Case, research vice president for the National Association of Real Estate Investment Trusts, the industry trade group.

As banks’ fortunes improve with economic recovery, regulators are thought to be less likely to let them keep benefiting from a practice of “pretend and extend,” under which they have deferred recognition of obvious real estate losses.

“As concern about systemic risk diminishes, they’ll have to sell distressed property,” Mr. Case said. “It’ll get started in 2010 but it really won’t peak until 2012.”

In fact, several new REITs, so-called blind pools because they currently own no property, might be able to take advantage of the situation by buying. One of them, Pebblebrook Hotel Trust, raised $350 million in an initial public offering last month and now sells at a premium (8.75 percent on Wednesday).

Hotels, lacking long-term leases, are considered one of the riskiest types of REIT, followed by those invested mainly or heavily in office buildings and shopping centers. In June, for example, Sunstone Hotel Investors, which bought the W San Diego Hotel in 2006, turned it over to lenders when it could not make a mortgage payment. Apartment REITs and self-storage REITs are among the least risky, though the self-storage variety has recently performed poorly.

Jason Luker, a principal at Cardinal Group Investments, said that while he finds prices of many older REITs with soured so-called legacy assets “a little frothy right now,” certain new REITs could be rewarding. “Some of these I.P.O.’s are going to be very attractive,” he said.

One of his favorite established REITs is Essex Property Trust, which owns and operates apartment houses on the West Coast.

Financing fire-sale merchandise should be relatively easy, analysts say, because REITs have been raising huge amounts of equity capital since the liquidity crisis lifted early in 2009, minimizing default risk, and the markets reopened.

REITs are highly sensitive to capital market conditions, which remain very difficult for many borrowers, because REITs must pay at least 90 percent of earnings as dividends to shareholders to keep from being taxed themselves.

Mr. Kirby of Green Street agrees that many REITs, especially private ones, are overleveraged and will be hard-pressed to refinance some $1.5 trillion of mortgage debt coming due over the next four years. But while he considers the market “on the pricey side of a fair range,” he shuns pessimism.

“There’s a lot of press about how the fundamentals are horrible,” he said, acknowledging that cash flows will decline for another year or so. “It’s ugly. But we all know it’s ugly — so now let’s price it,” he added.

Although investors can directly buy shares of REITs, which are hands-on managers of the properties they own, mutual funds and exchange-traded funds that buy REIT shares generally provide more diversification and less risk. Public REITs and funds are also highly transparent because property holdings are typically individually disclosed.

There are some 400 classes of REIT fund shares, including ones sponsored by Fidelity, Vanguard, Cohen & Steers, ING, John Hancock and Barclays.

Vanguard’s entry, the REIT Index fund, is managed passively to mimic the entire market, a generally sound strategy but one that also means there is no manager trying to produce stellar results in a market that may be inefficiently analyzed and priced.

“A savvy active manager can make a difference,” said Mr. Roseen.

Taxes on REIT earnings and gains can be a bit complicated, and many specialists suggest holding them in I.R.A.’s or other tax-deferred accounts.

“Be very selective,” advised Scott L. Robinson, professor of real estate capital markets at New York University, who is a particular admirer of the retail-oriented Simon Property Group, the nation’s largest public real estate company, and who finds REIT shares generally “fairly priced” these days.

Insist on solid balance sheets and dividend coverage, make sure that portfolio companies have access to capital and, especially, “look for management teams that have been around for a few recessions,” he said.
