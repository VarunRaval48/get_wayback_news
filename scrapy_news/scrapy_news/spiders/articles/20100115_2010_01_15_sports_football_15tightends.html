Tight Ends Pose a Bigger Receiving Headache for Defenses

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By JUDY BATTISTA
Published: January 14, 2010

INDIANAPOLIS — When Mike Ditka played for the Chicago Bears in the 1960s, he took great pride in blocking for running back Gale Sayers. Tight ends then were used mostly like another tackle. Their primary job was to wall off the edge of the defense for the most important offensive weapon of the time: the runners. But the Bears’ coach, George Halas, had other ideas. He sometimes put space between Ditka and the offensive line, making Ditka one of the first tight ends to split out a bit and become a receiving threat. As a rookie in 1961, Ditka was the team’s leading receiver, catching 56 passes for 1,076 yards. In 1964, he caught 75 passes.

The Chargers’ Antonio Gates has taken up Ditka’s mantle, and then some, averaging 14.6 yards a catch this season.

But even Ditka was amazed at what he saw from his old position this season. This was the year of the tight end, when the proliferation of spread offenses shined a spotlight on the end result of what Halas and Ditka helped to start: the tight end as overgrown receiver. Big, fast and with such good hands that some teams use cornerbacks to defend them, they are a terrifying matchup for defenses.

“They are power forwards, guys that are 6-5 and 250 pounds and can run,” Ditka said. “Our linemen were 6-4 and 245 pounds. The game has changed. Any coach that doesn’t take advantage of his tight end is leaving a major part of his offense on the field.”

This weekend’s divisional playoff games will showcase many of the best of them. According to FootballOutsiders.com, which performs statistical analysis of the N.F.L., the San Diego Chargers’ Antonio Gates, who had 1,157 receiving yards and a staggering 14.6 yards per reception, had the most valuable season of any tight end in the last 16 years. The Indianapolis Colts’ Dallas Clark had 100 receptions, tying him for the fifth most in the N.F.L., giving him the 10th most valuable season in the last 16 years, FootballOutsiders.com said.

The Dallas Cowboys’ Jason Witten caught more passes than any of his teammates, with 94. The Minnesota Vikings’ Visanthe Shiancoe caught 56 passes, 11 for touchdowns. The Baltimore Ravens’ Todd Heap had 53 receptions, 10 for 20 yards or more. Even Dustin Keller, the Jets’ usually anonymous tight end, broke out last weekend in the wild-card victory over the Cincinnati Bengals with three receptions, two for more than 40 yards.

“They’re a totally different thing to prepare for now than they used to be,” said Jets safety Jim Leonhard, who will most likely have some responsibility for Gates on Sunday. “You weren’t as worried about the vertical threat. You could get away with putting a linebacker on a tight end. Some still try with linebackers and generally that doesn’t work out that well. It is a chess game, and it’s not the same old game. That chess piece now has another move in its arsenal.”

As the passing game proliferated, first in the N.F.L. and then in college football, tight ends with good blocking skills became less valued and have nearly disappeared. That has forced teams to spread tight ends out and treat them like a receiver, said Kyle Shanahan, the Washington Redskins’ new offensive coordinator. Defenses have adjusted and started using defensive backs — usually safeties — to defend them.

“If you’ve got the right tight end, he’ll do real good with that matchup,” said Shanahan, who had tight end Owen Daniels in Houston last year and now will coach Chris Cooley. “Defenses are taking away some of the things on the outside, so you’ve got to answer on the inside. If tight ends are having a big year, I bet a lot of defenses will see that and it will be harder to get to tight ends next year.”

But the new-wave tight ends are so multidimensional that no matchup is a perfect fit. They have the speed to run away from a linebacker. The Houston Texans put linebacker Brian Cushing on Clark on Nov. 8 and Clark caught 14 passes, tying for the third-most receptions in a game by a tight end in league history.

And tight ends have the size to get their body between a safety and the ball, leaving a safety in no-man’s land. Most defenses, then, try to give tight ends different looks. They will use linebackers to bump them at the line of scrimmage to disrupt their route. Or they will use a defensive back when they play off the line of scrimmage, forcing a tight end to run a route and beat the defense like a receiver. The Vikings planned to mix man and zone coverage against Witten on Sunday.

“I think he has a great ability and a great rapport with the quarterback to be able to shake both safeties as well as linebackers he can beat man to man,” Vikings Coach Brad Childress said of Witten. “He can play at the line of scrimmage in the run game. He’s sneaky deep, he can get deep.”

That, too, is a change. In the past, the tight end was meant as a safe outlet for a short dump pass from the quarterback or perhaps as a change-of-gear option on early downs. In 1995, the Cowboys’ Jay Novacek caught 62 passes for 705 yards, but just 5 passes for 20 or more yards. The former San Francisco quarterback Steve Young, now an ESPN analyst, cited Shannon Sharpe as the bridge to the current tight end prototype. In 1996, Sharpe caught 80 passes for 1,062 yards, 16 of them for at least 20 yards.

Because of their speed, tight ends are more of a threat to catch intermediate and deep passes now, which makes them a greater threat on third down. Clark caught 28 passes on third down, the fourth most in the league, and his overall reception average was 11.1 yards. Gates had 21 receptions on third down, with an average third-down reception of 15.5 yards. In all, he had 18 receptions for at least 20 yards this season and an average of 14.6 yards per reception.

In his 13-year playing career, Ozzie Newsome, the Cleveland Browns’ Hall of Fame tight end, who is now the Ravens’ general manager, had three seasons of at least 900 yards, and he had two seasons in which he caught 89 passes. Gates has caught at least 71 passes in five of his seven seasons, and has passed 900 yards five times.

Clark’s breakthrough this year, though, has been a matter of circumstance. In 2008, as receiver Marvin Harrison faded, Clark had his career high of 77 receptions for 848 yards. But in 2009, with Harrison gone, Anthony Gonzalez injured in the opening game, and the Colts working in two inexperienced wide receivers, Austin Collie and Pierre Garcon, Peyton Manning leaned on Clark more than ever. The Colts had found Harrison’s replacement, in a much bigger body.

“For most of my career, we’ve had the luxury of having phenomenal receivers,” Clark said. “You know your place early. Now, I’m everywhere, in the backfield, in the slot, out wide. Everywhere but under center — that job is taken.”
