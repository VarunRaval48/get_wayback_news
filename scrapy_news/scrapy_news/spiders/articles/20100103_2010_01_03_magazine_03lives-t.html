Reality Knocks

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By ROBIN BLACK
Published: December 30, 2009

On a sunny afternoon last spring, a young man rang our doorbell, clipboard in hand, looking like every earnest canvasser who ever came to call. But he wasn’t there for my signature. He was there to inform me that the spectacularly unkempt appearance of our house had made us likely candidates for a free facade makeover on a new TV show titled: “Your Neighbors Must Really Hate You.” (Not actually, but that was the none-too-subtle subtext.)

Though my visitor waxed rhapsodic about our busted shutters and two-story-high hedge, I felt more as if I were receiving a diagnosis than a prize. I’d spent years convincing myself that the house wasn’t really that bad, and now here was this oddly enthusiastic stranger dismantling my delusion. At one point, as he took pictures, I muttered, “Lord,” and he muttered, “I really do hate this part of my job.” But very soon, with only negligible resistance on my part, we slipped from the norms of decent society into another set: the norms of reality TV.

It’s a bit like “A Christmas Carol.” One message of Scrooge’s big night is that if you behave hideously enough, otherworldly beings will intervene. And today if you step too far outside what society expects of you, the denizens of reality TV swoop in. So the extremely obese are fair game, the rampant procreators, the homeowners lowering the neighborhood’s property values. . . .

By the time I called my husband, Richard, I was already a naturalized citizen of that world, and I felt a distinct impatience with his reactions, never mind that they had just been my own. How could he be embarrassed or have doubts? We’d been invited into a reality in which our failings would turn us into stars. And it doesn’t get any better than that. He didn’t hesitate for long.

The network loved the documentation of our disrepair but warned us that we were in competition with another house in the neighborhood, a property equally . . . distinct. My son took a practical approach. “I think we should find it and fix it up, weed the place, trim their hedge.” We decided that was brilliant and never once puzzled over why, if we had it in us to do that in the name of sabotage, we had never managed to do it for more conventional reasons. In the end, though, our investigative drives through streets of monolithically well tended properties never turned up the house, and we focused instead on our pitch for the audition tape.

“So what’s our story?” I asked Richard. “How did this happen?” They aren’t quite the same question, of course; and in the distinction lies the immeasurable gulf between reality TV and reality. Our story was one of good-natured overachievers who couldn’t possibly do it all — the sitcom version of us. When they came to film, I bubbled over about how in lives as rife with interest and accomplishment as ours, something’s gotta give. Richard gave a mock serious performance as the husband of the zany wife, the man who’s just trying to keep up with all the hilarity. And in the end, we stood together, arms wrapped around each other’s backs, and called out with some heartfelt passion: “Please! We need your help!!”

As for how it really happened, that was mercifully irrelevant by then.

“God, imagine if they don’t take us,” I said, as days passed. “That must be the worst. They tell you your property is a disgrace, then abandon you.” And of course that’s what happened. The other house won. “I’m really sorry,” the producer said. “The network loved you guys. They loved your story.” Sure they had. So had we.

As the sun set that evening, Richard and I stood out on the curb, looking at our home of 14 years. The price of admission into the world in which your failings become attributes is the clarity with which you see your failings. No more pretending. No more willful blindness. I glanced down the street at the other houses, neat and tidy, conforming to expectation, the words All happy families are alike . . . crossing my mind.

How did this happen? Really? There were unanticipated losses, grief that enveloped us for years. A stillbirth. A beloved child with special needs. Challenges we never imagined we’d confront. None of it amusing. Nothing like a situation comedy. We’d let go of so many easy assumptions, and in the process we let other things go as well. Gradually, we adjusted, even became stronger. But the evidence of our faltering remained, the facade of our home stubbornly unable to mend itself.

Beside me, Richard sighed.

“Reality’s a bitch,” I said, reaching for his hand. “Let’s go back inside.”

Robin Black is the author of a story collection, If I Loved You, I Would Tell You This, to be published this spring.
