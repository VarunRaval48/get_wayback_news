Film Series and Movie Listings

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By THE NEW YORK TIMES
Published: December 31, 2009

MOVIES

Your guide to the news and the nonsense of awards season. Join the discussion.

Go to Awards Season

Ratings and running times are in parentheses; foreign films have English subtitles. Full reviews of all current releases, movie trailers, showtimes and tickets: nytimes.com/movies.

‘ALVIN AND THE CHIPMUNKS: THE SQUEAKQUEL’ (PG, 1:28) The singing male chipmunks return, and this time three singing female chipmunks are on hand as well. Chipmunk love is in the air as the two trios compete in a rock contest to win money to keep a school music program alive. The kiddies may love it — they certainly turned the 2007 original into box-office gold — but grown-ups won’t find their childhood memories here; mostly they’ll be annoyed.

(Neil Genzlinger)

★ ‘AVATAR’ (PG-13, 2:46) With his latest, James Cameron has turned one man’s dream of the movies into a trippy joy ride about the end of life — our moviegoing life included — as we know it. A song to the natural world that was largely produced with software, an Emersonian exploration of the invisible world of the spirit filled with Cameronian rock ’em, sock ’em, the movie is glorious and goofy and blissfully deranged. (Imax is optional, but try to see it in 3-D.)

(Manohla Dargis)

‘THE BLIND SIDE’ (PG-13, 2:00) Sandra Bullock does good, and then does good some more in this true story of a rich white Memphis family that adopts a homeless young African-American football prodigy.

(A. O. Scott)

★ ‘BRIGHT STAR’ (PG, 1:59) Jane Campion, following the biographical record closely, tells the story of John Keats (Ben Whishaw) and Fanny Brawne (Abbie Cornish), star-crossed literary lovers of the Regency period. Thanks to the passionate performances and the director’s rough, almost breathless style, what might have been a prettified period drama becomes a seething, swooning inquiry into the nature of love. (Scott)

★ ‘BROKEN EMBRACES’ (R, 2:08, in Spanish) Exuberant melancholy from Pedro Almodóvar. A blind writer (Lluís Homar) is pulled back into his past, when he was a filmmaker in an ill-fated love affair with an actress played by Penélope Cruz. (Scott)

‘BROTHERS’ (R, 1:50) In Jim Sheridan’s remake of Susanne Bier’s 2004 film, Tommy (Jake Gyllenhaal) is back from prison just in time to see his brother, Sam (Tobey Maguire), go off to war, leaving behind a wife (Natalie Portman) and two young daughters. An earnest melodrama ensues, at times affecting but never entirely convincing. (Scott)

★ ‘CRAZY HEART’ (R, 1:51) An old country song — whiskey, women, second chances — about an old country singer, played with sly, exquisite craft by Jeff Bridges. (Scott)

’DID YOU HEAR ABOUT THE MORGANS?’ (PG-13, 1:40) There are barely enough titter-worthy one-liners in Marc Lawrence’s good-natured romantic comedy about high-achieving New Yorkers transplanted to Wyoming to prevent it from sinking under the weight of its clichés.

(Stephen Holden)

‘DISNEY’S A CHRISTMAS CAROL’ (PG, 1:35) A big, 3-D spectacle, directed by Robert Zemeckis and starring Jim Carrey as Ebenezer Scrooge (and also as some of the ghosts who haunt him), this film has a magic that lies not in its effects but in its fidelity to the language and mood of Charles Dickens’s immortal tale of greed and grace. (Scott)

★ ‘FANTASTIC MR. FOX’ (PG, 1:28) A children’s adventure story or a Wes Anderson movie. Both, of course, and Mr. Anderson, using a painstakingly old-fashioned stop-motion animation technique, turns Roald Dahl’s slender fable into his most satisfying film since “Rushmore.” (Scott)

‘THE FOURTH KIND’ (PG-13, 1:38) This supernatural thriller may be humorless, paranoid nonsense, but its biggest failure is its inability to scare. (Holden)

★ ‘THE HURT LOCKER’ (R, 2:10) In this tense, insightful film about a United States Army bomb-disposal squad in Iraq, Kathryn Bigelow has made a crackerjack action picture that is also a drama of character, psychology and ideas. Jeremy Renner, as a soldier who approaches danger with the conviction of an artist and the compulsiveness of an addict, is simply amazing, and the rest of the cast, notably Brian Geraghty and Anthony Mackie, is nearly as strong. (Scott)

★ ’INVICTUS’ (PG-13, 2:14) Clint Eastwood’s film, with Morgan Freeman as the newly elected South African president Nelson Mandela and Matt Damon as Francois Pienaar, captain of that country’s rugby team, is a stirring story of athletic triumph and racial reconciliation. It is also a subtle and intricate study in political leadership. (Scott)

‘THE LOVELY BONES’ (PG-13, 2:19) Adapting Alice Sebold’s best-selling sentimental Gothic about the afterlife of a murdered girl, Peter Jackson and his collaborators have created a brightly colored fantasia that is visually arresting but emotionally incoherent. (Scott)

★ ‘ME AND ORSON WELLES’ (PG-13, 1:53) A charming and modest backstage tale set in 1937, when Orson Welles (Christian MacKay) was preparing a revolutionary modern-dress production of “Julius Caesar” at the Mercury Theater on Broadway. Zac Efron is the teenager who falls in with Welles’s company, and in love with Sonja (Claire Danes), the backstage manager. (Scott)

★ ‘THE MESSENGER’ (R, 1:45) Ben Foster and Woody Harrelson are Army officers with the grim task of informing the families of soldiers that their loved ones have died overseas. Samantha Morton plays a widow who takes the news with calm sorrow. The terrific performances complement Oren Moverman’s sensitive, understated direction and the rich, observant script he wrote with Alessandro Camon. (Scott)

‘MY SON, MY SON WHAT HAVE YE DONE’ (R, 1:31) Werner Herzog directs Michael Shannon in a true-to-life, if truer-to-Herzog, story about a Southern California man who one morning did in his mother. Chloë Sevigny, Willem Dafoe and Grace Zabriskie add some extra tang.

(Dargis)

‘NINE’ (PG-13, 1:52) Nein! (Scott)

‘NINJA ASSASSIN’ (R, 1:36) World governments are hiring ninjas to do their dirtiest deeds, and only a rogue ninja (played by Rain, the South Korean former boy-band member) and an Interpol-style cop (Naomie Harris) can expose them. Perhaps in homage to his rippling star, the director, James McTeigue, drenches the film in water; but not even some lovely flashbacks — first love, first wounds, first downpour — can redeem a movie more committed to bloodletting than to any of its characters. (Jeannette Catsoulis)

★ ‘POLICE, ADJECTIVE’ (No rating, 1:53, in Romanian) The second feature by the Romanian director Corneliu Porumboiu (“12:08 East of Bucharest”) is, at first glance, a modest, dourly funny procedural about the ethical predicament of a young detective. But its linear structure and uninflected style reveals a rich character study, full of insight into the workings of a country still shaking off the ingrained habits of a rigid and bureaucratic dictatorship. (Scott)

★ ‘PRECIOUS: BASED ON THE NOVEL “PUSH” BY SAPPHIRE’ (R, 1:49) This potent blend of naturalism and melodrama, directed by Lee Daniels, unites a cast that includes the pop stars Mariah Carey and Lenny Kravitz, the comedian Mo’Nique and a fearless newcomer named Gabourey Sidibe in the story of an abused Harlem teenager struggling for dignity and survival. Ms. Sidibe, in the title role, is the key to the film, and her steady, moving performance is a revelation. (Scott)

‘THE PRINCESS AND THE FROG’ (G, 1:35) It’s not easy being green, as the heroine of this polished animated fairy tale — the first from Disney with a black heroine — discovers on kissing a frog. But to judge from how this period movie deals with race, it is a lot more difficult to be black.

(Dargis)

‘RED CLIFF’ (R, 2:28, in Mandarin) The seminal action director John Woo returns after a six-year layoff with this tale of warring kingdoms, set in the third century A.D., which reunites him with the marvelous Hong Kong actor Tony Leung. While handsome and intelligent and perfectly easy to sit through, it never really approaches the visceral tug of Mr. Woo’s crime dramas of the 1980s and ’90s. (Mike Hale)

‘THE ROAD’ (R, 1:59) How does the world end? Not with a bang or a whimper, but with polite applause for this noble, earnest adaptation of Cormac McCarthy’s novel, and something stronger for Viggo Mortensen’s performance as a father trying to help his young son survive a postapocalyptic nightmare. (Scott)

★ ‘A SERIOUS MAN’ (R, 1:45) Old Testament mishigas from Joel and Ethan Coen, who retell the Book of Job as a suburban shaggy-dog story about a harmless physics professor (Michael Stuhlbarg) whose life comes unraveled. The usual Coen brothers’ whimsy, with its edge of nihilism, takes on a surprising metaphysical resonance. You don’t have to be Jewish to like this movie, but it will hurt more if you are. (Scott)

‘A SINGLE MAN’ (No rating, 1:39) Colin Firth, with a magnificent depth of feeling, plays the grieving, closeted gay professor of the film’s title in Tom Ford’s ambitious and touching adaptation of the 1964 landmark novel by Christopher Isherwood. (Dargis)

‘2012’ (PG-13, 2:38) Roland Emmerich serves up Old Testament-style destruction with a smile. (Dargis)

★ ‘UNTIL THE LIGHT TAKES US’ (No rating, 1:33) Aaron Aites and Audrey Ewell’s absorbing documentary about the Norwegian black-metal music scene examines how a few musicians hijacked an obscure offshoot of heavy metal and made it world-famous, by moving from clown paint and anti-Christian imagery into vandalism, church burning and killing. It also fills in the Norwegian context — cold and dark, liberal but ultraconformist, increasingly globalized — in which these diffident, smart young men came to feel so alienated.

(Hale)

★ ‘UP IN THE AIR’ (R, 1:47) A laugh-infused stealth tragedy from Jason Reitman about a corporate assassin (George Clooney) whose life is upended both by a no-nonsense colleague (Anna Kendrick) and a seductively high-flying female executive (Vera Farmiga). (Dargis)

★ ‘WHERE THE WILD THINGS ARE’ (PG, 1:40) Spike Jonze directs this alternately perfect and imperfect if always beautiful live-action adaptation of the Maurice Sendak children’s book about a solitary boy, Max (Max Records), liberated by his imagination. The film has been pitched to a distinctly older audience than the picture book’s original readers, and some younger children might be scared by the snuffling, growling wild things (voiced by James Gandolfini, among others). (Dargis)

‘THE YOUNG VICTORIA’ (PG, 1:44) As the young Queen Victoria, Emily Blunt flounces, frowns and finally swoons for Rupert Friend’s Prince Albert in a frivolously entertaining portrait directed by Jean-Marc Vallée and written by Julian Fellowes.

(Dargis)

Film Series

‘FRANKENSTEIN MEETS THE SPACE MONSTER’ (Saturday) The impaired cognition of New Year’s weekend makes it an ideal time to encounter Robert Gaffney’s enjoyable, goofy drive-in movie from 1965, here in an improbable screening at the Museum of Modern Art as the final presentation in the long-running series “Nuts and Bolts: Machine-Made Man in Films From the Collection.” The mechanical man in this one is an android astronaut named Frank (Robert Reilly) who inconveniently runs amok just as NASA is doing its best to battle an invasion of pointy-eared aliens out to kidnap all the pretty girls in Puerto Rico. James Karen, who stars as a heroic scientist, survived this one to become a hard-working character actor in film and television. At 1:30 p.m., Museum of Modern Art Roy and Niuta Titus Theaters, (212) 708-9400, moma.org; $10.

(Dave Kehr)

A LOUIS MALLE SAMPLER (Friday through Thursday) This series offers six films from this French director, whose career ran at a more prosaic parallel to the French New Wave. The program opens on Friday with his early succès de scandale, “The Lovers” (1958), and continues with the rarely screened “The Fire Within” (1963) and the art-house hit “Murmur of the Heart” (1971). From Malle’s American period comes the 1981 “My Dinner With André” (Wallace Shawn, who starred in and co-wrote the film with André Gregory, will answer questions after the 6:15 p.m. screening on Wednesday) and the 1994 “Vanya on 42nd Street,” the first film that allowed Julianne Moore to demonstrate the depth of her talent. Malle’s considerable gifts as a documentarian are represented by the three-part “Phantom India,” originally made for French and British television and presented here on Sunday. Walter Reade Theater, 165 West 65th Street, Lincoln Center, (212) 975-5600, filmlinc.org; $11; $27 for any three films in the series. (Kehr)

SHORT FILMS BY ARTUR ZMIJEWSKI (Monday and Wednesday) In conjunction with a show of his photographs in the Museum of Modern Art’s second-floor Projects Gallery, this program offers a selection of short works by Mr. Zmijewski, a Polish artist who specializes in provoking average people to political acts: his video short “80064,” for example, finds the artist taking an elderly Auschwitz survivor to a tattoo shop to have the faint blue numbers on his forearm re-inscribed. “A weird, unconscious sadism animates many of his works,” Ken Johnson wrote in The New York Times. Museum of Modern Art Roy and Niuta Titus Theaters, (212) 708-9400, moma.org; $10. (Kehr)
