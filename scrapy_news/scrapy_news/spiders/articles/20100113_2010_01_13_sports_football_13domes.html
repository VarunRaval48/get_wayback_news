Its Football in January, but Minus the Weather

Associated Press

The Superdome, home of the Saints.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

By JOHN BRANCH
Published: January 12, 2010

It is that part of the N.F.L. calendar when meteorology collides with muscle and momentum, and the results are condensed to shorthand — the Ice Bowl, the Fog Bowl, the Tuck Rule Game, the Freezer Bowl, the holy-cheese-it-is-cold-here-in-Green-Bay game two years ago. (That conference championship game with the Giants, played below zero, begs for a snappier moniker.)

The N.F.L. championship game (Green Bay-Dallas) on Dec. 31, 1967, known as the Ice Bowl.

Playoff football is often about images as much as action: steam rising from bare heads, players huddled around heaters in long coats and quarterbacks sporting that least masculine of all football equipment, the hand muff.

Not this season.

Unless something freakish happens — an unforeseen downpour in San Diego, a hole in the roof of the Metrodome, wins on the road again by the Jets and the Ravens — weather will not be a factor for the rest of the playoffs.

Brett Favre, the N.F.L.’s version of Snow Miser, now leads a team in a dome. Retractable roofs are being kept shut to keep out the hint of chill and keep in the noise. The only ice inside the stadiums will be the manufactured kind placed on sore knees and in glass tumblers in the luxury boxes. Football in January is being played in conditions almost identical to your living room.

Sigh.

Three of this weekend’s four games will take place indoors. The fourth is in San Diego. Even the National Weather Service finds it boring. The on-the-field forecasts for games around the country this weekend: roughly 70, 70, 70 and 69.

Football fans in the stands this weekend may be removing their shirts — not to display their loyalty (and I.Q.) in subfreezing temperatures, but simply because they are warm.

The only chance that weather will be a factor during the rest of the postseason is if the Jets go to San Diego and win, and the Ravens go to Indianapolis and win.

In that case, the Ravens and the Jets will play at Giants Stadium in the conference championship game, with Jets fans probably so excited that some, surely, will strip out of their shirts no matter the temperature. Here’s hoping Rex Ryan does not join them.

Barring that — a Jets home game, not Ryan undressing — the only room-temperature postseason comparable to this one came 10 years ago. Jacksonville, in 75 degrees at home, lost to Tennessee in one conference championship, and St. Louis (in a dome) beat Tampa Bay in the other. The week before that, the four playoff games were held in two domes (St. Louis and Indianapolis) and in two Florida cities deemed warm enough to host Super Bowls (Jacksonville and Tampa).

It is O.K. to admit not remembering any of those games. The one that does strike a chord was that season’s wild-card game at Tennessee, better known as the Music City Miracle. It had little to do with weather, but it was quite obvious that the earth’s rotation turned the field just enough to keep Frank Wycheck’s lateral from being a forward pass.

This postseason, featuring more dome teams, could be even less affected by outside elements. That would be a breakthrough of sorts. For whatever reason, postseason odds are still against warm-blooded teams, which seem to rarely make it into January or crack into pieces when they do.

In the American Football Conference, 18 of the last 23 title games have been home games for Pittsburgh, New England, Denver, Buffalo, Cincinnati or Cleveland, cold-weather cities with outdoor stadiums. In the National Football Conference, home to seven of the league’s nine enclosed stadiums, only four title games have taken place indoors, including last year’s game in Glendale, Ariz. (It is O.K. not to remember that game, either.)

The year before that, the Giants beat Favre’s Packers in overtime, with the temperature at Green Bay hovering just below numbing.

Had Lawrence Tynes missed another field goal, Coach Tom Coughlin’s face would very likely have fallen off.

Now Favre is in Minneapolis, probably to play muffless and in short sleeves against the Cowboys.

And the Giants and the Jets, are proposing to hold a Super Bowl at their soon-to-open outdoor stadium.

That would give rise to a long unforeseen wintertime possibility for the N.F.L.: all the playoff games being played at room temperature, and the Super Bowl being played in a blizzard. Now that would be something to remember.
