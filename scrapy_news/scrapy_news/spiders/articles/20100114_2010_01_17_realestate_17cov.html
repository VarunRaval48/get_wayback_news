A Brooklyn Apartment Sold Too Soon?

Michael Appleton for The New York Times

TIME WAS In the real estate doldrums of 1993, Harvey Araton sold his apartment in a problem-plagued Brooklyn Heights co-op for a song. He later came down with a bad case of real estate regret. As a cure, he revisited the place.

By HARVEY ARATON
Published: January 14, 2010
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

MY face betrayed me, disbelief slowly turning to distress as I stared silently at the Sunday real estate listings spread out on the kitchen table. It was a look my wife had seen too many times.

“Brooklyn?” she said.

I nodded, obediently and guiltily.

“Why do that to yourself?”

I shook my head and pretended I hadn’t heard the question.

“I can’t believe what these apartments are going for.”

I couldn’t blame my wife, Beth Albert, for wanting to scream, “Enough already!” She has had to live with my real estate remorse for years, but I just couldn’t stop myself from rubbernecking when passing by the wreckage of our long-ago investment.

It has been 16 1/2 years since we sold a two-bedroom apartment in Brooklyn Heights, en route to the suburbs and the birth of a second child. Actually, I should say that we gave it away: We lost about $80,000 on a $240,000 purchase made in 1988.

We were far from alone in our selling frenzy during the housing downturn of the early 1990s. In our four-unit brick town house, everyone wanted out of a bad marriage to the dowdy adjoining building with 10 smaller apartments, officially part of our co-op. We were all fed up with internecine issues that created financial stress and tension among neighbors and friends.

With credit tight and our co-op financials barking like rabid dogs, Beth and I were ecstatic when our banker told us he would allow a qualified buyer to assume our mortgage. Fantasizing, we put our place on the market for what we had paid for it, $240,000. Weeks later, a nice young doctor and his wife came along to pick up the roughly $140,000 we owed and to cover our closing and moving expenses.

In return, they received 1,000-plus square feet in a third-floor walk-up that featured a loftlike living room with a custom-built full wall unit (our addition), a modern bath and kitchen, and a closet containing a stacked washer-dryer. With a health club and a train station around the corner, it was bright, convenient living, gloriously enhanced by rear-building parking.

We moved away in early summer 1993, and I had no reason to look back, not until 2 1/2 years later when the doctor’s wife called. She needed me — the co-op board president at the time of our departure — to sign over a document to my successor to execute the sale of her apartment.

When I asked how she had made out, she said, “You really want to know?” Her pitying tone told me everything. The market had taken off and she and the good doctor had made a quick killing. I soon had a new hobby, punishing myself with an occasional browsing of the Brooklyn listings, the fine print shouting, “What were you thinking?” I’ve indulged in this torture ever since.

I will admit that dwelling on the past is nonproductive and so not cool. I’ve wanted to stop, but how?

Write about it, a friend suggested. My exasperated wife supported the idea of self-intervention.

So I sent an e-mail message to a former fellow shareholder, Simon Brady, explaining that I was planning to revisit my real estate decision of 1993. “Wow, that would be painful,” he shot back. And he would know, having lost even more on his apartment than I did on mine. But he agreed: “It could be cathartic.”

I called the man who had lived with his wife and young children on the floor below us. Those Brooklyn days, he said, were a bad dream that he did not wish to publicly return to for personal reasons.

But for the record, he said he had paid $255,000 for his apartment and had sold it two months after we sold ours. He received $155,000, and paid $25,000 to the bank at closing, to make up a shortfall.

“We needed to find a place quickly after moving to New York and starting work, and that is how you lose $100,000 in a real estate deal,” my old neighbor said. “You’re desperate to get in and desperate to get out. But I’ve never gone back to see what prices there were. I just put that part of my life behind me.”

Now, I am perfectly capable of chalking up a used car as a lemon. But I was going to have to dig deeper into myself to get over blowing what might have been a college education fund for two.
