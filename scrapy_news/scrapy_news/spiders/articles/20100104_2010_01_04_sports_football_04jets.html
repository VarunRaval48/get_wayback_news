Jets 37, Bengals 0

Jets Rout Bengals and Clinch Playoff Berth

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By GREG BISHOP
Published: January 4, 2010

EAST RUTHERFORD, N.J. — On Sunday night, in the bitter cold, the Jets played in the final N.F.L. game of the regular season. They most likely played in the final N.F.L. game at Giants Stadium. But they did not play in the final game of their season, a notion that seemed impossible even to their coach three games ago.

Back then, after a dismal loss to Atlanta, Rex Ryan had mistakenly declared the Jets’ season over, their playoff chances finished. They needed to defeat division leaders Indianapolis and Cincinnati over the final two weeks, and they needed half a dozen other results to fall their way.

As the weeks unfolded, every domino fell just right, and when the Jets took the field against the Bengals on Sunday night, a playoff berth was at stake. The Jets (9-7) earned the fifth seed in the American Football Conference with a 37-0 thrashing of the Bengals (10-6), tying the most lopsided home regular-season shutout in team history in front of a packed house that endured freezing temperatures.

When it ended, the Jets went on a victory lap, and offensive tackle D’Brickashaw Ferguson leapt into the stands, or at least tried to. At 6 feet 6 inches and 310 pounds, Ferguson nearly pulled down the fans who tried to lift him.

“It’s almost like divine intervention,” he said of the Jets’ run. “I don’t think it’s coincidence. I hope God’s a Jets fan.”

These teams will meet again in the first round, late Saturday afternoon in Cincinnati. The Jets enter the rematch having won five of their last six games, behind the league’s top-ranked defense and rushing attack.

The Jets see themselves as an opponent few teams want to face, a team built for cold-weather playoff football. All week, the Jets invoked Arizona, pointing to the way the Cardinals got hot last season and rode that streak into the Super Bowl. Or the Pittsburgh Steelers, who won the 2005 Super Bowl as the sixth seed.

"Maybe we’re catching fire at exactly the right time," defensive end Marques Douglas said.

These Jets say they feel good about their chances. Wide receiver Braylon Edwards guaranteed that the Jets would beat the Bengals on Sunday. Defensive end Shaun Ellis, who in his 10th season is the longest-tenured Jet, said the win would mark redemption, or "make up for all the times we’ve had a letdown."

The Jets clinched a playoff spot for the first time since 2006, erasing memories of last season’s collapse, when they lost four of their last five games. This for a franchise with more than 40 years of disappointment since its only Super Bowl appearance, for a team that blew three leads in the fourth quarter this season and started a turnover-prone rookie quarterback and lost three games ago at home.

Afterward, the Jets gave the game ball to Ryan, their loud, loquacious first-year coach. It was Ryan who set the Jets’ agenda, who talked boldly and brashly and put the bull’s-eye on his team to deliver on his words.

It was Ryan who asked the Jets to line up last week. He told the players who had made the playoffs once to step forward. About 15 did. Then he asked the players who had made the playoffs twice to take another step, and repeated this until his point was clear — these opportunities are rare.

Players like Edwards and linebacker Calvin Pace needed no such reminders. Neither had previously made the playoffs. Pace is in his seventh season, most spent with Arizona, except for this one and the last one, which just happened to be when the Cardinals reached the Super Bowl.

“That’s the most fun I’ve ever had in a game,” Pace said. “That’s the power of belief.”

He felt the urgency. Same as Dwight Lowery. He boarded the team bus Sunday afternoon, and instead of the usual chatter, he heard silence, as teammates bobbed their heads to music or stared straight ahead. Lowery said he knew then that they would win.

The Jets opened the game desperate, as their situation dictated. On their opening possession, wide receiver Brad Smith continued his string of strong performances with a 57-yard scamper up the middle of the field. This led to a 1-yard score by Thomas Jones, and the Jets, ahead by 7-0, never felt remotely threatened.

The Bengals were missing four injured starters on defense, but they played their offensive stars in the first half and still failed to move the ball. The Jets’ defense, which returned this month to its swarming style, held the Bengals to 7 yards of offense in the first half. Quarterback Carson Palmer had one completion, an interception, 0 passing yards and a 1.7 quarterback rating.

Safety Kerry Rhodes, back in the starting lineup, broke up two passes and leveled wideout Chad Ochocinco on another throw. Ochocinco had said he would change his last name back to Johnson if Darrelle Revis stopped him, and Revis blanketed Johnson the same way he shadowed every receiver he faced this season. Ochocinco had no receptions when he left the game in the third quarter with a left knee injury.

(Asked if he would change his name, Ochocinco said, “Child, please.”)

The Bengals’ offense gained only 72 yards in the game, a strikingly low tally, backups or no.

“We feel like the backbone of this team,” Pace said of the defense. “No disrespect to the offense, but this team goes as our defense goes.”

The Jets’ offense consumed more than 11 minutes on its second drive, which bridged the first and second quarters. The drive took 21 plays and went 74 yards, no play longer than 14, and ended in the first of three field goals by Jay Feely. Quarterback Mark Sanchez played it smart and safe, mixing short completions with plenty of handoffs, following the game plan.

The Bengals’ second-ranked rush defense missed too many players to challenge the Jets’ rushing attack. The Jets scored four touchdowns on the ground — two by Jones, one by Smith on a 32-yard option and another by receiver Jerricho Cotchery — en route to a franchise record for rushing yards in a season.

Now, the Jets must turn their attention to their rematch against the Bengals, who will play their starters for the full game and use their full playbook next weekend.

In the stands, thousands of fans braved temperatures in the 20s, sometimes lower, not interested in beating traffic. Ever since the Jets moved here in 1984, their fans, and the franchise, have often felt like second-class citizens, playing home games in a stadium named after another team.

Not on Sunday. Not with Joe Klecko and Mark Gastineau and Wesley Walker and Al Toon and Vinny Testaverde in attendance. Not with a playoff berth on the line. Not on an evening when the Jets, the same old Jets, delivered.

“I don’t know that same old Jets,” linebacker Bart Scott said. “Because I’m a Jet, and this is my reality. We’re in the playoffs.”
