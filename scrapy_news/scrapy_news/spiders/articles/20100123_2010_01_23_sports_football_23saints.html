Veterans Like Sharper Found a Fit With the Saints

Left, Paul Sancya/Associated Press; Chris Graythen/Getty Images

The Saints’ Darren Sharper played for the Vikings for the previous four seasons and was also was a teammate of Minnesota quarterback Brett Favre’s at Green Bay.

NEW ORLEANS — The deal was struck during a business meal late last winter at Emeril’s.

Among those dining were Saints linebacker Jonathan Vilma, General Manager Mickey Loomis and the defensive coordinator Gregg Williams.

Their guest of expense-account honor was Darren Sharper, a veteran safety who had recently left the Minnesota Vikings as a free agent.

“We were basically trying to recruit him,” Vilma said. “I told him he would be a perfect fit. He was the guy we needed.”

Some veterans in Sharper’s position, Loomis said, look only for a paycheck, but he sensed Sharper sought team success. He also believed he could fill some of the Saints’ needs.

“At the tail end of his career, what can this guy bring to the table for us?” Loomis said. “We needed a veteran safety to help with the young secondary.”

Sharper was 33 and contemplating a 13th season in the N.F.L.

“At my age, you don’t want to have a wasted year,” Sharper said. “I just had that feeling that night. It just felt right. Something just kind of clicked.”

So he signed a one-year contract with the Saints in March and both sides have benefited.

Sharper, who tied for the league lead this season in interceptions with nine, will play for the Saints on Sunday in the National Football Conference Championship game against Minnesota in the Superdome.

The winner will go to the Super Bowl, and there is more than one coincidence involved.

Not only did Sharper play for the Vikings for the previous four seasons, but he also was a teammate of Minnesota quarterback Brett Favre’s at Green Bay, beginning in 1997, when Sharper was a rookie and the Packers lost the Super Bowl to the Broncos.

And after Favre left the Jets after last season and contemplated new employment, he consulted Sharper, who told Favre that the Vikings were good enough to contend for the championship.

“So it is kind of ironic that we are facing off,” Sharper said. “I just hope I can renege on those words and make us the Super Bowl-caliber team.”

Signing Sharper was part of a pattern for the Saints. Many of their pivotal players started with other teams and came here through trades or free agency.

Vilma is one of them, traded by the Jets after expressing, through his agent, a desire to play in New Orleans. Quarterback Drew Brees arrived as a free agent from San Diego.

Defensive end Bobby McCray signed as a free agent from Jacksonville. Linebacker Scott Fujita signed as a free agent from Dallas. Tight end Jeremy Shockey came in a trade from the Giants.

Vilma said the Saints had a growing reputation around the league as a good place for veteran players because the owner, Tom Benson, delegates football decisions to football experts like Loomis and Coach Sean Payton.

“He understands that he’s not the football guy,” Vilma said of Benson.

Vilma did not refer specifically to other teams, but it is well known that owners like Jerry Jones in Dallas and Dan Snyder in Washington get involved in football decisions, with mixed results.

Vilma said he became interested in the Saints “on the outside, looking in” after they signed Brees for the 2006 season and went to their first N.F.C. title game.

In addition, Loomis said, the Saints play a 4-3 defense, an alignment that suits his talents. “This is the situation I wanted to be in,” Vilma said.

Shockey knew Payton when Payton was the Giants’ offensive coordinator in Shockey’s rookie year. Without mentioning Giants Coach Tom Coughlin, with whom he had differences, Shockey offered a comparative opinion.

He said Payton “respects the players first and foremost as people” but that “some coaches are not very good at interacting with players.”

“I’m not going to say any names or anything,” Shockey said, “but you know what I’m saying.”

McCray said Payton created a locker room culture in which “guys come in and bond instantly.”

As McCray spoke Thursday, a group of teammates surged past, laughing and shouting and carrying above them an equipment manager, fully clothed, to be dunked in the therapeutic cold tub.

The mood will be more serious Sunday when Sharper and the Saints try to stop Favre and the Vikings and win the first Super Bowl berth in the franchise’s 43-year history.

Last weekend, in a 45-14 victory against Arizona, Sharper recovered one Cardinals fumble and had an interception nullified by a penalty.

Payton played down the impact of Sharper’s knowledge of the Vikings and Favre, though Vilma, without elaborating, called it important.

“The game within the game,” Vilma said, adding that Sharper was “definitely the guy we’re going to for the tips.

“A little edge can make the difference.”

Speaking of Favre and the Vikings and his relationships with them, Sharper said he saw the situation materializing in which the Saints were seeded first in the conference and the Vikings second.

Now, he said, it is like a 10-chapter book with nine finished.

“You have some great story lines,” Sharper said. “You want to continue reading until the end.”
