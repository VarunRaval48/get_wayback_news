JOSEPH ARTHUR (Friday) Romance, despair and the struggle for faith intermingle in Joseph Arthur’s songs, which can float in a gorgeous acoustic reverie or flare up in electrified anger. With Charlie Mars, a singer and songwriter from Brooklyn by way of Mississippi. At 10 p.m., City Winery, 155 Varick Street, near Spring Street, South Village, (212) 608-0555, citywinery.com; $15 for bar stools; $20 for reserved tables; $20 for reserved best tables; $25 for V.I.P. seating. (Ben Sisario)

AU REVOIR SIMONE (Thursday) The three young women of Au Revoir Simone, from Brooklyn, could be the terminally depressed daughters of the members of Human League, singing of “empty beds and lonely dinners” over milky, wistful synth-pop. With Class Actress and Alexa Wilding. At 9 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea, (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; $15 in advance, $20 at the door. (Sisario)

RYAN CABRERA (Monday) Perhaps still best known as Ashlee Simpson’s ex-boyfriend and reality-show cohort (and, in certain circles, for his abnormally spiky hair), the singer Ryan Cabrera performs mostly unremarkable pop-rock in the congenial spirit of John Mayer. With Dion Roy and Matt Lowell. At 8 p.m., Highline Ballroom, 431 West 16th Street, Chelsea, (212) 414-5994, highlineballroom.com; $12 in advance, $15 at the door. (Amanda Petrusich)

THE DETROIT COBRAS (Friday) This scrappy garage band from Detroit has made a career out of crate-digging: It doesn’t write its own material, but does a fairly spectacular job of reinventing semi-obscure hits from decades past — reworking songs from Gino Washington, Garnet Mimms, the Flirtations and more. The band’s tracks are surprisingly fresh, mostly because of the muscular, drive-it-like-it’s-stolen pipes of the lead vocalist Rachel Nagy. With the Swingin’ Neckbreakers and Sister Anne. At 9:30 p.m., Maxwell’s, 1039 Washington Street, Hoboken, (201) 653-1703, maxwellsnj.com; $15. (Petrusich)

★ THE DUTCHESS AND THE DUKE (Thursday) As the Dutchess and the Duke, Kimberly Morrison and Jesse Loritz play spare folk songs that linger and last. Their lyrics can be dark and riddled with heartbreak (“I can’t win, I can’t lose, and everything I do is wrong/One hand clawing at the other hand, tryin’ to hold on,” they opine in “Hands”), recalling Bonnie Prince Billy and Castanets, but at best they’re invigorating and stompy, as in the excellent, Dylan-evoking “Reservoir Park.” With Medication. At 7:30 p.m., Union Hall, 702 Union Street, at Fifth Avenue, Park Slope, Brooklyn, (718) 638-4400, unionhallny.com; $10. (Petrusich)

★ ROKY ERICKSON (Friday and Saturday) One of the godfathers of psychedelic rock, who howled like a werewolf over the flutter of an electric jug in his 1960s band the 13th Floor Elevators, Roky Erickson is also one of that era’s most notorious casualties. Now 60 and in steady health, he is back on the road with a tough, no-nonsense band. With the Fleshtones on Friday at 9 p.m., Southpaw, 125 Fifth Avenue, near Sterling Place, Park Slope, Brooklyn, (718) 230-0236, spsounds.com; $25. With Love City on Saturday at 9:30 p.m., Maxwell’s, 1039 Washington Street, Hoboken, (201) 653-1703, maxwellsnj.com; $25. (Sisario)

★ LEVON HELM BAND, OKKERVIL RIVER (Thursday) A drummer and singer in the Band, Levon Helm possesses one of rock’s most glorious voices: loose, country-tinged and vaguely devilish. He has had considerable success on his own, working as an actor, leading his eponymous band, and hosting Midnight Rambles at his sprawling barn-turned-studio in Woodstock, N.Y. Okkervil River, from Austin, Tex., is renowned for its twittering country-rock and the bookish lyrics of its frontman, Will Sheff; the band’s discography is riddled with enough literary allusions to make a professor of comparative literature beam. (The band takes its name from a short story by the Russian writer Tatyana Tolstaya.) At 8 p.m., Terminal 5, 610 West 56th Street, Clinton, (212) 582-6600, terminal5nyc.com; $31 in advance, $35 at the door. (Petrusich)

NICK JONAS AND THE ADMINISTRATION (Thursday) The tween sensation the Jonas Brothers was initially conceived as a mouthpiece for Nick Jonas, the youngest (some might say cutest) Jonas of the bunch, but instead, that trio took off as a family band. Now Mr. Jonas is seeking to reclaim a bit of autonomy, and who can blame him? Nick Jonas and the Administration, Mr. Jonas’s side project, plays smooth and earnest pop-rock that belies its frontman’s 17 years. With Diane Birch, whose songs are retro-leaning in a nonspecific way (Zooey Deschanel meets Joni Mitchell, with the tiniest dash of Norah Jones), and whose new album, “Bible Belt” (S-Curve), is a promising debut, full of honest proclamations and golden horns. “I gotta go out and buy myself one of those little black dresses, ’cause I’m so tired of this T-shirt,” she trills on “Nothing but a Miracle.” (Through Jan. 8.) At 8 p.m., Beacon Theater, 2124 Broadway, at 74th Street, (212) 307-7171, beacontheatrenyc.com; $39.50 and $49.50. (Petrusich)

THE RURAL ALBERTA ADVANTAGE (Thursday) This trio from Ontario plays whimsical, keyboard-filled indie-folk: there are declarations of love, twittering percussive bits and just the right amount of earnestness. With Roadside Graves. At 9 p.m., Maxwell’s, 1039 Washington Street, Hoboken, N.J., (201) 653-1703, maxwellsnj.com; $12. (Petrusich)

JIM WHITE (Wednesday) This Florida singer-songwriter plays gothic folk songs about his native habitat. His portraits of the South are both celebratory and defeating: “Dixie is a scourge and a scar and a girl in my heart and a state of mind,” he croons in “Jailbird,” a track from “Transnormal Skiperoo” (Luaka Bop). Mr. White is performing as part of the Happy Ending Reading Series, which successfully pairs musicians and writers; this show will feature readings by the authors Jonathan Dee (“The Privileges”) and Rich Benjamin (“Whitopia”). At 7 p.m., Joe’s Pub, at the Public Theater, Lafayette Street, at Astor Place, East Village, (212) 967-7555, joespub.com; $15. (Petrusich)
