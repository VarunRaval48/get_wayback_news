Citigroup Replaces Head of Consumer Banking

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By ERIC DASH
Published: January 11, 2010

Citigroup announced on Monday that it would replace the head of its consumer banking business — the most troubled part of its financial empire — as the company braces for fresh troubles with home mortgages and credit cards this year.

The executive, Teresa A. Dial, stepped down after a controversial 18 month tenure, during which she struggled to turn around the division and win the confidence of Citigroup’s chairman and chief executive, Vikram S. Pandit.

Ms. Dial, who will stay on as a senior advisor, will be replaced by Manuel Medina-Mora, who runs Citigroup’s business in Latin America and will assume responsibility for consumer banking in North America as well.

The move comes after federal regulators ordered a comprehensive review of the bank’s leadership. That review, according to people briefed on the situation, gave low marks to Ms. Dial and two other members of Mr. Pandit’s inner circle: Don Callahan, Citigroup’s administrative chief, and Lewis B. Kaden, a vice chairman. Jon Diat, a Citigroup spokesman, declined to comment.

Ms. Dial, the most senior woman executive at Citigroup, had a rocky tenure since her arrival in July 2008. A former executive at Wells Fargo & Company and then Lloyds TSB Bank of Britain, she spent much of her first year bringing in new managers. But she failed to chart a clear course for the troubled consumer banking business, which was plagued by billions of dollars in losses stemming from bad mortgages and credit card loans, Citigroup insiders say.

Indeed, Ms. Dial quickly became a controversial figure inside Citigroup, where some employees disparagingly referred to her as the “human cyclone” — a nickname she had earned in the past — because of what they considered her erratic management style. Several Citigroup directors panned a presentation about the bank’s global consumer strategy that was given at a retreat for the company’s board members last summer.

By the fall, after the findings of the leadership review were shared with Citigroup’s regulators and board, bank insiders buzzed that Ms. Dial had received a poor assessment. At the time, Citigroup promised the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation and other federal regulators that it would dismiss or reassign executives with low ratings. It is unclear whether Ms. Dial’s demotion was directly related to the review. The bank said the move will allow her to “devote more time and attention” to an ailing family member.

In a statement, Ms. Dial noted the improvement of certain performance metrics, like deposit and sales growth, and said that she was pleased with the new team and the strategy that was now in place. “I am very proud of all that we have accomplished,” she said.

Mr. Medina-Mora, a longtime banker who ran Citigroup’s Banamex subsidiary for several years, has received high praise for his leadership. At a 2007 investor conference, Mr. Pandit held out Banamex’s unified banking strategy as a blueprint for Citigroup worldwide.

In his new role, Mr. Medina-Mora will oversee Citigroup’s large credit card operation and its retail and commercial banking franchises in the Americas. He will also run a new “Consumer Council,” working with the bank’s regional leaders to shape its global strategy for the businesses.

Mr. Pandit, in a statement, called Mr. Medina-Mora an “exceptional banker” whose track record and experience would help Citigroup’s consumer business “achieve sustained profitability and growth.”

Mr. Pandit is likely to make other management changes. Mr. Kaden could be stripped of his responsibilities overseeing Citigroup’s legal and compliance staff — a move that has been under consideration since his poor review, according to a person briefed on the situation.

Other board-level changes are afoot. John M. Deutch, a longtime Citigroup director, recently announced plans to step down from the board this spring. C. Michael Armstrong, another longtime board member who oversaw the Citigroup’s audit and risk committee from 2004 to mid-2008, is also expected to depart before the April shareholder meeting, this person added. Scott Sobel, a spokesman for Mr. Armstrong, declined to comment.
