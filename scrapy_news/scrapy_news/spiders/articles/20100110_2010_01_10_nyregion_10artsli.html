Where a Close Look Goes Right Through

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By BENJAMIN GENOCCHIO
Published: January 8, 2010

The pleasures and challenges of working with glass are explored in “See Through,” the current group exhibition at the Islip Art Museum, organized by Karen Shaw. But the show also invites wider contemplation of transparency.

With its ability to be coiled, twisted and bent into a variety of shapes and forms, glass is especially popular with sculptors and other artists who make three-dimensional work. It is also durable and relatively inexpensive.

A majority of the 14 artists assembled for “See Through” use glass either as the sole material or in a hybrid work employing different media. Some of them, however, work with Plexiglas, plastic, Mylar and other glasslike materials that allow you to see through them.

The exhibition starts in the foyer, with Tom Wolf’s inventive collages. They consist of photographs of everyday street scenes laminated with thick Plexiglas sheets and then painted with translucent colored paint, creating a second, abstract composition on top of the original image. You have to look through the painting and Plexiglas to make out the hazy scenes buried below.

Similar inventiveness is found in the first gallery, where Barry Goldberg has installed three assemblage sculptures employing many materials, including glass, wax and latex, among others, to explore the effects of light, translucency and transparency.

Mr. Goldberg’s assemblages are often loosely inspired by iconic artworks. They are also rather puzzling. “Monk by the Sea (In the Shop Window Cutaway)” (2006-7) is an actual tree branch from which hangs a Plexiglas panel filled with measurements, a shoe and a model of a shoe made of clear resin. According to the information sheet in the gallery, it is a riff on “The Monk by the Sea” (1808-1810), by the German Romantic painter Casper David Friedrich, in which a lone figure contemplates a dark sky and a choppy sea. To me, Mr. Goldberg’s work seems to be about modern social alienation.

Among the more curious and thoughtful works in the show is Jill Moser’s installation made from light bulbs wired together in clusters and brightly lighted from above, casting shadows on the wall. Stripped of practical use, the bulbs now only reflect light from another source.

A number of works exude a sense of joy. Among them are Karen LaMonte’s life-size cast glass sculptures of women’s bodices and Barbara Campisi’s simple but sensual line drawings on Mylar.

Along these lines, but even more impressive, is Beth Lipman’s “Peach and Tulip Centerpiece” (2007), the highlight of the exhibition. It consists of chunks of clear glass mimicking a vase of tulips and peaches, in the spirit of a traditional Dutch still life painting. It is a masterfully executed sculpture.

Still life painting depicts a grouping of inanimate objects as the main subject of the composition. The image can be enjoyed on aesthetic grounds or interpreted on a symbolic level: for instance, flowers in still life painting are a symbol of human frailty and mortality. By taking perishable flowers and fruit and making them everlasting, Ms. Lipman turns this symbolism on its head.

I would like to have seen more than one of Ms. Lipman’s sculptures in this exhibition; along with Josiah McElheny, she is among the most exciting artists working with glass. Her sculptures blur boundaries between art, craft and design. She is also one of the few glass artists whose work is in the collections of contemporary art museums.

Ms. Lipman’s sculptures perfectly fit the exhibition theme, in that all her work is see-through. Other displays seem somewhat out of context, like the late Sue Fuller’s assemblages made of different weights of string wound together to create complex geometric patterns. They are beautiful, but have little to do with glass, transparency or translucence.

I was also puzzled by the inclusion of several works by Jim Butler, including a large painting of a roll of chicken wire. While the work’s hyper-realism converts a two-dimensional image into a three-dimensional one, there is really no connection that I can see to the exhibition theme.

Some art here borders on kitsch, like Hank Adams’s assemblages combining tin cans and blown glass jars decorated with crudely painted faces. They are intended as a study in emotional states, but unfortunately the overall result resembles child’s play.

Although there is a little looseness to the curatorial premise, there is nonetheless some vital and important work here. This is the virtue of shows at the Islip Art Museum, which in spite of budget cuts has continued presenting regular exhibitions. That is to be applauded.

See Through, Islip Art Museum, 50 Irish Lane, East Islip, through Jan. 24. Information: islipartmuseum.org or (631) 224-5402.
