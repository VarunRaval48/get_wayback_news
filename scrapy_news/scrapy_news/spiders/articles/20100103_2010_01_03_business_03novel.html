Bearings That Pack a Punch (and Their Own Controls)

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By ANNE EISENBERG
Published: January 2, 2010

TRAINS that float on air may be the most famous objects borne by magnetic fields.

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image

In these magnetic bearings made by Synchrony, all of their electronic controls are housed within them, and not in some bulky outside cabinet.

But magnetic forces can also support other, less visible objects: the spinning shafts, for example, hidden at the mechanical core of industrial equipment like pumps, generators, motors and compressors.

Electromagnets surround these rotating shafts and keep them suspended in the air — with the help of sensors and computer algorithms that adjust the position of the shafts thousands of times a second, keeping them centered.

Magnetic bearings may play an increasingly important role in future industrial systems, including environmentally friendlier ones. The low-friction technology of these bearings eliminates the need for lubrication to keep a system operating smoothly.

“With magnetic bearings, there’s no oil in the shaft, no grease, no ball bearings,” said David Trumper, a professor of mechanical engineering at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

Despite their advantages, magnetic bearings have been slow to catch on widely in industry, said Eric Maslen, a professor of mechanical and aerospace engineering at the University of Virginia in Charlottesville. One reason, he said, has been the complications involved in installing the control system that runs the electromagnets. That system is typically housed in a bulky cabinet far from the rotating shaft.

A company called Synchrony Inc., in Salem, Va., has miniaturized the control system — which often includes dozens of cables, digital processors and power amplifiers, as well as other electronics — and tucked it into the housing of the magnets themselves. That eliminates the need for extensive cables or separate cabinets and may thus simplify installation. The control system detects and then adjusts the position of the shaft about 15,000 times a second by redistributing the current to the electromagnets.

The compact systems, Dr. Maslen said, are an important innovation. “This is a way to make magnetic bearings an off-the-shelf technology,” he said.

At present, companies that want to use magnetic bearings typically must give detailed information about their machines to the vendor, which then makes customized bearings.

Synchrony’s new bearings, called Fusion, have been installed on test motors at Baldor Electric in Fort Smith, Ark., said William Pizzichil, vice president for engineering at Baldor, which makes electric motors.

“When you hold the bearing in your hand,” he said, “you are also holding all of the electronics.”

Victor Iannello, the chief executive of Synchrony, said the bearings they replace typically had at least 40 wires connecting the electromagnets and sensors to a control cabinet. Now there is just a connection to the power supply, and an ethernet cable to each bearing so that information on vibration, for example, is available to devices monitoring the system.

Mr. Iannello says that, in general, magnetic bearings have a higher initial price tag than their oil-lubricated counterparts, but that because the magnetic bearings eliminate other expenses, they are ultimately cheaper.

In an estimate made by Synchrony for a pump system, for example, the magnetic bearings would cost about $45,000 more than oil-lubricated bearings, he said. But the magnetic version would save all the capital costs of lubrication equipment, as well as the cost of installing and maintaining that equipment.

Dr. Maslen said the “life cycle” costs — including the initial purchase price and money spent on lubricants, spare parts, energy, maintenance and disposal at the end of service — were much higher for oil-lubricated equipment than for comparable magnetic bearing equipment.

Magnetic bearings can handle the high speeds — 10,000 to 30,000 revolutions a minute — of new motors that Baldor is developing, Mr. Pizzichil said. It has considered other types of bearings that require grease or oil. But, he said, “eliminating the lubricants means there’s no potential for contamination.”

And lubrication systems must be pumped, filtered and maintained. “All of that goes away with magnetic bearings,” he said.

JIGGER JUMONVILLE of Santa Maria, Calif., a consulting engineer and specialist in turbomachinery, said that in harsh environments — sites with very high temperatures, for example — miniaturized control systems might be less suitable than separate control cabinets And any failure of the control electronics built into the bearings, he said, would require at least a partial disassembly of the machine — a repair that is likely to be more costly than that for a conventional magnetic control system.

Dr. Iannello agreed that high-temperature environments would be better served with controls outside the machines, but he said that most pumps, fans and motors aren’t operated in such extreme conditions. He also said that exposed wires and connectors were a primary factor in the failure of conventional magnetic bearings.

Packaging the controls inside may increase the popularity of magnetic bearings, Mr. Jumonville said, in part because of the reduced costs for cable installation. “It has a lot of promise,” he said of the new technology.
