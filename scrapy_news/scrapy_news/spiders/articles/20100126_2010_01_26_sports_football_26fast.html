No. 1 vs. No. 1: Its Rarer Than You Think

Chris Graythen/Getty Images

Drew Brees and the Saints didn’t look much like a No. 1 seed Sunday, barely escaping with a victory against a Vikings team that exposed plenty of weaknesses.

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

By JUDY BATTISTA
Published: January 25, 2010

The New Orleans Saints probably didn’t feel much like a No. 1 seed Monday, considering they barely escaped with a victory against a Vikings team that exposed plenty of weaknesses. Still, when the Saints and the Colts meet in Miami Gardens, Fla., in two weeks, the Super Bowl will feature the two most dominant regular-season teams, and the top seeds in each conference.

Since 1975, the top seeds in each conference have met in the Super Bowl eight times, the last time in the 1993 season, when the Cowboys beat the Bills in the Georgia Dome. Think that guarantees a classic game? Consider that the greatest Super Bowl — the Giants’ victory over the undefeated Patriots two years ago — featured a No. 1 seed and a wild card.

That is a testament to the parity that the former N.F.L. commissioner Pete Rozelle craved for the league. But the Saints’ shaky performance against the Vikings might have illustrated best why both top seeds rarely advance. The Saints and the Colts were the dominant teams in the regular season, starting 13-0 and 14-0, respectively. But while the Colts passed on a run at perfection, the Saints swooned, a skid that included an alarming loss to Tampa Bay at home in the next-to-last week of the regular season.

“I don’t know the No. 1, 2 or 3 seeds correlates to the best team,” said Brian Billick, whose Ravens team knocked off the top-seeded Tennessee Titans on its way to a Super Bowl title in the 2000 season. “You played best over a 16-game schedule, but that could have to do with your division, with the way you stayed healthy. Minnesota is clearly a better football team, and you know that because with five turnovers they still only lost by 3 points. New Orleans played the best in the regular season, but does being the No. 1 seed mean you are the best team?”

Billick said his Ravens team was not cowed by the Titans’ top seeding because it had beaten them in Tennessee during the regular season. But Billick also discounted the idea that top seeds felt more pressure because opponents were targeting them.

“When Baltimore went in to play New England, was it, ‘They’re not the one or two seed, so we can beat these guys?’ ” Billick said. “You don’t take that mentality. Being No. 1 does carry some pressure, but does that mean you don’t want to be the No. 1 seed? Everybody and their mothers want the bye.”

At least one top seed regularly makes it to the Super Bowl. In the 2004 season, the Steelers didn’t, but the Eagles did (they lost to the Patriots). In the 2005 season, the Colts didn’t, but the Seahawks did (they lost to the Steelers). Last year, neither top seed made it, and it wound up being a thrilling last-minute Steelers victory over the Cardinals.

Marv Levy was the coach of the Bills team that took the top seed against the Cowboys in Super Bowl XXVIII, so he knows better than most why it is so difficult for the best regular-season teams to complete their mission.

“The teams are much more closely matched than people realize,” Levy wrote in an e-mail message. “Injuries, weather conditions, a fumble at a crucial time, a missed field goal, a bad snap, a penalty, such as one that calls back a long punt return that would have been a touchdown, or a pass interference penalty of 45 yards on third-and-15, or a blown call by an official, a trick play that backfires, or works, can all make the wisdom of pregame analysts come up short. That is what makes it so much fun. And painful.”

Favre Watch Begins

The Brett Favre retirement-a-thon is in full swing again, but is Favre’s dithering even relevant until August?

There is plenty of blame to go around for the Vikings’ loss — let’s start with having 12 men in the huddle, an inexcusable coaching blunder that pushed the Vikings out of field-goal range.

But Favre’s horrible across-the-body interception — arguably just as bad as the one he threw to Corey Webster when the Packers lost the National Football Conference championship game to the Giants two years ago — was one reason the Packers ditched him, setting off his peripatetic march around the N.F.L. looking for another Super Bowl contender.

The Packers did not believe that Favre could carry a team to the Super Bowl again, no matter how outstanding his regular season was. Favre was beaten physically Sunday night, but he can clearly still play, and it’s hard to imagine the Vikings would have gotten this far with Tarvaris Jackson — last year the Vikings lost in the wild-card round, after all. Still, Favre’s last three seasons have ended almost identically.

Favre’s final pass for the Packers was intercepted by Webster. His next-to-last pass for the Jets was intercepted, completing a late-season, injury-aided meltdown that cost the Jets a trip to the playoffs last season. And if he goes out now, his final Vikings pass was an interception, too, that cost the team a chance to kick the game-winning field goal.

Was it all worth it for the Vikings? Brad Childress, who personally chauffeured Favre to the Vikings, then clashed with him late in the season about whether Favre should be pulled from games, may be wondering that for the rest of his career.

“I’d love to win the Super Bowl — who wouldn’t — but I can’t print anything for you guys, but I’m going out on top one way or the other,” Favre said after the game. “I didn’t think I had anything to prove coming in, but if there were doubters out there maybe I sold notice to them.” Referring to the possibility that he had played his last game, Favre said, “My goal was to get to Miami and obviously that is not going to happen, but if it is, no doubt I’m on top.”
