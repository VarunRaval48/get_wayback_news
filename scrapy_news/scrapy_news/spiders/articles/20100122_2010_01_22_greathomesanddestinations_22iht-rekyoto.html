An Oasis of Calm Near the Liveliest District of Kyoto

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By MIKI TANIKAWA
Published: January 26, 2010

KYOTO, JAPAN — This 1,200-year-old ancient capital of Japan, the birthplace of fine arts and cultural attractions like the maiko, or kimono-clad dance girls, is also home to one of the country’s most distinctive properties.

Jyun-shin-an, which literally means “a house of mellow heart,” stands on the elegant cobblestone street of Ishibe-Koji, just behind the touristy boulevard that runs in front of the Kodaiji Temple. The residence, now offered for sale, was constructed in 2003 in the sukiya style, an architectural format that was popularized in the 16th century for tea houses. “Suki” means “as you like” and “ya” means house.

It is not often that such a luxurious property — whose living space totals 290 square meters, or 3,121 square feet — goes up for sale, local real estate experts said. It is listed at ¥600 million, or $6.6 million.

The house was created as a discreet meeting place for politicians and business leaders, said Masao Chihara, chairman of Shinkobe-Tochi, the Kobe-based real estate agency that commissioned the structure and is now selling it. The property is within walking distance of the city’s legendary Gion district, perhaps the sexiest area in downtown Kyoto, where catching a glimpse of maiko dancers going in and out of tea houses and ryotei restaurants can be commonplace.

It has been used as a private membership guest house for Toyota and Panasonic executives, and it counts a former prime minister, Yoshiro Mori, among its guests.

The living space is divided between two structures, which have a central courtyard garden. There are two master bedrooms, two bathrooms, several meeting and conference rooms and a professional kitchen.

Takaaki Yamamoto, a celebrated Japanese architect, designed the structure, using a variety of precise woodwork patterns to decorate the ceilings, walls and sliding doors. The styles vary from room to room, and sometimes from wall to wall.

The selection of wood used in construction matched the effort used in its design. “It took one year for the builder just to find and collect the materials even before they started construction,” Mr. Chihara said. And as a result, a mild woody smell permeates much of the house.

A single piece of pine with a length of 18 meters, or 59 feet, was used to create one bedroom wall. A local cedar wood called kitayama sugi was used for the living room posts, while the bathrooms were lined with koya maki, an evergreen pine native to Japan and considered the most luxurious finish available for bathrooms.

While the design style is traditional, the property also has contemporary fittings like underfloor heating, futuristic style flush toilets, and an electronically controlled shutter that closes over the delicate window frames. Sales material also touts the house’s security features, like windows of reinforced glass and three emergency exit routes.

The property’s centerpiece is a spacious living/entertainment room, carpeted with tatami mats and a tokonoma, or alcove, adorned with tapestries. Sliding doors can also open onto an adjacent tatami room, where maiko dancers or other entertainers can perform, so the room “functions like a stage,” said Ryoichi Horikawa, a real estate agent with Mitsubishi Real Estate Services, which is marketing the property.

Jyun-shin-an stands on about 256 square meters of land among the century-old houses in the Higashiyama district, where the exterior walls of the residences line the narrow, winding streets, creating a special enclave that appeals to many top business executives looking for privacy. The quiet surroundings were especially noticeable during a daytime visit to the house, not far from streets that are packed and noisy on weekends.

Kyoto properties have long attracted international buyers. Recently, an unidentified American bought Kaiuso, a 19th-century-era home surrounded by one of the city’s most famous gardens on 6,000 tsubo, or almost 5 acres. The home, adjacent to the Nanzenji Temple in the city’s Sakyo district, was listed at ¥8 billion. The sale price was not disclosed.
