A Parish Tested

Haitian New Yorkers Hold On to Their Hope

Ruby Washington/The New York Times

Emmanuelle Deslouches's brother and many other relatives are missing in the devastation of Haiti. On Friday, she prayed at SS. Joachim and Anne Church in Queens Village. More Photos »

At SS. Joachim and Anne School on the eastern edge of Queens, a first grader has lost his father. A third grader is mourning an uncle. His father is still missing.

At the parish church opposite the school, the young Haitian priest who counsels the grieving struggles to comprehend that the landscape of his childhood has been obliterated: the elementary school his parents had sacrificed to send him to, their neighborhood church, the presidential palace he proudly passed on his way to his first communion at the National Cathedral — now, too, a ruined shell.

Jean Beni, 45, who sings in the choir of the church, in Queens Village, stared into space Friday night at the nearby Duch Travel Agency and shipping service on Linden Boulevard, absorbing the fact that it could take him weeks to get to Port-au-Prince to search for his mother, brother, sister and cousin.

One of the agency’s owners, David Duchatellier, had just left the office to rush home. He was pondering his own loss — a cousin’s toddler was crushed when a family house collapsed in Port-au-Prince — but his errand was for survivors. Four strangers, New Jersey high school teachers, were unloading four vanloads of clothing and food into his backyard to be shipped to Haiti — once he figures out which of his contacts there is left to receive it.

Across Linden Boulevard, identical television screens — flashing “Catastrophe in Haiti” — blared at the stylists in Lucille Grace and Beauty Hair Care and the equally glum barbers at De Jean Binette Hair Palace beside it.

Suddenly, a woman burst from a tiny wire-transfer office in the back of the barbershop, a cellphone pressed to her ear. Her face was illuminated with an almost wild expression of joy. Her husband was on the phone from Haiti. He had survived.

The parish of SS. Joachim and Anne is one of the hubs of New York’s Haitian community. The people are used to feeling the ripple effects of Haiti’s problems — hurricanes, political violence, grinding poverty — but have never had to absorb a crisis like the earthquake last week.

It instantly set off private chain reactions of pain and compassion that radiated through the church, its sister parish of Sacred Heart, surrounding neighborhoods like Cambria Heights, and parishioners’ workplaces from Nassau County to Manhattan. Even as the people were groping their way through intensely personal fears and losses, they tried to mobilize help for the larger crisis in their home country.

“This is too big for us. It’s way too big for us,” said the Rev. Jean-Moise Delva, the parochial vicar at SS. Joachim and Anne, just three years out of seminary, who by Friday had counseled four bereaved parishioners as he tried to reach his father.

“As a priest, what I can do is pray. Still, we question God. We question why,” he said. “But he promises, ‘I am with you.’ ”

The parish was founded in 1896 by German and Irish immigrants; the school opened in 1924. By the 1970s, the church began Masses in Creole for a small Haitian population. By 1992 — after decades of dictatorship, poverty and unrest in Haiti — the church was mostly Haitian. It’s now about 80 percent Haitian, with most of the rest from Latin America or English-speaking Caribbean countries.

The influx from Haiti, which is 80 percent Catholic, has kept the church fervent, said Linda Freebes, the school principal for the past 20 years. “I’m not Haitian, but I might as well be,” she said. She travels from one side of Queens to the other for Mass at SS. Joachim and Anne.

Catholicism, she said, has become “milk-warm” in other parishes; “It’s not milk-warm here.”

“They’re a little bit like Job,” she said of her adopted community. “Whatever is put in front of them — they’re faith-filled people — they come out the other side.”

On Tuesday evening, as Father Delva, 34, held a weekly prayer service, people ran into the church screaming and crying. The aftershocks were starting to be felt. That night, at least one parent at the school rushed to board a plane for the Dominican Republic, planning to drive across the border to look for relatives.

On Wednesday, the Rev. Robert Robinson, the pastor, decided to keep the church open for worship all day for the nine days of a novena, a traditionally powerful prayer, starting an ebb and flow of grief and contemplation that continues in the modern, rectilinear sanctuary.

At school, Mrs. Freebes had her students write the names of the missing on blue construction paper and linked the strips into a giant rosary that now lies in the church.
