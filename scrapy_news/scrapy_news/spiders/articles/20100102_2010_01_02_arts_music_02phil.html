A New Years Eve Rite, Without a Formal Tradition

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
By ALLAN KOZINN
Published: January 1, 2010

Orchestras and their audiences like traditions, and for many, New Year’s Eve concerts are reserved either for the sheer celebratory frothiness of Strauss waltzes and polkas or for Beethoven’s Ninth Symphony, with its loftier paean to joy and hope.

Well into the 1990s, the New York Philharmonic was mostly inclined toward Strauss, but during Kurt Masur’s tenure as music director, it began to experiment. Since then it has offered everything from Leroy Anderson miniatures (if New Year’s Eve music must be lightweight, why not revive some American examples?) to theater songs, with Beethoven and Strauss each making an occasional return.

The Philharmonic has, in other words, not quite settled on a tradition of its own, and that is probably for the best, since in purely musical terms, even its most successful experiments have not yielded programs you might want to come back for year after year. One reason may be that it has never settled the debate about whether these concerts should offer a moment of sobriety (however celebratory) or jump headlong into the evening’s festivities.

In his first New Year’s Eve concert as the Philharmonic’s music director on Thursday, Alan Gilbert had it both ways. Devoting his program fully to American music, he started with Copland’s “Appalachian Spring” Suite, the evening’s most substantial and reflective work, then moved steadily toward the party: a group of Copland folk song settings gave way to a handful of Cole Porter show tunes, and a vigorous, clattering account of Gershwin’s “American in Paris” closed the program, leaving time enough for an audience singalong of “Auld Lang Syne.”

Beginning with “Appalachian Spring” was an inspired touch. There are few better known or more widely beloved American symphonic works. And the suite from Copland’s ballet score moves easily between the meditative and the ebullient, with a touch of the devotional, as well, in its inventive variations on the Shaker hymn “Simple Gifts.”

It is also music this orchestra has always played magnificently, and it did so for Mr. Gilbert, whose interpretive style is coming more clearly into focus with each performance. Unlike his two immediate predecessors, Mr. Masur and Lorin Maazel, he seems intent on presenting the Philharmonic’s virtuosity as a given, rather than as a quality that demands to be shown off constantly. Here the richness of the string and woodwind tone and the punchy clarity of the brasses were offered almost matter-of-factly within a remarkably trim, focused reading.

That trimness may seem coolly distant to listeners used to the Philharmonic as a symphonic whirlwind, but seen from a different perspective, it is a refreshing change. And it is not as if Mr. Gilbert had prevented his players from pulling out all the stops when the music demanded it. The Gershwin could not have been more energetic or assertive, nor could its painterly, cosmopolitan vision have been presented more vividly.

The baritone Thomas Hampson was the soloist in the Copland and Porter songs. Drawing on both sets of “Old American Songs,” Mr. Hampson tapped into the music’s many accents and varieties of straightforward folksiness, offsetting the gracefulness of “Simple Gifts” with the cowpoke charm of “The Little Horses.”

The Porter songs were less satisfying. Undoubtedly because these favorites — among them, “Night and Day,” “In the Still of the Night” and “Begin the Beguine” — were offered in dense, theatrical orchestrations, Mr. Hampson used a microphone, which gave his voice a distractingly tinny cast and magnified the operatic approach to tone production that always seems beside the point in popular songs. But Mr. Hampson was clearly having fun with this music, and in his best moments, that was enough.
