The Latest Gadgets to Distract Drivers

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
Published: January 14, 2010

To the Editor:

Skip to next paragraph
Enlarge This Image
Ted McGrath

Re “Despite Risks, Internet Creeps Onto Dashboard” (“Driven to Distraction” series, front page, Jan. 7):

“Infotainment” systems mounted on the dashboard of cars were rolled out at the Consumer Electronics Show. But don’t worry. When you fire up the Audi system, it warns, “Please only use the online services when traffic conditions allow you to do so safely.” I feel better already!

I propose that insurance companies fight this idiocy by refusing to cover vehicles with such systems. They also need to develop ways to tag drivers involved in accidents while on a cellphone or texting. If that happens, increase the deductible by a factor of 10. Instead of $1,000, for example, make it $10,000.

Tort lawyers should jump all over this, win huge settlements and make it too expensive for companies to give lethal toys to a public all too willing to tempt fate.

Michael Pennock
Alexandria, Minn., Jan.

7, 2010

The writer is a safety engineer.

•

To the Editor:

In 1970 the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration was formed for the express purpose of reducing death and injury caused by motor vehicle crashes. Given the abundant research clearly showing that any distraction while driving drastically raises the risk of an accident, it baffles me that the agency hasn’t taken strong action against the increasing number of things that take a driver’s attention away from the road.

Your article underscores the fact that there is currently no federal barrier to manufacturers’ insanity.

Whatever its past accomplishments, the time is now for the agency to take decisive action to block the current reckless and irresponsible trends. Otherwise, it’s a toothless tiger, and a captive of the industry it is supposed to be monitoring.

Theodora Briggs Sweeney
Horsham, Pa., Jan.

9, 2010

•

To the Editor:

The development of yet another way to create highway mayhem will inevitably have an effect on automobile insurance rates. Increased bloodshed seems to be a trifling matter to automobile manufacturers compared with sales and profits. But as an avowed nonuser of any such high-tech device, I resent the prospect of having to pay for the damages caused by other drivers’ addiction to constant electronic stimulation.

Lewis Banci
Simsbury, Conn., Jan.

7, 2010

•

To the Editor:

In December 2006 my Honda Civic went up the side of the Palisades Parkway in New Jersey, flipped over backward and landed on its top. I had taken my hands entirely off of the wheel to dial my cellphone. I was unhurt but had difficulty explaining why I had done this.

Driving is above all a mental activity. The time that it would take to read the warning about using Audi’s screen only “when traffic conditions allow” would be just enough to cause an accident. Ford’s new screens next to the speedometer could easily make you miss an expressway exit or forget how fast you are driving.

All of these new systems should be restricted to use when the car is stationary.

Peter A. Joseph
New York, Jan.

9, 2010

•

To the Editor:

I sympathize with the automakers and the financial woes that they face, but the plan to have Internet access in cars, even in a limited way, is a recipe for death, plain and simple. If, as you say, cellphones alone were the cause of 2,600 fatal accidents back in 2003, then I fear for my life once the Internet is on most drivers’ dashboards.

I talk about changing my lifestyle so that I can live car-free. This might be the catalyst that I need to actually do it.

Jonathan Praff
Silver Spring, Md., Jan.

8, 2010
