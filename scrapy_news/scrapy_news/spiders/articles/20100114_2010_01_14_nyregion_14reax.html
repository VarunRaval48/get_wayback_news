Anxious Haitians in U.S. Search for Information

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints
By ANAHAD OCONNOR and DAMIEN CAVE
Published: January 13, 2010

As news of the devastation wrought by a powerful earthquake trickled out of Haiti on Tuesday evening, Ricot Dupuy, a disc jockey at Radio Soleil d’Haiti in Brooklyn, sat alone in his radio booth fielding a flurry of phone calls from local listeners.

One caller shared a rumor that the country’s presidential palace had collapsed. (A Haitian official said the palace had indeed been damaged but was still standing.) Another told Mr. Dupuy that the wife of the owner of Radio Melodie, a popular radio station in Haiti and a main source of news from the country, had died in the quake, a rumor that was unconfirmed. Others called in seeking information that Mr. Dupuy did not have.

From the heart of Brooklyn’s Haitian community in Flatbush to Little Haiti in Miami, the reports of the devastating earthquake left Haitian Americans scrambling for information about the fate of relatives. Many crowded around television screens for any reports of casualties, or desperately tried to reach friends and family in their homeland to no avail.

At Louis Market in Miami’s Little Haiti, customers began streaming in shortly after news of the quake hit the airwaves, buying $5 phone cards to make calls to Haiti.

“Everyone who walks in here is crazy, worried, depressed,” said Myrlande Cherenfant, 20, whose family owns the market. “They want to talk to their family members but they can’t get through.

Her own family, which left Haiti more than a decade ago, was also trying to reach relatives left behind in Cap-Haitien, but with no success, not even a busy signal. “It’s a dead line,” Ms. Cherenfant said. “There’s nothing.”

United States Representative Kendrick Meek, who represents, portions of Miami where many Haitians live, advised any citizens wondering about their family in Haiti, to call 1-888-407-4747, a phone line set up by the State Department in conjunction with American Citizen Services.

In Flatbush, where Little Haiti is situated among other Caribbean-American communities, Mr. Dupuy, the disc jockey, was mostly in the dark as well, left to make sense of troubling reports that could not be confirmed because communications links had been knocked out. New York has more than 100,000 people of Haitian ancestry, and in Flatbush, many said they had spent hours trying to make calls to their homeland for any scraps of information, but mostly to hear a voice or two and confirm that their loved ones were still alive.

What they got instead were news reports that left little hope. The earthquake, which struck just before 5 p.m. Eastern time with an estimated magnitude of 7.0, leveled homes and buildings throughout the capital of Port-au-Prince and sowed panic and chaos in the streets. The extent of the devastation was unknown, but it was clear to those trying to reach friends and family on the island that the nation’s communication infrastructure had also taken a blow.

As Mr. Dupuy sat in front of his microphone and radio controls on Tuesday evening doing a live broadcast, he also repeatedly clicked on the Web site of Radio Melodie, trying to get updates. But to no avail. The site had been down for hours after the quake. For a moment, Mr. Dupuy turned his thoughts to relief efforts, before deciding that it was too soon even for that.

“It’s too early at this time,” he said. “People are more concerned to find out about their families.”

Across Flatbush, many concerned Haitians gave up dialing and left their apartments to gather at restaurants, stores and community centers, where they shared news and rumors and stared at television screens. At Factor Music at 1698 Nostrand Avenue, Frantz St. Germain, a Haitian-American limousine driver, stood around with others watching CNN. He said he had been in the United States for 20 years, but was worried about the fates of a cousin, a nephew, a niece and an uncle who were still in Haiti. The owner of the store, Fritz Duverter, stood next to him.

“Every other minute it is getting worse,” he said, staring at the images of collapsed buildings flashing on the screen, worry written on his face.
