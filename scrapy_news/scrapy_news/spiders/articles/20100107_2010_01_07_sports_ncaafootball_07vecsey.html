Namath Has His Own Memories of 65

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By GEORGE VECSEY
Published: January 6, 2010

No doubt about it, Joe Namath said Wednesday, he crossed the goal line with the ball on that fateful New Year’s Day of 1965.

“But I didn’t score,” Namath added quickly.

That was the sportsmanlike logic of Coach Bear Bryant on the sideline that evening, and it remains the way Namath looks at the play, 45 years later.

Namath and other Alabama players were sure they saw one official raise his arms to signal a touchdown on Namath’s quarterback sneak from a foot or two out, but the official whose decision counted moved the ball back and signaled a first down to Texas.

That was the end of the most famous play of that epic Orange Bowl game, won by Texas, 21-17, now being reprised Thursday night in the Rose Bowl between Texas and Alabama for the national championship.

Namath was calling from his home in Florida on Wednesday. He is not going out to Pasadena, Calif., for the big game because airports have become too much of a hassle, he said.

•

“I got both knees replaced in 1992,” Namath said with that lush Alabama drawl that long ago replaced his original western Pennsylvania accent.

“Ever since 9/11, I always get patted down when I go through airport security,” Namath said. “Only two times since that awful day did I not set off the alarm, and both times it was at J.F.K. in New York. I asked the guys and they said sometimes it’s just set differently.”

Namath has had enough of the increased misery of air travel. He’s staying home, to watch Alabama on Thursday night and then his old Jets team in the N.F.L. playoffs at Cincinnati on Saturday afternoon.

He will miss his old Jets teammate Pete Lammons, who is going out to the Rose Bowl. Lammons bedeviled Alabama on offense and defense in that Orange Bowl showdown in 1965. Alabama was undefeated and had already been voted the national champion according to the rules that season. Texas had lost once.

Namath insists he crossed the goal line with the ball. “I can’t tell from the films,” he said, referring to one blurry version in which his right shoulder dips forward before contact. But at the time, Namath said, he looked down and saw the ball past the goal line.

A few years later, one of the Texas tackles on that play, Diron Talbert, was playing for Washington in the N.F.L. and delighted in telling how Namath had shouted “touchdown.” But, Talbert insisted, he had made the famous Texas Hook ’em Horns signal with index finger and pinkie, moving the two fingers as close together as he could and telling Namath, “Sorry, Joe, you missed it by that much.” It made for a good story for Talbert.

The Alabama players were sure they had been cheated, but Bryant immediately told them on the sideline that the referees made the call, and that was that. Besides, there were seven minutes left in the game.

Namath said the sporting tone for that game was set a few days before when the two squads attended a dinner at a country club near Miami.

•

“There was great camaraderie between the two squads,” Namath recalled. “That was because of the great admiration Coach Bryant and Coach Darrell Royal had for each other. I learned from both those coaches.”

According to Lammons — who became Namath’s tight end in the Jets’ Super Bowl victory in 1969 — the Texas players did their best not to hurt Namath, who was known to have a bad knee.

“Maybe Pete didn’t,” Namath said Wednesday, chuckling. “But I don’t know if old Tommy Nobis and Diron Talbert and Tom Currie got that message.” Anyway, Namath survived that game, and a few hours later he was in New York, signing with the Jets for $400,000 — a monumental figure in those days.

At that point, it hardly mattered whether Namath had squirmed across the line on his weakened knee. He was a legend. And remains so to this day.

In another recounting of that Texas-Alabama game on Tuesday, two details were overlooked: it was the first time the Orange Bowl was played at night, for prime-time television; and both the Texas and Alabama squads were all white.

Texas would play its first African-American player, Julius Whittier, in 1969. Alabama would suit up its first African-American player, John Mitchell, in 1971.

“How’s that song go?” Royal, now 85, said in 2005, getting the gist of the Bob Dylan ’60s anthem. “Things they are a-changing. But they weren’t changing that quickly around here at the time.”

Mitchell is the Pittsburgh Steelers’ defensive line coach, in his 37th season of coaching; Whittier is a prosecutor in Dallas. The times were indeed a-changing.

One sign of these times is that Joe Namath’s knees set off alarms in airports. If there had been electronic surveillance on that goal line in Miami in 1965, Namath might have set off different sounds. Forty-five years later, Namath is comfortable with Bryant’s edict that night. No whistle, no touchdown.

An earlier version misstated the given name of a Texas tackle in the 1965 Orange Bowl game. He is Diron Talbert, not Don, who is his brother.

This article has been revised to reflect the following correction:

Correction: January 8, 2010
The Sports of The Times column on Wednesday, about the 1965 Orange Bowl game, between Alabama and Texas, the same teams that played in the Bowl Championship Series title game on Thursday night, misstated the given name of a tackle who helped stop Alabama’s Joe Namath at the goal line on the game’s pivotal play. He is Diron Talbert, who played at Texas from 1964 to 1966 — not Don, his brother, who played there from 1959 to 1961. The error was repeated on Thursday in a Sports of The Times column about Namath’s memories of the game.
