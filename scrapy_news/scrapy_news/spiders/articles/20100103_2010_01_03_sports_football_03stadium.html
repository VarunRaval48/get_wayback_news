Even at Home, Jets Have Felt Like Visitors

  • Print
  • Reprints
By JOE LAPOINTE
Published: January 2, 2010

After the Dodgers left town in 1957, Alan Titan of Brooklyn was a fan without a team. That changed when he and some City College classmates took the subway one Sunday afternoon to see the debut of a new professional football franchise.

By 1969, Alan Titan had been a Jets fan for nine years; he was there from the beginning when they were the Titans and played in the A.F.L.

Despite wet and windy weather and few fans in the decaying Polo Grounds in Upper Manhattan, it was love at first sight for Titan as the New York Titans of the fledgling American Football League defeated the Buffalo Bills, 27-3, on Sept. 11, 1960.

“It was starting up right before my eyes,” Titan said. “Everything was new and hopeful. I became an anti-Giants fan. You could say I hate the Giants.”

The Titans changed their name to Jets in 1963 and moved to the new Shea Stadium in 1964, when Titan first bought season tickets for $5 a game. He has had them ever since, including the last 26 seasons at Giants Stadium, the Jets’ third home.

Titan, a retired electronics engineer, will watch on television Sunday night from his winter home in Boynton Beach, Fla., when the Jets, not the Giants, play the Cincinnati Bengals in what is likely to be the final game at Giants Stadium.

Despite franchise roots of a half-century, some Jets fans cannot shed the underdog status that comes with being the second franchise in a two-team town, perpetual underclassmen to the Giants’ upperclassmen.

One such observer is Boomer Esiason, a former Jets quarterback who grew up on Long Island and now works as a New York-based sportscaster in radio and television.

“The Jets fan has always had a complex, feeling like a second-class citizen,” Esiason said. “It’s an inferiority complex. It’s the negative nature of the Jets fan.”

Success on Sunday when they can qualify for the playoffs with a win would elevate the Jets (8-7) — for this season, anyway — over the Giants (8-7), who close their season Sunday at Minnesota and will miss the playoffs for the first time since 2004.

Esiason said the stereotype of a Jets fan was a working-class union member, perhaps a police officer or a firefighter from Brooklyn or Queens, who drove an old Chevrolet to the games and played football during his tailgate picnic.

The cliché Giants fan, Esiason said, might be an older white-collar worker from a suburb who drives a shiny BMW and grills steaks to enjoy with wine before kickoff. Jets fans, he said, always fear the worst.

“The prevailing thought among Jets Nation is that the Jets will lose to the Bengals because that’s what the Jets do,” Esiason said. “They bring us to the brink of happiness, and then they tear our hearts out.”

Esiason attended Jets games during the Joe Namath era at Shea Stadium with his father who, he said, was a devoted Giants fan.

Recalling the Jets’ period of his 14-year career — 1993 through 1995, when the team went 17-31 — Esiason somberly said: “I was in the midst of the dark ages. Parts of it I’d rather forget.”

Even after the franchise’s greatest moment of glory — the Namath-led upset over the N.F.L.’s Baltimore Colts in the Super Bowl after the 1968 season — the Jets remained second-tier tenants at home.

They played their first five games of 1969 on the road because the Mets occupied Shea. Paul Farash, who attended his first Jets game at age 8 in 1966, said the Jets’ home opener was postponed from Sunday to Monday to clean up the field from the Mets’ World Series celebration. Farash, who saves Jets memorabilia, said, “I have been to at least 325 Jets games and plan on going to at least 325 more.”

Among those 1969 champion Jets were receiver Don Maynard and linebacker Larry Grantham, original Titans from 1960.

Maynard recalled that if the Mets were in a late-season homestand, the Jets sometimes practiced on Rikers Island and prisoners from the nearby jail watched.

If a ball landed near them, Maynard said, players forced equipment managers to get it. After practice, he said, they would bus back to Shea to shower and stay for baseball.

Maynard, a member of the Pro Football Hall of Fame, also played for the Giants in the N.F.L. championship game of 1958, which the Colts won in overtime.

Maynard said the horseshoe shape of the Polo Grounds provided great sideline angles for fans, but Grantham said it was not a pleasant place.

“The Polo Grounds had rats bigger than some of our linemen,” he said, adding that the lights were dim and there were so few fans “my wife had her own personal hot dog vendor.”

Shea Stadium was a step up. Randy Beverly, a cornerback in those days, said, “Shea was home, we almost lived there.” He said he used to walk to work from his home in Corona, Queens.

At practice on Rikers, Beverly said, “you might see an old friend from the neighborhood” in custody. Beverly, who intercepted two passes in the Super Bowl victory, recently visited the new $1.6 billion stadium the Jets and the Giants will share.

Beverly, alongside the former Jets Bruce Harper and Joe Klecko, walked across the artificial turf, posed for pictures and gazed up at the stands with suites and club seats.

“It’s modern,” Klecko said. “It’s opulent.”

Harper said he preferred Shea to Giants Stadium because of its lush natural grass (except for the dirt of the baseball infield). Harper even disliked the name of the current place.

“I never called it G-Stadium,” he said, refusing to speak the full word. “It was always the Meadowlands.”

But Harper also recalled driving from his New Jersey home to college at Kutztown State in Pennsylvania in the 1970s and pulling off the turnpike to peer through a fence and watch the construction of Giants Stadium, wondering if he would ever play in such a place.

The Jets left Shea after the 1983 season. Until last season, when they moved into their new training facility in New Jersey, they were a team of two states, practicing on Long Island and playing in New Jersey. A proposal for a $2 billion stadium on the West Side of Manhattan for the Jets and potentially the Olympics fell through.

Klecko said it was better that the team now trained closer to its stadium. He was a defensive lineman and a member of the so-called New York Sack Exchange. He said Jets fans were in some ways superior to their blue-clad neighbors.

“The Giant fan sort of sits there and claps his hands,” Klecko said. “The Jet fans are screaming. They are different. They are more passionate.”

Certainly there are more of them than there used to be. Bob Mischak, an offensive lineman who was the first captain of the Titans, recalled opening day at the Polo Grounds and being stunned at the emptiness.

“There wasn’t anybody in the stadium,” he said. The players joked among themselves, he said, about skipping the pregame introductions to just shake hands with every customer.

Like Maynard, Mischak also played for the 1958 Giants. He had mistakenly assumed that the enthusiasm for the N.F.L. would transfer instantly to the A.F.L.

“When you’re young, you’re optimistic,” he said.

The announced attendance at the opener at the Polo Grounds was 9,607, with paid admissions announced as 5,727. Close to 80,000 are expected Sunday night, and Mischak said he would watch on television from his California home.

He said that he hoped the Jets would reach the playoffs and that he could attend a game in the new stadium next season.

“They’ve come a long way,” Mischak said of the Titans/Jets franchise. “I think that’s great. I wish them well.”
