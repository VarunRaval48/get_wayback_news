Following Long Wait, Dawson Enters Hall Alone

  • comments
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Reprints
By TYLER KEPNER
Published: January 6, 2010

Andre Dawson studied the voting from last year’s Hall of Fame election, and two figures stood out: 67 percent and 63 percent. The first was his percentage of votes, and the second was Bert Blyleven’s. Dawson predicted that this time, they would clear the 75 percent threshold needed for election.

“We were both right there,” Dawson said Wednesday, “and I felt that this is the year we would get in.”

Dawson was half right. He was elected to the Hall of Fame on Wednesday, receiving 77.9 percent of the votes cast by 10-year members of the Baseball Writers’ Association of America. Blyleven came agonizingly close, with 74.2 percent, missing by just five votes.

Dawson, an outfielder for the Montreal Expos, the Chicago Cubs, the Boston Red Sox and the Florida Marlins, received 420 votes among the 539 ballots cast in his ninth season of eligibility. Blyleven had 400 votes, and Roberto Alomar had 397 in his first appearance on the ballot. Candidates needed 405 to be elected.

A combination of power, speed and defense made Dawson an eight-time All-Star and the 1987 National League most valuable player. He is one of only three players to exceed 400 homers and 300 stolen bases, with Barry Bonds and Willie Mays, and he won eight Gold Gloves.

Dawson’s on-base percentage was just .323, the lowest among Hall of Fame outfielders, and he never played in the World Series. But his performance in the bedrock statistics — home runs and runs batted in — elevated his candidacy.

There are 22 players eligible for the Hall of Fame who had at least 400 homers and 1,500 runs batted in, as Dawson did. All are now enshrined except Fred McGriff, a first-time candidate who got just 21.5 percent of the vote on Wednesday.

After Dawson, Blyleven and Alomar, Jack Morris was next with 52.3 percent, followed by Barry Larkin (51.6 percent), Lee Smith (47.3 percent), Edgar Martinez (36.2 percent) and Tim Raines (30.4 percent).

Mark McGwire, whose 583 career homers are obscured by his role in the steroids scandal, received just 23.7 percent. McGwire’s totals have been virtually unchanged over his four years on the ballot. He reached 23.5 percent in 2007, 23.6 percent in 2008 and 21.9 percent last year.

Among players appearing on the ballot for the first time, only Alomar, Larkin, Martinez and McGriff will appear on future ballots. Eleven others failed to reach the 5 percent needed to remain. Players lose their eligibility after 15 years on the ballot.

Blyleven has two more tries to make it through the writers’ association and is all but assured of election. Every player who has received at least 64 percent of the vote has eventually been enshrined.

“I’m a little disappointed, but I just got a phone call from Harmon Killebrew and he just said, ‘Hey, hang in there, you’re going to be in there,’ ” Blyleven said. “I think that’s right. Brooks Robinson sent me a note the other day, you know, ‘Hey, your time is coming,’ so it’s just wait. Five votes short is a little bit on the sad side, but my percentages jumped up a lot from last year and I really appreciate that.”

Blyleven, a 287-game winner who ranks fifth on the career strikeout list, has experienced a remarkable surge in support from his debut on the ballot in 1998, when he got just 18 percent of the vote.

By 2006, Blyleven had more than half the vote, and now he is at the doorstep. Statistically minded writers, in particular, have taken up Blyleven’s cause, pointing not only to his 3,701 strikeouts and 60 shutouts but also to his bad luck. Blyleven lost 18 games in which he allowed one or no earned runs, and won just four times while allowing at least five earned runs.

“You can’t always go off wins,” Blyleven said. “You have to go off of performance and if you kept your club in the game.”

Blyleven and Alomar, a 12-time All-Star who won 10 Gold Gloves, will probably make it next year, when the first-time candidates include Jeff Bagwell, Rafael Palmeiro and Larry Walker. The 2012 class is weaker, headlined by Tim Salmon and Bernie Williams.

Alomar, who did not respond to a voice-mail message, might have made it this year if not for the notorious 1996 incident in which he spit on the umpire John Hirschbeck.

“I cannot control what they’re going to say,” Alomar told Newsday. “The only thing I can control is that I’m friends with him. He’s a fan of mine. We became friends. He forgave me. That’s all that matters to me. I don’t think they should use that as an excuse. I think they should look at the numbers and my statistics, what I did on the field. And I think I did a good job.”

Dawson, now an executive with the Marlins, is revered around baseball for his integrity. He endured persistent knee pain from the artificial turf in Montreal and has had two replacement surgeries.

In 1987, when baseball owners were colluding against the players, Dawson was so eager to play on natural grass that he gave the Cubs a blank check. General Manager Dallas Green asked Dawson if he would take $500,000. Dawson had made more than $1 million in 1986, but he quickly accepted.

“I think they made an offer for me to turn down,” he said. “But I wanted the game to be fun again.”

The Cubs finished last, but Dawson smashed 49 homers and was named M.V.P. It was the greatest honor of his career until Wednesday, when he received the highest honor of all.
